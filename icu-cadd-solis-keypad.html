ICU Medical CADD-Solis

Keypad Not Responding

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

ICU Medical

Model

CADD-Solis

What This Guide Helps With

This guide walks you through simple checks when a CADD-Solis pump has keypad issues.

  • One or more buttons not responding
  • Entire keypad unresponsive
  • Delayed or intermittent button response
  • Unable to program infusion due to keypad failure

These steps focus on the most common and easily verified causes before assuming keypad membrane failure, ribbon cable issues, or internal PCB faults.

You do not need advanced technical skills — just follow the steps carefully and observe what happens.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Confirm the Pump Is Not Frozen

  • Observe whether the display is updating.
  • Check for active alarms or error messages.
  • Attempt multiple buttons.

If the display is static and nothing responds, the pump may be frozen rather than experiencing keypad failure.

2. Power Cycle the Pump

  • Turn the pump off.
  • Wait at least 10 seconds.
  • Turn it back on and allow it to fully boot.

Temporary software or logic faults can cause the keypad to stop responding. This resolves many intermittent issues.

3. Inspect the Keypad Surface

Examine:

  • Keypad membrane for cracks or bubbling
  • Worn or faded button areas
  • Signs of fluid intrusion
  • Sticky or hardened residue

High-use buttons (Start/Stop, Enter, navigation keys) are most likely to fail first.

4. Check for Cleaning Residue or Fluid Intrusion

  • Inspect edges of keypad for signs of liquid seepage
  • Ensure no pooling of disinfectant under membrane
  • Clean gently per facility policy and allow to fully dry

Repeated aggressive cleaning can degrade membrane contacts over time.

5. Test All Buttons Individually

  • Press each button one at a time.
  • Note whether specific buttons fail consistently.
  • Determine if failure is isolated or entire keypad is unresponsive.

If only certain keys fail, likely membrane contact failure. If all keys fail, suspect internal ribbon connection or main board issue.

6. Check Battery and Power Status

  • Ensure battery is adequately charged.
  • Confirm pump is not in low-power or fault state.
  • Verify proper external power connection if applicable.

Low voltage conditions can sometimes cause erratic keypad behavior.

7. Observe Behavior Pattern

Document:

  • Does keypad work after reboot but fail later?
  • Are only high-use keys affected?
  • Does pressing firmly temporarily restore function?
  • Is failure constant or intermittent?

If the Keypad Remains Unresponsive

If the keypad remains unresponsive after completing the steps above, the issue is likely internal and cannot be corrected through bedside troubleshooting.

  • Keypad membrane failure
  • Ribbon cable disconnection
  • Main PCB fault

The best next step is to remove the pump from clinical service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

Example: “Keypad buttons not responding; unable to program infusion.”

Cause

Examples:
“Pump power-cycled and inspected. Specific keypad buttons remain non-functional. Suspected keypad membrane failure.”
“Entire keypad non-responsive after reboot. Suspected internal PCB or ribbon cable issue.”

Resolution

Examples:
“Removed pump from service, labeled out of service, sent for repair.”
“Tagged for CE bench evaluation.”
“Cleaned keypad surface; function restored and returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Power cycled (yes / no)
  • Visible membrane damage (yes / no)
  • Specific buttons affected
  • Entire keypad unresponsive (yes / no)
  • Signs of fluid intrusion
  • Final disposition

Final Thought

Most keypad issues are caused by temporary software freeze, membrane wear from high use, or fluid/cleaning damage. Internal failures are less common but do occur in high-use clinical environments.

That is successful troubleshooting.
