ZOLL R Series – Battery Fails Self-Test

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

ZOLL

Model

R Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide walks you through simple checks when a ZOLL R Series defibrillator reports that the battery has failed its self-test. These steps focus on the most common and easiest things to verify first before assuming the battery or device has failed.

You do not need advanced technical skills to perform these checks.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Make Sure the Battery Is Fully Seated

  • Confirm the battery is installed correctly in the defibrillator.
  • Remove the battery and reinsert it, making sure it clicks or seats firmly into place.
  • A loose battery can cause self-test failures even if the battery itself is good.

Check the Battery Contacts

  • Inspect the battery and device contacts for:
    • dirt or debris
    • corrosion
    • bent or damaged pins
  • If safe to do so, gently clean visible debris from the contacts and reinsert the battery.
  • Poor contact between the battery and device can cause charging and self-test issues.

Verify the Battery Has Been Charging

  • Confirm the defibrillator has been plugged into AC power when stored.
  • Check for any charging or status indicators that show the battery has been receiving power.
  • A battery that has not been charging may fail self-test due to low capacity.

Try a Known-Good Battery (If Available)

  • Remove the current battery and insert a known-good battery from another unit.
  • Allow the unit to complete its self-test again.
  • This helps determine whether the issue is with the battery or the defibrillator itself.

Observe the Self-Test Message or Indicator

  • Note exactly what the display or status indicator shows:
    • Does it say “Battery Fail”?
    • Does it say “Replace Battery”?
    • Does it fail immediately or after charging?
  • Even small details can help determine the cause.

If the Battery Still Fails Self-Test

If the battery continues to fail the self-test after completing the steps above, that’s okay — you have already checked the most common and simplest causes.

At this point, the issue is likely related to the battery’s internal cells or electronics and cannot be resolved through basic external checks.

You have already:

  • confirmed the battery is seated properly
  • checked the contacts
  • verified charging
  • tested with another battery (if available)
  • observed the self-test message

The best next step is to:

  • remove the battery from clinical use
  • clearly label it as Out of Service
  • replace it with a known-good battery or send it for evaluation

Knowing when to remove a battery from service is part of good troubleshooting and patient safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the user.

Example: “Defibrillator reports battery failed self-test.”

Cause

What you observed or determined during troubleshooting.

Example: “Battery fails self-test after reseating and charging. Alternate battery passes self-test. Suspected failed battery pack.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:

  • “Removed battery from service and replaced with known-good battery.”
  • “Tagged battery out of service and sent for evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Battery reseated: yes / no
  • Contacts inspected: yes / no
  • Alternate battery tested: yes / no
  • Self-test message displayed
  • Any unusual heat or swelling
  • Final status (battery replaced or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Batteries are consumable parts and will eventually fail over time. A failed self-test is the device’s way of preventing unreliable operation during an emergency.

Completing these steps means you have:

  • ✔ ruled out simple connection and charging issues
  • ✔ protected patient safety
  • ✔ documented properly
  • ✔ avoided unnecessary guesswork

That is successful troubleshooting.
