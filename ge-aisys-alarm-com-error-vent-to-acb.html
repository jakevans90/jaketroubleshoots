GE Healthcare Aisys

Alarm: COM ERROR VENT TO ACB

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Aisys

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the COM ERROR VENT TO ACB alarm on a GE Healthcare Aisys anesthesia machine. This alarm indicates a communication problem between the ventilator side of the system and the Anesthesia Controller Board, which may cause a system malfunction and affect ventilator control or reliability.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the anesthesia machine from patient use if the alarm is active, recurring, or associated with ventilation failure.
  • Have clinical staff support the patient with another anesthesia machine, manual ventilation, or an appropriate backup device.
  • Do not continue using the machine for patient care with an unresolved system malfunction alarm.
  • Confirm the patient is safely supported before troubleshooting the machine.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the displayed alarm is COM ERROR VENT TO ACB.
  • Check whether the screen also displays System Malfunction.
  • Note whether the alarm occurs during startup, POST, when ventilation begins, intermittently during use, or immediately after reboot.
  • Check for related symptoms such as ventilator unavailable, Vent Fail condition, monitoring-only operation, loss of ventilation controls, repeated system malfunction alarms, or alarm entries in the error log.

3. Understand What the Alarm Means

  • This alarm indicates that communication had already been established between the ventilator side of the system and the Anesthesia Controller Board, but communication was then lost.
  • The anesthesia machine expected regular messages from the Vent SIB CPU but stopped receiving them long enough to declare a communication failure.
  • Treat this as a ventilator communication fault, not a breathing circuit or patient circuit problem.
  • Possible causes include a temporary software or startup fault, loose or damaged cabling, Ventilator Interface Board issue, Anesthesia Controller Board issue, or related harness problem.

4. Reboot the System

  • Remove the machine from clinical use.
  • Power the Aisys system down using the normal shutdown process.
  • Wait long enough for the system to fully power down.
  • Restart the machine and allow it to complete startup and POST.
  • Observe whether the COM ERROR VENT TO ACB alarm returns.
  • If the alarm clears, complete the required checkout and verify normal ventilator operation before returning the device to service.

5. Check Power Source and Startup Conditions

  • Confirm the anesthesia machine is connected to a known-good AC power outlet.
  • Verify the power cord is secure at the wall and at the machine.
  • Check for signs of power interruption, loose plug connection, damaged cord, or failed outlet.
  • If appropriate, test the outlet or move the machine to a known-good power source.
  • Restart the machine and confirm it powers up normally without unstable power behavior.

6. Check for Obvious External or Physical Issues

  • Inspect the machine for signs of damage, fluid intrusion, impact, loose panels, or recent movement.
  • Ask staff whether the alarm started after the machine was moved, cleaned, bumped, serviced, or exposed to a power outage.
  • Look for any visible condition that may point toward a connection, power, or hardware issue.

7. Review Alarm Timing and Event History

  • Check when the alarm occurs and whether it repeats.
  • Look for related communication, ventilator, VIB, SIB, ACB, or system malfunction messages.
  • Note whether the alarm appears once, repeatedly, or immediately after every startup.
  • Use the failure pattern to separate a one-time event from a repeatable communication failure.

8. Check Cabling and Connections

  • With the machine removed from service and following facility procedures, inspect accessible cabling and connectors related to ventilator communication.
  • Look for loose connectors, damaged harnesses, bent pins, poor seating, signs of fluid contamination, or evidence of recent service disturbance.
  • Reseat appropriate accessible connectors only if trained and authorized to do so.
  • If a loose or damaged connection is corrected, restart the system, confirm the alarm does not return, complete checkout, and verify ventilator operation.

9. Isolate Whether the Issue Appears Cable-Related or Device-Related

  • After reboot and cabling checks, determine whether the alarm clears or returns.
  • If the alarm clears and does not return, the issue may have been temporary or connection-related.
  • If the alarm returns after cabling checks, suspect an internal communication hardware issue.
  • Do not assume the unit is safe for use until checkout passes and ventilator operation is verified.

10. Escalate to Ventilator Interface Board Evaluation

  • If the alarm continues after reboot and cabling checks, remove the machine from service for repair evaluation.
  • The next likely repair path is evaluation of the Ventilator Interface Board communication path.
  • Follow facility procedure and manufacturer service requirements for board-level diagnosis or replacement.

11. Escalate to Anesthesia Controller Board Evaluation if Needed

  • If Ventilator Interface Board evaluation or replacement does not resolve the issue, escalate to Anesthesia Controller Board evaluation.
  • Evaluate the ACB only after simpler causes, cabling, and ventilator-side communication issues have been ruled out.
  • Complete all required checkout and functional testing before returning the anesthesia machine to service.

If the Problem Persists

If COM ERROR VENT TO ACB continues after rebooting, checking the power source, inspecting physical condition, reviewing alarm history, and checking cabling, the common external causes have been ruled out.

A persistent alarm may indicate an internal communication failure involving the ventilator communication path, Ventilator Interface Board, Anesthesia Controller Board, or related harnessing.

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send it for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Evaluate according to facility procedure and manufacturer service requirements.

Do not return the anesthesia machine to patient use until the alarm is cleared, checkout passes, and ventilator operation is verified.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm on an active patient. A system malfunction involving ventilator communication can affect ventilation reliability. Move the patient to a backup anesthesia machine, manual ventilation, or another appropriate support method before troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"GE Healthcare Aisys anesthesia machine displayed COM ERROR VENT TO ACB with System Malfunction alarm. Staff reported ventilator communication fault during startup or use and removed machine from service."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified COM ERROR VENT TO ACB alarm. Rebooted system and checked power source, external condition, alarm history, and accessible cabling. Alarm indicates loss of communication between ventilator side of system and Anesthesia Controller Board after communication had been established."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed machine from patient use. Rebooted system, inspected power and cabling, and reviewed alarm behavior. If alarm cleared, completed checkout and verified ventilator operation before returning to service. If alarm persisted, labeled Out of Service and escalated for bench repair with VIB and ACB evaluation as needed."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

COM ERROR VENT TO ACB should be treated as a serious anesthesia machine communication fault. Start with patient safety, confirm the alarm, reboot the system, check power and cabling, then escalate logically toward VIB and ACB evaluation if the issue persists. Clear documentation shows what was verified, what was ruled out, and why the device was either returned to service or removed for repair.

That is successful troubleshooting.
