Welch Allyn Connex 6000 Series

NIBP Failure – Leaky Hose / Transducer Drift

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Welch Allyn

Model

Connex 6000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide walks you through simple checks when a Connex 6000 monitor fails to measure blood pressure or reports an error due to a leaky hose or transducer drift. These steps focus on the most common and easily verified causes before assuming the module has failed internally.

No advanced technical skills are needed — just follow the steps and observe carefully.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Inspect the NIBP Hose

  • Ensure the hose connecting the cuff to the monitor is fully seated at both ends.
  • Look for cracks, holes, kinks, or wear along the hose.
  • Replace the hose if any visible damage or leaks are observed.
  • Even a small leak can prevent proper inflation and trigger an NIBP error.

2. Check the Cuff and Connections

  • Confirm the cuff is the correct size for the patient.
  • Make sure the cuff tubing is fully inserted and the hook/Velcro is secure.
  • Inspect for tears or leaks in the bladder.
  • A poorly seated or damaged cuff can cause failed or inaccurate readings.

3. Verify the Module / Transducer Seating

  • Make sure the NIBP module is fully inserted in the monitor.
  • Check the connector pins for cleanliness and damage.
  • Look for visible moisture or debris.
  • Loose or misaligned transducers can cause drift or persistent errors.

4. Observe the Display

  • Note any error codes (e.g., “NIBP Fail,” “Low Pressure,” “Leak Detected”).
  • Record whether the error happens immediately or intermittently.
  • Documenting details helps CE staff identify recurring issues.

5. Try a Simple Reset

  • Remove the cuff and hose from the patient.
  • Power cycle the monitor.
  • Reconnect the hose and cuff, and attempt another measurement.
  • If the measurement succeeds, the issue may have been a temporary connection or seating problem.

If the NIBP Still Fails

If the monitor continues to fail NIBP measurements after completing the steps above, that’s okay — you have already checked the most common and simplest causes.

You have already:

  • inspected the hose for leaks or damage
  • verified the cuff is correct and seated properly
  • checked the module / transducer connections
  • attempted a simple reset

The issue is now likely internal (transducer drift, module malfunction, or sensor calibration problem) and cannot be resolved at the bedside.

The best next step is to:

  • remove the monitor from clinical service
  • clearly label it as Out of Service
  • send it for repair or bench evaluation

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What the user reported.

Example: “NIBP measurement failed; error displayed.”

Cause

What you observed or determined during troubleshooting.

Example: “Hose inspected and intact, cuff verified, module seated correctly. Measurement still fails. Suspected transducer drift or internal module fault.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Removed monitor from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for repair.”
or
“Tagged for bench evaluation by CE.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Hose checked / replaced
  • Cuff verified / replaced
  • Module reseated
  • Error cleared / persisted
  • Any visible damage or moisture
  • Final disposition (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Not every NIBP failure is caused by a hose or cuff — sometimes the transducer or module develops drift or internal faults that cannot be corrected at the bedside. Completing these steps ensures you have:

  • ✔ ruled out common external causes
  • ✔ protected patient safety
  • ✔ documented properly
  • ✔ avoided unnecessary guesswork

That is successful troubleshooting.
