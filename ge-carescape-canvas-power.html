GE CARESCAPE Canvas Monitor

Power Issue – Will Not Turn On

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE Canvas

What This Guide Helps With

This guide provides a structured Clinical Engineering approach when a CARESCAPE Canvas monitor will not power on.

The focus is on logical, external, and easily verifiable checks before assuming internal failure. These steps rule out common power-related causes while maintaining patient safety and minimizing unnecessary escalation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the monitor is in clinical use, transfer the patient to a functioning monitor first.
  • Do not troubleshoot while the monitor is actively required for patient care.
  • Confirm the device is safe to remove from service before proceeding.

2. Verify AC Power at the Outlet

  • Confirm the monitor is connected to a known-working hospital-grade outlet.
  • Test the outlet with a known-good device.
  • If plugged into a power strip, isolation transformer, or UPS, verify that device is powered on and functioning.
  • If available, try a different known-working wall outlet.

Why this matters: Loss of upstream power is the most common and simplest cause of a “dead” monitor.

3. Inspect and Reseat the Power Cord

Visually inspect the power cord for:

  • Cuts or fraying
  • Bent or damaged prongs
  • Loose connector ends

Reseat the cord firmly at:

  • The wall outlet
  • The rear AC inlet of the monitor

If available, swap with a known-good hospital-grade power cord and attempt to power on again.

Why this matters: A loose or internally failed cord can mimic total device failure.

4. Confirm the Rear Power Switch Position

  • Verify the rear power rocker switch is in the ON position.
  • Then press the front power button.
  • Do not assume the switch position without physically checking it.

5. Observe for Any Signs of Activity

  • Front LED indicators
  • Brief screen flash
  • Audible fan noise
  • Clicking sounds

Even minimal activity suggests partial power presence and helps narrow down whether the failure is complete or localized.

If the Problem Persists

If the monitor shows no indicator lights, no fan noise, no display activity, and no response with a known-good outlet and power cord, then common external causes have been ruled out.

The issue is likely internal (power supply board, internal fuse, or power distribution failure).

  • Remove the monitor from service
  • Label clearly as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a non-powering monitor on an active patient.

  • Move the patient to a working monitor first
  • Confirm monitoring continuity
  • Then remove the failed unit from service

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

Example: “Monitor will not power on.”

Cause

Example: “Unit does not power on with known-working outlet and known-good power cord. Rear switch verified ON. No indicator lights, fan noise, or display activity observed. Suspected internal power supply failure.”

Resolution

Examples:

“Removed from service and tagged Out of Service.”
“Sent to Biomed shop for bench evaluation.”
“Dispatched for vendor repair.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested (yes/no)
  • Alternate outlet attempted
  • Power cord swapped (yes/no)
  • Rear switch position confirmed
  • Indicator light behavior
  • Fan noise present/absent
  • Any unusual odor or heat
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with the simplest external checks and progresses logically. Ruling out power source and connection issues protects patient safety, reduces unnecessary part replacement, and supports accurate repair escalation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
