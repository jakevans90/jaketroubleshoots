ICU Medical CADD-Solis

Occlusion Alarms – Especially False Occlusions

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

ICU Medical

Model

CADD-Solis

What This Guide Helps With

This guide walks you through simple checks when a CADD-Solis pump triggers occlusion alarms, including alarms during infusion, at unexpected times, or frequent false alarms.

These steps focus on the most common and easily verified causes before assuming tubing failure, pump occlusion sensor failure, or internal pressure sensor malfunction.

You do not need advanced technical skills — just follow the steps carefully and observe what happens.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Check Tubing Path for Obstructions

  • Stop the pump if running.
  • Inspect the entire infusion set from the cassette to the patient.
  • Ensure tubing is free of kinks or sharp bends, not pinched under clamps or clothing, and correctly seated in the cassette pressure channel.
  • Even minor kinks can trigger false occlusion alarms.

2. Inspect the Cassette Pressure Plate and Tubing Channel

  • Remove the cassette.
  • Look for debris or dried medication in the tubing channel, warped or cracked pressure plate, improperly seated tubing.
  • Clean per facility policy. Misalignment or debris can cause the pump to detect pressure incorrectly.

3. Confirm Correct Cassette and Tubing

  • Ensure tubing type matches therapy type (continuous, PCA, or intermittent).
  • Verify tubing is compatible with CADD-Solis cassettes.
  • Confirm tubing is not expired or damaged.
  • Incorrect or worn tubing can trigger intermittent occlusion alarms.

4. Check Pump Orientation and Placement

  • Position pump per manufacturer guidelines (usually upright or at specified angle).
  • Ensure tubing runs freely to the patient without sharp bends or tension.
  • Avoid hanging the pump upside-down or placing tubing under tension.
  • Pressure sensors can falsely trigger if pump orientation is incorrect.

5. Power Cycle the Pump

  • Turn the pump off.
  • Wait at least 10 seconds.
  • Turn it back on.
  • Reinstall cassette and tubing.
  • Temporary sensor glitches can clear after a reboot.

6. Test with a Known-Good Cassette and Tubing

  • Replace cassette and tubing with new or verified working components.
  • Attempt infusion at same settings.
  • If false alarms stop: original cassette or tubing is likely defective.
  • If alarms continue: internal occlusion/pressure sensor issue or pump logic error is likely.

7. Observe Alarm Patterns

  • Document if alarm occurs immediately upon starting infusion or at specific rates/pressures.
  • Check for fluid in tubing or cassette causing air bubbles.
  • See if alarm clears when tubing is gently adjusted or re-seated.
  • Pattern observation helps determine whether cause is external or internal.

If Occlusion Alarms Persist

If alarms continue after completing the steps above:

  • Inspect tubing and cassette
  • Confirm proper placement and orientation
  • Power-cycle the pump
  • Test with known-good components

Likely causes at this point include internal pressure sensor failure, pressure plate detection issues, or pump logic/firmware problems. These cannot be corrected through bedside troubleshooting.

Next steps:

  • Remove the pump from clinical service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

Example: “Pump repeatedly alarms for occlusion; tubing appears patent.”

Cause

Example: “Tubing inspected and re-seated, cassette replaced, pump power-cycled. False occlusion persists. Suspected internal pressure sensor or pressure plate failure.”

Resolution

Examples:
“Removed pump from service, labeled out of service, sent for repair.”
“Tagged for CE bench evaluation.”
“Replaced defective tubing/cassette; infusion resumed without alarms.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Tubing path clear (yes / no)
  • Tubing tested (yes / no)
  • Cassette replaced/tested
  • Pump orientation correct
  • Alarm occurs immediately or during infusion
  • Final disposition (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Most false occlusion alarms are caused by kinks or tension in tubing, tubing misalignment in cassette, or debris/warping of pressure plate. Internal sensor failures are less common but can occur, particularly with high-use pumps.

Completing these steps ensures you have:

  • ✔ ruled out simple external causes
  • ✔ protected patient therapy continuity
  • ✔ documented properly
  • ✔ avoided unnecessary guesswork

That is successful troubleshooting.
