About Jake Troubleshoots

A free troubleshooting resource built by a working BMET — for BMETs, clinical engineers, and anyone keeping medical equipment running.

Who's Behind This

I'm Jake. Before I ever worked on medical equipment professionally, I spent years as a contractor troubleshooting electronics across hospitals — AV systems, nurse call, door access, paging, intercoms, TV headends, alarm systems. I worked in most of the hospitals in my area and spent a lot of time around biomed departments watching what they did.

I got interested. I networked. In 2018 I made the jump into biomedical equipment technology and I've been here since — working my way up through a department that manages a 15,000+ device inventory. My background is built entirely on field experience, and I've personally worked on nearly every device category you'd find in a hospital setting.

If you can troubleshoot one piece of equipment well, you can troubleshoot all of it. That's something I learned early and have watched prove itself out over and over.

Why This Site Exists

I've seen what happens when equipment fails under pressure in a clinical environment. Stress spikes, logical thinking takes a back seat, and people start chasing parts instead of causes. I've watched experienced technicians — people I respect — completely lose the thread when a critical device goes down.

I've always been the opposite. Calm, methodical, working the problem from symptom to cause. That mindset comes from troubleshooting a wide range of systems across a lot of different environments — and it's something I think can be taught.

Service manuals tell you how to replace parts. They rarely tell you how to think through a failure. At some point I realized I was sitting on exactly the kind of niche, specific experience that could fill that gap — and that if I didn't build it, it probably wasn't getting built. So I did.

What These Guides Focus On

Every guide on this site is built around a real symptom pattern — not just a parts list. You'll find:

  • Logical paths from symptom to likely cause
  • Common failure modes seen in real clinical use
  • Simple checks to run before chasing expensive parts
  • Notes on what usually wastes time and what doesn't
  • Clear documentation habits built into every guide

The goal isn't just to fix today's problem — it's to help you approach the next one more confidently.

Troubleshooting & Documentation

In clinical engineering, a repair isn't finished until it's documented well enough that someone else can understand what failed, what was checked, and what fixed it. These guides try to model that habit — not just "replaced part," but context that actually means something six months later.

Who This Site Is For

This site is built for clinical engineers and biomedical technicians who:

  • Want clearer, real-world troubleshooting guidance
  • Feel overwhelmed when equipment fails under pressure
  • Learn better from logic and examples than dense manuals
  • Want to improve both their troubleshooting and documentation habits
