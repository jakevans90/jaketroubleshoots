Nihon Kohden NKV-330

Alarm: CO2 Cell Disconnect

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

NKV-330

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the CO2 Cell Disconnect alarm on a Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator. When using the TG-980P CO2 sensor setup, this alarm indicates that the airway adapter is disconnected from the CO2 sensor kit or is not being detected correctly.

This condition can interrupt EtCO2 monitoring and may make the displayed CO2 value unavailable or unreliable until the airway adapter and sensor connection are corrected.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot CO2 monitoring issues while assuming the displayed EtCO2 value is reliable.
  • Verify the patient’s respiratory status directly.
  • Confirm ventilation is being delivered appropriately.
  • Use alternate monitoring if CO2 readings are unavailable or questionable.
  • Notify clinical staff that CO2 monitoring may be interrupted until the alarm is resolved.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ventilator is displaying Alarm: CO2 Cell Disconnect.
  • Verify that the alarm occurs while using the TG-980P CO2 sensor setup.
  • Check whether the alarm appears at startup, after circuit movement, after patient repositioning, after changing the airway adapter, or intermittently during ventilation.
  • Confirm whether any other CO2 sensor alarms are active at the same time.

Inspect the Airway Adapter Connection

  • Check that the airway adapter is fully connected to the CO2 sensor kit.
  • Remove and reconnect the airway adapter if it appears loose, partially seated, or misaligned.
  • Confirm the adapter locks or seats properly according to the accessory design.
  • Observe whether the alarm clears after the adapter is reseated.

Check for Adapter Misalignment or Poor Seating

  • Inspect the airway adapter and CO2 sensor interface for misalignment, incomplete insertion, loose fit, cracked plastic, damaged latch points, or contamination.
  • Reconnect the adapter without twisting or forcing the connection.
  • Confirm the adapter sits cleanly in the CO2 sensor kit and remains connected during normal circuit movement.

Check for Circuit Pulling or Strain

  • Inspect the breathing circuit, sensor placement, and patient connection for tension.
  • Look for tubing pulling on the CO2 sensor or airway adapter.
  • Reposition the circuit to reduce strain on the sensor assembly.
  • Support the circuit if needed so the airway adapter does not loosen during movement.

Inspect the CO2 Sensor Kit and Cable

  • Check the TG-980P CO2 sensor kit for visible damage.
  • Inspect the sensor cable for cuts, kinks, pinched sections, loose strain relief, or damage near the sensor head.
  • Confirm the CO2 sensor cable is securely connected to the ventilator or related module.
  • Do not continue using a visibly damaged CO2 sensor kit for patient monitoring.

Replace the Airway Adapter

  • Install a known-good compatible airway adapter.
  • Reconnect it to the CO2 sensor kit.
  • Allow the ventilator to recognize the CO2 accessory.
  • Observe whether the CO2 Cell Disconnect alarm clears.

Test With a Known-Good CO2 Sensor Kit

  • If available, connect a known-good compatible TG-980P CO2 sensor kit and airway adapter.
  • Verify the cable connection and adapter seating.
  • Observe whether the alarm returns.
  • Use this step to help isolate whether the issue follows the adapter, the sensor kit, the cable, or the ventilator-side connection.

Verify Alarm Resolution Before Return to Use

  • Confirm the CO2 Cell Disconnect alarm is no longer active.
  • Confirm stable CO2 monitoring if clinically expected.
  • Gently move the circuit and sensor assembly to check for intermittent disconnects.
  • Confirm no new CO2 sensor alarms appear.
  • Return the device to use only after the CO2 sensor setup remains stable.

If the Problem Persists

If the CO2 Cell Disconnect alarm continues after the airway adapter has been properly connected, reseated, replaced, and tested with a known-good CO2 sensor kit, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Remove the ventilator from service if CO2 monitoring is required or alarm reliability is questionable. Label the device Out of Service. Send the ventilator and CO2 sensor kit for repair or bench evaluation.

Document which airway adapter and sensor combinations were tested. Persistent CO2 Cell Disconnect alarms may indicate a faulty CO2 sensor kit, damaged accessory cable, connector issue, or ventilator-side detection problem.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient when EtCO2 monitoring is clinically required and the CO2 monitoring system is unreliable. Move the patient to a backup device or use alternate approved monitoring first, then troubleshoot the ventilator and CO2 accessories separately.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator displaying Alarm: CO2 Cell Disconnect while using TG-980P CO2 sensor kit. CO2 monitoring unavailable due to reported airway adapter disconnect."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Airway adapter was not fully connected to the CO2 sensor kit. Alarm cleared after adapter was properly seated and CO2 monitoring was verified."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated airway adapter on TG-980P CO2 sensor kit, verified secure connection, confirmed CO2 Cell Disconnect alarm cleared, and checked for stable CO2 monitoring. Device returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The CO2 Cell Disconnect alarm should be treated as a CO2 accessory connection problem first. Start with patient safety, confirm the exact alarm, check the airway adapter connection, inspect the sensor kit and cable, and only escalate after the simple external causes have been ruled out. Clear documentation helps show whether the issue was corrected at the bedside or required repair evaluation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
