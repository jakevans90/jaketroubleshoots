GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series

Error: HV GENERATOR ERROR

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

OEC 9900 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot HV GENERATOR ERROR on a GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series C-Arm. This error indicates that the system detected an issue in registers associated with the high-voltage generator. The system may shut down, block X-ray generation, or become unavailable for imaging.

Because this condition involves the high-voltage generator path, it should be treated as a serious imaging-system fault. Basic troubleshooting should focus on patient safety, stable power, startup behavior, external conditions, cooling, and clear documentation before escalation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not continue using the C-Arm if it shuts down or reports a high-voltage generator error.
  • Remove the OEC 9900 from patient use if the error occurs during startup, system initialization, fluoro, exposure, or self-check.
  • If imaging is needed for an active case, have clinical staff use another imaging system or follow the facility backup imaging process.
  • Do not repeatedly clear, reboot, or attempt exposure on a patient while the generator fault is unresolved.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Power the system on and observe the startup sequence.
  • Confirm the displayed message is exactly HV GENERATOR ERROR.
  • Note whether the error appears during bootup, system initialization, fluoro attempt, exposure, after warmup, or after movement or transport.
  • Check whether the system shuts down immediately, blocks fluoro, produces no X-ray output, restarts, or shows additional kV, mA, exposure, or generator-related errors.

Check Facility Power First

  • Verify that the C-Arm is plugged into an approved, known-good medical-grade outlet.
  • Inspect the wall outlet, power cord, plug, and any facility power source being used.
  • Avoid shared outlets with heavy-load equipment if possible.
  • Confirm the system receives stable input power before suspecting a deeper generator fault.

Inspect the Power Cord and Main Connections

  • Check the power cord for cuts, crushed sections, bent prongs, heat marks, looseness, or intermittent connection.
  • Confirm the system power connection is fully seated.
  • Gently observe the cord and connection area for flickering, rebooting, power interruption, or error recurrence.
  • Do not use the system if damaged power components are found.

Confirm Normal Startup and Power Cycling

  • Power the system down fully.
  • Allow the system to shut down completely before restarting.
  • Restart using the normal facility-approved startup process.
  • Do not repeatedly cycle power rapidly to force the system into operation.
  • If the issue does not return, document the event as non-reproducible and perform a basic operational check before return to service.

Check for Recent Movement, Impact, Cleaning, or Service

  • Ask staff whether the C-Arm was recently moved, struck, cleaned heavily, serviced, reassembled, plugged into a different room, or used after a power outage.
  • Inspect for visible damage, loose panels, fluid residue, or signs of impact.
  • Note any change in behavior after transport or room relocation.
  • Do not remove covers or perform internal disassembly during basic troubleshooting.

Check Cooling and Ventilation

  • Inspect the system for blocked vents, heavy dust buildup, drapes, covers, or equipment stored against airflow openings.
  • Make sure the C-Arm has adequate clearance for cooling.
  • If the error appeared after extended imaging use, allow the system to cool before retesting off the patient.
  • Document whether the error appears cold, only after warmup, or only after generator demand.

Perform a Controlled Functional Check Off the Patient

  • Only perform testing when the unit is removed from patient use and it is safe to check.
  • Power the system on and allow it to initialize.
  • Observe whether the error appears before exposure, during fluoro attempt, or only after generator demand.
  • If the error returns during generator demand, treat the condition as a likely device-level generator fault.

Rule Out External Conditions

  • Confirm the unit has been tested with a known-good outlet.
  • Confirm the power cord and main external connections appear secure and undamaged.
  • Confirm startup sequence, cooling, ventilation, and obvious environmental issues have been checked.
  • If the error remains after these checks, stop basic troubleshooting and escalate.

If the Problem Persists

If HV GENERATOR ERROR continues after checking facility power, power cord condition, startup behavior, recent movement, physical condition, cooling, ventilation, and basic off-patient operation, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent HV generator error may indicate an internal generator control, high-voltage drive, feedback, PCB, or regulation issue.

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send it for repair, vendor service, or bench evaluation.
  • Do not return it to clinical use until repair and operational verification are complete.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this error on an active patient. A C-Arm with a high-voltage generator error may fail to produce imaging when needed or may have an unsafe X-ray generation control problem. Move the patient or procedure to a backup imaging system first, then troubleshoot the OEC 9900 off the patient.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series C-Arm displayed HV GENERATOR ERROR and shut down during startup or fluoro attempt. Unit unavailable for imaging."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified HV GENERATOR ERROR. Checked approved power outlet, power cord condition, startup behavior, cooling, ventilation, and obvious physical damage. Error persisted, indicating likely device-level high-voltage generator control or regulation fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed C-Arm from service and labeled Out of Service. Sent for service-level generator troubleshooting or vendor repair. Unit not returned to clinical use pending repair and operational verification."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

HV GENERATOR ERROR should not be treated as a nuisance message. Start with patient safety, verify the exact behavior, rule out power and environmental causes, then escalate when the fault remains. Clear CCR documentation helps protect the patient, the clinical team, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
