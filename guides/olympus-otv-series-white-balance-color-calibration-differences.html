Olympus OTV Series

White Balance / Color Calibration Differences

Asset Type

Endoscopic / OR Camera System

Manufacturer

Olympus

Model

OTV Series

What This Guide Helps With

Addresses inconsistent color reproduction or white balance shifts between procedures, ensuring accurate tissue visualization and reliable surgical imaging.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

Confirm the system is not in use on an active patient. Move the procedure to a backup system before beginning any checks.

2. Verify Ambient Lighting

Ensure the OR lights or room lighting have not changed significantly, as this can affect perceived color.

Expected outcome: color differences are minimized.

3. Check Camera Head and Coupler

Inspect for dirt, debris, or moisture on the camera lens or coupler. Clean with approved lens wipes.

Outcome: clearer and more consistent color if external contamination was the cause.

4. Confirm Proper Connector Seating

Ensure the camera head is fully seated in the processor port. Loose connections can cause signal issues affecting color calibration.

Outcome: color stabilization.

5. Perform White Balance Procedure

Execute the system’s white balance routine according to manufacturer instructions, using a proper reference target if required.

Outcome: restored color accuracy.

6. Compare with Other Systems

If available, connect the same camera head to a known working OTV processor.

Outcome: determines whether the variation is from the camera head or the processor.

7. Check for Software/Mode Differences

Verify video output settings, image enhancement modes, and color profiles match between systems.

Outcome: eliminates software-induced color differences.

If the Problem Persists

If the above steps do not resolve the color discrepancy:

  • External causes have been ruled out.
  • Remove the system from service.
  • Label Out of Service.
  • Send for manufacturer evaluation or bench calibration.

Knowing when to escalate protects patient safety and prevents ineffective troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not perform white balance on an active patient.

Move patients to another verified system first.

Only troubleshoot when the OR environment is safe and non-critical.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Surgeon reports that tissue colors appear inconsistent between procedures."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Camera head lens and processor connections verified; white balance had drifted; ambient lighting and settings differences noted."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleaned camera head, reseated connections, performed white balance, verified color accuracy against reference processor."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Lens cleanliness checked
  • Camera head connection verified
  • White balance routine completed
  • Ambient lighting noted
  • Video mode and output settings recorded
  • Device tested on reference processor

Final Thought

Maintaining consistent color and accurate white balance is crucial for surgical safety. Use logical, stepwise troubleshooting, rule out external factors first, and escalate promptly when needed. Document each step carefully for future reference and regulatory compliance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
