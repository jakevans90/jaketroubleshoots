Hamilton C1

Alarm: 244009 Battery 2: Wrong battery

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm 244009 Battery 2: Wrong battery on a Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm means the ventilator does not recognize Battery 2 as the expected compatible battery, which may affect backup power reliability during transport, unplugging, or loss of external power.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the ventilator is in active patient use, do not remove batteries or interrupt power without clinical support.
  • Transfer the patient to another approved ventilator if Battery 2 replacement or power testing could affect ventilation.
  • Confirm external AC power is connected before performing battery checks.
  • Do not rely on a ventilator with unresolved battery compatibility alarms for transport or backup power.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ventilator displays: Alarm 244009 Battery 2: Wrong battery.
  • Observe whether the alarm appears at startup, after inserting Battery 2, during charging, after switching to battery operation, or intermittently during operation.
  • Check whether Battery 1 is normal and whether the ventilator recognizes Battery 2 at all.
  • Confirm the issue is specific to Battery 2 before replacing parts.

3. Confirm External Power Is Stable

  • Verify the AC power cord is fully connected to the ventilator and wall outlet.
  • Confirm the outlet is active.
  • Check that the external power indicator is present on the ventilator.
  • If available, test with a known-good power cord or outlet.

4. Inspect Battery 2 Installation

  • Remove Battery 2 only when it is safe to do so and the ventilator is not supporting a patient.
  • Inspect the battery compartment for poor seating, obstruction, debris, or damage.
  • Reinstall Battery 2 fully and confirm it locks into place.
  • Restart or allow the ventilator to recheck the battery.

5. Verify Battery 2 Is an Approved Compatible Battery

  • Compare Battery 2 to a known approved battery used with the Hamilton C1.
  • Check the part number, labeling, battery type, and physical fit.
  • Look for aftermarket, incorrect model, expired, rebuilt, or non-approved batteries.
  • Do not force a battery that does not fit correctly.

6. Inspect Battery Contacts

  • Remove Battery 2 and inspect the battery contacts.
  • Check for dirt, corrosion, bent contacts, damage, residue, or fluid contamination.
  • Inspect the ventilator-side contacts in the Battery 2 bay.
  • Clean only according to approved facility and manufacturer cleaning practices.
  • Do not scrape, bend, or aggressively manipulate contacts.

7. Try a Known-Good Compatible Battery

  • Install a known-good approved Hamilton-compatible battery into the Battery 2 slot.
  • Confirm whether the ventilator recognizes the battery.
  • Check whether the alarm clears.
  • If the known-good battery works, the original Battery 2 is incorrect, defective, or incompatible.
  • If the alarm remains with a known-good battery, suspect the Battery 2 bay, contacts, or internal battery communication hardware.

8. Confirm Recognition and Charging

  • Check that Battery 2 appears in the ventilator battery status display.
  • Confirm Battery 2 shows charging or an appropriate charge level.
  • Verify no battery-related alarms remain.
  • Allow the ventilator to remain on AC power long enough to confirm stable recognition.
  • If required by facility policy, perform a battery runtime or charge verification.

9. Check for Physical Damage or Contamination

  • Inspect the battery bay and surrounding device exterior.
  • Look for cracked plastic, broken latches, loose battery fit, liquid intrusion, corrosion, or overheating signs.
  • Check for abnormal heat or burning odor around the battery area.
  • Remove the ventilator from service if damage, heat, odor, corrosion, or contamination is present.

10. Return to Service Only If Fully Resolved

  • Verify Battery 2 is approved, properly seated, recognized, and charging.
  • Confirm Battery 1 and external power status are normal.
  • Run any required pre-use check or operational verification per facility procedure.
  • Document the corrective action and final device status.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm 244009 remains after installing a known-good approved Hamilton-compatible battery and verifying contacts, seating, AC power, and external conditions, common external causes have been ruled out.

Persistent Battery 2 wrong battery alarms may indicate a Battery 2 bay contact issue, battery identification circuit issue, internal communication problem, or battery management hardware fault.

The ventilator should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Do not keep the ventilator in clinical use if Battery 2 cannot be reliably recognized.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot battery compatibility alarms on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator first if battery replacement, power testing, or battery bay inspection could affect ventilation. A ventilator with unresolved battery alarms may not provide reliable backup power during transport, unplugging, or power failure.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator displayed Alarm 244009 Battery 2: Wrong battery. Staff reported Battery 2 was not recognized correctly and battery status was abnormal."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery 2 was not an approved compatible battery or was not being identified correctly by the ventilator. Battery seating, contacts, AC power, and compatibility were checked."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed incorrect Battery 2 and installed an approved Hamilton-compatible battery. Inspected Battery 2 contacts and battery bay. Confirmed Battery 2 was recognized and charging normally. Alarm cleared and device passed operational verification."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Alarm 244009 should be treated as a backup power reliability issue. Start with patient safety, verify the exact Battery 2 wrong battery alarm, check power and battery seating, confirm the battery is approved and compatible, inspect contacts, and use a known-good battery to isolate the fault. If the ventilator still rejects a correct battery, remove it from service and escalate for repair. Clear documentation protects patient safety and supports proper follow-up.

That is successful troubleshooting.
