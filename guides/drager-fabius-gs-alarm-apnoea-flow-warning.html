Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: APNOEA FLOW !!!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

APNOEA FLOW !!! alarm indicating low or absent tidal volume (<20 mL) causing loss of ventilation due to disconnection, leak, or ventilator issue.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient and ventilation status
  • If ventilation is compromised, remove the machine from use
  • Ventilate manually using a bag-valve device or alternate system

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm APNOEA FLOW !!! is displayed
  • Observe when the alarm occurs (startup, during ventilation, spontaneous mode)
  • Check tidal volume readings (<20 mL)
  • Confirm whether the patient is receiving breaths

3. Check Breathing Circuit Connections

  • Ensure full connection from machine to patient
  • Inspect Y-piece, elbow, and filter connections
  • Verify connections at bag, absorber, and ventilator ports

4. Check for Circuit Leaks

  • Listen for audible leaks
  • Inspect hoses for cracks or damage
  • Check CO₂ absorber canister seal and seating
  • Verify APL valve and bag connections

5. Verify Ventilator Settings and Mode

  • Confirm correct ventilation mode is selected
  • Check respiratory rate and tidal volume settings
  • Verify apnea ventilation settings if applicable

6. Check Patient Interface and Airway

  • Inspect endotracheal tube or mask placement
  • Check cuff inflation if intubated
  • Look for obstructions or kinks in airway or tubing

7. Test Manual Ventilation (Bag Mode)

  • Switch to manual/spontaneous mode
  • Attempt ventilation using the reservoir bag
  • If bag does not fill → possible gas supply or internal issue
  • If bag works normally → likely ventilator-related issue

8. Check Gas Supply

  • Verify oxygen and air supply pressures
  • Confirm pipeline connections
  • Check backup cylinder status

9. Evaluate Ventilator Function

  • Observe bellows movement (if applicable)
  • Check delivered tidal volume readings
  • Assess consistency of breaths

If the Problem Persists

If external causes such as disconnections, leaks, gas supply, and settings have been ruled out and ventilation cannot be restored:

  • Remove device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or further bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilation alarms while relying on the device for active patient support. Move the patient to a backup ventilation method first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Device alarming APNOEA FLOW !!! with no tidal volume delivery during ventilation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Loose breathing circuit connection causing loss of delivered volume."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated breathing circuit connections; verified normal ventilation and alarm cleared."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Apnea flow alarms represent a critical loss of ventilation. Always prioritize patient safety, verify simple causes first, and escalate appropriately when ventilation cannot be restored.

That is successful troubleshooting.
