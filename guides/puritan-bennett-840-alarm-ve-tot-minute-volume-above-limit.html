Puritan Bennett 840

Alarm: ↑VE TOT Minute volume above limit

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Puritan Bennett

Model

840

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the ↑VE TOT alarm indicating total minute ventilation exceeds set limits, which may lead to over-ventilation, respiratory alkalosis, or patient instability.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s respiratory status
  • Monitor for signs of over-ventilation or distress
  • Be prepared to provide manual ventilation or switch to a backup ventilator if needed

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ↑VE TOT alarm is displayed
  • Check total minute volume readings exceeding alarm limits
  • Observe respiratory rate and tidal volume trends

3. Check Patient Condition

  • Assess for increased respiratory effort or rate
  • Check for pain, anxiety, or agitation
  • Evaluate for metabolic acidosis or hypoxia driving increased ventilation

4. Check Ventilator Settings

  • Verify tidal volume (VT) settings
  • Review respiratory rate settings
  • Check support modes (e.g., pressure support)
  • Confirm alarm limits are appropriate

5. Inspect Patient Circuit and Sensors

  • Check flow sensor for moisture or contamination
  • Inspect tubing for leaks or improper assembly
  • Ensure expiratory valve is functioning correctly

6. Perform System Self Test (SST)

  • Run SST according to manufacturer recommendations
  • Verify test completes without errors

7. Perform Extended Self Test (EST) if Needed

  • Run EST for deeper diagnostics if issue persists
  • Confirm no faults are detected

8. Stop When Resolved

  • Confirm minute volume returns within limits
  • Ensure alarm no longer triggers
  • Return device to service if stable

If the Problem Persists

If the ↑VE TOT alarm continues after all external checks and testing:

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label the device as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilation alarms on an active patient. Always transition the patient to a backup ventilator before continuing diagnostics.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming ↑VE TOT with high minute ventilation readings during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Minute volume exceeded set limits; patient-driven hyperventilation or possible sensor inaccuracy identified."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Verified settings, checked circuit and sensors, performed SST/EST. Removed from service if unresolved."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

High minute ventilation alarms are often patient-driven but require careful verification. Always confirm settings, evaluate patient condition, and rule out sensor or circuit issues before escalating.
