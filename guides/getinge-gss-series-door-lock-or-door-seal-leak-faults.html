Getinge GSS Series Washer-Disinfector

Door Lock or Door Seal Leak Faults

Asset Type

Washer-Disinfector

Manufacturer

Getinge

Model

GSS Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting door lock or door seal issues on Getinge GSS Series washer-disinfectors. Faults may present as alarms indicating door not properly closed, leaks detected, or cycle aborts. The focus is on external, verifiable checks before assuming internal mechanical failure, ensuring patient and staff safety.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Device Power and Ready Status

  • Confirm the washer-disinfector is powered on and in standby mode.
  • Check that the control panel shows “ready” and no prior fault messages persist.
  • Why: Door interlock sensors may not register if the system is not properly powered.

Inspect Door Seal (Gasket) for Damage

  • Examine the silicone or rubber door seal for cracks, tears, foreign objects, or residue buildup.
  • Clean gently with a manufacturer-approved cleaning solution if residue is present.
  • Why: Even small debris or damage can prevent a proper seal, triggering leak faults.

Check Door Lock Mechanism

  • Ensure the door closes fully without resistance.
  • Observe the mechanical lock or latch as it engages.
  • Verify that the door lock indicator on the panel lights appropriately.
  • Why: Faulty door lock sensors or mechanical interference can prevent the cycle from starting.

Inspect Door Hinges and Alignment

  • Check that the door sits evenly and that hinges are not bent or obstructed.
  • Adjust alignment if allowed by manufacturer guidance.
  • Why: Misalignment can prevent proper sealing and engagement of interlock switches.

Check for Water or Residue on Door Frame

  • Wipe the frame and the inner perimeter of the door.
  • Ensure no water pooling or chemical residues are present.
  • Why: Residues can compromise the seal and trigger leak detection alarms.

Run a Door Test (Empty Cycle)

  • If the system allows, run a short diagnostic or “door test” cycle without load.
  • Observe if the system still reports a leak or door lock fault.
  • Why: Confirms if the issue persists under controlled conditions before escalating.

If the Problem Persists

All external checks have been performed and no visible defects are found.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation by qualified personnel

Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting and ensures safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt to operate the washer-disinfector with a door lock or seal fault on an active cycle.

Move any instruments scheduled for cleaning to a different washer-disinfector if available.

Troubleshooting should be performed only when the device is empty and safe to handle.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reported alarms stating “door not closed” or “leak detected.””

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Observed minor residue on door seal and slightly misaligned door hinges preventing full engagement of the door lock.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Cleaned door seal, wiped door frame, and adjusted door alignment. Ran empty cycle successfully; alarms cleared. Device returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Door seal inspected: Yes
  • Lock mechanism observed: Yes
  • Alignment check: Yes
  • Leak alarms observed: Initial fault present
  • Residue or debris removed: Yes
  • Final device status: Returned to service after verification

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting begins with external and easily verifiable issues like seals, alignment, and cleanliness. Escalating to repair only after these checks prevents unnecessary downtime and ensures patient safety. Clear CCR documentation captures the issue, corrective action, and final verification for compliance and traceability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
