Midmark 411 / 417 Series

Hand Control or Footswitch Failure

Asset Type

Exam Table

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

411 / 417 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Table not responding to hand control or footswitch due to power, connection, or external control-related issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the table from active patient use before troubleshooting.
  • Expected: Patient safely relocated to another table.
  • Why it matters: Loss of control can result in unsafe positioning or unexpected movement.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to operate all table functions (hi-lo, backrest) using both the hand control and footswitch.
  • Expected: No response, intermittent response, or only one control working.
  • Why it matters: Determines if the issue is isolated to one control or system-wide.
  • If one control works → suspect the non-working control
  • If neither works → proceed with power/system checks

Check Power to the Table

  • Confirm the table is plugged into a known working outlet. Test the outlet with another device if needed.
  • Expected: Table has consistent power (indicator lights or audible relay activity if applicable).
  • Why it matters: No power = no control response.
  • If no power → restore power and retest

Inspect Power Cord and Connection

  • Check for loose plugs, frayed wiring, or damage at the cord entry point.
  • Expected: Cord intact and securely connected.
  • Why it matters: Intermittent or lost power can mimic control failure.

Inspect Hand Control and Footswitch Connections

  • Ensure both controls are fully seated in their ports (typically under the table or base).
  • Expected: Secure, tight connections with no looseness.
  • Why it matters: Loose or partially connected cables are a common failure point.
  • Reseat connectors and retest

Check for Visible Damage to Controls

  • Inspect cables, buttons, and housings for cracks, pinched wires, or fluid intrusion.
  • Expected: No visible damage; buttons move freely.
  • Why it matters: Physical damage can prevent signal transmission.
  • If damaged → remove defective control from service

Test Controls Independently

  • Disconnect one control and test operation using the other (if both are present).
  • Expected: Table responds with at least one control.
  • Why it matters: Helps isolate whether the issue is with a specific control or the system.
  • If one works → replace the non-working control
  • If neither works → continue

Check for Stuck or Continuous Activation

  • Inspect footswitch and hand control for stuck buttons or debris causing continuous input.
  • Expected: Buttons return to neutral position when released.
  • Why it matters: A stuck input can disable or override other commands.
  • Clean or free stuck buttons and retest

Perform Power Reset

  • Unplug the table, wait 30–60 seconds, then reconnect power.
  • Expected: System resets and controls respond normally.
  • Why it matters: Clears minor control or communication faults.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely related to internal wiring, control interface, or control board failure.

Remove the table from service
Label as Out of Service
Send for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and ensures proper escalation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot control issues while a patient is on the table. Always transfer the patient first to avoid unintended movement or positioning hazards.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Table not responding to hand control or footswitch; unable to adjust height or backrest."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Loose hand control connection found under table base."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated connector, verified full function with both controls."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Power cord condition
  • Which control failed (hand, foot, or both)
  • Connector condition and seating
  • Button response (stuck/intermittent)
  • Indicator lights or sounds
  • Final operational status

Final Thought

Control failures are often caused by simple external issues like loose connections or damaged accessories. A structured approach ensures patient safety, efficient troubleshooting, and timely escalation when needed.

That is successful troubleshooting.
