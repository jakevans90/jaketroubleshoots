Nihon Kohden NKV-330

Alarm: MAIN BATTERY LOW

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

NKV-330

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the MAIN BATTERY LOW alarm on the Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator. This alarm indicates that AC power has been lost and the main battery has less than 30% remaining power.

This condition should be handled quickly because the ventilator is already operating from battery power and available runtime is limited. The goal is to keep the patient safely ventilated, restore AC power, check simple external causes first, and remove the device from service if the alarm cannot be resolved safely.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the patient is being ventilated appropriately.
  • Notify clinical staff immediately that the ventilator is operating on low battery power.
  • Prepare an alternate ventilator or manual ventilation method if AC power cannot be restored quickly.
  • Do not leave the patient dependent on a ventilator with a low remaining battery charge.
  • Do not continue troubleshooting if ventilation safety is uncertain.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Check the ventilator display for the MAIN BATTERY LOW alarm.
  • Confirm the ventilator is operating on main battery power.
  • Check the remaining battery percentage or battery status indicator.
  • Confirm the AC power indicator is not present or AC power is not being received.
  • Check for any additional power or battery alarms.

Check the AC Power Connection

  • Check that the AC power cord is fully seated at the ventilator.
  • Check that the power cord is connected to a working hospital-grade outlet.
  • Verify the plug has not been pulled loose.
  • Make sure the cord is not connected to a switched-off power strip or unreliable power source.
  • Look for the AC power indicator on the ventilator after reconnecting power.

Test the Wall Outlet

  • Plug the ventilator into a known-good hospital-grade outlet.
  • Use an emergency power outlet if available and appropriate.
  • Check whether other equipment connected to the same outlet has also lost power.
  • Notify Facilities if the outlet, circuit, or emergency power source appears to be failed.

Inspect the Power Cord

  • Inspect the AC power cord for loose connections.
  • Look for cuts, crushed sections, damaged insulation, or exposed wiring.
  • Check for bent plug blades or damaged connectors.
  • Look for signs of overheating or damaged strain relief.
  • Swap with an approved compatible power cord if available and permitted by facility policy.

Replace the Main Battery if Needed

  • If AC power cannot be restored immediately, check whether a fully charged spare main battery is available.
  • Coordinate with clinical staff before replacing the battery.
  • Maintain patient ventilation during any battery change.
  • Replace the main battery only if it can be done safely without interrupting ventilation.
  • If safe battery replacement is not possible, move the patient to a backup ventilator first.

Confirm Battery Charging After AC Power Is Restored

  • Confirm the AC power indicator is present.
  • Verify the ventilator is no longer relying on battery operation.
  • Check that the main battery begins charging.
  • Confirm the MAIN BATTERY LOW alarm clears or changes appropriately.
  • Allow the battery to recharge while the ventilator remains connected to AC power.

Check for Repeated Battery-Related Alarms

  • Check whether the battery drains unusually fast.
  • Check whether the battery fails to charge after AC power is restored.
  • Watch for intermittent switching between AC power and battery power.
  • Observe whether the AC power indicator flickers or disappears while plugged in.
  • Test with a known-good battery if available and permitted by facility procedure.

Stop When Resolved

  • Confirm stable AC operation.
  • Confirm battery charging is active.
  • Confirm the alarm has cleared.
  • Confirm no other power or battery alarms are active.
  • Document the findings before returning the ventilator to service.

If the Problem Persists

If the AC power connection, wall outlet, power cord, and battery condition have been checked and the MAIN BATTERY LOW alarm persists or returns, remove the ventilator from service.

External causes such as a loose power cord, failed outlet, power strip issue, depleted battery, or unavailable AC power should be ruled out before suspecting an internal power problem.

  • Remove the ventilator from patient use.
  • Label the device Out of Service.
  • Send the ventilator for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Do not continue clinical use with unresolved power or battery alarms.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a ventilator power problem casually while it is actively supporting a patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or approved manual ventilation method first if the alarm cannot be corrected quickly and safely.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator reported MAIN BATTERY LOW alarm while operating on battery power after loss of AC power."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Ventilator was not receiving AC power and main battery charge had dropped below 30%. Outlet, AC power cord, and battery status were checked to determine whether the issue was caused by external power loss, cord damage, depleted battery, or internal charging/power fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Restored AC power to the ventilator, confirmed AC power indicator returned, verified main battery charging, and confirmed MAIN BATTERY LOW alarm cleared. Device passed required checks and was returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The MAIN BATTERY LOW alarm should be handled quickly because it means the ventilator has lost AC power and battery reserve is limited. Clinical Engineering should verify the alarm, restore reliable AC power, check simple external causes first, and escalate the device if the alarm cannot be cleared safely.

That is successful troubleshooting.
