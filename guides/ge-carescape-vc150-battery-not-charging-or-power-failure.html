GE Healthcare CARESCAPE VC150

Battery Not Charging or Power Failure

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE VC150

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the CARESCAPE VC150 does not charge its battery or experiences power-related failures. Symptoms may include the device not powering on, shutting down when unplugged, battery not charging, or power-related alarms. The focus is on verifying external power sources, connections, and battery condition before assuming internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot while the device is in use on a patient
  • Move the patient to a functioning monitor before proceeding

Verify AC Power Source

  • Confirm the outlet is functional by plugging in another device.
  • If connected to a power strip or boom outlet, ensure it is powered.
  • This rules out facility power issues.

Check Power Cord and Adapter

  • Inspect the AC power cord for damage, fraying, or bent prongs.
  • Ensure the cord is fully seated in both the wall outlet and the monitor.
  • Try a known-good power cord if available.
  • Loose or faulty cords are a common cause of intermittent power.

Confirm Power Indicator Behavior

  • Observe if any LED indicators illuminate when plugged in.
  • Check for charging icons or messages on the display.
  • No indicators may suggest no input power or internal power supply failure.

Check Battery Charging Status on Screen

  • Power on the unit (if possible) and verify battery icon status.
  • Look for messages such as “battery not charging” or “low battery.”
  • This helps determine if the system recognizes the battery.

Inspect Battery Installation (External Check Only)

  • If accessible without disassembly, confirm the battery is properly seated.
  • Look for signs of swelling, leakage, or damage.
  • Do not open internal compartments beyond normal access points.

Test with Known-Good Battery (If Available)

  • Swap with a known-good battery to determine if the issue follows the battery.
  • If the device works normally with a different battery, the original battery is likely failed.

Check for Intermittent Power Loss

  • Gently move the power cord while plugged in to see if power cuts in/out.
  • This may indicate a loose input connector or worn cord.

Review Alarm History (If Accessible)

  • Check for recorded power or battery-related alarms.
  • Repeated power faults may indicate a failing internal power supply.

Attempt Operation on AC Only

  • Remove the battery (if safely accessible) and power the unit using AC only.
  • If the unit does not power on with confirmed AC input, suspect internal power supply failure.

If the Problem Persists

If all external power sources, cords, and battery checks have been verified and the issue continues, the problem is likely internal (battery circuitry, charging circuit, or internal power supply).

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Recognizing when external troubleshooting has been exhausted is an important part of proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot power or battery issues while the monitor is actively in use on a patient. Transition the patient to another functioning monitor before removing the device from service.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor will not power on or battery does not charge when plugged in.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Failed battery identified after swapping with known-good battery / or no AC input detected despite verified outlet and power cord.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced defective battery and verified charging function / or removed unit from service and sent to vendor for power supply repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Power cord condition and swap performed
  • Battery status indicator behavior
  • Charging icon present or absent
  • Any alarms or error messages
  • Physical condition of battery (swelling, damage)
  • Device powers on with AC only (yes/no)
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Power-related issues are often caused by simple external factors such as cords or batteries. A structured approach ensures these are ruled out quickly while maintaining patient safety. When internal faults are suspected, timely escalation prevents downtime and ensures reliable equipment. Proper documentation supports future troubleshooting and trending.

That is successful troubleshooting.
