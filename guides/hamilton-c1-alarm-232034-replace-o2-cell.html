Hamilton C1

Alarm: 232034 Replace O2 cell

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm 232034 Replace O2 cell on a Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm may be caused by a defective, expired, disconnected, or non-calibrating oxygen cell. Because the O2 cell supports oxygen monitoring accuracy, this alarm should be treated as a patient safety and device reliability issue.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the ventilator from patient use if oxygen monitoring is unreliable or if the ventilator cannot safely verify delivered oxygen concentration.
  • Transfer the patient to another ventilator or approved backup ventilation device if the alarm occurs during active use.
  • Do not continue using the ventilator on a patient with an unresolved oxygen cell alarm unless clinical staff have confirmed an approved temporary workflow.
  • Confirm patient ventilation and oxygen delivery are maintained with a reliable device before troubleshooting continues.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Check the ventilator display for Alarm: 232034 Replace O2 cell.
  • Confirm whether the alarm appears at startup, during pre-use check, during O2 calibration, during active ventilation, or intermittently after warm-up.
  • Ask staff whether O2 calibration failed, the oxygen reading was missing or unstable, or repeated oxygen sensor warnings occurred.
  • Confirm the failure pattern before replacing parts.

Remove the Ventilator from Clinical Use for Sensor Troubleshooting

  • Label the ventilator as not ready for patient use while the oxygen cell issue is being evaluated.
  • Move the ventilator to a safe bench area if possible.
  • Do not return the ventilator to service until O2 calibration and required pre-use checks pass.

Check Basic Power and Startup Condition

  • Connect the ventilator to AC power.
  • Allow the ventilator to complete normal startup.
  • Confirm whether the alarm clears or remains present after initialization.
  • If the alarm clears and O2 calibration passes, continue with required operational checks before returning the ventilator to service.

Inspect the O2 Cell Installation

  • Inspect the oxygen cell area according to facility-approved access procedures.
  • Check that the O2 cell is present, fully seated, properly connected, not physically damaged, not expired, and not contaminated by moisture.
  • If the cell is loose or disconnected, reseat or reconnect it, then retest the ventilator.
  • Do not perform internal disassembly unless following approved service procedures.

Replace the O2 Cell

  • Replace the oxygen cell with the correct approved replacement part.
  • Confirm the replacement cell is new, compatible, and not expired.
  • Avoid touching sensing surfaces or contaminating the cell during installation.
  • Verify that the ventilator recognizes the replacement O2 cell.

Allow Required Stabilization Time

  • After installing the replacement O2 cell, allow any required stabilization or warm-up time before calibration.
  • Keep the ventilator connected to the appropriate gas supply and power source during this period.
  • Do not rush calibration if the sensor output has not stabilized.

Perform O2 Calibration

  • Run the ventilator O2 calibration or pre-use check as required by facility procedure.
  • Confirm whether the calibration passes.
  • Verify that Alarm 232034 Replace O2 cell clears after successful calibration.
  • If calibration passes, continue with required pre-use testing before returning the ventilator to service.

Check the Oxygen Source and Supply Path

  • Confirm the ventilator has a valid oxygen source during calibration.
  • Check the wall oxygen outlet, O2 hose, O2 inlet connection, cylinder supply if used, oxygen supply pressure, and O2 inlet filter condition if externally accessible.
  • Correct any unstable, disconnected, or low-pressure oxygen source issue before repeating calibration.
  • An unstable or missing oxygen supply can cause calibration failure and may be mistaken for an oxygen cell problem.

Inspect O2 Sensor Connection and Related External Conditions

  • If the new O2 cell does not resolve the alarm, inspect the sensor connection area for loose connection, bent contacts, moisture, contamination, corrosion, evidence of fluid intrusion, or damage near the O2 cell compartment.
  • Clean or correct only what is allowed by facility and manufacturer-approved procedures.
  • Do not proceed into board-level repair unless following approved service procedures.

Repeat Startup, Calibration, and Pre-Use Check

  • Power cycle the ventilator if appropriate.
  • Repeat O2 calibration.
  • Run the required pre-use check or self-test.
  • Confirm the alarm does not return, the O2 reading is stable, O2 calibration passes, and the ventilator passes required pre-use testing.
  • If all checks pass, document the repair and return the ventilator to service.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm 232034 Replace O2 cell remains after replacing the O2 cell, allowing stabilization time, verifying the oxygen source, checking the sensor connection, and repeating O2 calibration, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent alarm conditions may indicate an internal oxygen sensing circuit, connector, calibration, or related hardware failure.

The ventilator should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair, bench evaluation, or manufacturer-level service support.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not keep cycling oxygen cells or repeatedly attempting calibration on a ventilator that continues to reject the sensor.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot an unresolved O2 cell alarm on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or approved backup ventilation device first, then evaluate the ventilator. A ventilator with unreliable oxygen monitoring should not be trusted for routine clinical use until calibration and pre-use checks pass.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator displayed Alarm 232034 Replace O2 cell during startup and failed O2 calibration."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Oxygen cell was defective, expired, disconnected, or no longer able to calibrate. Oxygen source and external setup were checked before replacement."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service, replaced O2 cell, allowed stabilization time, performed O2 calibration, completed pre-use check, verified alarm cleared, and returned ventilator to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Alarm 232034 Replace O2 cell should be treated as an oxygen monitoring reliability issue. Start with patient safety, confirm the alarm, replace and calibrate the O2 cell, verify the oxygen source, and escalate when the ventilator still cannot read or calibrate the sensor correctly. Strong documentation protects both the patient and the repair history.

That is successful troubleshooting.
