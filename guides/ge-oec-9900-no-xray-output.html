GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series

No X-Ray Output During Exposure

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

OEC 9900 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series C-Arm that fails to produce X-ray output during exposure. Symptoms may include:

  • System powers on normally
  • Displays and lights function correctly
  • Footswitch and control panel respond
  • No X-ray beam observed when exposure initiated

The goal is to check simple, external, and easily verifiable causes before assuming internal generator or tube faults.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Source

  • Confirm the C-Arm is connected to a properly grounded, live outlet.
  • Use a known-good outlet or verify outlet voltage.

Why: Inadequate power can prevent X-ray generation even if the system appears “on.”

Check Main System Indicators

  • Ensure all control panel LEDs, display indicators, and warning lights are normal.
  • Note any error codes displayed.

Why: Fault codes can indicate interlocks or safety issues preventing exposure.

Inspect Footswitch and Control Inputs

  • Test the footswitch for continuity using a multimeter.
  • Ensure footswitch is securely connected.
  • Attempt exposure from the console (if allowed).

Why: A faulty footswitch or broken signal path can prevent X-ray activation.

Verify Tube and Collimator Interlocks

  • Check that the tube is correctly seated and all collimator interlocks are engaged.
  • Inspect for physical damage or obstruction.

Why: Interlocks prevent X-ray output if the tube or accessories are unsafe.

Check Exposure Settings

  • Confirm kVp, mA, and time settings are within normal ranges.
  • Ensure the system is not in a mode that prevents exposure (e.g., standby or demo mode).

Why: Invalid settings may inhibit the generator from firing.

Examine Cables and Connectors

  • Inspect high-voltage cables, tube connectors, and control cables for visible damage, loose connections, or corrosion.
  • Re-seat connectors as needed.

Why: Poor connections can prevent the tube from receiving trigger signals.

Observe Error Codes or Alarms During Attempted Exposure

  • Document any messages.
  • Cross-check with service manual error descriptions if available.

Why: Provides evidence for internal faults or manufacturer-specific guidance.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks pass and no X-ray output occurs:

  • Device likely has internal generator or X-ray tube failure.
  • Action:
    • Remove from service
    • Label as Out of Service
    • Send for repair or bench evaluation

    Knowing when to stop avoids unsafe attempts and prevents further damage.

    Clinical Use Tip

    • Do not attempt troubleshooting on a patient.
    • Move the patient to another working C-Arm if imaging is required.
    • Only perform tests with the system clear and safe to operate.

    Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

    CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

    Complaint

    What was reported by the clinical staff.

    Example:
    “No X-ray output when footswitch is pressed; system powers on normally.”

    Cause

    What was observed during troubleshooting.

    Example:
    >External checks performed: outlet verified, footswitch tested, interlocks engaged, cables inspected. No faults found externally.

    Resolution

    What action was taken.

    Example:
    >Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for internal repair evaluation.

    Helpful Details to Include

    • Outlet tested with multimeter
    • Footswitch continuity checked
    • Indicator lights normal during power-on
    • Exposure attempted from footswitch and console
    • No unusual sounds, smells, or heat from tube or generator
    • Final status: Device Out of Service, awaiting repair

    Final Thought

    Patient safety and proper escalation are critical. Logical troubleshooting starts with external factors, progresses methodically, and stops before unsafe internal interventions. Accurate CCR documentation ensures repair and clinical staff understand the issue clearly.

    That is successful troubleshooting.
