Philips HeartStart MRx

No / Poor ECG Signal Quality

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

HeartStart MRx

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the HeartStart MRx defibrillator displays:

  • No ECG waveform on the screen
  • Flatline or intermittent ECG signal despite patient connection
  • Low-quality or noisy ECG traces

The focus is on external, logical troubleshooting steps first, avoiding internal board-level repairs, and ensuring patient safety.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Confirm Patient Safety First

  • Ensure the patient is monitored safely and has a backup ECG source if available.
  • Do not rely on this device alone if ECG is unclear.

Check Lead Connections

  • Verify all ECG leads are firmly attached to the device and to the patient.
  • Inspect for bent or broken pins, loose connections, or connector corrosion.
  • Ensure limb electrodes are placed correctly and skin is clean and dry.

Inspect Electrodes / Pads

  • Check for expired or damaged electrodes.
  • Replace electrodes if adhesive is poor or gel is dry.
  • For pediatric or adult selection, ensure the correct pad type is in use.

Examine Lead Wires for Damage

  • Look for cuts, frays, or kinks in the ECG cables.
  • Swap with a known-good lead set if available.

Confirm Device Settings

  • Verify ECG leads are properly selected (e.g., 3-lead vs 5-lead configuration).
  • Ensure filters (AC filter, baseline filter) are correctly configured.
  • Adjust gain or sweep speed if waveform appears small or squashed.

Check for Environmental Interference

  • Move away from high-voltage devices, electrosurgical units, or mobile phones.
  • Ensure no metal objects or grounding issues are causing artifact.

Power Cycle the Device

  • Turn off and then on the MRx to clear transient software or signal errors.

If the Problem Persists

External causes (leads, electrodes, placement, interference) have been ruled out.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation by qualified service personnel

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot on an active patient without an alternate monitoring method.
  • Always maintain continuous patient monitoring while addressing ECG signal issues.
  • Reassess patient placement and electrode site if motion artifact is suspected.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“ECG waveform is absent or low-quality despite patient connection.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Leads were disconnected and electrodes were expired; device settings correct.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced electrodes, reconnected leads, verified proper lead placement; waveform restored.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested (if using external power)
  • Lead set swapped
  • Alarm behavior noted
  • Indicator lights on leads/device
  • Presence of motion artifact or electrical interference
  • Final device status after troubleshooting

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting balances patient safety with systematic checks. Begin with easily verifiable external factors before escalating. Document all steps clearly using the CCR method to support maintenance records and regulatory compliance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
