Smiths Medical Level 1 Series H-1000 / H-1200

Heater Not Reaching Set Temperature

Asset Type

Fluid & Blood Warmer

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Level 1 Series H-1000 / H-1200

What This Guide Helps With

Low or unstable fluid temperature despite heater being on, often caused by setup, flow rate, or accessory-related issues rather than internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove device from active patient use.
  • Switch to an alternate warming method before troubleshooting.

Verify Power and Unit Status

  • Confirm the unit is powered on.
  • Check for no active alarms.
  • Ensure the heater is enabled.
  • Expected: Display shows active heating mode and target temperature.

Check Set Temperature vs Actual Temperature

  • Compare the setpoint with displayed outlet temperature.
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether the heater is attempting to reach the correct value.

Inspect Disposable Cassette / Set Installation

  • Ensure the cassette is properly seated and fully latched.
  • Expected: No gaps, secure fit, no “cassette not detected” warnings.

Confirm Correct Disposable Type

  • Verify the correct Level 1 compatible cassette/set is being used.
  • Why it matters: Incorrect disposables can limit heat transfer efficiency.

Check Fluid Flow Rate

  • Assess current infusion rate.
  • Extremely high flow rates may prevent adequate heating.
  • Action: Reduce flow temporarily and observe temperature response.
  • Expected: Temperature should rise when flow is reduced.

Inspect Fluid Source Temperature

  • Determine if fluids are starting very cold (e.g., refrigerated blood).
  • Why it matters: The unit may not compensate fast enough at high flow rates.

Check for Air in Line or Improper Priming

  • Look for air gaps or incomplete priming in the cassette.
  • Expected: Fully primed line with continuous fluid column.

Inspect Tubing and Connections

  • Ensure no kinks, occlusions, or loose connections restricting flow or heat transfer.

Evaluate Environmental Factors

  • Check if the unit is in a cold OR or exposed to airflow (vents, doors).
  • Why it matters: Ambient conditions can impact heating performance.

Perform a Power Cycle

  • Turn the unit off.
  • Wait 30 seconds.
  • Restart the unit.
  • Expected: System resets and resumes normal heating operation.

Test with Known Good Setup

  • Swap cassette and tubing with a new set.
  • Expected: If resolved, issue was related to disposable or setup.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely internal (heater element, temperature sensor, or control circuitry).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and saves time.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a fluid warmer while connected to a patient. Always transition to another warming method before evaluating performance.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Fluid warmer not reaching set temperature during transfusion."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"High flow rate combined with improperly seated cassette reduced effective heat transfer."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated cassette and adjusted flow rate; verified unit reached and maintained set temperature."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and stable power confirmed
  • Cassette type and lot number checked
  • Flow rate during issue documented
  • No active alarms present
  • No unusual sounds, heat, or odor
  • Device passed/failed functional test after intervention
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent to repair)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple, external checks—especially flow rate and disposables in fluid warmers. Protecting patient safety comes first, and recognizing internal failure early ensures proper escalation. Clear documentation supports both safety and long-term reliability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
