Puritan Bennett 840

Alarm: ↓VE TOT Minute volume below limit

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Puritan Bennett

Model

840

What This Guide Helps With

↓VE TOT alarm indicating total minute ventilation below set limit, risking inadequate patient ventilation and CO₂ retention.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s respiratory status
  • If ventilation is inadequate, provide manual ventilation (BVM)
  • Switch to a backup ventilator if needed

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ↓VE TOT alarm is active on the ventilator
  • Observe low displayed minute volume (VE)
  • Check if alarm occurs during operation and not just startup
  • Look for accompanying low tidal volume or apnea alarms

3. Check Patient Condition

  • Observe chest rise and respiratory effort
  • Check airway patency (ET tube, trach)
  • Assess for sedation, apnea, or decreased respiratory drive

4. Check Breathing Circuit for Leaks or Disconnection

  • Ensure all circuit connections are secure
  • Inspect for loose fittings or partial disconnections
  • Check for leaks, cracks, or water accumulation

5. Check Ventilator Settings

  • Verify tidal volume (Vt) settings
  • Verify respiratory rate
  • Confirm alarm limits are appropriate for the patient

6. Check Flow Sensors and Measurement Accuracy

  • Inspect flow sensor for contamination or moisture
  • Ensure sensor connections are secure
  • Look for erratic or inconsistent readings

7. Run Short Self Test (SST)

  • Initiate SST per manufacturer procedure
  • Ensure the test completes without errors

8. Isolate the Cause

  • Patient-related: Reduced effort, apnea, clinical condition
  • Circuit-related: Leak or disconnection
  • Configuration-related: Low Vt or respiratory rate
  • Device-related: Sensor or internal measurement issue

If the Problem Persists

If the ↓VE TOT alarm continues after external causes and settings are verified and SST is completed, a device-related issue is likely.

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilation issues on an active patient. Always move the patient to a backup device before continuing troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming ↓VE TOT with low measured minute volume during patient use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Leak identified in patient circuit causing reduced delivered and measured ventilation volume."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Re-secured circuit connections, verified no leaks, confirmed normal VE readings. Alarm cleared and ventilator returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Low minute ventilation alarms should always be treated as a patient safety priority. Start with patient assessment and simple external checks before progressing to device evaluation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
