Drager Infinity Kappa Series

Alarm / Audio System Failure or No Sound

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Infinity Kappa Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Infinity Kappa monitor alarm system fails to produce sound, emits inconsistent or low-volume tones, or behaves abnormally. Focus is on external checks, system settings, and safe escalation rather than internal electronics repair. Patient safety is paramount—alarms must be reliable to alert staff of critical patient events.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Monitor Power and Connections

  • Ensure the monitor is powered on and has no low battery or UPS alarms.
  • Confirm the power cable is fully seated and that the monitor has stable AC or battery power.
  • Reason: Insufficient power can affect the alarm circuitry or mute audio output.

Check Audio Volume and Settings

  • Access the monitor’s settings menu to verify that alarm volume is not muted or set too low.
  • If the monitor has separate volume levels for different alarm priorities (high, medium, low), check all levels.
  • Reason: Accidental changes in volume settings are a common cause of “no sound.”

Inspect External Speakers or Cables (if applicable)

  • Some Infinity Kappa monitors can be connected to external audio systems; verify connections.
  • Swap cables or connectors if needed to rule out broken wires.
  • Reason: External speaker failure or cable damage can mimic an internal alarm failure.

Verify Alarm Configuration

  • Confirm the monitor is configured to sound alarms for all active parameters.
  • Check that alarms are not silenced globally or per parameter.
  • Reason: Incorrect alarm configuration may appear as a failure of the audio system.

Test With Another Monitor

  • If possible, place the patient on a known-good monitor and check that alarms function properly.
  • Reason: This isolates the issue to the monitor or environment rather than patient parameters.

Observe Indicator Lights or Error Messages

  • Look for any on-screen alerts or warning icons indicating audio system faults.
  • Note any beeping patterns or LED indicators that might signify internal errors.
  • Reason: Error codes may indicate the problem is beyond external checks.

If the Problem Persists

External causes have been ruled out; the issue is likely internal to the monitor’s audio system or main board.

Action:

  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label the monitor “Out of Service.”
  • Send the unit to the biomedical repair bench or authorized service for evaluation.
  • Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper Clinical Engineering troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not troubleshoot alarms while the monitor is attached to a patient.
  • Move the patient to another functioning monitor to maintain patient safety before performing any further tests.
  • Confirm audible alarms on the replacement monitor before leaving the patient unattended.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor alarms are not producing sound; staff cannot hear alerts.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Alarm volume was verified; external connections and settings checked; no audible output detected.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Monitor removed from service, labeled ‘Out of Service,’ and sent for repair. Patient transferred to another functional monitor.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet and power verified
  • Volume settings checked
  • External speaker connections verified
  • Alarm configuration reviewed
  • Any unusual display alerts or error messages noted
  • Final device status documented as out of service

Final Thought

Patient safety relies on functional alarms. Following logical, stepwise troubleshooting protects patients and ensures only faulty devices are escalated for repair. Proper documentation ensures traceability and supports future preventive maintenance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
