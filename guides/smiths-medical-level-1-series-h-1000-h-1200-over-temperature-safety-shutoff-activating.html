Smiths Medical Level 1 Series H-1000 / H-1200

Over-temperature safety shutoff activating

Asset Type

Fluid & Blood Warmer

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Level 1 Series H-1000 / H-1200

What This Guide Helps With

Frequent over-temperature alarms or automatic heater shutdowns, often caused by flow issues, improper setup, or disposable-related problems rather than internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from patient use immediately.
  • Transition to an alternate warming method before troubleshooting.

Verify Alarm Condition

  • Confirm the unit is displaying an over-temperature or safety shutdown alarm.
  • Expected: Heater disabled with alarm indication.

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn the unit off, wait 10–15 seconds, then power back on.
  • Why it matters: Clears temporary faults or sensor glitches.
  • If alarm clears and does not return → issue resolved.

Check Set Temperature

  • Verify the setpoint is within normal operating range (typically ~37–41°C).
  • Why it matters: Excessively high setpoints can trigger safety shutoffs.

Inspect Disposable Cassette / Set Installation

  • Ensure cassette is fully seated, properly latched, and aligned.
  • Expected: No gaps, no “cassette” errors.
  • Why it matters: Poor thermal contact can cause localized overheating.

Confirm Correct Disposable Type

  • Verify the cassette/set is correct for Level 1 use and not expired or damaged.
  • Why it matters: Incorrect or degraded disposables can disrupt heat transfer.

Assess Fluid Flow Rate

  • Check infusion rate. Extremely low or no flow can cause overheating.
  • Expected: Adequate continuous flow through the cassette.
  • If flow is too low → adjust rate or confirm upstream infusion source.

Check for Line Occlusions or Kinks

  • Inspect all tubing for clamps, kinks, or blockages.
  • Why it matters: Restricted flow traps heat in the cassette, triggering shutdown.

Evaluate Fluid Source and Temperature

  • Confirm fluid is not already excessively warm prior to entering the unit.
  • Why it matters: Pre-warmed fluids can push outlet temperature above limits.

Inspect Temperature Sensors (External Check Only)

  • Ensure no visible damage, contamination, or improper placement of temperature probes within the cassette.
  • Why it matters: Incorrect readings can falsely trigger safety shutdown.

Check for Environmental Factors

  • Ensure the unit is not near external heat sources or vents blowing hot air.
  • Why it matters: Ambient heat can affect sensor readings and system response.

If the Problem Persists

If over-temperature shutdown continues after verifying setup, flow, and disposables, common external causes have been ruled out.

The issue is likely internal (heater control board, temperature sensor failure, or calibration drift).

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a fluid warmer while connected to a patient. Always transfer to another warming method before evaluating alarms or performance issues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Unit repeatedly shuts down with over-temperature alarm during use in OR."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Low flow rate due to partially occluded IV tubing causing localized overheating in cassette."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleared occlusion, restored proper flow, and verified normal operation without further alarms."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet and power verified
  • Disposable cassette condition and type
  • Flow rate at time of alarm
  • Presence of kinks or occlusions
  • Alarm frequency and reset behavior
  • Ambient conditions
  • Final device status after testing

Final Thought

Over-temperature shutdowns are most often caused by flow or setup issues, not internal failure. A structured, external-first approach protects patients and avoids unnecessary repairs. Always escalate appropriately and document clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
