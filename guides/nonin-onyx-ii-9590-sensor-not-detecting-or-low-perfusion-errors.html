Nonin Onyx II 9590

Sensor Not Detecting or Low Perfusion Errors

Asset Type

Pulse Oximeter

Manufacturer

Nonin

Model

Onyx II 9590

What This Guide Helps With

Addresses failure to detect signal or low perfusion readings due to positioning, patient condition, battery, or environmental factors.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from active patient use if readings are unreliable.
  • Use an alternate monitoring method if needed.

Expected: Patient monitoring continues safely
Why it matters: Inaccurate SpO2 readings can lead to improper clinical decisions

Verify Device Power and Startup Behavior

  • Power on the device and observe the display.
  • Confirm the unit powers on normally.
  • Check for low battery indicator.
  • Observe if display activates fully.

Expected: Device powers on with normal display behavior
Why it matters: Low power can cause weak signal detection or failure to read

Check Battery Condition

  • Inspect and replace batteries if needed.
  • Open battery compartment.
  • Verify correct battery orientation.
  • Replace with fresh, high-quality batteries.

Expected: Device powers reliably with no low battery warning
Why it matters: Weak batteries reduce sensor sensitivity and signal processing

Verify Proper Finger Placement

  • Ensure the patient’s finger is correctly positioned in the device.
  • Insert finger fully into the clip.
  • Align fingernail facing upward (typical orientation).
  • Ensure no movement during reading.

Expected: Device begins detecting pulse within a few seconds
Why it matters: Improper placement is the most common cause of no detection

Assess Patient Condition (Perfusion)

  • Evaluate for factors affecting circulation.
  • Check for cold extremities.
  • Look for poor capillary refill.
  • Warm the finger if needed.

Expected: Improved signal detection with adequate perfusion
Why it matters: Low perfusion directly impacts pulse oximeter accuracy

Remove External Interference

  • Check for anything that may block or distort the sensor signal.
  • Remove nail polish or artificial nails.
  • Clean finger if soiled.
  • Avoid bright ambient light (cover with towel if needed).

Expected: Improved signal acquisition and stable readings
Why it matters: Optical interference can prevent proper detection

Test on Known Good Subject

  • Verify device function using a healthy individual.
  • Place device on yourself or another staff member.
  • Confirm consistent SpO2 and pulse readings.

Expected: Normal readings on known good subject
Why it matters: Helps determine if issue is patient-related vs device-related

Inspect Device for Physical Damage

  • Check the sensor clip and internal surfaces.
  • Look for cracked housing or loose hinge.
  • Inspect internal emitter/detector area for debris or damage.

Expected: No visible damage or obstruction
Why it matters: Physical damage can prevent proper signal transmission

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and the device still fails to detect or shows low perfusion errors, the issue is likely internal.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot on a patient requiring continuous monitoring. Always move the patient to a functioning pulse oximeter before testing this unit.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Pulse oximeter not detecting signal and showing low perfusion on patient."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Weak batteries and improper finger placement identified during testing."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced batteries and corrected finger positioning; device returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Battery condition verified
  • Finger placement confirmed
  • Patient perfusion status assessed
  • Device tested on known good subject
  • Presence of nail polish or interference
  • Physical condition of sensor clip
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple, external checks like placement, power, and patient condition. Recognizing when the issue is environmental versus device-related ensures safe patient care and efficient escalation when needed. Proper documentation supports long-term reliability tracking.

That is successful troubleshooting.
