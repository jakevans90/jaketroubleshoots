Abbott Precision Xceed Pro

Connectivity or Data Transfer Failure

Asset Type

Blood Glucose Meter

Manufacturer

Abbott

Model

Precision Xceed Pro

What This Guide Helps With

Failure to upload results, sync data, or communicate with docking station or data management system due to connection, configuration, or accessory issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not delay patient care while troubleshooting connectivity
  • Use another meter if results are needed urgently

Expected: Patient testing continues without interruption

Why it matters: Connectivity issues do not affect test accuracy but can delay documentation

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to upload data or dock the meter and observe behavior
  • Check for no upload activity, error messages, or dock not recognizing meter

Expected: Issue is reproducible

Why it matters: Confirms this is a real communication failure, not user error

Check Docking Station Power and Status

  • Verify power cable is plugged in
  • Confirm outlet is working
  • Observe LED indicators on dock

Expected: Dock shows power and readiness

Why it matters: No power = no communication

Inspect Physical Connections

  • Reseat Ethernet/USB cables
  • Look for damaged or loose connectors
  • Try a known-good cable if available

Expected: Secure, intact connections

Why it matters: Loose or damaged cables are a common cause of failure

Clean Meter and Dock Contacts

  • Inspect for debris, residue, or corrosion
  • Clean contacts with approved wipes
  • Allow to dry fully

Expected: Clean, unobstructed contacts

Why it matters: Poor contact prevents both charging and data transfer

Verify Proper Meter Docking

  • Remove and reseat the meter
  • Confirm it sits firmly and evenly

Expected: Meter is recognized by dock

Why it matters: Improper seating interrupts communication pins

Check Network Availability (if applicable)

  • Check network port status lights
  • Verify other devices on same network are functioning

Expected: Active network connection

Why it matters: Meter may be functioning correctly but unable to reach server

Restart the Meter

  • Turn the meter off
  • Wait 10–15 seconds
  • Turn it back on and retry upload

Expected: Temporary faults are cleared

Why it matters: Resets minor software or communication glitches

Try a Different Docking Station (if available)

  • Test the meter in another known-good dock

Expected: Successful communication on alternate dock

Why it matters: Helps isolate whether the issue is meter-related or dock-related

Check Date/Time Settings

  • Verify the meter’s date and time are correct

Expected: Accurate system time

Why it matters: Incorrect timestamps can cause upload rejection by data systems

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and connectivity still fails, the issue is likely internal (communication hardware or firmware fault).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or further bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary downtime and ensures reliable devices remain in use.

Clinical Use Tip

Always move the patient to another meter before troubleshooting connectivity. Never delay glucose testing due to data upload issues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Meter would not upload results when placed in docking station."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Dirty communication contacts preventing proper docking connection."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleaned meter and dock contacts; verified successful data transfer."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Dock powered and LED status confirmed
  • Cables reseated or replaced
  • Alternate dock tested
  • Contact points cleaned
  • Network status verified
  • Meter reboot performed
  • Final upload status confirmed

Final Thought

Connectivity issues are often external and preventable. A structured approach—starting with power, connections, and docking—resolves most problems quickly. Escalate only after these are ruled out, and always prioritize patient care and accurate documentation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
