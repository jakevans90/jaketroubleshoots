Physio-Control LIFEPAK 20 / 20e

Shock Delivery Issues / Charging Errors

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

Physio-Control

Model

LIFEPAK 20 / 20e

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a LIFEPAK 20 / 20e defibrillator fails to charge properly, does not deliver shocks as expected, or displays errors during charge. Common external causes include battery issues, poor electrode connections, or environmental factors. Internal circuitry or capacitor issues are possible but require escalation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify the device is powered on

  • Check that the main power switch is on and the display is functional.
  • A non-responsive unit can indicate dead batteries or a blown fuse.

Check the battery status and connections

  • Ensure the battery is fully seated in its compartment.
  • Inspect for corrosion, debris, or physical damage on battery terminals.
  • Swap with a known good battery if available to rule out battery failure.

Inspect charging system and cables

  • Check the AC power connection if the unit uses mains charging.
  • Confirm the charger indicator shows active charging.
  • Look for frayed, pinched, or damaged charging cables.

Examine electrode pads and connections

  • Ensure pads are properly connected to the device and patient (or test load).
  • Verify the correct pad type for the model.
  • Inspect for torn or dried-out pads that can prevent proper shock delivery.

Perform a device self-test

  • Navigate to the device’s built-in self-test function.
  • Note any error codes related to charging, shock delivery, or battery issues.
  • Document the exact error for work order purposes.

Check environmental factors

  • Ensure the device is at proper operating temperature (not too cold or hot).
  • Remove any metal objects that could interfere with charge or shock delivery.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out (power, battery, pads, cables).

The issue is likely internal (capacitor failure, PCB issue, or internal charger fault).

Escalation:

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send to Physio-Control-authorized repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Engineering should avoid internal board-level repairs for safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt shocks on a patient if the device shows charge errors.
  • Always have a backup defibrillator ready before troubleshooting.
  • Prioritize patient safety over completing the troubleshooting on a live device.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Defibrillator fails to charge and will not deliver a shock.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Battery seated properly but unit fails self-test; charging circuit and cables verified as functional.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for authorized repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Battery voltage and status at the time of testing
  • Charger indicator lights (if present)
  • Any error codes from self-test
  • Electrode pad type and expiration date
  • Environmental conditions during testing
  • Final device status (OOS and sent for repair)

Final Thought

Troubleshooting LIFEPAK 20 / 20e defibrillators requires a methodical approach that prioritizes external checks first and patient safety at all times. Knowing when to escalate prevents harm and preserves device integrity. Accurate CCR documentation ensures clear communication and compliance with Clinical Engineering standards.

That is successful troubleshooting.
