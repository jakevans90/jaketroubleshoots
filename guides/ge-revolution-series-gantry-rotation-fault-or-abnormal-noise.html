GE Healthcare Revolution Series

Gantry Rotation Fault or Abnormal Noise

Asset Type

CT Scanner

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Revolution Series

What This Guide Helps With

Gantry rotation faults, abnormal grinding, rubbing, clicking, vibration, or loud mechanical noise during CT gantry movement that may prevent scanning, interrupt exams, or indicate a mechanical rotation system problem.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Stop the scan immediately if abnormal gantry noise, vibration, rotation fault, or unexpected movement occurs.

Action:

  • Remove the patient from the scanner if safe to do so
  • Do not continue scanning if the gantry noise is severe or worsening
  • Notify CT staff that the scanner should not be used until evaluated
  • Place the system out of service if rotation faults repeat

Expected: Patient is removed safely and the scanner is prevented from causing injury or equipment damage

Why it matters: Gantry rotation involves high-speed mechanical movement. A bearing, drive, brake, encoder, cable, or obstruction issue can worsen quickly if scanning continues.

Verify the Reported Fault and Noise

Confirm the exact symptom reported by CT staff.

Action:

  • Ask when the issue occurs:
  • During startup
  • During gantry reset
  • During scout/localizer
  • During axial scan
  • During helical scan
  • Only at certain gantry angles
  • Only at high-speed rotation
  • Record any displayed error message, fault code, or system event
  • Ask whether the noise is new, intermittent, or progressively worse
  • Determine if image acquisition stopped or if the scan completed

Expected: The failure condition is clearly documented before service troubleshooting begins

Why it matters: A fault during high-speed rotation points more toward the drive, bearing, encoder, slip ring, or gantry control system. Noise during slow movement may point more toward obstruction, cover interference, brake drag, or mechanical rubbing.

Listen for the Type of Abnormal Noise

Different sounds can point toward different failure areas.

Action:

  • With the system idle and safe, ask staff to describe the sound:
  • Grinding
  • Scraping
  • Rubbing
  • Clicking
  • Thumping
  • High-pitched squeal
  • Heavy vibration
  • Uneven rotation sound
  • Metal-on-metal noise

Expected: The noise description helps narrow down the likely mechanical area

Possible meaning:

  • Grinding: bearing, roller, brake, or rotating assembly issue
  • Scraping/rubbing: cover interference, loose component, cable drag, debris
  • Clicking/thumping: loose hardware, gantry imbalance, mechanical impact, drive issue
  • Squealing: belt, bearing, brake, or motor-related issue
  • Vibration: imbalance, bearing wear, drive instability, or rotor issue

Check for External Obstruction Around the Gantry

Look for anything interfering with gantry movement.

Action:

  • Inspect around the gantry opening
  • Check for linens, straps, positioning aids, IV lines, ECG leads, oxygen tubing, or patient accessories
  • Verify nothing is caught between the table, gantry, or bore area
  • Inspect visible covers for cracks, displacement, or signs of rubbing
  • Check if any trim panels appear loose or shifted

Expected: No external objects or covers are interfering with gantry rotation

Why it matters: A simple obstruction or displaced cover can create abnormal noise and may trigger a rotation fault.

Check Table and Gantry Clearance

A table-positioning or patient setup issue can sometimes be mistaken for gantry noise.

Action:

  • Inspect the patient table position
  • Verify the table is not contacting the gantry covers
  • Check for unusual resistance, collision marks, or rubbing near the gantry opening
  • Move the table only if safe and permitted by site procedure
  • Ask whether the fault occurred during table movement, gantry movement, or scanning

Expected: Table movement is normal and there is no contact with the gantry

Why it matters: CT faults may be reported as “gantry noise” when the actual issue is table-to-gantry interference or accessory contact.

Inspect Gantry Covers and Visible Mechanical Areas

Loose covers can cause scraping, rattling, or vibration.

Action:

  • Visually inspect the front and rear gantry covers
  • Check for loose fasteners, cracked panels, missing screws, or shifted covers
  • Look for scrape marks, plastic dust, metal shavings, or impact marks
  • Do not remove covers unless authorized and trained

Expected: Covers are secure and there are no signs of rubbing or mechanical contact

Why it matters: Even minor cover misalignment can make loud noise during rotation and may trigger protective faults.

Review System Error Messages and Event Logs

Use the scanner interface or service access to identify the exact fault.

Action:

  • Record the displayed error message or code
  • Check system logs for gantry rotation, rotor, drive, encoder, brake, or speed-related faults
  • Look for repeated faults around the same time as the reported noise
  • Note whether the system reports overspeed, underspeed, position error, rotation timeout, servo fault, or gantry control fault

Expected: Logs identify the affected subsystem or provide direction for further service action

Why it matters: Gantry rotation faults are usually controlled by safety monitoring. The system may shut down rotation if position, speed, drive current, or feedback signals are abnormal.

Attempt a Controlled Restart Only if Noise Was Minor

If the issue was a single fault with no severe noise, a controlled restart may clear a temporary control error.

Action:

  • Follow facility and manufacturer shutdown procedure
  • Power down the CT system normally
  • Wait the required shutdown interval
  • Restart the system
  • Observe startup and gantry initialization
  • Do not run patient scans if abnormal noise returns

Expected: System initializes normally with no abnormal gantry noise or rotation error

Why it matters: Some gantry control faults can be temporary, but repeated faults or mechanical noise should not be treated as a simple reboot issue.

Do Not Continue Scanning if Noise Repeats

Repeated gantry noise should be treated as a mechanical or drive-related failure.

Action:

  • Stop use of the scanner
  • Notify the imaging supervisor
  • Contact GE service or qualified CT service personnel
  • Document the fault code, time, scan type, and noise description
  • Leave the system unavailable for patient use until evaluated

Expected: The scanner remains out of service until the gantry rotation system is checked

Why it matters: Continuing to scan with abnormal gantry noise can damage the rotating assembly, bearing system, drive system, slip ring, or internal cabling.

Check for Recent Service, Movement, or Facility Changes

Recent work can create gantry rotation problems.

Action:

  • Ask whether the scanner was recently serviced
  • Check if covers were recently removed
  • Ask if the room had power work, HVAC issues, vibration, or construction
  • Check whether the scanner was recently calibrated, moved, or had tube/detector service
  • Review whether the issue started after a specific event

Expected: Any recent change is identified and reported to service

Why it matters: Loose covers, disturbed cabling, calibration issues, or mechanical adjustments after service can cause rotation faults or noise.

Escalate for Internal Gantry Inspection

Internal gantry inspection should be performed only by qualified service personnel.

Possible areas to inspect:

  • Gantry drive motor
  • Drive belt or drive mechanism
  • Gantry bearing system
  • Brake assembly
  • Encoder or position feedback system
  • Slip ring assembly
  • Rotating cable harnesses
  • Loose internal hardware
  • Cooling components
  • Foreign debris inside gantry
  • Rotor balance or mechanical alignment

Expected: Internal inspection identifies the source of noise or confirms the gantry is safe to operate

Why it matters: Most true gantry rotation faults require trained CT service support because the gantry contains high-voltage, high-speed, and precision-aligned components.

Verify System Operation After Repair

After service correction, confirm safe operation before returning the scanner to clinical use.

Action:

  • Run manufacturer-approved system checks
  • Verify gantry rotation completes normally
  • Confirm no abnormal noise during startup or test rotation
  • Run required calibration or QC tests
  • Confirm no repeat gantry rotation faults in logs
  • Have CT staff verify normal scan workflow

Expected: Gantry rotation is smooth, quiet, and fault-free

Why it matters: The scanner should not return to patient use until both mechanical operation and system diagnostics are normal.

If the Problem Persists

If the gantry rotation fault or abnormal noise continues after basic checks, external causes such as visible obstruction, table-to-gantry contact, loose visible covers, recent setup changes, and environmental contributors have been ruled out. Keep the CT scanner out of service, label it Out of Service, do not perform additional patient scans, and escalate to GE service or a qualified CT service provider for repair or bench evaluation.

  • Provide the exact fault message, error code, time of occurrence, and scan type
  • Include whether the noise is grinding, rubbing, clicking, squealing, or vibration
  • Note whether the issue happens during startup, scout, axial scan, or helical scan

Clinical Use Tip

Abnormal gantry noise should never be ignored. A CT gantry rotates at high speed during scanning, and a small mechanical issue can become a major failure if the system continues to run. Do not troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup scanner first when imaging is required so therapy and diagnostic continuity are maintained safely.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"GE Healthcare Revolution Series CT scanner reported gantry rotation fault with abnormal noise during scan operation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Removed scanner from service. Verified reported noise/fault with CT staff. Inspected external gantry area, table clearance, visible covers, and possible obstructions. Reviewed available system error information. No patient injury reported. External causes were ruled out and gantry rotation system required qualified CT service inspection."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Scanner labeled Out of Service and removed from clinical use pending GE service or qualified CT service evaluation and verification."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Exact error message or fault code
  • Time and date of fault
  • Scan type being performed
  • Whether fault occurred during startup, scout, axial, or helical scan
  • Description of alarm behavior or fault behavior
  • Description of noise
  • Whether noise is intermittent or constant
  • Whether gantry completes rotation
  • Accessories, positioning aids, tubing, leads, or linens checked or swapped
  • Power behavior during startup, reset, or scan attempt
  • Environmental factors such as HVAC issues, construction, vibration, or recent room work
  • Indicator lights, system messages, event logs, or service warnings
  • Whether table movement was involved
  • Any recent service or room work
  • Photos of visible damage or loose covers if present
  • Final device status, including whether the scanner was returned to service or remains Out of Service

Final Thought

A gantry rotation fault or abnormal noise on a GE Healthcare Revolution Series CT scanner should be treated as a serious equipment issue. Basic checks should focus on patient safety, obstruction removal, table clearance, visible cover problems, and fault documentation. If the noise repeats or sounds mechanical, the scanner should stay out of service until qualified CT service personnel inspect the gantry rotation system.

That is successful troubleshooting.
