Brewer Access High-Low Series

Powered Backrest Does Not Raise or Lower

Asset Type

Exam Table

Manufacturer

Brewer

Model

Access High-Low Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting a powered backrest that will not move due to power, foot control, obstruction, safety-switch, actuator, or control-system issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

Remove the exam table from patient use before troubleshooting the powered backrest.

If a patient is on the table, assist clinical staff with transferring the patient safely to another appropriate exam surface before attempting movement checks.

Expected outcome: Troubleshooting can be performed without risk of unexpected table movement or patient injury.

2. Confirm the Table Has a Powered Backrest

Verify that the table is equipped with powered backrest controls and that the complaint concerns the BACK UP and BACK DOWN functions, not the table height function.

Brewer Access High-Low powered-back models include the 6500, 6501, 6800, and 6801 series.

Expected outcome: The reported failure is confirmed as a powered backrest issue.

3. Inspect for Obstructions or Mechanical Binding

Inspect around and beneath the backrest for paper, linens, exam accessories, stored items, wall contact, or other objects that could prevent movement.

Confirm the backrest is not contacting the rear cabinet, wall, or surrounding furniture.

Expected outcome: The backrest movement path is clear.

If an obstruction is found: Remove it, retest the backrest, and stop troubleshooting if normal movement is restored.

4. Verify Facility Power and Table Power Status

Confirm the power cord is firmly connected to the exam table and plugged into a known-working receptacle.

Check the power cord and plug for visible damage, pinching, or looseness.

Verify the table’s rear ON/OFF switch is in the ON position.

Expected outcome: The table has reliable incoming power and is enabled for operation.

If power is restored and the backrest functions normally: Return the table to service only after completing a safe operational check and documenting the repair.

5. Determine Whether Other Powered Functions Operate

Using the foot control, test the table height function.

Observe whether the table raises and lowers normally.

Expected outcome:

  • If table height also does not operate, suspect a general power, foot control, fuse, or control-system issue.
  • If table height operates but the backrest does not, the problem is isolated to the powered backrest circuit, backrest controls, safety switches, or actuator.

6. Position the Table Safely for Backrest Testing

If table height movement is functional, raise the table above approximately 28 inches before attempting to lower the powered backrest.

This provides clearance so the backrest does not contact the rear cabinet during testing, as specified in Brewer’s operating test.

Expected outcome: The backrest can be safely tested through its available range of motion.

7. Test the Backrest Controls

Press and hold BACK UP, then press and hold BACK DOWN on the foot control.

Observe whether the backrest:

  • Moves normally in either direction
  • Moves in only one direction
  • Hums without moving
  • Clicks without moving
  • Produces no sound or movement

Expected outcome: The backrest should move smoothly upward and downward when commanded.

If the backrest functions normally during testing: Perform a complete operational check before returning the table to service.

8. Inspect the Foot Control Externally

Inspect the foot control for:

  • Damaged or stuck BACK UP or BACK DOWN pedals
  • Fluid intrusion, cracking, or excessive wear
  • Pinched, cut, stretched, or disconnected foot-control cable
  • Loose connection where the foot control attaches to the table

Reseat any accessible external foot-control connection with the table powered off, then restore power and retest.

Expected outcome: A secure and undamaged foot control sends backrest commands reliably.

If an approved known-good compatible foot control is available: Substitute it for testing according to department practice. If normal backrest movement returns, remove the defective foot control from service and replace it.

9. Check for Recent Heavy Use or Temporary Overtemperature Condition

Ask whether the table stopped operating after repeated repositioning or heavy use.

If the backrest stopped after extensive cycling, remove the table from use and allow approximately 30 minutes for cooling before retesting. Brewer identifies control-box overtemperature as a possible cause of stopped operation after heavy use.

Expected outcome: Temporary thermal protection resets after cooling.

If function returns: Document the condition and complete operational testing before releasing the table.

10. Observe for Backrest Safety-Switch Behavior

If the backrest begins moving downward, gently press on a corner of the back portion of the seat only during a controlled, unloaded test.

The backrest should momentarily stop when the safety-switch area is activated.

Expected outcome: Safety-switch operation interrupts downward backrest movement as designed.

If the backrest does not respond properly or will not move at all: Do not bypass safety switches or continue clinical use.

11. Identify When Bench Evaluation Is Required

If incoming power, table power switch, external connections, foot control condition, clearances, and cooling time have been checked but the powered backrest still does not raise or lower, suspect an internal fault such as:

  • Backrest actuator failure
  • Backrest safety-switch failure
  • Damaged internal wiring
  • Control-box failure

Brewer’s troubleshooting guidance identifies backrest actuator and backrest safety-switch faults as possible causes of powered-backrest no-movement conditions.

Expected outcome: The device is correctly escalated rather than repeatedly operated with an unresolved movement or safety fault.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out, and the problem is likely related to the powered backrest actuator, safety-switch circuit, internal wiring, or control system.

The exam table should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation according to Clinical Engineering procedure

Do not bypass safety switches or continue using the table with an unreliable powered backrest. Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot powered table movement while a patient is on the table. Transfer the patient first, clear the movement path, and test the backrest only when unexpected motion cannot cause injury.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the Brewer Access High-Low exam table powered backrest would not raise or lower using the foot control."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified table height function operated normally, but powered backrest remained inoperative after checking power, external connections, foot control condition, and movement clearances; internal backrest actuator or control-circuit failure suspected."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the exam table from service, labeled it Out of Service, and routed the unit for bench evaluation and repair of the powered backrest system."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified functional
  • Power cord and table connection inspected
  • Rear ON/OFF switch position verified
  • Table height function operational or inoperative
  • BACK UP and BACK DOWN response documented
  • Foot control cable and connector condition
  • Any relay clicking or motor humming observed
  • Obstructions or backrest clearance issues found
  • Whether failure occurred after heavy use
  • Safety-switch behavior, if backrest moved during testing
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A powered backrest failure can affect patient positioning and safe transfers. Begin with patient safety and simple external checks, then escalate once controllable causes are ruled out. Clear CCR documentation supports safe repair decisions and future troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
