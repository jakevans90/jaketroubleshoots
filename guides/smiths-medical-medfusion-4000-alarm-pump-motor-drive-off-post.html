Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000

Alarm: Pump Motor Drive Off POST

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Medfusion 4000

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot a Pump Motor Drive Off POST alarm on the Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000 infusion pump. This alarm occurs during power-on self-test and may indicate that the pump cannot verify safe motor drive control, motor-off behavior, drive train movement, motor sensing, or related control circuitry.

Because the motor drive system controls syringe plunger movement, a repeated POST failure should be treated as a patient safety concern and the pump should not be used for medication delivery until evaluated and verified safe.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the Medfusion 4000 from patient use immediately.
  • Do not attempt to use the pump for infusion therapy if it fails POST or reports a motor drive safety alarm.
  • Transfer the infusion to another verified pump if therapy must continue.
  • Expected outcome: The patient is not connected to a pump with an unverified motor control system.
  • Why it matters: A motor drive fault can affect syringe plunger movement, delivery accuracy, or the pump’s ability to safely control infusion.

Verify the Reported Error/Alarm and Behavior

  • Power the pump on and confirm the exact alarm displayed is Pump Motor Drive Off POST.
  • Note whether the alarm occurs at startup, during power-on self-test, intermittently after restart, or after a drop, spill, repair, or battery event.
  • Confirm whether the pump stops at POST and cannot continue into normal operation.
  • Expected outcome: The alarm is reproducible or clearly observed during startup.
  • Why it matters: This confirms a true POST motor drive safety failure rather than a syringe loading issue, user setup issue, or unrelated alarm.

Remove Accessories and Check for Obvious External Issues

  • Remove the syringe, tubing, syringe accessories, and anything that may interfere with the syringe drive area.
  • Check for pole clamp interference, tubing caught around the pump, or accessories pressing against the plunger driver.
  • Inspect the exterior for cracked housing, impact damage, sticky residue, contamination, bent syringe clamp parts, or foreign material in the drive path.
  • Expected outcome: No external obstruction or visible damage is preventing proper plunger drive movement.
  • Why it matters: The motor and drive train must move freely for the pump to verify motor control during POST.

Check Power Source and Startup Condition

  • Connect the pump to a known-good AC power source.
  • Confirm the pump powers on normally before the POST alarm appears.
  • If the pump was running on battery only, retry startup while connected to AC power.
  • Check for unstable power behavior, unexpected shutdowns, dim display, or repeated rebooting.
  • Expected outcome: The alarm is not caused by low power, unstable power, or an incomplete startup condition.
  • Why it matters: POST failures should be verified under stable power before internal motor or PCB failure is suspected.

Inspect the Syringe Drive Area Without Disassembly

  • Visually inspect the plunger driver, syringe clamp, flange area, and drive path.
  • Look for a plunger driver that appears jammed, misaligned, physically damaged, contaminated with fluid residue, or unable to travel smoothly.
  • Do not force the mechanism.
  • Do not open the pump or perform internal disassembly during external troubleshooting.
  • Expected outcome: The visible syringe drive components are clean, aligned, and not mechanically obstructed.
  • Why it matters: A motor drive alarm may be triggered if the drive system cannot move or verify safe motor-off behavior.

Restart Once to Confirm the POST Failure

  • Power the pump off.
  • Wait briefly, then power it back on while connected to AC power.
  • Confirm whether the Pump Motor Drive Off POST alarm returns.
  • If the alarm clears, continue with full functional verification before returning the pump to service.
  • If the alarm returns, treat it as a device-level safety failure.
  • Expected outcome: The alarm either clears after a controlled restart or repeats and confirms a persistent POST failure.

Enter Biomed Diagnostics, If Available and Allowed

  • Place the pump in the appropriate Biomed diagnostic mode according to facility procedure.
  • Check motor drive function and any related motor, plunger, or rotation verification tests available to Clinical Engineering.
  • Watch for abnormal movement, failure messages, unusual motor noise, hesitation, binding, or unexpected drive behavior.
  • Expected outcome: The motor drive function can be verified without abnormal movement, failure messages, or unsafe behavior.
  • Why it matters: The Medfusion 4000 motor control system uses a stepper motor monitored by motor rotation and plunger-position sensing. The pump must be able to verify safe and controlled motor operation.

Evaluate Whether the Issue Is Mechanical or Control-Related

  • If diagnostics indicate abnormal motor movement, binding, or failure to move, suspect a motor assembly or drive train issue.
  • If diagnostics indicate control failure, motor drive-off failure, or repeated POST failure without obvious mechanical obstruction, suspect the motor drive circuit or Main PCB-related failure.
  • Document whether the failure appears related to movement, sensing, control, power, or physical damage.
  • Expected outcome: The failure is narrowed toward drive train, motor assembly, motor sensing, plunger-position sensing, or control electronics.
  • Why it matters: This alarm is not typically corrected by user setup changes once external obstruction and power issues are ruled out.

Stop When Resolved

  • If the alarm clears after correcting an external obstruction or contamination issue, complete full operational testing according to facility procedure.
  • Verify the pump completes startup without POST alarms.
  • Verify motor drive diagnostic checks pass.
  • Confirm syringe loading is normal.
  • Confirm plunger movement is controlled.
  • Confirm no abnormal noise, hesitation, binding, heat, or smell occurs.
  • If all required checks pass, return the pump to service according to facility policy.

If the Problem Persists

If the Pump Motor Drive Off POST alarm continues after external checks, restart, power verification, and diagnostic review, the likely external causes have been ruled out.

Remove the pump from service.

Label the pump Out of Service.

Send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Persistent motor drive POST failure may indicate an internal motor drive, Main PCB, motor assembly, drive train, motor rotation sensing, or plunger-position sensing problem.

Do not return the pump to patient use until the motor drive system has been repaired and verified safe.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first. A pump with an unresolved motor drive safety alarm should not be trusted for medication delivery.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Medfusion 4000 infusion pump failed startup with Pump Motor Drive Off POST alarm and could not complete power-on self-test."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Pump Motor Drive Off POST alarm confirmed during startup. Syringe setup, external obstruction, visible contamination, damage, and power source issues were checked. Alarm repeated on AC power, indicating likely internal motor drive, drive train, motor sensing, plunger-position sensing, or Main PCB-related fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed pump from service and labeled Out of Service. Sent for bench evaluation and motor drive diagnostic testing before return to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A motor drive POST alarm on an infusion pump is a patient safety concern. Start with power, obstruction, damage, and simple external checks, but do not minimize a repeated motor drive safety failure. Proper interpretation of the alarm, timely removal from service, and clear CCR documentation protect the patient and support effective repair.

That is successful troubleshooting.
