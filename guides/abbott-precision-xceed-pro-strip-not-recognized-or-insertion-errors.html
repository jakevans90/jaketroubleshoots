Abbott Precision Xceed Pro

Strip Not Recognized or Insertion Errors

Asset Type

Blood Glucose Meter

Manufacturer

Abbott

Model

Precision Xceed Pro

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting when test strips are not detected, improperly inserted, or causing repeated read errors due to external factors.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from patient use if testing is delayed or unreliable.
  • Use an alternate meter if needed.

Expected: Patient testing continues safely on another device

Why it matters: Delayed or inaccurate glucose readings can impact clinical decisions

Verify Correct Strip Type

  • Confirm the strips being used are compatible with the Precision Xceed Pro (glucose vs ketone strips).
  • Check strip vial labeling
  • Ensure strips match device capability

Expected: Correct strip type is used

Why it matters: Incorrect strips will not be recognized by the meter

Inspect Strip Orientation and Insertion Technique

  • Ensure the strip is inserted fully and in the correct direction.
  • Insert strip firmly until it stops
  • Confirm contact end is inserted first

Expected: Device detects strip and initiates test mode

Why it matters: Partial or reversed insertion prevents proper contact

Check Strip Condition and Expiration

  • Inspect strips for damage, contamination, or expiration.
  • Check expiration date on vial
  • Inspect for bent, dirty, or exposed strips

Expected: New, clean, in-date strips function normally

Why it matters: Damaged or expired strips may not be recognized

Inspect the Strip Port for Contamination

  • Look for debris, dried blood, or residue inside the strip port.
  • Visually inspect port using a light
  • Gently clean using approved method (dry, lint-free applicator or manufacturer guidance)

Expected: Clean port allows proper electrical contact

Why it matters: Contamination blocks communication between strip and meter

Power Cycle the Device

  • Restart the meter to clear any temporary recognition errors.
  • Turn device off and back on
  • Reinsert strip after reboot

Expected: Device resets and recognizes strip

Why it matters: Clears minor software or detection faults

Test with a New Strip from a Different Lot (If Available)

  • Rule out defective strip batch.
  • Use a strip from a new vial or different lot

Expected: Device recognizes strip normally

Why it matters: Confirms whether issue is strip-related vs device-related

Observe for Repeated Failures or Intermittent Detection

  • Determine if the issue is consistent across multiple attempts.

Expected:

  • Consistent failure suggests device issue
  • Intermittent detection may indicate worn contacts

Why it matters: Helps differentiate accessory vs device failure

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and strips are still not recognized, the issue is likely internal (e.g., strip port contact failure or internal circuitry issue).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot strip recognition issues while relying on the device for active patient testing. Always transition to a functioning meter first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Meter not recognizing test strips during patient glucose testing."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Contamination found in strip port preventing proper strip contact."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleaned strip port and verified proper operation with new test strip."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Strip type verified (glucose vs ketone)
  • Strip expiration date checked
  • Multiple strip lots tested
  • Strip port condition (clean/contaminated)
  • Device response (no detection vs intermittent)
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple, external checks like strip type, condition, and port cleanliness. This approach protects patient safety, avoids unnecessary repairs, and ensures accurate documentation when escalation is required.

That is successful troubleshooting.
