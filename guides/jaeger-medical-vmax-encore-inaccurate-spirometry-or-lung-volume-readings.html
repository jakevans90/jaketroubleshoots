Jaeger Medical Vmax Encore

Inaccurate Spirometry or Lung Volume Readings

Asset Type

Pulmonary Function Tester

Manufacturer

Jaeger Medical

Model

Vmax Encore

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a Jaeger Medical Vmax Encore pulmonary function testing system produces inaccurate spirometry or lung volume readings. Such issues may be caused by sensor miscalibration, leaks, tubing obstructions, or external factors rather than internal electronics. The focus is on external checks and safe, stepwise evaluation before escalating for repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Patient Setup

  • Ensure correct use of mouthpiece, nose clip, or mask.
  • Confirm patient is seated properly and follows instructions.
  • Why: Improper technique can cause false readings.

Inspect Tubing and Connections

  • Check all airflow tubing for cracks, kinks, or loose connections.
  • Make sure connectors are fully seated.
  • Why: Leaks or obstructions directly affect spirometry and volume readings.

Check Flow/Volume Sensor Calibration

  • Perform automatic or manual calibration per device prompts.
  • Observe calibration values and note deviations from expected results.
  • Why: Sensors drift over time, causing measurement errors.

Examine Filters and Adapters

  • Remove and inspect disposable filters for blockage or moisture.
  • Confirm adapters are compatible and clean.
  • Why: Any obstruction can distort airflow measurement.

Inspect Environmental Factors

  • Ensure device is on a stable surface, away from drafts or direct airflow.
  • Check room temperature and humidity are within manufacturer recommendations.
  • Why: Environmental conditions can alter sensor performance.

Power Cycle the Device

  • Turn off the Vmax Encore, wait 30 seconds, then power back on.
  • Verify that device software initializes properly.
  • Why: Resets temporary software or communication glitches.

Verify PC Connection

  • Check USB or network connections to the workstation.
  • Ensure latest software updates or drivers are installed.
  • Why: Communication issues can cause delayed or inaccurate data logging.

If the Problem Persists

All external factors and simple calibration issues have been ruled out.

The device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Stopping at this point ensures patient safety and proper escalation.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot on an active patient if readings are suspect.
  • Move the patient to another verified device first.
  • Only perform checks when the device is safe to handle.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Clinician reports “spirometry and lung volume readings appear inaccurate and inconsistent with patient baseline.””

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Observed minor leaks in tubing and a partially blocked filter; calibration initially failed until filters were replaced.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced disposable filter, verified tubing connections, performed full calibration. Device readings returned to expected range.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested: Yes
  • Cables swapped: Confirmed connected and functional
  • Alarm behavior: None observed
  • Indicator lights: Normal
  • Unusual sounds: None
  • Unusual heat or smell: None
  • Final device status: Operational after calibration and filter replacement

Final Thought

Accurate pulmonary measurements are essential for patient diagnosis and treatment. Logical troubleshooting—starting with external, easy-to-check factors—ensures patient safety, avoids unnecessary disassembly, and supports timely repair if needed. Proper documentation using CCR captures the process and provides clear evidence for device performance and maintenance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
