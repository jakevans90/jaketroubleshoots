Spacelabs Healthcare UltraView SL Series

ECG Lead Connection / Module Errors

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Spacelabs Healthcare

Model

UltraView SL Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting an UltraView SL Series patient monitor experiencing ECG lead connection issues or module errors. Common signs include:

  • “Lead Off” or “Check Lead” messages
  • Missing or intermittent ECG waveforms
  • Abnormal or noisy ECG tracings
  • Module error alerts related to the ECG acquisition system

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Confirm Patient Safety First

  • Ensure the patient is on a stable monitor or other device before troubleshooting.
  • Do not attempt ECG troubleshooting on an actively unstable patient.

Verify Power and Monitor Status

  • Check that the monitor is fully powered on and has completed boot.
  • Confirm no startup errors are present that could affect module detection.

Inspect ECG Cables and Connectors

  • Examine the ECG lead wires for fraying, cracks, or bent pins.
  • Ensure connectors are fully seated in both the module and patient electrodes.
  • Swap with a known good cable if available to rule out cable faults.

Check Electrode Placement and Skin Contact

  • Confirm electrodes are properly placed and have adequate skin contact.
  • Replace dried-out or poorly adhering electrodes.
  • Clean skin if necessary to reduce impedance.

Verify ECG Module Seating

  • Check that the ECG module (if removable) is fully seated in the monitor.
  • Listen for a click or visual confirmation on the monitor display.

Observe Monitor Behavior

  • After reconnecting leads and module, observe for lead detection messages.
  • Verify waveforms for stability and noise reduction.

Check for Environmental or Electrical Interference

  • Ensure leads are not running near strong electrical equipment.
  • Move the monitor if necessary to minimize 60 Hz interference or EMI.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes (cables, electrodes, module seating) have been ruled out.

The ECG module may have an internal fault.

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send the monitor for repair or bench evaluation by qualified personnel.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Always switch the patient to an alternate monitor if ECG is critical before attempting troubleshooting.
  • Minimize downtime by having spare cables and electrodes readily available.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor intermittently shows ‘Lead Off’ and ECG waveform drops out.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Leads tested; cable was damaged; electrode placement inconsistent. Module seated properly; issue persists with known-good cables.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for repair. Patient monitored on backup device during evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet and power supply status verified
  • All ECG cables inspected or swapped
  • Electrode type and placement verified
  • Module seating confirmed
  • Monitor display for any error codes or messages
  • Waveform quality documented

Final Thought

Proper ECG troubleshooting relies on systematic checks from simple to more complex issues, prioritizing patient safety and preventing unnecessary internal intervention. Documentation ensures accountability and supports cost-effective repair management.

That is successful troubleshooting.
