STERIS AMSCO 4085

Table Drift or Position Instability

Asset Type

Surgical Table

Manufacturer

STERIS

Model

AMSCO 4085

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a STERIS AMSCO 4085 surgical table that drifts unintentionally or cannot maintain a set position. Symptoms may include:

  • Table slowly lowering, raising, or tilting without user input
  • Difficulty keeping lateral, Trendelenburg, or reverse Trendelenburg positions
  • Table “creeping” during procedures despite controls being idle

The focus is on external checks and logic-based troubleshooting before assuming internal hydraulic or electronic failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Move the patient to another stable table if possible.
  • Do not troubleshoot on an active patient to prevent injury.

Verify Table is Properly Powered

  • Confirm the table is connected to a known good outlet.
  • Check that the main power switch is on and indicator lights are illuminated.

Check Hand and Foot Controls

  • Inspect for visible damage or loose connections.
  • Swap control pendant or hand control with a known working unit if available.

Inspect Hydraulic System Externally

  • Look for visible hydraulic fluid leaks around the base, column, or lift mechanisms.
  • Observe if any hoses or fittings appear wet or loose.

Examine Table Lock Mechanisms

  • Confirm that all position locks or brakes are engaged properly.
  • Some models have mechanical locks that prevent drift when engaged.

Test Table Movement Without Load

  • With no patient or heavy equipment on the table, raise/lower and tilt slowly.
  • Observe if the table drifts under its own weight.

Check for Uneven or Jerky Motion

  • Uneven movement may indicate low hydraulic fluid levels or air in the system.
  • Listen for abnormal noises such as hissing or knocking, which may indicate internal leaks.

If the Problem Persists

After ruling out external causes (controls, power, mechanical locks), internal hydraulic or valve issues are likely.

  • Remove table from service.
  • Label Out of Service.
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation by qualified personnel.

Knowing when to escalate is part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Always move patients to a stable table before attempting troubleshooting.
  • Ensure the OR team is aware of any table instability to prevent accidental injury.
  • Avoid applying sudden forces to the table during tests.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Table drifts down when set at Trendelenburg, even with controls idle.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Observed minor hydraulic fluid seepage at the base and drift during unloaded tests.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Table removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation by STERIS-approved repair.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Pendant swapped/tested
  • Indicator lights checked
  • Any audible hissing or jerky movement noted
  • Hydraulic fluid level observed if accessible externally
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount. Logical, stepwise troubleshooting protects patients and staff, prevents unnecessary internal disassembly, and ensures proper escalation when external checks do not resolve the issue. Clear CCR documentation captures the troubleshooting process for accountability and continuity.

That is successful troubleshooting.
