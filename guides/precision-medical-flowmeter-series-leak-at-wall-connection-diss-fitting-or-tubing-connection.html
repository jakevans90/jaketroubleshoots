Precision Medical Flowmeter Series

Leak at wall connection, DISS fitting, or tubing connection

Asset Type

Oxygen Flowmeter

Manufacturer

Precision Medical

Model

Flowmeter Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting oxygen flowmeter leaks caused by loose fittings, damaged seals, tubing issues, or incorrect wall outlet connection.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Oxygen leaks can reduce delivered flow and create an oxygen-enriched environment.

Action:
Confirm whether the flowmeter is currently being used on a patient. If it is in active use and the leak is audible, visible, or affecting oxygen delivery, have clinical staff move the patient to another oxygen source before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome:
The patient receives stable oxygen delivery from a known-good source.

Why it matters:
Troubleshooting should not interrupt oxygen therapy or leave the patient connected to equipment with an unresolved leak.

Verify the Reported Leak Location

Action:
Ask staff where the leak was noticed:

  • Wall outlet connection
  • DISS fitting
  • Flowmeter body connection
  • Humidifier bottle connection
  • Patient tubing connection
  • Flow tube or control knob area

Listen for hissing and feel carefully near connection points without blocking oxygen flow.

Expected outcome:
The leak location is narrowed down before parts are swapped.

Why it matters:
Many oxygen “flowmeter leaks” are actually caused by wall outlets, humidifier bottles, adapters, or tubing.

Check That the Flowmeter Is Fully Seated in the Wall Outlet

Action:
Remove the flowmeter from the wall outlet and reconnect it firmly. Confirm it locks or seats properly depending on the outlet style.

Expected outcome:
The flowmeter connects securely with no movement, looseness, or hissing at the wall.

If resolved:
Return the device to service after verifying normal flow adjustment.

Inspect the Wall Outlet and Adapter Interface

Action:
Try the flowmeter on a known-good oxygen wall outlet. Also try a known-good flowmeter on the original wall outlet if available.

Expected outcome:
The leak follows either the flowmeter or the wall outlet.

Why it matters:
A worn wall outlet, damaged latch, bad outlet seal, or loose adapter can mimic a flowmeter failure.

If the wall outlet is the issue:
Remove the outlet from use per facility process and notify Facilities, Clinical Engineering, or the responsible gas system group.

Inspect the DISS Fitting

Action:
Check the DISS connection for looseness, cross-threading, damaged threads, missing seal, cracked adapter, or improper attachment.

Tighten only by hand or per normal connection practice. Do not overtighten.

Expected outcome:
The DISS fitting is secure, straight, and leak-free.

Why it matters:
DISS leaks are commonly caused by loose fittings, damaged threads, or missing/worn sealing surfaces.

Check the Tubing Connection

Action:
Remove and reconnect the oxygen tubing. Inspect the tubing end for stretching, cracking, hardening, poor fit, or contamination.

Replace the tubing with known-good tubing.

Expected outcome:
Tubing fits snugly and does not leak at the outlet nipple or adapter.

If resolved:
Document the tubing issue and return the flowmeter to service if it passes functional check.

Check the Humidifier Bottle or Accessory Connection

Action:
If a humidifier bottle is attached, remove it and test the flowmeter without the bottle. Inspect the humidifier threads, gasket, bottle cap, and adapter.

Expected outcome:
The leak stops when the faulty humidifier bottle or accessory is removed.

Why it matters:
Cracked humidifier bottles, bad gaskets, and misthreaded bottles are common causes of leaks blamed on the flowmeter.

Inspect the Flowmeter Body for Damage

Action:
Look for cracks, damaged plastic, damaged flow tube, loose fittings, missing parts, or signs of impact.

Do not use the flowmeter if the body, tube, or connection points are cracked or damaged.

Expected outcome:
The flowmeter housing and visible connection points are intact.

Verify Flow Control Operation

Action:
Connect the flowmeter to a known-good oxygen source. Slowly adjust the control knob through the normal flow range.

Confirm the float moves smoothly and the leak does not appear when the valve is opened or closed.

Expected outcome:
Flow adjusts normally without leaking at the wall connection, DISS fitting, tubing connection, or flowmeter body.

Perform a Final Leak and Function Check

Action:
With the flowmeter connected and oxygen flowing, verify:

  • No audible leak
  • Flow adjusts smoothly
  • Float responds normally
  • Tubing remains secure
  • Wall connection remains stable
  • No cracks or damaged fittings are present

Expected outcome:
The oxygen flowmeter operates normally and can be returned to service.

If the Problem Persists

If the leak continues after checking the wall outlet, DISS fitting, tubing, humidifier bottle, accessories, and visible damage, the likely issue is internal or related to a damaged fitting or valve assembly.

The device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair, replacement, or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Oxygen delivery equipment with an unresolved leak should not remain in clinical use.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot an oxygen flowmeter while it is actively supporting a patient unless clinical staff confirm it is safe. Move the patient to another oxygen source first if the leak affects oxygen delivery or cannot be corrected immediately.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported a leak from the Precision Medical oxygen flowmeter at the wall/DISS connection during setup."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found oxygen tubing stretched at the flowmeter outlet, causing a poor seal and audible leak during flow."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced tubing, verified secure connection, confirmed flow adjustment, and leak-tested flowmeter on a known-good oxygen outlet."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Oxygen outlet tested
  • Flowmeter tested on known-good wall outlet
  • Known-good flowmeter tested on original outlet
  • DISS fitting inspected
  • Tubing swapped
  • Humidifier bottle removed or replaced
  • Leak location identified
  • Audible hissing present or not present
  • Flow range checked
  • Visible cracks or damage noted
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Oxygen flowmeter leak troubleshooting should stay simple and logical. Confirm patient safety first, then isolate the leak by checking the outlet, fittings, tubing, humidifier bottle, and visible damage before assuming the flowmeter has failed internally. Good documentation helps separate equipment failure from accessory or wall outlet problems.

That is successful troubleshooting.
