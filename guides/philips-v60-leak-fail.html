Philips V60 Ventilator

Fails Self-Test / Leak Detected

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

V60

Check the Patient Circuit and Tubing

  • Ensure all connections are fully seated.
  • Inspect for cracks, holes, or loose fittings.
  • Verify the circuit is properly attached to the ventilator outlet.
  • Reason: Even a small gap can cause a leak detection during self-test.

Inspect the Exhalation Port and Filter

  • Confirm the exhalation port is installed correctly.
  • Check the filter is present, seated properly, and unobstructed.
  • Look for moisture buildup or blockages.
  • Reason: Improper installation or obstruction can trigger a leak error.

Check the Humidifier (If Installed)

  • Ensure the humidifier chamber is seated correctly and intact.
  • Inspect tubing connections for tightness and leaks.
  • Reason: Humidifier leaks are a common cause of self-test failure.

Inspect the Mask or Test Plug

  • If a mask is attached, ensure a proper seal.
  • If running a self-test without a patient, verify the test plug or circuit cap is secure.
  • Reason: Any open patient interface will cause the ventilator to detect a leak.

Perform a Simple Reset

  • Power the ventilator off.
  • Re-seat the patient circuit, exhalation port, and humidifier connections.
  • Power the ventilator back on and rerun the self-test.
  • Reason: Resetting can clear temporary errors caused by connection issues.

If the Problem Persists

  • If the ventilator continues to fail the self-test after completing all external checks:
    • The most common causes have been ruled out.
    • The issue is likely internal (e.g., valve failure, internal leak).
  • Next Steps:
    • Remove the ventilator from clinical service.
    • Clearly label it as Out of Service.
    • Send for repair or bench evaluation.
    • Clinical Engineering Note: Knowing when to stop is an essential part of proper troubleshooting and patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not troubleshoot on an active patient; move the patient to another ventilator if possible.
  • Only run self-tests when safe and with no patient attached or appropriately capped.
  • Ensure all bedside staff are aware that the ventilator is being evaluated.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

  • Complaint: Example: “Ventilator fails self-test; leak detected.”
  • Cause: Example: “Patient circuit, exhalation port, and humidifier inspected and reseated. No visible damage noted. Self-test continues to fail with leak detected. Suspected internal leak or valve failure.”
  • Resolution: Examples:
    • “Removed ventilator from service, labeled out of service, sent for repair.”
    • “Tagged for bench evaluation by CE.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Circuit inspected or replaced
  • Exhalation port reseated
  • Humidifier chamber checked
  • Self-test result (pass/fail)
  • Visible damage or moisture
  • Final device disposition (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

  • Not all self-test failures are caused by external setup issues. By following these steps, you have:
    • ✔ Ruled out common bedside causes
    • ✔ Maintained patient safety
    • ✔ Documented troubleshooting properly
    • ✔ Avoided unnecessary guesswork

That is successful troubleshooting.
