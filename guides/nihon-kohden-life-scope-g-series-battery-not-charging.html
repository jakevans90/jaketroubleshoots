Nihon Kohden Life Scope G Series

Battery Not Charging

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

Life Scope G Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Life Scope G Series monitor battery does not charge. Symptoms may include:

  • Monitor does not indicate charging when plugged in
  • Battery level remains the same or decreases while connected to AC power
  • Alarms or messages such as “Battery Error” or “Check Battery”

Most charging issues are related to external factors such as power supply, battery seating, or connection integrity before considering internal battery or circuit failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify AC Power Source

  • Confirm the wall outlet is supplying power using a known working device.
  • Check that outlet is not switched off or connected to a GFCI that has tripped.
  • Why: No power at the source is the simplest cause of charging failure.

Inspect AC Power Cord and Plug

  • Examine the cord for cuts, fraying, or bent pins.
  • Ensure the plug is fully inserted into the monitor and outlet.
  • Why: A damaged or loose cord prevents proper charging.

Check the Battery Module Seating

  • Remove and reinstall the battery, ensuring it clicks securely into place.
  • Inspect battery terminals for dirt, corrosion, or debris.
  • Why: Improper seating or poor terminal contact prevents charging.

Observe Monitor’s Charging Indicator

  • Plug in the monitor with the battery installed and watch the charging light or on-screen indicator.
  • Record any error messages displayed.
  • Why: Confirms whether the monitor detects the battery and attempts to charge.

Swap with a Known Good Battery

  • If available, insert a known working Life Scope G Series battery and attempt charging.
  • Why: Determines if the issue is battery-specific or monitor-related.

Inspect for Monitor Settings or Lockouts

  • Check monitor configuration to ensure battery charging is enabled (some units may limit charging in certain modes).
  • Why: Software or mode restrictions may prevent charging.

If the Problem Persists

All external checks (power, cord, battery seating, known good battery) have been ruled out.

The issue is likely internal, either the battery is defective or the monitor’s charging circuitry is failing.

  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label “Out of Service – Battery Charging Fault.”
  • Send the unit for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not use the monitor on a patient if battery reliability is uncertain.
  • Always move the patient to another functional monitor before troubleshooting.
  • Only perform troubleshooting in a safe environment away from patient care if charging faults are suspected.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Monitor battery will not charge when connected to AC power.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Battery terminals inspected; cord and outlet verified; monitor does not indicate charging; battery replacement with known good unit confirms monitor failure.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested with another device: pass/fail
  • AC cord and plug inspected
  • Battery terminals cleaned or checked
  • Charging indicator behavior
  • Any error messages observed
  • Known good battery test results
  • Final device status: Out of Service if unresolved

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount. Always confirm that a monitor is reliable before use. Troubleshooting should follow a logical, stepwise approach, escalating only when external causes are eliminated. Proper documentation ensures clarity and supports future maintenance or repair actions.

That is successful troubleshooting.
