Hamilton G5

Error: TF 5513 Error Fan Signal Active

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

G5

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting Error: TF 5513 Error Fan Signal Active, indicating abnormal cooling fan operation that may allow ventilation to continue but risks overheating or later ventilator failure.

The Hamilton G5 may continue delivering ventilation while displaying this technical fault, but continued operation does not confirm that the ventilator is safe for ongoing patient use.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

Although the Hamilton G5 may continue ventilating during TF 5513, do not rely on a ventilator reporting a high-priority technical fault involving its cooling system.

  • Notify clinical staff of the technical fault.
  • Provide alternative ventilation or transfer the patient to a known-working ventilator before troubleshooting.
  • Do not remove, reposition, or test the ventilator while a patient remains dependent on it.

Expected outcome: The patient is safely supported without dependence on a ventilator with an unresolved cooling-fan technical fault.

Why it matters: A fan-related failure may lead to overheating or additional device failure even when ventilation initially continues.

2. Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

Confirm that the ventilator displays TF 5513 and that the reported condition is associated with a high-priority technical fault.

  • Determine whether the error occurred during startup or during ventilation.
  • Determine whether the error remains active or clears intermittently.
  • Confirm whether ventilation continued when the fault appeared.
  • Check for additional technical faults or temperature-related alarms.
  • Review the event log for TF 5513 and related faults.

Expected outcome: The technical fault, its timing, and any associated device behavior are documented.

Why it matters: Confirming the exact error and event history helps distinguish a persistent cooling-fan fault from an isolated report or unrelated alarm condition.

3. Check the Device Exterior for Cooling Obstruction

With the ventilator removed from patient use, inspect the external cooling intake and exhaust openings.

  • Look for dust buildup, debris, or visible contamination.
  • Confirm ventilation openings are not blocked by walls, drapes, bedding, carts, or other equipment.
  • Check for unusual heat, odor, or abnormal fan-related noise.
  • Remove only external obstructions or surface contamination according to approved cleaning practices.

Expected outcome: The ventilator has clear external airflow paths and is not being restricted from normal cooling.

Why it matters: Restricted airflow can contribute to overheating, increased fan load, or fan fault behavior.

4. Confirm Power and Operating Environment

Verify that the ventilator is connected to an approved power source and positioned in an appropriate operating environment.

  • Confirm a stable AC power connection.
  • Check whether any power interruption occurred when the fault appeared.
  • Confirm the ventilator is not exposed to excessive heat.
  • Confirm equipment placement permits normal ventilation and cooling airflow.

Expected outcome: External power and environmental conditions are not contributing to the fan-related technical fault.

Why it matters: Ruling out external conditions prevents unnecessary repair actions and supports accurate escalation.

5. Observe Error Recurrence During a Controlled Bench Check

Only after the ventilator has been removed from patient use, power the unit in a controlled bench setting and observe whether TF 5513 reappears.

  • Listen for grinding, clicking, scraping, abnormal vibration, or lack of expected fan sound.
  • Observe whether the fan-related fault returns during startup or operation.
  • Check whether the high-priority technical fault recurs.
  • Review the event log again after the controlled observation.

Do not return the ventilator to clinical use solely because the fault clears temporarily.

Expected outcome: The fault is either reproduced or its intermittent behavior is documented for repair evaluation.

Why it matters: An intermittent fan failure may progress to loss of cooling during future patient use.

6. Recheck After Correcting an External Issue

If an external airflow restriction, environmental condition, or placement issue was identified and corrected, restart the ventilator in a controlled bench setting and monitor for recurrence of TF 5513.

  • Confirm external airflow paths remain clear.
  • Confirm the fault does not immediately recur.
  • Review the event log for repeated or related technical faults.
  • Complete applicable functional and safety checks before any return-to-service decision.

Expected outcome: The error does not recur, no related technical faults remain present, and required evaluation is completed successfully.

Why it matters: A temporary clearing of the fault is not enough to confirm that the cooling system is reliable for patient use.

7. Isolate a Likely Device-Related Fan Failure

If TF 5513 persists after external cooling airflow, placement, operating environment, and controlled bench observations have been checked, treat the condition as a likely internal device failure.

  • Document the repeated TF 5513 fault and event log findings.
  • Do not repeatedly return the ventilator to clinical use.
  • Refer the ventilator for qualified service evaluation.
  • Qualified repair may require evaluation of the fan assembly, fan connection, or associated servo/inspiratory module circuitry.

Expected outcome: The ventilator is appropriately escalated for repair rather than returned to use with an unresolved cooling-fan technical fault.

Why it matters: Persistent fan-related faults may lead to overheating or future ventilator failure.

If the Problem Persists

Once external cooling obstruction, device placement, environmental conditions, power concerns, and observable fan-related behavior have been ruled out, persistent TF 5513 indicates a likely internal cooling fan or associated module failure.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label the ventilator Out of Service.
  • Send the device for qualified bench evaluation or repair.
  • Include event log findings and observed fan-related behavior in the repair record.
  • Escalate for fan or servo/inspiratory module evaluation or replacement if indicated by qualified service personnel.

Do not rely on continued ventilation as proof that the ventilator is safe for patient use. Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a high-priority ventilator technical fault on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or approved alternative ventilation method first, even when the affected ventilator appears to continue delivering breaths.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton G5 ventilator displayed high-priority technical fault TF 5513 Error Fan Signal Active during operation. Ventilation continued, but the unit was removed from patient use due to possible cooling fan failure."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Event log confirmed TF 5513. External cooling openings and operating environment were inspected with no airflow restriction identified. Fault recurred during controlled bench observation, indicating a likely internal fan assembly or associated module fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Recommended alternative ventilation, removed ventilator from service, labeled unit Out of Service, documented TF 5513 event log findings, and referred device for qualified bench evaluation and repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A Hamilton G5 ventilator that continues operating during TF 5513 may still be unsafe for continued clinical use. Patient safety requires backup ventilation first, logical verification of external cooling and environmental causes, careful event log documentation, and timely repair escalation when the fan-related fault remains unresolved.

That is successful troubleshooting.
