Siemens Healthineers Ysio Max

Wireless Detector Not Recognized or Losing Connection

Asset Type

Radiographic System

Manufacturer

Siemens Healthineers

Model

Ysio Max

What This Guide Helps With

Wireless detector recognition, intermittent detector connection, acquisition readiness issues, or lost detector communication on a Siemens Ysio Max radiographic system.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

Do not troubleshoot detector communication problems during an active patient exposure setup.

Action:

  • Remove the patient from the table or wall stand area if imaging cannot continue safely.
  • Notify clinical staff that the detector is not reliably connected.
  • Use another room, detector, or imaging system if patient care is time-sensitive.

Expected outcome: Patient care continues without relying on an unstable detector connection.

Why it matters: A wireless detector that drops connection can delay imaging, cause repeat positioning, or interrupt acquisition workflow.

2. Verify the Reported Issue

Confirm whether the detector is completely unrecognized or intermittently disconnecting.

Action:

  • Ask staff when the issue occurs:
  • At system startup
  • When selecting the detector
  • When moving the detector
  • During exam setup
  • At exposure attempt
  • After image acquisition

Expected outcome: The failure pattern is identified before replacing hardware or escalating.

Why it matters: A detector that never appears may have a power, battery, registration, or network issue. A detector that drops out during movement may suggest battery, wireless signal, docking, or detector damage.

3. Check Detector Power Status

Inspect the wireless detector for power indication.

Action:

  • Confirm the detector is powered on.
  • Check detector indicator lights, display, or status LEDs if present.
  • Power cycle the detector if allowed by site procedure.

Expected outcome: The detector powers on normally and becomes available to the system.

If this resolves the issue, return the detector to service after confirming normal recognition and test image acquisition.

4. Check Detector Battery Charge

A low or weak detector battery can cause recognition failure or intermittent wireless dropouts.

Action:

  • Remove the detector battery if applicable.
  • Inspect for obvious damage, swelling, poor seating, contamination, or loose fit.
  • Install a fully charged known-good battery.
  • Place the original battery on the charger and confirm charging indication.

Expected outcome: The detector remains connected with a known-good charged battery.

If the detector works normally with another battery, document the failed or weak battery and remove it from service.

5. Verify Detector Docking or Charging Station Function

If the detector uses a charging dock or storage slot, confirm it is charging and communicating normally.

Action:

  • Place the detector in the dock.
  • Check for charging status or system recognition.
  • Inspect the dock contacts for debris, bent pins, damage, or fluid contamination.
  • Confirm the dock has power.

Expected outcome: The detector charges normally and is detected when docked.

If the dock does not charge or recognize any detector, the dock or associated connection may require repair evaluation.

6. Confirm the Correct Detector Is Selected

The system may not acquire images if the wrong detector, room receptor, or acquisition mode is selected.

Action:

  • Check the selected detector/receptor on the acquisition workstation.
  • Confirm the exam is set up for the wireless detector being used.
  • Verify staff are not trying to use a detector assigned to another room or system.

Expected outcome: The correct wireless detector is selected and ready for acquisition.

If this resolves the issue, no repair is needed unless the incorrect selection keeps recurring due to configuration problems.

7. Check Detector Seating and Physical Condition

Wireless detectors are frequently handled, moved, and placed under patients, which increases the chance of damage.

Action:

  • Inspect the detector exterior for cracks, impact damage, loose covers, damaged handles, or signs of fluid intrusion.
  • Gently check whether the battery latch, detector cover, or connector areas appear secure.
  • Do not open the detector housing.

Expected outcome: No obvious physical damage is found.

If damage is found, remove the detector from service and send it for repair evaluation.

8. Check Wireless Signal Conditions

Wireless detector communication may drop if the detector is too far from the system, shielded, or blocked.

Action:

  • Test the detector near the table or wall stand in its normal clinical position.
  • Avoid placing the detector behind thick barriers, metal carts, equipment, or other signal-blocking objects.
  • Ask staff whether the issue happens only in certain detector positions or only near certain equipment.

Expected outcome: The detector maintains connection in normal use positions.

If connection only fails in specific locations, document the location and possible wireless interference pattern.

9. Restart the Acquisition Workstation and Imaging System

Software or communication services may fail to refresh detector status.

Action:

  • Follow site-approved shutdown and restart procedure for the workstation/system.
  • Allow the system to fully boot.
  • Power cycle the wireless detector after the system is ready.
  • Check whether the detector appears available.

Expected outcome: Detector communication is restored after a clean restart.

If this resolves the issue, perform a basic functional check before returning the room to use.

10. Test With Another Known-Good Detector if Available

This helps separate a detector problem from a room/system communication problem.

Action:

  • Connect or select another compatible known-good wireless detector.
  • Check whether the second detector is recognized and remains connected.
  • Then test the suspect detector again if safe and appropriate.

Expected outcome:

  • If the known-good detector works, the original detector or battery is likely the issue.
  • If no detectors are recognized, the issue may be with the room system, workstation, wireless communication hardware, or configuration.

11. Check Network or Room Communication Indicators

A detector may rely on room communication, workstation services, wireless access points, or system configuration.

Action:

  • Check for room network alerts, workstation communication errors, or detector status warnings.
  • Confirm the acquisition workstation is otherwise functioning normally.
  • Verify that other room functions are not also showing communication errors.

Expected outcome: No broader system communication issue is present.

If multiple communication-related problems are present, escalate the room/system for service evaluation.

12. Perform a Controlled Functional Test

After any corrective action, confirm the detector is stable before returning the room to service.

Action:

  • Select the wireless detector.
  • Verify ready status.
  • Perform a non-patient test exposure or site-approved detector check if allowed.
  • Confirm image acquisition and transfer.
  • Move the detector through normal use positions and watch for connection drops.

Expected outcome: The detector remains recognized and image acquisition completes normally.

If the detector disconnects again, remove it from service or escalate the room depending on test results.

If the Problem Persists

If the Siemens Ysio Max wireless detector is still not recognized after checking power, battery, detector selection, docking, physical condition, wireless positioning, system restart, and known-good detector comparison, common external causes have been ruled out.

The device or affected detector should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair, vendor service, or bench evaluation

Do not continue swapping settings or repeatedly restarting the system if the detector connection remains unreliable. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot detector recognition problems while a patient is positioned for imaging. Move the patient to another working radiographic room or use another available detector first. Troubleshooting should only continue once patient care is no longer dependent on the unstable detector.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Radiology reported the Siemens Ysio Max wireless detector was not recognized and intermittently lost connection during exam setup."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found the detector battery was low and the detector connection dropped when moved away from the charging area."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Installed a fully charged known-good battery, verified detector recognition, completed test acquisition, and returned the system to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Detector serial number or ID
  • Battery tested or swapped
  • Battery charger/dock status
  • Whether issue occurs docked or wireless only
  • Detector indicator light behavior
  • System or workstation error messages
  • Whether another detector was tested
  • Whether image acquisition completed
  • Any drops, fluid exposure, cracks, or visible damage
  • Room location where connection was lost
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Wireless detector issues should be approached logically: protect the patient first, confirm the exact behavior, check battery and detector status, verify correct selection, and rule out simple connection or wireless problems before escalating. Clear CCR documentation helps separate detector, battery, dock, workstation, and room-level communication failures.

That is successful troubleshooting.
