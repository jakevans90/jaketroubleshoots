Drager Evita V500

Alarm: Inspiration too long inspiratory time exceeds allowed limit

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses the “Inspiration too long” alarm indicating that inspiratory time exceeds the allowed limit. This can result in prolonged breaths, impaired exhalation, air trapping, and potential patient-ventilator mismatch.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s condition
  • Provide manual ventilation if needed
  • Prepare backup ventilator if instability is suspected

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the alarm is displayed
  • Observe if breaths are prolonged
  • Determine if alarm is continuous or intermittent

Check Ventilation Settings

  • Review inspiratory time (Ti)
  • Check I:E ratio for excessive inspiration
  • Confirm correct ventilation mode is selected

Assess Patient Interaction

  • Check for patient-ventilator dyssynchrony
  • Observe for breath stacking or delayed cycling
  • Confirm patient effort aligns with ventilator timing

Inspect Breathing Circuit

  • Check for kinks or obstructions
  • Inspect for water buildup in tubing
  • Verify proper circuit assembly

Check Expiratory Valve Function

  • Ensure valve is not obstructed
  • Confirm proper seating and operation

Run System/Self Test

  • Perform system test per device procedure
  • Check for errors or failed checks

Swap Accessories

  • Replace breathing circuit with known-good components
  • Verify expiratory valve condition

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm continues after all external checks, settings verification, and accessory replacement, internal timing or sensor failure is likely.

  • Remove device from service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilator timing alarms on an active patient. Always move the patient to a backup device before continuing diagnostics.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming Inspiration too long with prolonged breaths observed."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Inspiratory time set too high and minor circuit restriction affecting cycling."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Adjusted inspiratory time, cleared circuit obstruction, verified normal ventilation operation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

This alarm is commonly related to settings or external flow issues. Always verify configuration and circuit conditions first before escalating. Proper troubleshooting ensures safe ventilation and reliable device performance.
