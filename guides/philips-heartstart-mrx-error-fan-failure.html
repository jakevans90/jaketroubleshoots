Philips HeartStart MRx

Error: Fan Failure

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

HeartStart MRx

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Fan Failure error on a Philips HeartStart MRx. This condition may be related to blocked ventilation, restricted airflow, debris, fan obstruction, abnormal fan operation, or an internal cooling-system fault.

Because the HeartStart MRx is emergency-response equipment, a fan-related service error should be treated as a readiness and reliability concern until the device passes required checks.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the HeartStart MRx is in clinical use or staged for emergency response, remove it from service immediately and provide staff with another ready defibrillator.
  • Do not leave a defibrillator with an unresolved service error in a ready-use location.
  • Expected: Patient care is supported by a reliable backup defibrillator.
  • Why it matters: A fan-related failure may affect internal cooling and device reliability during use.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Power the device on and confirm whether the exact error appears as Fan Failure.
  • Document whether the error appears during startup, after the unit has been running, during an Operational Check, intermittently, or after charging, transport, cleaning, or storage.
  • Check for associated symptoms such as abnormal noise, no fan sound, overheating smell, excessive warmth, or repeated service messages.
  • Expected: The Fan Failure error is reproducible or clearly documented.
  • Why it matters: This confirms a true system-reported cooling fault rather than a one-time startup or environmental issue.

Remove the Device From Clinical Use

  • Do not return the HeartStart MRx to patient care while the Fan Failure error remains active.
  • Attach an Out of Service tag if the error is present or recurring.
  • Expected: The affected defibrillator is separated from ready-use equipment.
  • Why it matters: Defibrillators must be dependable during emergency use, and cooling-related faults should not be ignored.

Check the Power Source and Startup Condition

  • Connect the device to a known-good AC power outlet.
  • Confirm the AC power indicator behaves normally and that the device powers on without unrelated power errors.
  • If available, test with a known-good charged battery.
  • Expected: The unit powers normally and the Fan Failure error can be evaluated without a low-power or unstable-power condition.
  • Why it matters: Power instability can sometimes create misleading startup or hardware-related complaints.

Inspect External Vents

  • Inspect all visible ventilation openings on the HeartStart MRx.
  • Look for blocked vents, dust buildup, tape, labels, packaging covering airflow, debris from storage areas, or obstruction from mounting brackets, bags, or carts.
  • Check for signs of liquid residue or contamination near the vents.
  • Clear only external debris that can be removed safely without opening the device.
  • Expected: Vents are open, clean, and not restricted.
  • Why it matters: A fan may be functional but still fail cooling expectations if airflow is blocked.

Check the Device Environment

  • Confirm the device is not being operated in an enclosed case, tightly packed crash cart drawer, or against a surface blocking airflow.
  • Move the unit to an open bench area and power it on again.
  • Expected: The device has adequate airflow around the housing.
  • Why it matters: Restricted airflow can cause cooling alarms or thermal stress even when the fan itself is not physically damaged.

Listen for Fan Operation

  • With the device powered on in a quiet area, listen for fan activity.
  • Note whether the fan does not run at all, runs briefly and stops, makes grinding or rattling sounds, runs inconsistently, or appears to run normally while the error remains.
  • Expected: Fan behavior is identified without opening the device.
  • Why it matters: Noise or absence of airflow helps separate a likely obstruction from a failed fan assembly or fan-control issue.

Inspect for External Physical Damage

  • Check the device housing near the ventilation area for impact damage, cracked plastic, deformation, or signs the unit was dropped.
  • Inspect for fluid intrusion, sticky residue, or cleaning solution buildup near vent openings.
  • Expected: No physical condition is found that could affect fan movement or airflow.
  • Why it matters: Drop damage or contamination may cause fan obstruction or internal cooling damage.

Review Ready For Use Status

  • Check the Ready For Use indicator and confirm whether the device is reporting a service condition.
  • If the Ready For Use indicator is not normal, keep the device out of service.
  • Expected: Ready For Use status agrees with the reported Fan Failure condition.
  • Why it matters: A failed readiness state confirms the device should not be considered ready for clinical deployment.

Run an Operational Check if the Device Allows It

  • If the device powers on safely, run the available Operational Check according to facility practice.
  • Document whether the Fan Failure error appears during the check or whether the check fails.
  • Expected: The automated check either passes after simple airflow correction or confirms a persistent fan-related fault.
  • Why it matters: Automated testing provides useful evidence for repair escalation and final verification.

Check for Accessory or Mounting-Related Airflow Issues

  • Remove the device from any case, shelf, mount, bracket, or transport bag that could restrict vents.
  • Power the device on again in a clear bench setup.
  • Expected: The Fan Failure error clears if the issue was caused by external airflow restriction.
  • Why it matters: This helps avoid unnecessary repair when the problem is caused by how the device is stored or mounted.

Determine Whether the Issue Is External or Device-Related

  • If blocked vents, debris, or restricted mounting were found and corrected, repeat startup and testing.
  • If the fan still fails to run, makes abnormal noise, or the Fan Failure error remains, treat the issue as device-related.
  • Expected: The troubleshooting path clearly identifies whether the failure was external or internal.
  • Why it matters: Clinical Engineering should rule out simple causes before escalating, but should not continue using a defibrillator with an unresolved cooling fault.

Stop When Resolved

  • If the error clears after correcting an external airflow issue, complete an Operational Check.
  • Confirm the Ready For Use indicator is normal.
  • Return the device to service only after it passes required checks and no Fan Failure error returns.
  • Expected: Device passes testing and is safe to return to clinical use.

If the Problem Persists

If the Fan Failure error remains after checking power, vents, airflow, physical condition, mounting, and basic operation, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent Fan Failure condition may indicate an internal fan, fan wiring, fan-control, or cooling-system fault.

The device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Fan assembly replacement or further internal service evaluation may be required. Do not perform deep internal disassembly during frontline troubleshooting unless authorized and trained for Philips HeartStart MRx service procedures.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a Fan Failure error on a defibrillator that is assigned to active patient care or emergency response. Move patient care coverage to a backup defibrillator first, then evaluate the failed unit on the bench. A defibrillator with an unresolved cooling or service error should not be trusted as ready-use equipment.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Philips HeartStart MRx reported Fan Failure error during startup. Staff noted the unit was not ready for use and required Clinical Engineering evaluation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External vents and power source were checked. No blocked vent or accessory-related obstruction was found. Fan failure condition remained, indicating likely internal fan or fan-control failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed HeartStart MRx from service, labeled Out of Service, verified failure during startup or Operational Check, and escalated for repair or fan assembly evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A Fan Failure error on a Philips HeartStart MRx should be treated as a readiness and reliability concern. Clinical Engineering should first rule out external airflow restriction, debris, power issues, and mounting-related causes. If the error remains, the device should be removed from service and escalated for repair rather than returned to clinical use with an unresolved cooling fault. Clear CCR documentation protects patient safety and supports efficient repair follow-up.

That is successful troubleshooting.
