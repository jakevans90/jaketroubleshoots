Midmark M11 UltraClave Series

Control Board or Keypad Failure

Asset Type

Sterilizer

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

M11 UltraClave Series

What This Guide Helps With

Unresponsive keypad, incorrect inputs, display issues, or inability to start cycles due to external power, connection, or interface-related causes.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Ensure no instruments needed for immediate patient care are dependent on this unit.
  • Expected: Backup sterilization method in place.
  • Why it matters: Loss of sterilization capability impacts patient safety and workflow.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to power on the unit and press multiple keypad buttons. Observe display response and cycle initiation.
  • Expected: No response, delayed response, or incorrect inputs.
  • Why it matters: Confirms true keypad/control issue vs user error.

Check Power to the Unit

  • Verify the unit is plugged into a known working outlet. Check circuit breaker if applicable.
  • Expected: Display powers on consistently.
  • Why it matters: Low or unstable power can mimic control or keypad failure.
  • If no power → restore power and retest.

Inspect Display and Indicator Behavior

  • Observe the screen for partial display, flickering, or error messages.
  • Expected: Clear, stable display.
  • Why it matters: Display issues may indicate broader control board problems rather than keypad alone.

Check for Keypad Obstruction or Contamination

  • Inspect keypad surface for debris, moisture, or chemical residue.
  • Expected: Clean, dry, and unobstructed buttons.
  • Why it matters: Residue or moisture can prevent proper button contact.
  • If contamination is present → clean per facility guidelines and retest.

Test Individual Keypad Buttons

  • Press each button and observe if some respond while others do not.
  • Expected: Consistent response across all buttons.
  • Why it matters: Partial failure suggests keypad membrane issue rather than full control board failure.
  • If only some buttons fail → likely keypad membrane issue.

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn the unit off, unplug it for 30–60 seconds, then power back on.
  • Expected: System reboots and restores normal function.
  • Why it matters: Clears temporary software or control logic faults.
  • If resolved → monitor for recurrence.

Inspect External Connections (If Accessible)

  • Check any accessible external connectors (e.g., keypad ribbon entry point if visible without disassembly).
  • Expected: Secure and undamaged connections.
  • Why it matters: Loose or partially connected interfaces can cause intermittent control issues.

Check for Environmental Factors

  • Assess for excessive humidity, recent cleaning exposure, or fluid ingress near the control panel.
  • Expected: Dry, normal operating environment.
  • Why it matters: Moisture intrusion is a common cause of keypad malfunction.

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal (keypad membrane failure or control board fault).

Remove the unit from service

Label as Out of Service

Send for repair or bench evaluation

Do not attempt internal board-level repair in the field.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a sterilizer while instruments are actively being processed. Always use an alternate sterilization method before taking the unit out of service.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Sterilizer keypad unresponsive; unable to start cycle."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Keypad buttons non-responsive after cleaning and power cycle; likely keypad membrane failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed unit from service and sent for repair evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Power cycle performed
  • Display behavior noted (normal/flickering/blank)
  • Specific buttons working vs not working
  • Presence of moisture or residue on keypad
  • Final device status (removed from service or operational)

Final Thought

Control issues often appear complex but should always be approached by ruling out simple causes like power, contamination, and temporary faults first. Proper escalation protects both patient safety and equipment integrity, while clear documentation supports efficient repair and tracking.

That is successful troubleshooting.
