Tuttnauer Elara Series

Drain, Water Retention, or Plumbing Faults

Asset Type

Sterilizer

Manufacturer

Tuttnauer

Model

Elara Series

What This Guide Helps With

Identifies causes of slow drainage, water retention, or plumbing blockages to ensure proper sterilization cycle completion and patient instrument safety.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Staff Safety

Confirm the sterilizer chamber is not pressurized and no personnel are near the unit. Avoid burns or injury when opening doors or handling hot water.

Check Drain Pathway and Connections

Inspect the external drain hose for kinks, clogs, or improper connections. Ensure the hose is properly seated and water can flow freely.

Verify Facility Plumbing

Confirm that the floor drain or plumbing receiving the sterilizer discharge is clear, functional, and capable of handling the sterilizer’s outflow. A blocked floor drain can cause retention errors.

Inspect Water Level Sensors

Check that the reservoir or chamber water level sensors are clean, correctly positioned, and undamaged. Faulty sensors may falsely indicate retention issues.

Test Drain Pump Function

If the unit has a drain pump, activate a drain cycle. Listen for pump operation and observe water flow. No flow or unusual noise may indicate pump failure or obstruction.

Check for Residual Water in Chamber

Open the chamber after a cycle. If water remains, confirm whether it’s from incomplete drainage or condensation. Excessive residual water may indicate a clogged drain or faulty valve.

Examine Inlet and Outlet Valves

Ensure valves controlling water entry and exit are fully opening and closing. Sticking valves can cause water retention or improper cycle completion.

Cycle Test

Run a short drain or water purge cycle. Confirm proper water evacuation and that no alarms are triggered. Successful drainage indicates external and sensor components are functioning.

If the Problem Persists

External and easily verifiable causes have been ruled out. The device may have an internal plumbing blockage, pump failure, or valve malfunction.

  • Remove the sterilizer from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Send for bench repair or manufacturer evaluation

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting to prevent further damage or risk to patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt to troubleshoot drainage while the sterilizer is in an active cycle with instruments inside. Move patient-critical instruments to another sterilizer first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Sterilizer left residual water in chamber after cycle completion."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External drain hose was partially kinked, restricting water flow."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Straightened hose, verified proper drainage, and ran test cycle successfully."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Floor drain tested and cleared
  • Drain hose inspected and repositioned
  • Drain pump audible and operational
  • Water level sensors verified
  • Chamber free of residual water post-test

Final Thought

Patient safety is the priority. Stepwise, logic-based troubleshooting helps isolate external issues first. Escalate only when necessary and document all findings to support maintenance records and reduce repeat failures.

That is successful troubleshooting.
