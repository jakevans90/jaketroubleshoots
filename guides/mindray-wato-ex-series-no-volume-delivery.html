Mindray WATO EX Series

Ventilator Alarms / No Volume Delivery

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

WATO EX Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Mindray WATO EX Series anesthesia machine:

  • Triggers ventilator alarms (e.g., “No Volume Delivery,” “Low Tidal Volume”)
  • Fails to deliver set tidal volumes to the patient
  • Shows abnormal pressure or flow readings

The focus is on external, easily verifiable causes first, ensuring patient safety and avoiding unnecessary internal repairs.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power and Startup

  • Ensure the machine is connected to a reliable power source and switched on.
  • Check that the battery (if applicable) is charged and the backup battery is functional.
  • Why: Insufficient power can prevent the ventilator from delivering volume even if settings appear correct.

Check Gas Supply

  • Confirm that oxygen and air pipelines are connected and supplying correct pressures.
  • Check pipeline pressure indicators on the machine.
  • Why: Ventilator volume delivery requires proper gas supply; alarms often occur if pressures are too low.

Inspect the Breathing Circuit

  • Check for disconnections, kinks, or leaks in the inspiratory and expiratory limbs.
  • Ensure the circuit is properly assembled and all valves are seated correctly.
  • Why: Leaks or disconnections can prevent the set tidal volume from reaching the patient.

Check Patient Valve and Filters

  • Inspect the patient valve for obstruction or improper installation.
  • Replace filters if clogged or saturated.
  • Why: Blocked valves or filters increase resistance, reducing delivered volume.

Verify Ventilator Settings

  • Confirm correct mode, tidal volume, respiratory rate, and inspiratory pressure.
  • Ensure that any patient-specific alarms or safety limits are not preventing delivery.
  • Why: Incorrect settings or active safety limits can inhibit ventilation.

Observe Alarms and Indicators

  • Note any specific alarm codes or messages.
  • Refer to the Mindray quick reference card for external checks related to the alarms.
  • Why: Alarm codes often indicate whether the cause is mechanical, flow-related, or sensor-based.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks have been completed and the ventilator still fails to deliver volume:

  • Remove the anesthesia machine from service.
  • Label the machine “Out of Service” to prevent patient use.
  • Escalate for bench evaluation or service by qualified Biomedical Engineering or Mindray service personnel.

Note: Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt troubleshooting on a patient actively connected to the ventilator.
  • Move the patient to a backup ventilator before performing any checks.
  • Only troubleshoot when the patient is safely supported by an alternative device.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reports ventilator alarm “No Volume Delivery” and patient is not receiving set tidal volume.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Upon inspection, all power, gas supply, circuit connections, and ventilator settings were verified. No external issues found; machine fails to deliver volume.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Machine removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for service/repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Gas pressures checked and within specification
  • Circuit assembled correctly, no kinks or disconnections
  • Filters and patient valve checked
  • Alarm codes recorded
  • Machine status and settings noted prior to removal

Final Thought

Always prioritize patient safety, follow logic-based troubleshooting, and escalate when external factors are ruled out. Proper documentation ensures continuity of care and supports service decisions.

That is successful troubleshooting.
