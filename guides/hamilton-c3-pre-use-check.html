Hamilton C3 Ventilator

Pre-Use Check Failures

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C3

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Hamilton C3 ventilator:

  • Fails its pre-use self-test (startup check)
  • Displays error codes or warnings before patient use
  • Does not proceed to normal operational mode

Focus is on external checks first—power, connections, setup, and consumables—before assuming internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Source

  • Ensure the ventilator is connected to a live outlet or fully charged battery.
  • Confirm the line voltage matches specifications.

Why: Inadequate or unstable power can prevent successful pre-use checks.

Check Battery and Battery Seating

  • Remove and reseat the battery module.
  • Inspect for corrosion or loose connections.
  • If using external power, ensure battery bypass is properly engaged.

Why: The ventilator will fail checks if it cannot verify battery status.

Inspect Circuit and Humidifier Connections

  • Ensure patient circuit is properly attached and no leaks are present.
  • Check that all tubing, sensors, and filters are correctly seated.

Why: Disconnected or misconnected circuits can trigger pre-use alarms.

Check Flow Sensor / CO₂ Module (if applicable)

  • Ensure modules are plugged in and recognized by the system.
  • Look for any warning LEDs or disconnected cables.

Why: Missing or unrecognized modules will halt pre-use testing.

Examine Filters and Water Traps

  • Ensure airway filters and water traps are installed and not saturated.
  • Replace if needed.

Why: Blocked sensors or saturated filters can cause check failures.

Restart Ventilator

  • Power down completely, wait 30 seconds, then power on.
  • Observe self-test sequence carefully.

Why: Temporary software glitches or communication errors can sometimes be cleared with a restart.

Review Error Codes / Alarms

  • Note any displayed messages and consult internal code definitions.

Why: Error codes often identify the subsystem causing pre-use check failure.

If the Problem Persists

External checks have been ruled out; internal failure is likely (mainboard, sensors, or software).

  • Remove ventilator from service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for manufacturer repair or internal bench evaluation

Knowing when to escalate is a key part of safe troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt troubleshooting on a patient.
  • Ensure patient is switched to a standby ventilator if needed.
  • Only perform checks in a safe, controlled environment.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator failed pre-use check during startup; displayed “Check Failure” error.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Circuit not properly connected; battery seating verified; filters installed correctly.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Reconnected circuit, reseated battery, replaced saturated filter; pre-use check passed after restart.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Power outlet tested
  • Battery voltage verified
  • Patient circuit connection checked
  • Flow/CO₂ modules seated
  • Filters and water traps inspected
  • Error codes logged
  • Final device status after troubleshooting

Final Thought

Safe, logic-based troubleshooting protects patients and staff. Start with simple, verifiable external checks and escalate only when necessary. Thorough documentation ensures continuity of care and supports quality assurance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
