Spacelabs Healthcare Qube

Power-On / Boot Failure

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Spacelabs Healthcare

Model

Qube

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Spacelabs Qube patient monitor that fails to power on or complete its boot sequence. Symptoms may include:

  • Blank display when power is applied
  • No response from the power button
  • Partial or stalled boot sequence
  • Audible beeps without screen activity

The focus is on external checks and safe troubleshooting before escalating to repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Never attempt troubleshooting on a monitor that is attached to a patient. Move the patient to a functioning monitor before troubleshooting.

Check the Power Source

  • Verify that the wall outlet is supplying power (use a known working device or a multimeter).
  • Confirm that the outlet is not switched off or on a tripped circuit.

Why: Ensures the monitor isn’t failing due to missing mains power.

Inspect the Power Cord and Connection

  • Check for visible damage to the power cord or plug.
  • Ensure the power cord is fully seated in both the monitor and the wall outlet.

Why: Loose or damaged cords are common causes of no-power issues.

Test with Battery (if equipped)

  • If the Qube has a removable battery, check if the battery is seated correctly.
  • Attempt to power on the device using battery only.

Why: Confirms whether the issue is related to AC power or the battery.

Reset External Power

  • Disconnect the monitor from all power sources (battery and AC) for at least 30 seconds.
  • Reconnect AC power (and battery if used) and attempt to power on.

Why: Some monitors may lock in an unresponsive state and require a power reset.

Check External Indicators

  • Look for any LEDs that light briefly or flash during boot attempts.
  • Note any audible error tones.

Why: LED patterns or beeps can indicate specific hardware faults.

Remove Non-Essential Accessories

  • Disconnect external modules, sensors, or network cables.
  • Attempt to boot the monitor with only essential components.

Why: A failing accessory can prevent the monitor from completing boot.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks are complete and the device still fails to power on or boot:

  • The issue is likely internal (mainboard, power supply, or internal battery).
  • Remove the monitor from service immediately.
  • Label it “Out of Service” clearly.
  • Send the unit for repair or bench evaluation.

Note: Knowing when to escalate is proper troubleshooting and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt troubleshooting on a monitor that is attached to a patient.
  • Move the patient to a functioning monitor before troubleshooting.
  • Avoid opening the monitor or attempting internal repairs; internal service should be performed only by qualified technicians.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor will not power on; display remains blank and no response to power button.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“External power and battery verified; power reset and accessory removal did not restore function. Device fails to boot, indicating possible internal fault.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and confirmed live
  • Power cord inspected and confirmed intact
  • Battery condition checked
  • External modules disconnected
  • LED indicators or error beeps noted
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount: ensure no patient is left on a non-functional monitor. Follow a logical, stepwise approach to isolate simple external causes before escalating to internal repair. Proper documentation supports both patient safety and departmental accountability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
