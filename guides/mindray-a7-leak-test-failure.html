Mindray A7 Anesthesia Machine

Leak Test Failure

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

A7

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Mindray A7 anesthesia machine fails its automatic leak test during startup or daily checkout.

Symptoms may include:

  • “Leak Test Failed” message
  • Inability to complete system checkout
  • Audible leak detected during test
  • Circuit pressure not holding during occlusion

This guide focuses on ruling out common, external, and user-related causes before assuming internal pneumatic failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Remove the Machine From Clinical Use

  • Do not troubleshoot while connected to a patient.
  • Move the patient to another anesthesia machine if necessary.
  • Leak test failures must be addressed before clinical use.

2. Verify Breathing Circuit Assembly

  • Incorrectly assembled circuits are the most common cause.
  • Confirm inspiratory and expiratory limbs are fully seated
  • Ensure the Y-piece is connected securely
  • Check that all connections are tight
  • Verify the reservoir bag is installed and not loose
  • Why this matters: Even a small loose connection will cause test failure.

3. Inspect the Reservoir Bag

  • Check for tears, pinholes, or dry rot
  • Replace with a known-good bag if unsure
  • A deteriorated bag can leak during positive pressure testing.

4. Check the APL Valve Position

  • Ensure the APL valve is fully closed during the manual leak test
  • Verify it turns smoothly and is not stuck partially open
  • An open or faulty APL valve will prevent pressure from building.

5. Confirm CO₂ Absorber Installation

  • Ensure the absorber canister is fully seated
  • Check gasket condition
  • Look for cracks in the canister housing
  • Improper seating or damaged seals frequently cause leaks.

6. Inspect Water Trap and Sampling Lines

  • Ensure gas sampling line is connected properly
  • Check water trap for cracks or improper seating
  • A loose sampling line may introduce a leak pathway.

7. Verify Vaporizers

  • Ensure vaporizers are fully locked in place
  • Confirm filler caps are tight
  • Check for visible cracks or loose mounting
  • Improperly seated vaporizers can create internal circuit leaks.

8. Perform a Manual Positive Pressure Test

  • If the automatic test fails:
  • Occlude the Y-piece
  • Close APL valve
  • Pressurize to ~30 cmH₂O
  • Observe for pressure drop
  • If pressure holds manually but automatic test fails, issue may be sensor-related rather than a true leak.

9. Inspect External Gas Hoses

  • Verify pipeline hoses are secure
  • Listen for hissing at connections
  • Check auxiliary O₂ outlets
  • Though not part of the breathing circuit, supply leaks may affect system checks.

10. Power Cycle and Reattempt Test

  • After correcting any identified issue:
  • Power off machine completely
  • Wait 30 seconds
  • Restart and re-run full system checkout
  • Transient sensor errors may clear after reboot.

If the Problem Persists

If:

  • Circuit components are verified
  • Reservoir bag replaced
  • Absorber properly seated
  • Vaporizers confirmed secure
  • Manual test confirms pressure loss

Then the issue is likely internal, such as:

  • Internal pneumatic leak
  • Faulty pressure sensor
  • Internal valve malfunction
  • Manifold leak

At this point:

  • Remove the machine from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Document findings
  • Send for qualified repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary disassembly and protects patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never bypass a failed leak test to “get through a case.”

If a machine fails checkout, it is not safe for patient ventilation.

Always transfer the patient to a verified working anesthesia machine before troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Anesthesia reported ‘Leak Test Failed’ during morning checkout. Machine would not complete system test.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“CO₂ absorber canister not fully seated; gasket partially misaligned causing pressure loss during positive pressure test.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Reseated absorber, verified gasket integrity, performed manual leak test (held 30 cmH₂O for 10 seconds). Automatic checkout passed. Machine returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Was a known-good breathing circuit used?
  • Was reservoir bag replaced?
  • APL valve position confirmed?
  • Vaporizers locked?
  • Manual test results (pressure and time)
  • Any audible leak noted?
  • Final device status (Returned to service or Sent for repair)

Final Thought

Most anesthesia machine leak test failures are caused by simple assembly or seal issues. A logical, structured approach prevents unnecessary internal repair and reduces OR downtime. Always prioritize patient safety, verify external components first, and escalate appropriately when internal faults are suspected. Proper documentation protects both the department and the patient.

That is successful troubleshooting.
