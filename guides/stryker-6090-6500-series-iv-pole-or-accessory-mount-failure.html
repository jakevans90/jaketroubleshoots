Stryker 6090 / 6500 Series

IV Pole or Accessory Mount Failure

Asset Type

Stretcher

Manufacturer

Stryker

Model

6090 / 6500 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Loose, unstable, or non-functional IV pole or accessory mount caused by improper installation, worn components, or mounting hardware issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot while the stretcher is in active patient use.
  • Remove any IV bags or accessories from the pole before inspection.

Expected: Safe working condition with no load on the mount
Why it matters: Prevents injury from falling equipment or sudden instability

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Check what is failing:
    • IV pole wobbling or loose
    • Pole will not lock into place
    • Mount bracket shifting or detaching
    • Accessory (monitor, pump, etc.) not securing properly

Expected: Clear identification of failure mode
Why it matters: Helps determine whether the issue is mechanical, mounting-related, or user setup

Inspect IV Pole Seating and Installation

  • Remove and reinsert the IV pole into the mount.
  • Ensure it is fully seated and aligned correctly.
  • Check for:
    • Improper insertion
    • Misalignment
    • Partial engagement

Expected: Pole sits fully and locks securely
Why it matters: Improper seating is a very common cause of instability

Check Locking Mechanism or Set Screw

  • Inspect the locking knob, clamp, or set screw securing the IV pole.
  • Tighten the knob or screw
  • Ensure threads are not stripped
  • Confirm locking mechanism engages fully

Expected: Pole remains stable when secured
Why it matters: A loose or worn locking mechanism will prevent proper stabilization

Inspect Mounting Bracket and Hardware

  • Check the mount where the IV pole or accessory attaches to the stretcher frame.
  • Look for:
    • Loose or missing screws/bolts
    • Cracked or bent bracket
    • Worn mounting holes
  • Tighten any loose hardware as needed.

Expected: Mount is firmly secured to stretcher frame
Why it matters: Structural looseness at the base will affect all attached accessories

Test with a Known Good IV Pole or Accessory

  • Swap with a known working IV pole or accessory.

Expected:
If new pole works → original pole is defective
If issue remains → mount is the problem
Why it matters: Isolates whether failure is with the accessory or the stretcher mount

Check for Physical Damage or Wear

  • Inspect both the pole and mount for:
    • Bent metal
    • Worn grooves
    • Cracks or deformation
    • Excessive play even when tightened

Expected: No visible structural damage
Why it matters: Mechanical wear over time leads to failure under load

Verify Weight Load and Usage

  • Confirm accessories are within intended weight limits.
  • Check for:
    • Multiple pumps stacked
    • Heavy equipment mounted improperly

Expected: Load is appropriate and evenly distributed
Why it matters: Overloading accelerates wear and causes instability

If the Problem Persists

If all external mounting, hardware, and accessory checks have been completed and the issue remains, the failure is likely due to internal structural wear or damaged mounting components.

Remove the stretcher from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or further evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unsafe use and repeated failures.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt to stabilize a loose IV pole during patient transport. Always transfer the patient to a properly functioning stretcher before use.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"IV pole loose and unable to securely hold infusion pumps during transport."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Worn locking mechanism and loose mounting bracket hardware identified."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Tightened mounting hardware and replaced IV pole; confirmed secure attachment and normal function."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Mounting hardware condition (tight/loose/missing)
  • Locking mechanism behavior
  • Stability under load
  • Presence of bending or cracks
  • Accessories tested
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Accessory mounting issues may seem minor but directly impact patient safety during transport. Always start with simple mechanical checks, confirm stability under load, and escalate when structural integrity is compromised. Proper documentation ensures recurring issues are tracked and addressed effectively.

That is successful troubleshooting.
