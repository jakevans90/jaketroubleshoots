QC Failure or Analyzer Lockout

Asset Type

Blood Gas Analyzer

Manufacturer

Roche

Model

cobas h 232

What This Guide Helps With

QC failures, analyzer lockout, or inability to run patient tests due to control issues, strip problems, or configuration-related restrictions.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot while the analyzer is needed for active patient testing. Move testing to a backup device.

Expected: No interruption to patient care
Why it matters: Lockout prevents timely cardiac marker testing and delays clinical decisions

2. Verify the Reported QC Failure or Lockout

  • Power on the analyzer and review the screen
  • Confirm QC failure message or lockout notification
  • Note any specific error codes or prompts
  • Attempt to access testing mode

Expected: Issue is clearly displayed and reproducible
Why it matters: Confirms a true lockout vs workflow or user-related issue

3. Check QC Material Validity (Most Common Cause)

  • Verify QC material is not expired
  • Confirm proper storage conditions (temperature sensitive)
  • Mix or prepare QC material per protocol

Expected: Valid QC material passes
Why it matters: QC integrity is required before patient testing is allowed

4. Verify Test Strip and Code Chip Compatibility

  • Confirm correct test strips are being used
  • Ensure code chip matches strip lot number
  • Reseat or replace code chip if applicable

Expected: Analyzer accepts strip and QC proceeds normally
Why it matters: The analyzer relies on lot-specific calibration data

5. Inspect Test Strip Storage and Handling

  • Check that strips are stored in sealed containers
  • Ensure strips are within expiration date
  • Avoid humidity or temperature extremes

Expected: Properly stored strips produce valid QC results
Why it matters: Damaged strips commonly cause QC failure and lockout

6. Check Analyzer Temperature Conditions

  • Confirm device is within operating temperature range
  • Allow analyzer to acclimate if moved from a different environment

Expected: QC passes once temperature stabilizes
Why it matters: Environmental conditions directly impact test accuracy

7. Clean Test Strip Port and Contacts

  • Inspect strip port for debris or residue
  • Clean externally per manufacturer-approved method
  • Do not insert foreign objects into the port

Expected: Improved strip recognition and QC success
Why it matters: Poor electrical contact can cause failed QC readings

8. Restart the Analyzer

  • Power the unit off
  • Remove battery briefly if applicable
  • Restart and repeat QC process

Expected: System resets and allows QC to proceed
Why it matters: Clears minor system faults without deeper intervention

If the Problem Persists

If QC continues to fail after verifying materials, strips, environment, and cleaning, the issue is likely internal.

Remove the analyzer from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary downtime and ensures safe, reliable testing.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt QC troubleshooting while a patient sample is pending. Always move testing to a functioning analyzer before investigating lockout conditions.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Analyzer locked out due to repeated QC failure and unable to run patient tests."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Expired QC material and mismatched code chip with test strip lot."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced QC material, installed correct code chip, and successfully passed QC; returned unit to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • QC lot numbers and expiration dates verified
  • Test strip condition and storage checked
  • Code chip matched to strip lot
  • Strip port inspected/cleaned
  • Temperature/environment conditions verified
  • Device successfully passed QC or removed from service

Final Thought

QC failures and lockouts are often caused by controllable external factors. A structured approach ensures patient safety, avoids unnecessary repairs, and maintains confidence in test accuracy.

That is successful troubleshooting.
