GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series

Error: HOUSING OVERHEATED

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

OEC 9900 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot HOUSING OVERHEATED on a GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series C-Arm. This condition occurs when the X-ray tube housing reaches an overheat cutoff state, causing thermal shutdown and preventing safe X-ray operation.

The goal is to protect the patient, confirm the reported error, allow the tube housing to cool, check for external cooling restrictions, and determine whether the event was caused by heavy imaging use or a likely device fault.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not continue using the C-Arm after a housing overheat shutdown.
  • Remove the OEC 9900 from patient use if the system has shut down due to the HOUSING OVERHEATED error.
  • If imaging is still required, have clinical staff obtain another C-Arm or use an approved alternate imaging method.
  • Do not attempt repeated restarts to continue the procedure.

Patient care should continue without relying on an overheated X-ray system.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Check whether the display showed HOUSING OVERHEATED.
  • Confirm whether the system shut down immediately after the message appeared.
  • Ask staff when the error occurred.
  • Determine whether the error happened during extended fluoroscopy, repeated high-load exposures, High Level Fluoro, cine or film-type use, startup, or normal positioning.

This confirms that the system entered a protective tube housing overheat condition.

Allow the Tube Housing to Cool

  • Leave the system out of patient use.
  • Allow the X-ray tube housing to cool naturally.
  • Do not immediately restart the system and attempt additional exposures.
  • Be aware that adequate cooling may require an hour or more.

This condition is not just a nuisance alarm. The system is protecting the tube housing from excessive heat.

Check Recent Imaging Use and Heat Loading

  • Ask the user what type of imaging was being performed before shutdown.
  • Check for long fluoroscopy time.
  • Check for repeated high-dose exposures.
  • Check for High Level Fluoro use.
  • Check for Digital Cine use.
  • Check for large patient imaging or frequent exposures with little cooling time.

A clear history of heavy X-ray use may explain a legitimate housing overheat condition.

Inspect External Airflow and Ventilation

  • Inspect the C-Arm and workstation area for blocked vents or restricted airflow.
  • Look for drapes, covers, wall placement, or other equipment crowded around cooling areas.
  • Remove anything blocking air intake or exhaust areas.
  • Make sure the device is not operating near excessive environmental heat.

Poor airflow can cause the tube housing and cooling system to overheat faster than expected.

Check for Fan Operation and Cooling Behavior

  • After the unit has cooled enough to safely power on, listen for expected fan operation.
  • Check for no fan sound, abnormal fan noise, rubbing, grinding, or weak airflow.
  • Check for hot smell, excessive heat around the tube housing area, or the error returning quickly after minimal use.

A recurring HOUSING OVERHEATED error after light use may indicate a fan, airflow, thermal sensing, or tube housing cooling issue.

Inspect for Physical Damage or Contamination

  • Inspect accessible exterior areas of the C-Arm, tube housing area, vents, and cooling paths.
  • Check for dust buildup, blocked vent openings, fluid contamination, damaged covers, loose panels, or signs of impact.
  • Check for burning smell, discoloration, or unusual heat from the tube housing area.

External damage or contamination can interfere with cooling and may indicate the device needs repair evaluation.

Restart Only After Adequate Cooling

  • After a proper cooling period, power the system back on.
  • Observe whether the error returns immediately or only after additional X-ray use.
  • Do not perform unnecessary exposures just to test the issue in a patient care area.
  • If the error clears after cooling and the cause was heavy procedural use, return the device to service only if operation is stable and facility policy allows.

The system should start normally after cooling and should not immediately return to the same error.

Determine Whether the Issue Is Use-Related or Device-Related

  • If the error followed extended high-load imaging and clears after cooling, it was likely heat-loading related.
  • If the error returns quickly after light use, suspect cooling failure or thermal monitoring issue.
  • If fans are not operating normally, suspect cooling system failure.
  • If the unit shuts down immediately after startup with the same error, suspect thermal cutoff, sensor, wiring, or internal device fault.

The correct response depends on whether the system was pushed to its thermal limit or whether it cannot cool properly.

If the Problem Persists

If the HOUSING OVERHEATED error returns after adequate cooling, occurs with minimal X-ray use, or the cooling fans or airflow appear abnormal, remove the C-Arm from service.

At that point, external and common causes have been ruled out. A persistent housing overheat condition may indicate a cooling system, thermal cutoff, tube housing, sensor, wiring, or internal control issue.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Do not keep returning the C-Arm to clinical use if the housing overheat shutdown repeats.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a tube housing overheat condition on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device or approved alternate imaging option first.

An unresolved HOUSING OVERHEATED shutdown means the C-Arm cannot be relied on for continued imaging.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"User reported GE OEC 9900 Series C-Arm displayed HOUSING OVERHEATED and shut down during use. X-ray operation was unavailable after thermal shutdown."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"X-ray tube housing reached overheat cutoff condition. Heavy heat-loading use was reported prior to shutdown. Device required extended cooling before further operation."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed C-Arm from patient use and allowed tube housing to cool. Checked for blocked airflow, fan operation, abnormal heat, odor, and visible damage. Unit restarted normally after cooling and error did not immediately return. Returned to service after confirming stable operation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

HOUSING OVERHEATED is a protective shutdown, not just a warning. Clinical Engineering should first protect the patient, confirm the exact error, allow adequate cooling, check for airflow and fan issues, and determine whether the event was caused by heavy use or a true cooling failure. If the error repeats after cooling or occurs under light use, escalation is the correct repair path.

That is successful troubleshooting.
