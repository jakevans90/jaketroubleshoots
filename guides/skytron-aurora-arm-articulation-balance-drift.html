Skytron Aurora Surgical Light

Arm Articulation or Balance Drift

Asset Type

Surgical Light

Manufacturer

Skytron

Model

Aurora

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Skytron Aurora surgical light arm drifts, sags, or does not hold its position properly. Common causes include worn or loose arm joints, tension spring issues, or improper adjustment. The focus is on external and verifiable checks before assuming internal component failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify the Environment and Mounting

  • Ensure the ceiling or floor mount is secure and level.
  • Check that no external obstruction (equipment, drapes) is interfering with the light arm.
  • Why: A poorly secured mount can mimic balance drift or sag.

Inspect Arm Joints for Looseness

  • Gently move the arm through its full range of motion.
  • Look for wobble, uneven movement, or excessive play at joints.
  • Why: Loose pivot points reduce stability and cause drift.

Check Tension Mechanisms

  • Locate external tension adjustment screws or knobs on the arm (if accessible).
  • Verify the arm resists movement slightly but can still be positioned smoothly.
  • Why: Over time, springs or tension mechanisms can weaken, causing sagging.

Look for Wear or Damage on External Components

  • Inspect arm covers, joints, and visible bearings for cracks, corrosion, or missing parts.
  • Why: Physical damage can impair balance even if tension is correct.

Test Light Head Weight and Accessories

  • Remove any added equipment or handles mounted on the light head.
  • Reposition the arm to see if drift improves.
  • Why: Extra weight can affect balance beyond designed limits.

Verify Manufacturer Adjustment Procedure

  • Some Aurora models have external spring tension adjustments or locking mechanisms.
  • Carefully follow manufacturer guidance for safe adjustments if indicated.
  • Why: Correct tension is critical for proper articulation.

If the Problem Persists

If arm drift continues after external adjustments and no visible damage is noted:

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for manufacturer or authorized repair/bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop and escalate ensures patient and staff safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt to reposition or work on the surgical light while it is over a patient.
  • Move the patient or schedule a procedure using another functional light until the issue is resolved.
  • Verify light stability before any procedure to prevent accidental movement.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Light arm drifts downward when positioned; cannot hold set height.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Arm joints show slight wobble and tension spring adjustment appears weak; no external obstructions detected.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Verified arm mounting and joints; attempted external tension adjustment per manufacturer guidelines; issue persists. Device removed from service and sent for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Ceiling or floor mount stability checked
  • Arm joints inspected
  • Tension adjustment attempted
  • Light head weight checked
  • No unusual sounds noted during movement
  • Final device status: Out of Service, sent for repair

Final Thought

Maintaining proper arm articulation and balance is critical for surgical precision and patient safety. Using a logical troubleshooting approach—checking mounts, joints, tension, and load—prevents unnecessary internal disassembly and ensures safe escalation. Accurate CCR documentation supports timely repair and safe clinical use.

That is successful troubleshooting.
