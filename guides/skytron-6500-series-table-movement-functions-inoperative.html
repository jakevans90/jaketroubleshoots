Skytron 6500 Series

Table Movement Functions Inoperative

Asset Type

Surgical Table

Manufacturer

Skytron

Model

6500 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Skytron 6500 Series surgical table when the table powers on but movement functions (raising, lowering, tilting, or lateral adjustments) do not operate. Common symptoms include:

  • Table powers on with indicator lights functioning
  • No response from hand or foot controls
  • Audible motor or hydraulic sounds may be absent or abnormal

Focus is on external, verifiable checks before assuming internal mechanical or electrical failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Source

  • Confirm the table is plugged into a live, dedicated outlet.
  • Check for tripped breakers or blown fuses.

Why: Even if the table shows partial power (lights), movement motors require full voltage.

Check Main Power Switch

  • Ensure the main power switch is in the “ON” position.
  • Some Skytron models have separate switches for power and motion enable.

Why: Movement functions may be disabled if the motion enable is off.

Inspect Hand and Foot Controls

  • Ensure all buttons are functioning and not stuck.
  • Verify connection cables are fully seated.
  • Swap with a known good pendant if available.

Why: Faulty or disconnected control units often mimic motor failure.

Observe Indicator Lights or Error Codes

  • Check display panel for error codes or flashing indicators.
  • Document any patterns for repair escalation.

Why: Error codes can indicate motor drive or safety interlock issues.

Check Safety Interlocks

  • Verify table brakes are disengaged and base locks are not activated.
  • Ensure any side rails or attachments are correctly positioned.

Why: Many Skytron tables will inhibit movement if safety conditions are not met.

Inspect Power Cables and Connectors

  • Look for loose or damaged cables, including those connecting the power module to motors.

Why: Intermittent or damaged wiring can prevent movement.

Test Motion Functions Individually

  • Attempt raising/lowering, tilt, and lateral movements one at a time.
  • Note any movement or sounds.

Why: Isolates which circuits or motors are affected.

If the Problem Persists

All external and easily verifiable causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely internal (motor controller, drive circuit, hydraulic actuator).

Action:

  • Remove the table from service
  • Label “Out of Service”
  • Send to manufacturer repair or qualified bench evaluation

Knowing when to escalate prevents unsafe troubleshooting and protects the table from further damage.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt troubleshooting while a patient is on the table.
  • Move the patient to an alternative table first.
  • Only attempt troubleshooting when the table is unoccupied and safely powered down.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Table powers on but will not move in any direction.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“All external checks performed; no movement observed from any control; no audible motor activity.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Table removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet verified live
  • Control cables checked or swapped
  • Brakes and interlocks verified disengaged
  • Indicator lights normal or error codes documented
  • No unusual noises, heat, or odor observed
  • Table removed from service for repair

Final Thought

Patient safety is the priority. Logical, stepwise troubleshooting helps identify external causes efficiently. Escalation to repair is appropriate when external checks fail. Document all observations clearly for continuity of care and future reference.

That is successful troubleshooting.
