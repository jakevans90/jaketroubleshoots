Olympus EVIS EXERA III

Image Quality Degradation (Noise, Color Issues, Artifacts)

Asset Type

Endoscopic / OR Camera System

Manufacturer

Olympus

Model

EVIS EXERA III

What This Guide Helps With

Poor image quality including noise, incorrect color balance, flickering, or visual artifacts caused by external components, connections, or setup issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If image quality is compromised during a procedure, notify clinical staff immediately and transition to a backup system if needed.
  • Expected: Procedure continues safely with adequate visualization
  • Why it matters: Poor image quality can impact clinical decision-making and patient safety

Verify the Reported Image Issue

  • Observe the display and confirm the exact symptom:
  • Noise/graininess
  • Color distortion (too red, blue, or washed out)
  • Flickering or intermittent signal
  • Lines or artifacts
  • Expected: Issue is clearly identified and reproducible
  • Why it matters: Helps isolate whether the problem is signal-related, lighting-related, or processing-related

Check Monitor and Display Settings (Common Cause)

  • Verify monitor input source, brightness, contrast, and color settings.
  • Ensure correct input (HDMI/DVI/SDI depending on setup)
  • Reset monitor to default settings if needed
  • Expected: Image returns to normal if display settings were altered
  • Why it matters: Display misconfiguration can mimic system image issues

Inspect Video Cable Connections

  • Check all video connections between:
  • Camera control unit (CCU)
  • Light source
  • Monitor
  • Reseat all cables firmly
  • Look for bent pins or damaged connectors
  • Swap with a known good cable if available
  • Expected: Stable, clear image if connection issue is resolved
  • Why it matters: Loose or damaged cables commonly cause noise and artifacts

Verify Camera Head and Scope Connection

  • Disconnect and reconnect the camera head to the scope and control unit.
  • Ensure proper alignment and secure locking
  • Check for contamination on optical connectors
  • Expected: Image clarity improves if connection was poor
  • Why it matters: Improper coupling affects signal transmission and image integrity

Check Light Source and Fiber Optic Cable

  • Inspect the light source and light cable:
  • Confirm light source is powered and functioning
  • Check brightness level settings
  • Inspect fiber optic cable for breaks or dark spots
  • Expected: Bright, evenly illuminated image
  • Why it matters: Poor illumination can appear as noise or color distortion

Inspect Scope Optics (High Probability Cause)

  • Examine the endoscope:
  • Look for debris, fluid, or fogging on the lens
  • Clean distal tip using approved methods
  • Check for internal damage (dark spots, uneven lighting)
  • Expected: Clear image after cleaning or scope swap
  • Why it matters: Dirty or damaged optics are a leading cause of poor image quality

Check White Balance and System Settings

  • Perform a white balance using a proper white reference.
  • Ensure correct mode/settings for procedure
  • Reset image settings if needed
  • Expected: Accurate color representation
  • Why it matters: Incorrect white balance causes major color distortion

Swap Components to Isolate the Issue

  • Swap one component at a time with a known good unit:
  • Camera head
  • Scope
  • Light cable
  • Monitor
  • Expected: Fault follows the defective component
  • Why it matters: Helps pinpoint the exact source without internal disassembly

Power Cycle the System

  • Shut down and restart the full system (CCU, light source, monitor).
  • Expected: Temporary glitches or processing issues resolve
  • Why it matters: Software or signal processing errors can affect image output

If the Problem Persists

If all external components, connections, and settings have been verified, the issue is likely internal (camera control unit, imaging processor, or internal optics).

Remove the system from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting and prevents unnecessary risk.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot image quality on an active patient case. Always transition to a backup system before performing any equipment checks.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"OR staff reported poor image quality with noise and incorrect color during endoscopic procedure."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Damaged fiber optic light cable causing reduced illumination and image artifacts."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced light cable with known good unit; verified normal image quality and returned system to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Monitor input and settings verified
  • Video cables reseated/swapped
  • Light source output confirmed
  • Scope cleaned or replaced
  • White balance performed
  • Any visible artifacts or flickering behavior
  • Final device status after testing

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple, external checks and progresses logically. Image quality issues are often caused by cables, optics, or lighting—not internal failures. Prioritize patient safety, isolate components methodically, and escalate appropriately when needed. Proper documentation ensures clarity and supports future repairs.

That is successful troubleshooting.
