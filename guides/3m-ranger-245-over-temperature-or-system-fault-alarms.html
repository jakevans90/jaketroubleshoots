3M Ranger 245

Over-Temperature or System Fault Alarms

Asset Type

Fluid & Blood Warmer

Manufacturer

3M

Model

Ranger 245

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a 3M Ranger 245 fluid and blood warmer displays over-temperature alarms or system fault alarms. These alarms may be triggered by airflow obstruction, disposable cassette issues, environmental conditions, or sensor detection problems. The guide focuses on verifying common external causes before assuming an internal heater or control failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Never troubleshoot a fluid or blood warmer while it is actively connected to a patient.
  • If an alarm occurs during clinical use, move the patient to another functioning warmer immediately.
  • Remove the affected unit from active therapy before beginning troubleshooting.

Confirm the Alarm Message

  • Check the display panel for the exact alarm wording.
  • Different alarms (over-temperature vs. system fault) may indicate different causes such as airflow blockage, sensor detection issues, or electronic faults.
  • Document the alarm message if possible.

Verify the Disposable Cassette Installation

  • Inspect the installed Ranger disposable set.
  • Check that the cassette is fully seated in the heater slot.
  • Confirm tubing is properly routed through the warming channel.
  • Inspect for kinks or twists in the tubing.
  • Verify the correct Ranger disposable set is being used.
  • Improper seating can interfere with heat transfer monitoring and trigger a fault or over-temperature condition.
  • Remove and reinstall the cassette if necessary.

Check for Airflow Obstruction

  • Inspect the heater unit ventilation openings.
  • Look for blocked air vents.
  • Check for dust buildup.
  • Ensure drapes or linens are not covering the unit.
  • Confirm no equipment is placed directly against the warmer.
  • The Ranger relies on proper airflow to regulate heater temperature. Blocked ventilation can cause internal heat buildup and trigger over-temperature alarms.
  • Ensure the vents are clear and the unit has adequate space around it.

Verify the Warmer Is Not Enclosed

  • Confirm the unit is not placed inside cabinets.
  • Check that it is not positioned inside warming carts.
  • Ensure it is not tightly surrounded by stacked equipment.
  • Restricted airflow around the device can cause internal temperature sensors to detect overheating.
  • Move the device to an open, ventilated location if necessary.

Power Cycle the Device

  • Turn the Ranger 245 off.
  • Wait 10–15 seconds before turning the unit back on.
  • Observe whether the alarm clears.
  • Confirm the unit completes its startup self-check.
  • Verify the heater begins normal operation.
  • Some system faults may occur due to temporary sensor or control errors that reset during a restart.

Test With a New Disposable Set

  • If the alarm persists, remove the current cassette.
  • Install a new disposable set.
  • Damaged or incorrectly manufactured disposable sets can interfere with heat transfer detection and cause safety alarms.

Check the Power Source

  • Verify the warmer is connected to a stable hospital-grade outlet.
  • Inspect for a loose power cord.
  • Check the plug for damage.
  • Ensure the outlet is not shared with high-load equipment.
  • Power irregularities may trigger system faults during heater operation.

Observe Heater Operation

  • If the unit starts normally after the above steps, monitor the device for immediate alarm recurrence.
  • Listen for abnormal fan noise.
  • Check for any burning smell or excessive heat.
  • Watch for repeated temperature faults.
  • Document observations.

If the Problem Persists

If over-temperature alarms or system faults continue after external checks, common external causes have been ruled out.

The problem is likely due to an internal issue such as:

  • Heater element malfunction
  • Temperature sensor failure
  • Internal fan failure
  • Control board fault

At this point:

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label the device Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop and escalate is an important part of proper troubleshooting and protects both the patient and the equipment.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a fluid or blood warmer while it is actively being used on a patient.

If a temperature alarm occurs during use:

  • Move the patient to another functioning warmer immediately.
  • Remove the faulty device from clinical use.
  • Troubleshoot only after the device is no longer connected to patient therapy.

Patient temperature safety must always come first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Unit alarming with ‘Over Temperature’ during blood warming. Staff unable to continue procedure.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Airflow vents partially blocked by surgical drape and dust buildup restricting cooling airflow.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Cleared ventilation openings, repositioned unit for proper airflow, and verified normal heater operation. Device returned to service.”

“Alarm persisted after cassette replacement, airflow inspection, and power reset. Device removed from service and sent to vendor for repair evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm message displayed
  • Outlet tested
  • Disposable cassette replaced
  • Air vents inspected
  • Fan noise present or absent
  • Any unusual smell or heat
  • Whether alarm cleared after power cycle
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Fluid and blood warmers play a critical role in preventing hypothermia during procedures. When alarms occur, Clinical Engineering should always start with logical external checks such as cassette installation, airflow, and power stability before assuming internal failure. Recognizing when the issue requires escalation ensures patient safety and efficient equipment management. Clear documentation helps future troubleshooting and supports clinical teams.

That is successful troubleshooting.
