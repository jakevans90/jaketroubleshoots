Midmark IQecg Series

ECG Not Acquiring Signal or Noisy Waveform

Asset Type

Electrocardiograph (EKG) Machine

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

IQecg Series

What This Guide Helps With

Poor or absent ECG signal, artifact, or excessive noise caused by lead issues, patient prep, cable faults, or environmental interference.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from active use or switch to another monitor if clinically needed.
  • Expected: Patient safely monitored by alternate equipment.
  • Why it matters: Inaccurate ECG data can lead to improper clinical decisions.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to acquire an ECG and observe waveform quality.
  • Expected: No signal, intermittent signal, or excessive noise/artifact.
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether the issue is consistent and reproducible.

Check Electrode Placement and Condition

  • Inspect electrode placement and ensure proper positioning per standard lead configuration. Verify electrodes are fresh and not dried out.
  • Expected: Electrodes properly placed with good adhesion.
  • Why it matters: Poor skin contact is the most common cause of signal loss or noise.
  • If electrodes are dried, loose, or expired → replace and retest.

Prepare Patient Skin Properly

  • Ensure skin is clean and dry. Shave excessive hair and lightly abrade skin if needed.
  • Expected: Improved electrode contact and reduced impedance.
  • Why it matters: Skin impedance directly affects signal quality.

Inspect Lead Wires and Patient Cable

  • Check for broken wires, fraying, bent pins, or loose connections at both ends.
  • Expected: Cables intact with secure connections.
  • Why it matters: Damaged leads can introduce noise or prevent signal acquisition.
  • If damage is found → replace cable/lead set and retest.

Verify Lead Connections to Module and PC

  • Ensure patient cable is fully connected to the IQecg module and USB module is properly connected to the PC.
  • Expected: Secure, fully seated connections.
  • Why it matters: Loose connections can cause intermittent or no signal.

Check for Electrical Interference

  • Look for nearby equipment (IV pumps, beds, power cords) that may cause artifact. Move cables away from power sources.
  • Expected: Reduction in waveform noise.
  • Why it matters: Electromagnetic interference commonly introduces ECG artifact.

Minimize Patient Movement

  • Ensure the patient is still and relaxed during acquisition.
  • Expected: Stable waveform with minimal artifact.
  • Why it matters: Motion artifact can mimic noise or poor signal.

Test with Known Good Accessories

  • Swap electrodes and lead wires with a known working set.
  • Expected: Signal improves with replacement accessories.
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether issue is accessory-related vs device-related.
  • If resolved → faulty accessories identified.

Check Software Settings and Lead Selection

  • Verify correct lead configuration and acquisition settings in the IQecg software.
  • Expected: Proper waveform display for selected leads.
  • Why it matters: Incorrect configuration can appear as signal loss.

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes (electrodes, cables, patient prep, interference, and connections) have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal to the acquisition module or system electronics.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ECG signal issues on an actively monitored patient. Always move the patient to a reliable device before testing.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"ECG machine not acquiring signal with excessive noise during patient use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Dried electrodes and worn lead wires causing poor signal acquisition."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced electrodes and lead set; verified normal waveform acquisition."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Electrodes replaced
  • Lead wires swapped
  • Cable condition verified
  • USB/module connection checked
  • Presence of electrical interference
  • Patient movement observed
  • Final waveform quality confirmed
  • Device returned to service or removed

Final Thought

Effective ECG troubleshooting starts with the basics—skin prep, electrodes, and cables—before assuming device failure. Patient safety and clean signal acquisition are always the priority. Proper escalation and clear documentation ensure reliability and accountability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
