Drager Evita V500

Alarm: PPEAK high Peak airway pressure exceeds limit

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

Alarm: PPEAK high indicating excessive airway pressure, which can prevent proper ventilation, indicate obstruction or resistance, and pose risk of lung injury or patient harm.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s respiratory status
  • If ventilation is compromised, remove from ventilator
  • Provide alternate ventilation (bag-valve mask or backup ventilator)

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm “PPEAK high” alarm is displayed
  • Observe when alarm occurs (startup, inspiration, continuous)
  • Review pressure waveform for elevated peak pressures
  • Check for audible alarms or ventilation interruption

Check Patient Condition

  • Assess for coughing, agitation, or asynchrony
  • Evaluate for bronchospasm or secretion buildup
  • Verify airway patency

Inspect the Breathing Circuit

  • Check for kinks, compression, or water accumulation
  • Ensure all tubing connections are secure
  • Verify filters are not clogged

Check Endotracheal Tube or Airway Interface

  • Inspect for mucus plugs or secretions
  • Suction if necessary
  • Verify correct placement and patency

Review Ventilator Settings

  • Check tidal volume and inspiratory pressure limits
  • Review flow and pressure settings
  • Ensure settings match patient condition and orders

Isolate Device vs External Cause

  • Replace breathing circuit with known-good components
  • Connect ventilator to a test lung
  • Observe if pressures normalize

Stop When Resolved

  • Clear alarm once condition is corrected
  • Confirm stable pressure readings
  • Return device to service if functioning normally

If the Problem Persists

If high peak pressure continues after external causes are ruled out, this may indicate an internal ventilator issue such as pressure sensing or control malfunction.

  • Remove device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot high airway pressure alarms on an active patient. Always move the patient to a backup ventilator or manual ventilation before investigating equipment.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming PPEAK high with elevated airway pressures during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Kinked breathing circuit causing increased resistance and elevated peak pressures."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced circuit and verified normal pressure readings. Alarm cleared and device returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

High airway pressure alarms demand immediate attention and systematic troubleshooting. Always rule out patient and circuit causes first before suspecting device malfunction, and escalate appropriately when needed.

That is successful troubleshooting.
