Smiths Medical Level 1 Series H-1000 / H-1200

Power Supply or Heater Plate Failure

Asset Type

Fluid & Blood Warmer

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Level 1 Series H-1000 / H-1200

What This Guide Helps With

Device not powering on, intermittent shutdowns, or no heat output despite correct setup, often related to power input, connections, or external factors.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from active patient use and transition to an alternate warming method.
  • Expected: No patient connected during troubleshooting.

Verify Power Source

  • Check that the unit is plugged into a known-good hospital-grade outlet. Test the outlet with another device if needed.
  • Expected: Outlet provides consistent power.
  • If outlet is faulty → move to a working outlet and retest.

Inspect Power Cord and Plug

  • Examine the power cord for damage, loose connections, or bent prongs. Ensure it is fully seated in the unit.
  • Why it matters: Damaged cords can mimic internal power failure.
  • If damaged → replace cord and retest.

Check Power Switch and Indicators

  • Turn the unit on and observe for any lights, display activity, or fan noise.
  • Expected: Normal startup indicators present.
  • If no response → continue troubleshooting.

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Unplug the unit, wait 15–30 seconds, then reconnect and power on.
  • Why it matters: Resets internal protection circuits.
  • If unit powers on normally → issue resolved.

Verify Circuit Breaker / Internal Protection (External Check Only)

  • If accessible externally, check for a resettable breaker or fuse indicator.
  • Expected: No tripped condition.
  • If reset restores function → monitor for recurrence.

Confirm Proper Cassette Installation

  • Ensure a compatible Level 1 cassette is fully inserted and latched.
  • Why it matters: Some units will not enable heating without proper cassette detection.
  • If reseating restores heating → issue resolved.

Check for Over-Temperature or Prior Alarm History

  • Determine if the unit recently shut down due to an alarm condition.
  • Why it matters: Safety shutdowns can disable heating temporarily.
  • If resolved after reset → monitor for repeat faults.

Assess Heater Function

  • With proper setup, confirm whether the unit attempts to heat (temperature rising or heater indicator active).
  • Expected: Temperature should begin increasing.
  • If powered but no heating occurs → possible heater plate issue.

Inspect for Signs of Physical Damage or Overheating

  • Check for unusual smells, discoloration, or excessive heat from the housing.
  • Why it matters: Indicates internal component failure.
  • If present → remove from service immediately.

If the Problem Persists

If the unit does not power on or fails to heat after verifying power source, cord, setup, and external conditions, internal failure is likely (power supply or heater plate).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a fluid warmer while connected to a patient. Always transfer to a backup warming method before evaluating power or heating issues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Unit would not power on / no heat output reported by OR staff."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Failed internal power supply or heater plate suspected after verifying outlet, cord, and setup."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed from service, labeled out of service, and sent to Biomed/vendor for repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Power cord condition checked
  • Indicator lights/display response
  • Cassette installed and verified
  • Alarm history (if present)
  • Any unusual smell, heat, or noise
  • Final device status (in service vs removed)

Final Thought

Power and heating failures should always be approached by ruling out external causes first. Once those are eliminated, escalation is the correct and safe decision. Proper documentation ensures clarity and supports repair efficiency.

That is successful troubleshooting.
