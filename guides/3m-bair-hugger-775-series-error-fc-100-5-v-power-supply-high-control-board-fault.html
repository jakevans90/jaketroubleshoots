3M Bair Hugger 775 Series

Error: FC-100 5 V Power Supply High / Control Board Fault

Asset Type

Patient Warming System

Manufacturer

3M

Model

Bair Hugger 775 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Error FC-100 on the 3M Bair Hugger 775 Series when the 5 V power supply is reported above 5.333 V. This condition may indicate a defective control board and can affect safe warming system operation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

Remove the warming unit from patient use if Error FC-100 is active or recurring.

  • Stop use of the Bair Hugger 775 if it is connected to a patient.
  • Have clinical staff switch to another warming unit if active warming is required.
  • Do not continue patient warming with a unit reporting an internal power supply fault.
  • Label the unit for Clinical Engineering evaluation.

Expected outcome: Patient warming continues safely using another device if needed.

Why it matters: A power supply voltage error may affect temperature control, alarms, display function, or safe system operation.

2. Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

Confirm the exact error code and how the unit behaves.

  • Power the unit on in a safe bench area.
  • Confirm Error FC-100 is displayed.
  • Note whether the error appears immediately at startup, during warm-up, or intermittently.
  • Check whether the unit allows blower operation, heat selection, or temperature control.
  • Listen for abnormal fan behavior, repeated resets, clicking, odor, or other signs of electrical failure.

Expected outcome: Error FC-100 is reproducible or clearly documented.

Why it matters: This confirms a true system-reported fault instead of a user complaint, setup issue, or unrelated warming problem.

3. Check Power Source First

Verify the unit is connected to a proper AC power source.

  • Plug the unit directly into a known-good hospital-grade outlet.
  • Avoid using power strips, extension cords, or questionable receptacles.
  • Confirm the outlet provides stable power.
  • Check the power cord for cuts, looseness, bent blades, or damage.

Expected outcome: The unit powers normally without external power issues.

Why it matters: External power problems should be ruled out before assuming the warming unit has an internal failure.

4. Inspect the Unit Externally

Check for obvious physical or environmental causes.

  • Inspect the housing for cracks, impact damage, missing screws, or signs of liquid intrusion.
  • Check the air inlet and filter area for blockage or contamination.
  • Confirm the hose is attached correctly and not damaged.
  • Look for signs of overheating, discoloration, burning smell, or internal rattling.

Expected outcome: No external damage or contamination is found that would explain abnormal operation.

Why it matters: Physical damage or liquid intrusion can cause electrical faults and may change whether the unit is safe to handle or repair.

5. Power Cycle Once in a Controlled Bench Setting

Perform one controlled restart only.

  • Turn the unit off.
  • Disconnect AC power.
  • Wait briefly.
  • Reconnect to a known-good outlet.
  • Power the unit back on and observe whether FC-100 returns.

Expected outcome: The unit either clears the error and operates normally, or FC-100 returns.

Why it matters: A one-time fault may clear, but a recurring FC-100 should be treated as a real internal fault.

6. Determine Whether the Error Is Persistent

Evaluate whether the unit can safely continue troubleshooting.

  • If FC-100 does not return, continue functional checks according to department procedure before returning to service.
  • If FC-100 returns, do not continue repeated startup attempts.
  • Document when the error appears and any associated symptoms.

Expected outcome: The error status is clearly identified as resolved or persistent.

Why it matters: Repeated power cycling does not repair an internal voltage fault and may create additional risk.

7. Isolate the Likely Failure

Based on the reported error explanation, FC-100 indicates the 5 V power supply is above 5.333 V and troubleshooting identifies the control board as defective.

  • Confirm external power, cord condition, and visible damage have been ruled out.
  • Treat recurring FC-100 as a device-level internal fault.
  • Do not return the unit to patient use with this error active.
  • Escalate for control board replacement according to service procedure and facility policy.

Expected outcome: The fault is isolated to a likely defective control board after simple external causes are ruled out.

Why it matters: The error is not a blanket user setup problem. It points toward an internal power/control issue.

8. Corrective Action After Repair

After control board replacement, the unit must be verified before return to service.

  • Replace the control board according to approved service procedure.
  • Perform required calibration.
  • Complete functional testing.
  • Verify the unit powers on without Error FC-100.
  • Confirm blower operation, temperature selection, alarms, and normal operation.
  • Perform any required electrical safety testing per facility procedure after internal repair.

Expected outcome: FC-100 no longer appears, calibration passes, and the unit operates normally.

Why it matters: Control board replacement alone is not enough. The unit must be calibrated and verified before clinical use.

9. Stop When Resolved

If the control board replacement, calibration, and post-repair testing resolve FC-100, complete final documentation and return-to-service steps.

  • Document the repair.
  • Remove the Out of Service label.
  • Return the unit to service only after all required checks pass.

Expected outcome: The Bair Hugger 775 is safe and ready for clinical use.

If the Problem Persists

If Error FC-100 remains after external checks, control board replacement, calibration, and post-repair testing, remove the unit from service.

At that point, external and common causes have been ruled out, and the device continues reporting an internal voltage-related fault. Further bench evaluation or vendor repair is appropriate.

The unit should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or deeper bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a patient warming system on an active patient when an internal power supply error is present. Move the patient to a backup warming device first, and keep the affected device out of service until the error is corrected, calibrated, and verified.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Bair Hugger 775 removed from service for Error FC-100. Unit reports 5 V power supply voltage in excess of 5.333 V and is not cleared for patient warming use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External power source, power cord, and visible external condition checked. Error FC-100 persisted during bench startup. Fault indicates likely defective control board."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed unit from service. Replaced control board per service procedure, completed calibration, verified normal startup and operation, and returned unit to service after post-repair testing passed."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Error FC-100 should be treated as an internal power/control fault after basic external checks are completed. Patient safety comes first, and the device should not be used clinically with an unresolved voltage error. Logical troubleshooting means confirming the error, ruling out simple causes, replacing the defective control board when indicated, calibrating, verifying operation, and documenting the repair clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
