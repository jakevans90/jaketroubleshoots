Drager Evita V500

Alarm: VE TOT high — minute volume above set limit

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses the “VE TOT high” alarm, indicating that the measured minute volume exceeds the configured alarm limit. This condition may be caused by patient hyperventilation, incorrect ventilator settings, or measurement inaccuracies, and can lead to overventilation if not properly evaluated.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s respiratory status
  • Confirm adequate oxygenation and ventilation
  • If ventilation is unstable, switch to a backup ventilator or manual ventilation

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm “VE TOT high” is displayed on the ventilator
  • Observe whether the alarm is continuous or intermittent
  • Review displayed values for respiratory rate and tidal volume

Check Alarm Limits and Ventilation Settings

  • Review minute volume (VE) alarm limits
  • Compare set tidal volume (VT) and respiratory rate (RR)
  • Adjust alarm thresholds based on patient condition

Assess Patient Contribution

  • Observe for rapid or labored breathing
  • Check for agitation, pain, or anxiety
  • Confirm ventilation mode (spontaneous vs mandatory)

Check for Circuit or Measurement Issues

  • Inspect the breathing circuit for leaks or loose connections
  • Verify the flow sensor is properly connected and clean
  • Check for condensation or obstruction in sensor lines

Perform System Self Test (SST)

  • Run the ventilator System Self Test
  • Verify the system completes without errors

Perform Extended System Test (EST) if Needed

  • Run the Extended System Test if issues persist
  • Confirm no faults are detected

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm continues after external causes and patient-related factors have been ruled out, this may indicate an internal measurement or control issue.

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label the device Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilator alarms while the device is connected to a patient. Always transfer the patient to a backup ventilator before making adjustments.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming VE TOT high with elevated minute volume readings during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Minute volume exceeded alarm limits due to high patient respiratory rate and low alarm threshold setting."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Adjusted alarm limits, assessed patient condition, verified circuit and sensor function, and returned device to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

High minute volume alarms are often related to patient condition or configuration settings rather than device failure. Always evaluate the patient first, then confirm settings and measurement accuracy before escalating.
