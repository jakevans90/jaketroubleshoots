Midmark Ritter 75L / 75LE Series

Backrest Not Adjusting or Drifting

Asset Type

Exam Table

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

Ritter 75L / 75LE Series

What This Guide Helps With

Backrest not moving, slow movement, or drifting down due to control, power, or external mechanical causes.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Remove the table from active patient use before troubleshooting.

  • Expected: Patient safely relocated.
  • Why it matters: Uncontrolled backrest movement can lead to patient injury.

Verify the Reported Issue

Operate the backrest using the hand control or foot control. Observe for no movement, delayed movement, or drifting after release.

  • Expected: Clear confirmation of failure mode (no movement vs drifting).
  • Why it matters: Helps determine if the issue is control-related or mechanical.

Check Power to the Table

Confirm the table is plugged into a working outlet. Test outlet with another device.

  • Expected: Consistent power available.
  • Why it matters: Low or unstable power can cause weak or inconsistent actuator performance.
  • If no power → restore and retest
  • If power is stable → continue

Inspect Hand/Foot Control Function

Check all buttons on the control. Confirm other functions (hi-lo) operate normally.

  • Expected: Other functions respond correctly.
  • Why it matters: If other movements work, the issue may be isolated to the backrest control or actuator.
  • If no functions work → likely power/control issue
  • If only backrest fails → continue

Inspect Control Cable and Connections

Check the hand/foot control cable for damage, pinching, or loose connections at the table base.

  • Expected: Cable intact and securely connected.
  • Why it matters: Damaged or loose cables can cause intermittent or failed commands.
  • If damaged → replace control
  • If secure → continue

Check for Obstruction or Binding

Inspect the backrest hinge area and surrounding frame for debris, misalignment, or mechanical interference.

  • Expected: Smooth, unobstructed movement path.
  • Why it matters: Physical obstruction can prevent movement or cause uneven load leading to drift.
  • If obstruction found → remove and retest

Assess Load and Weight Distribution

Ensure weight is within limits and evenly distributed on the backrest.

  • Expected: Normal operation under proper load.
  • Why it matters: Excess or uneven weight can cause actuator strain or back-driving (drifting).
  • If overloaded → correct and retest

Listen for Actuator Operation

Operate the backrest and listen for motor noise.

  • Motor runs but no movement: Possible mechanical linkage or actuator failure
  • No sound: Possible control or actuator failure
  • Expected: Audible motor operation with smooth movement.
  • Why it matters: Helps differentiate electrical vs mechanical issue.

Check for Backrest Drift After Movement Stops

Raise the backrest and release the control. Observe if it slowly lowers.

  • Expected: Backrest holds position.
  • Why it matters: Drifting typically indicates actuator internal failure or inability to hold load.
  • If drifting persists → likely actuator issue

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes (power, controls, cables, obstructions, and loading) have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal to the actuator or control system.

Remove the table from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and ensures proper repair.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot backrest movement with a patient on the table. Always transfer the patient first to prevent injury from unexpected movement.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Backrest not adjusting and drifting down during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Backrest actuator unable to hold position after ruling out power, control, and mechanical obstruction issues."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed from service and sent for actuator replacement/repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Other table functions operational or not
  • Control type used (hand vs foot)
  • Audible actuator noise present or absent
  • Presence of drift under load
  • Any visible obstruction or binding
  • Final device status (in service vs out of service)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple external checks and progresses logically. Prioritizing patient safety and recognizing likely actuator failure helps avoid unnecessary downtime and risk. Proper documentation ensures clear communication and supports efficient repair.

That is successful troubleshooting.
