GE Healthcare CARESCAPE R850 Ventilator

Internal Battery Failure

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE R860

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the CARESCAPE R860 reports an internal battery failure. Symptoms may include alarms indicating battery fault, failure to retain settings during mains power loss, inability to operate on battery power, or unexpected shutdowns. Most issues are related to battery degradation, improper charging, or external power issues rather than immediate internal hardware failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not rely on the ventilator’s battery during patient care if a fault is present.
  • Move the patient to another ventilator with a reliable power source.

Check mains power connection

  • Ensure the ventilator is plugged into a known-good outlet.
  • Verify the outlet is supplying correct voltage.
  • Why: The internal battery may appear failed if mains power has been unstable or absent.

Inspect battery connections

  • Check that the battery connector is fully seated and undamaged.
  • Inspect for corrosion, loose pins, or debris.
  • Why: Loose or poor connections can trigger battery failure alerts.

Verify battery status on display

  • Access the system menu to check the reported battery charge level and health.
  • Note any warnings or error codes.
  • Why: Confirms whether the failure is persistent or intermittent.

Perform a battery charge cycle

  • Leave the ventilator connected to mains power for a full charge cycle (per manufacturer recommendations).
  • Observe if the battery fault clears after charging.
  • Why: Some internal batteries may reset fault status after a complete charge.

Swap with known-good battery (if available)

  • If the device supports battery replacement, install a tested battery.
  • Check if the device operates normally on battery power.
  • Why: Confirms whether the internal battery itself is the root cause.

Check for software updates or system resets

  • Some battery alerts may be resolved by system firmware updates or soft resets.
  • Follow proper procedures to avoid data loss.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out.

The internal battery is likely defective or beyond serviceable life.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service – Battery Fault.”
  • Send to authorized repair or bench evaluation.

Note: Stopping at this point is proper troubleshooting; do not attempt internal repair beyond approved procedures.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to another ventilator first. Only troubleshoot or charge the device when it is safe and not connected to an active patient. Therapy continuity and safety must always be prioritized.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reported internal battery failure alert; device may not operate on battery power.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Battery health check indicated degraded or failed internal battery; charging did not resolve fault; connections verified.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Device removed from service, labeled ‘Out of Service – Battery Fault,’ and sent to authorized service for battery replacement or evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested
  • Battery connector inspected
  • Charge cycle attempted
  • Error codes noted: E.g., Battery Fault, Low Capacity
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting balances patient safety, logical assessment, and appropriate escalation. Document all steps carefully to support repair decisions and prevent patient risk. Batteries have limited life; knowing when to remove the device is critical.

That is successful troubleshooting.
