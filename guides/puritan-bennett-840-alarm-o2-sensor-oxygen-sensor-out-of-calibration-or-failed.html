Puritan Bennett 840

Alarm: O2 SENSOR Oxygen sensor out of calibration or failed

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Puritan Bennett

Model

840

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses the Alarm: O2 SENSOR Oxygen sensor out of calibration or failed, which indicates inaccurate oxygen readings or loss of FiO₂ monitoring. This condition can impact safe ventilation delivery and patient oxygen management.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess patient stability and oxygenation status
  • Confirm adequate ventilation is being delivered
  • Use alternate monitoring (SpO₂ or external analyzer) if needed
  • Prepare backup ventilation if oxygen delivery is uncertain

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the O2 SENSOR alarm is displayed on the ventilator
  • Observe if alarm occurs during startup, calibration, or operation
  • Check for drifting, inaccurate, or missing FiO₂ readings
  • Look for calibration failure messages

3. Perform O2 Sensor Calibration (Most Common Cause)

  • Initiate calibration using room air or 100% oxygen per device procedure
  • Follow on-screen prompts carefully
  • Ensure gas source remains stable during calibration

4. Check Oxygen Supply Source and Stability

  • Verify wall oxygen supply is connected and pressurized
  • Inspect oxygen hose for kinks or loose connections
  • Confirm no fluctuations in oxygen supply pressure

5. Inspect Sensor Connection and Physical Condition

  • Ensure the oxygen sensor is properly seated
  • Inspect for moisture, contamination, or damage
  • Reseat or reconnect the sensor if needed

6. Determine if Sensor Has Reached End of Life

  • Review maintenance or replacement history if available
  • Replace the sensor with a known-good unit if calibration fails
  • Repeat calibration after replacement

7. Optional: Disable O2 Sensor (If Clinically Appropriate)

  • Follow device procedure to disable oxygen monitoring
  • Notify clinical staff that FiO₂ monitoring is inactive
  • Use external monitoring methods if needed

If the Problem Persists

If calibration fails, connections are verified, and a known-good sensor does not resolve the issue, internal failure is likely.

Remove the ventilator from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Always move the patient to a backup ventilator before performing troubleshooting steps.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator displaying O2 SENSOR alarm with inaccurate FiO₂ readings during operation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Oxygen sensor failed calibration and showed unstable readings, indicating sensor degradation."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced oxygen sensor, completed successful calibration, and verified accurate FiO₂ readings."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Oxygen sensor alarms directly impact patient safety and monitoring accuracy. Always prioritize calibration and external checks first, but recognize when the sensor must be replaced or the device escalated for repair. Clear documentation ensures reliability and traceability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
