GE Healthcare CARESCAPE VC150

SpO2 Probe Not Detected

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE VC150

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the CARESCAPE VC150 does not detect the SpO2 probe. Symptoms may include no SpO2 waveform, “probe not detected” messages, or no response when a sensor is connected. The focus is on external connections, accessories, and setup before assuming internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Confirm Patient Safety First

  • Ensure the patient is moved to another monitor if SpO2 monitoring is required.
  • Do not troubleshoot while actively connected to a patient.

Verify Proper Probe Connection

  • Ensure the SpO2 probe is fully inserted into the correct SpO2 port.
  • Reseat the connector firmly—loose connections are a common cause.

Inspect the SpO2 Probe and Cable

  • Check for visible damage such as frayed wires, bent pins, or cracked housing.
  • Look for contamination (fluids, debris) in the connector.
  • Damaged probes will not be recognized by the monitor.

Check for Correct Probe Type

  • Confirm the probe is compatible with the VC150 (GE-compatible SpO2 sensor).
  • Using the wrong manufacturer or unsupported probe may result in “not detected.”

Swap with a Known Good Probe

  • Replace the probe with a verified working SpO2 sensor.
  • If the new probe works, the original probe is faulty.

Inspect the Extension Cable (if used)

  • If an extension cable is in use, remove it and connect the probe directly.
  • Swap the extension cable with a known good one if needed.

Check the SpO2 Port on the Monitor

  • Inspect the port for bent pins, debris, or looseness.
  • Gently clean if necessary using approved methods.
  • A damaged port can prevent probe detection.

Power Cycle the Monitor

  • Turn the unit off, wait 10–15 seconds, then power it back on.
  • This can reset communication with connected modules.

Observe Monitor Behavior

  • Check for any error messages or alarms related to SpO2.
  • Verify if the monitor detects connection intermittently (may indicate a loose port or cable issue).

If the Problem Persists

If all external components (probe, cable, connections) have been verified or replaced and the monitor still does not detect the SpO2 probe, the issue is likely internal (SpO2 module or port failure).

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Recognizing when the issue is beyond external troubleshooting is proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

SpO2 monitoring is critical for patient oxygenation status. Always transfer the patient to a functioning monitor before troubleshooting to avoid gaps in monitoring.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“SpO2 probe not detected; no waveform or reading displayed.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Faulty SpO2 probe cable identified after testing with known good probe restored function.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced defective SpO2 probe and verified proper operation with stable waveform and readings.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Probe condition (damaged, intact)
  • Cable/extension use
  • Port condition (clean, bent pins, loose)
  • Results of probe swap
  • Alarm or error messages observed
  • Final device status (working or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple, external checks—connections, accessories, and compatibility. Many SpO2 issues are probe-related rather than device failures. Always prioritize patient safety, and escalate appropriately when internal faults are suspected. Clear documentation ensures continuity and supports future repairs.

That is successful troubleshooting.
