Philips IntelliVue MP Series

Alarm: WIRELESS OUT OF RANGE

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

IntelliVue MP Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the WIRELESS OUT OF RANGE alarm on a Philips IntelliVue MP Series patient monitor. This condition may occur when wireless central monitoring is lost because the monitor is outside wireless coverage, cannot associate with an access point, or is affected by access point or network connectivity issues.

The bedside monitor may still display local patient data, but the central station may no longer receive patient values, waveforms, or alarms. Patient safety and monitoring continuity must be protected before troubleshooting wireless communication.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the patient is not relying on the wireless IntelliVue MP Series monitor as the only active source of monitoring.
  • If central monitoring is required, have clinical staff move the patient to a monitored area, reconnect to a wired network if available, or use another verified monitor.
  • Do not disconnect, reboot, or move the monitor until alternate monitoring is in place.
  • Expected: Patient monitoring continues safely while the wireless issue is evaluated.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the monitor displays the exact alarm text: WIRELESS OUT OF RANGE.
  • Determine whether the alarm occurs only in a specific room or hallway, during patient transport, after moving away from the unit, intermittently while stationary, or after network or access point changes.
  • Check whether the bedside monitor still displays local patient data.
  • Confirm whether the central station has lost communication with the monitor.
  • Expected: The alarm is confirmed and linked to wireless communication loss.

Confirm the Monitor Is in an Approved Wireless Coverage Area

  • Move the monitor back toward the clinical area where wireless monitoring normally works.
  • Watch for the WIRELESS OUT OF RANGE message to clear.
  • Verify that the monitor reconnects to the central station after returning to the approved coverage area.
  • Expected: The monitor reconnects once it is back inside known wireless coverage.

Compare With Another Wireless Monitor in the Same Area

  • Place a known working wireless IntelliVue monitor in the same location.
  • Check whether the second monitor also loses wireless communication or remains connected.
  • If both monitors fail, suspect wireless coverage, access point, VLAN, or network configuration issues.
  • If only the original monitor fails, continue isolating the device and wireless configuration.
  • Expected: Testing another wireless monitor separates an area-wide wireless issue from a monitor-specific issue.

Check for Recent Location or Workflow Changes

  • Ask staff where the monitor was being used when the message appeared.
  • Confirm whether the monitor was moved to an elevator area, imaging department, hallway with poor coverage, temporary care area, area outside the configured wireless network, or recently remodeled room.
  • Check whether the alarm correlates with patient transport or movement away from the normal unit.
  • Expected: The alarm correlates with movement into a weak or unsupported wireless area.

Check the Wireless Signal Status at the Monitor

  • Review network or wireless status indicators if available through normal user-accessible menus.
  • Look for weak signal, no access point association, loss of network connection, or failure to reconnect.
  • Do not enter service-level settings unless authorized by facility policy and manufacturer guidance.
  • Expected: Wireless status supports the reported out-of-range condition.

Power Cycle Only After Patient Monitoring Is Protected

  • If the monitor remains out of range after returning to a known coverage area, remove it from active patient use or ensure alternate monitoring is in place.
  • Power cycle the monitor and allow it to attempt wireless reconnection.
  • Do not reboot the monitor while a patient depends on it for active monitoring.
  • Expected: The monitor reconnects to wireless and central monitoring if the issue was a temporary communication state.

Verify Central Station Communication

  • Confirm with clinical staff or central monitoring that the monitor is visible again at the central station.
  • Check that patient data, alarms, and waveforms are transmitting as expected.
  • Verify local bedside monitoring and central monitoring are both functioning before returning the device to service.
  • Expected: The central station receives the monitor normally after wireless reconnection.

Check for Access Point or Network Area Problems

  • If multiple monitors report wireless loss in the same area, escalate to the network or clinical engineering team responsible for wireless infrastructure.
  • Verify whether the access point is powered, online, correctly configured, and providing coverage for the intended clinical area.
  • Ask whether recent network, access point, VLAN, or construction changes occurred in the affected area.
  • Expected: Area-wide failures point toward access point coverage, configuration, or network infrastructure.

Inspect the Monitor for Physical or Configuration Concerns

  • Check the monitor for physical damage, missing wireless hardware indicators if applicable, incorrect assigned care area, or recent configuration changes.
  • Confirm the device is approved and configured for the wireless network being used.
  • Check whether the issue follows the monitor when it is moved to another known working wireless area.
  • Expected: No obvious device damage or configuration mismatch is found.

Stop When Resolved

  • If moving the monitor into coverage, correcting the care area, restoring access point coverage, or rebooting resolves the alarm, verify central monitoring communication before returning the device to service.
  • Document the confirmed cause and final status.
  • Expected: The alarm is cleared, central monitoring is verified, and the device status is clear.

If the Problem Persists

If WIRELESS OUT OF RANGE remains present after the monitor is returned to a known wireless coverage area, compared against another working monitor, and basic network coverage checks are completed, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent wireless loss may indicate a monitor wireless configuration issue, wireless hardware problem, access point or network configuration problem, or central communication issue.

Remove the device from service if it cannot reliably communicate with central monitoring where required.

Label the monitor Out of Service and send it for repair, bench evaluation, or network escalation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot wireless central monitoring loss on an active patient without ensuring alternate monitoring is in place. Move the patient to a backup device, wired network connection, or approved monitored area first.

A bedside monitor may still display values locally while the central station is no longer receiving patient data or alarms. Maintain therapy and monitoring continuity before troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Philips IntelliVue MP Series monitor displayed WIRELESS OUT OF RANGE while assigned to central monitoring. Central station lost contact with the wireless monitor after it was moved away from normal coverage."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Monitor was outside reliable wireless access point coverage. Wireless communication recovered when the monitor was returned to the approved monitored area. No device hardware failure found during basic checks."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Moved monitor back into wireless coverage, verified alarm cleared, confirmed monitor reconnected to central station, and confirmed patient data and alarm communication restored. Device returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Exact alarm behavior, including whether WIRELESS OUT OF RANGE was constant or intermittent
  • Accessories swapped, including network cable, docking connection, or alternate monitor comparison if used
  • Power behavior, including whether the monitor was rebooted and whether reconnection occurred afterward
  • Environmental factors, including room, hallway, department, transport route, remodeled area, or known poor wireless coverage zone
  • Indicator lights or wireless status indications, including weak signal, no association, or central station disconnect
  • Final device status, including returned to service, Out of Service, sent for bench evaluation, or escalated for network review

Final Thought

Wireless alarm troubleshooting should protect the patient first, then separate location coverage issues from monitor-specific failures. WIRELESS OUT OF RANGE usually points to loss of access point contact, but repeated failure in known coverage areas should be escalated appropriately. Clear CCR documentation helps distinguish wireless infrastructure problems from device repair needs.

That is successful troubleshooting.
