Vyaire VELA Ventilator

Alarm Not Triggering or Misconfigured

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Vyaire

Model

VELA

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a Vyaire VELA ventilator fails to trigger alarms correctly or alarms appear misconfigured. Symptoms may include missing or delayed alarms, no audible or visual alert for high/low pressures, apnea, or disconnection warnings. The focus is on external checks, proper setup, and ensuring safety before considering internal repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Device Power and Battery

  • Ensure the ventilator is powered on and receiving adequate AC power or battery charge.
  • Reason: Alarm circuits may not function correctly if power is unstable or insufficient.

Check Alarm Settings

  • Review alarm limits and parameters on the touchscreen or control panel.
  • Confirm that alarms are enabled and thresholds are appropriate for the patient.
  • Reason: Misconfigured settings are a common cause of alarms not triggering.

Inspect Alarm Indicators

  • Confirm audible alarm volume is turned up and the visual indicators (LEDs or screen notifications) are functional.
  • Reason: Sometimes the alarm triggers internally but the user cannot perceive it.

Verify Sensor and Circuit Connections

  • Check all patient circuit connections, pressure lines, and sensors for secure attachment.
  • Reason: Disconnected or loose sensors can prevent proper alarm detection.

Perform a Self-Test / System Check

  • Run the built-in system diagnostic or alarm test mode if available.
  • Reason: Confirms internal alarm circuits and software logic are functional without risking patient safety.

Swap with a Known Good Unit

  • If possible, replace the ventilator with a tested device to ensure the issue is with the unit and not the environment or setup.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation by qualified biomedical personnel

Remember: Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not troubleshoot alarms on an active patient. Move the patient to another ventilator first.
  • Always ensure patient safety is maintained while troubleshooting external alarm issues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nurse reports ventilator fails to trigger high-pressure alarm during manual test.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Verified power, settings, and sensor connections; built-in self-test fails to sound alarm.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Ventilator removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Alarm volumes and types checked
  • Sensor/circuit connection status
  • System self-test results
  • Any unusual noises or indicator behavior

Final Thought

Troubleshooting alarms on ventilators requires methodical external checks, proper configuration, and patient safety first. Escalating to repair when internal issues are suspected protects both patients and equipment. Documentation ensures traceability and compliance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
