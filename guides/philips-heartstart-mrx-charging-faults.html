Philips HeartStart MRx

Charging Faults / Slow Charge

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

HeartStart MRx

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the HeartStart MRx battery:

  • Charges slower than expected
  • Shows intermittent charging faults
  • Does not reach full charge after normal charging time
  • Triggers “Battery Fault” or similar warnings during charge

The focus is on external, logical troubleshooting first, without opening the device or performing board-level repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check the Power Source

  • Verify the wall outlet is live using a meter or known working device.
  • Ensure there is no surge protector or extension cord causing voltage drop.
  • Reason: Weak or intermittent power can slow charging or trigger faults.

Inspect the Battery Connection

  • Remove and reseat the battery, ensuring it clicks firmly into place.
  • Check for debris, corrosion, or bent contacts.
  • Reason: Poor contact can prevent full charging.

Use a Known Good Battery

  • Swap with a battery that is known to charge correctly.
  • Observe if the charging rate improves.
  • Reason: Confirms whether the issue is with the battery or the defibrillator charger circuitry.

Check Charger / Charging Cradle

  • If using an external charging cradle, verify it is plugged in and indicator lights show normal operation.
  • Test another known working cradle if available.
  • Reason: Faulty charging hardware can slow or prevent battery charging.

Inspect for Environmental Factors

  • Ensure device and battery are at room temperature; extreme cold or heat can affect charging efficiency.
  • Reason: Batteries have optimal charging temperatures.

Review Device Logs / Alerts

  • Check for any error codes related to battery or charging (e.g., “Battery Fault,” “Charging Error”).
  • Record them for CCR documentation.
  • Reason: Provides guidance for further troubleshooting or escalation.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out; the issue is likely internal.

Action:

  • Remove the defibrillator from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service.”
  • Send for vendor repair or internal bench evaluation.
  • Note: Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt to use the defibrillator on a patient if the battery is not charging properly.
  • Use an alternate device while troubleshooting.
  • Always confirm battery capacity and device readiness before clinical use.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Battery charges very slowly and sometimes fails to charge fully.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Battery seated properly; wall outlet verified; charging cradle inspected; issue persists with multiple batteries.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service and labeled Out of Service for bench evaluation or vendor repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested (Yes/No)
  • Alternate battery tested
  • Charging cradle/charger tested
  • Battery temperature
  • Any error codes or indicator lights observed
  • Final device status: Out of Service if unresolved

Final Thought

Patient safety and device readiness are paramount. Troubleshooting must start with simple, external checks before assuming internal faults. Clear CCR documentation ensures proper follow-up and maintains hospital safety standards. Escalate promptly when external checks do not resolve the issue.

That is successful troubleshooting.
