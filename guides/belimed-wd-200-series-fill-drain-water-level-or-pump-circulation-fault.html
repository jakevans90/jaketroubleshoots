Belimed WD 200 Series

Fill, Drain, Water Level, or Pump Circulation Fault

Asset Type

Washer-Disinfector

Manufacturer

Belimed

Model

WD 200 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting fill, drain, water level, and circulation faults caused by utilities, obstructions, loading issues, or external connection problems.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure patient and staff safety first.

  • Confirm the washer-disinfector is not needed for an active instrument reprocessing workflow before troubleshooting. Do not bypass alarms or attempt to force a cycle to complete.
  • Expected outcome: The unit is safely available for troubleshooting without delaying critical sterile processing needs.
  • If this resolves the issue, stop.

Confirm the reported fault or symptom.

  • Ask SPD staff what happened: would not fill, overfilled, failed to drain, stopped mid-cycle, poor spray action, pump noise, or displayed a water-level/circulation-related fault.
  • Expected outcome: The failure pattern is clear before parts or internal faults are suspected.

Check for visible water in the chamber.

  • Open the chamber only when safe and allowed by the cycle status. Look for standing water, low water level, excessive water, or no water at all.
  • Expected outcome: The symptom matches either a fill problem, drain problem, water level sensing problem, or circulation problem.

Verify facility water supply.

  • Confirm hot and/or cold water supply valves are open, facility water pressure is available, and no nearby plumbing shutdown is occurring. Check whether other washers or sinks in the area have normal water flow.
  • Expected outcome: Adequate water supply is available to the washer.
  • If the water supply is off or weak, restore or escalate to facilities/plumbing and stop.

Inspect water inlet hoses and external connections.

  • Check for kinked, crushed, leaking, disconnected, or restricted water supply hoses. Make sure strainers or external filters are not obviously blocked if they are accessible without disassembly.
  • Expected outcome: Water can reach the washer without external restriction.
  • If a kinked hose or closed valve is corrected and the unit fills normally, stop.

Check the drain path.

  • Inspect the external drain hose, floor drain, air gap, and nearby drain plumbing for blockage, kinks, backup, or standing water.
  • Expected outcome: The washer has a clear path to drain.
  • If the drain is blocked or backing up, remove the washer from use and involve facilities/plumbing as needed.

Inspect the chamber drain screen, filters, and sump area.

  • When safe and cool, check for packaging material, labels, broken instrument parts, debris, lint, or soil blocking the chamber drain screen or sump filter area.
  • Expected outcome: Drain and recirculation flow are not restricted by debris.
  • If debris is removed and the next test cycle completes normally, stop.

Check for improper loading.

  • Verify trays, racks, instruments, basins, and accessories are not blocking spray arms, chamber drains, water level areas, or door closure.
  • Expected outcome: Water can circulate, drain, and reach all load surfaces correctly.
  • If reloading the chamber corrects the issue, document the loading-related cause and stop.

Inspect spray arms and rack connections.

  • Confirm spray arms spin freely and are not blocked, cracked, missing, or obstructed by the load. Check that rack/manifold connections are seated correctly if the unit uses rack-fed spray paths.
  • Expected outcome: Circulation flow can reach the spray arms and rack channels.
  • If a blocked spray arm or poorly seated rack is corrected and circulation returns, stop.

Listen for pump operation during a safe test cycle.

  • Run an empty test cycle only if facility policy allows and the unit is safe to operate. Listen for normal fill, drain, and circulation pump sounds. Note humming, grinding, cavitation, repeated start/stop, or silence when the pump should run.
  • Expected outcome: Pump behavior helps separate a flow restriction from a likely pump or control issue.

Check for leaks during fill and circulation.

  • Observe around the door, lower panels, hose connections, and drain area without removing covers beyond normal access.
  • Expected outcome: No external leak is present.
  • If leaking is found, remove the unit from service and escalate for repair.

Verify the door is closing and sealing properly.

  • A washer may fail to fill, circulate, or continue a cycle if the door is not fully closed, sealed, or interlocked. Check for obvious door obstruction, gasket damage, or misalignment.
  • Expected outcome: The door condition is not preventing safe cycle operation.
  • If door closure or seal problems remain, remove the unit from service.

Review the displayed fault or cycle history if available.

  • Record the exact alarm, error code, cycle phase, and time of failure. Note whether the fault occurs during fill, wash circulation, drain, rinse, or thermal disinfection.
  • Expected outcome: The failure is documented clearly enough for repair follow-up.

Repeat with an empty chamber if appropriate.

  • If no obvious external issue is found, run an empty cycle or service-approved test cycle according to facility procedure. Do not use patient-care instruments for testing.
  • Expected outcome: Confirms whether the issue follows the machine or was caused by loading/debris.
  • If the empty test cycle passes and the original load was obstructed or improperly loaded, document and stop.

Stop troubleshooting if the fault repeats.

  • Repeated fill, drain, water level, or circulation faults may involve valves, level sensing, pump components, internal tubing, controls, or heating/cycle logic. These are repair-level issues.
  • Expected outcome: The device is not repeatedly cycled in a failed state.

If the Problem Persists

If water supply, drain path, chamber filters, spray arms, loading, rack seating, and visible external issues have been ruled out, the fault is likely internal.

The washer-disinfector should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair, vendor service, or bench evaluation according to facility procedure

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Repeatedly restarting failed washer cycles can delay instrument availability and may create unsafe reprocessing conditions.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a washer-disinfector while it is needed for urgent instrument turnaround. Move the workflow to another validated washer if available. Never release instruments from a failed or incomplete cycle unless they are reprocessed according to SPD policy.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"SPD reported the Belimed WD 200 Series washer-disinfector stopped during the wash cycle with a water level/circulation fault and standing water in the chamber."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found debris obstructing the chamber drain screen and restricting drain flow during the cycle."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed debris from the drain screen, verified spray arms were clear, ran an empty test cycle, and confirmed the unit filled, circulated, and drained normally."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Exact fault message or error code displayed
  • Cycle phase where the failure occurred
  • Whether water was absent, low, high, or standing in the chamber
  • Outlet or disconnect status if electrical power was checked
  • Water supply valves verified open
  • Drain hose and floor drain checked
  • Chamber drain screen/filter condition
  • Spray arm movement checked
  • Rack/manifold seating verified
  • Any pump noise, humming, grinding, or no pump sound
  • Any unusual heat, smell, or visible leakage
  • Whether an empty test cycle passed or failed
  • Final device status: returned to service or removed from service

Final Thought

Fill, drain, water level, and circulation faults should be approached logically. Start with safety, utilities, drains, filters, loading, and visible restrictions before assuming an internal failure. If the fault repeats after basic external causes are ruled out, remove the washer-disinfector from service and document the findings clearly for repair follow-up.

That is successful troubleshooting.
