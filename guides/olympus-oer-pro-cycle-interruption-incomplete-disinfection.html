Olympus OER-Pro

Cycle Interruption or Incomplete Disinfection Cycle

Asset Type

Endoscope Reprocessor (AER)

Manufacturer

Olympus

Model

OER-Pro

What This Guide Helps With

Cycle stopping mid-process, incomplete disinfection, or aborted cycles due to door, water, chemical, or connection-related issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not attempt to troubleshoot while scopes are needed for immediate patient use.
  • Remove the unit from workflow if disinfection cannot be verified.

Expected: Backup reprocessing method or AER is used
Why it matters: Incomplete disinfection creates infection risk

Verify the Reported Issue and Cycle Behavior

  • Run or review a cycle and note when it stops.
  • Identify stage of failure (fill, wash, disinfect, rinse, dry).
  • Check for displayed alarms or messages.

Expected: Clear identification of interruption point
Why it matters: Narrows troubleshooting to specific subsystem

Check Door Closure and Seal (Most Common Cause)

  • Ensure door is fully closed and latched.
  • Inspect door gasket for debris, cracks, or moisture buildup.
  • Clean and reseat if needed.

Expected: Door closes securely with no gaps
Why it matters: Loss of seal interrupts pressure and fluid control

Verify Water Supply and Pressure

  • Confirm water supply valves are open.
  • Check inlet hoses for kinks or disconnection.
  • Verify facility water pressure is within range.

Expected: Steady water flow with no restrictions
Why it matters: AER relies on consistent water flow for all phases

Check Chemical Levels and Dosing System

  • Verify disinfectant container is not empty.
  • Ensure pickup tubes are properly seated.
  • Check for air bubbles or loose tubing.

Expected: Proper chemical level and secure connections
Why it matters: System will stop if proper concentration cannot be ensured

Inspect Scope Connections and Channel Hookups

  • Ensure all channel connectors are properly attached.
  • Verify correct adapters are used.
  • Check for loose or disconnected lines.

Expected: All channels securely connected
Why it matters: Flow verification failures will interrupt the cycle

Check Filters and Drain Path

  • Inspect and clean inlet filters if accessible.
  • Ensure drain line is not kinked or clogged.
  • Verify proper drainage during cycle.

Expected: Unrestricted flow and drainage
Why it matters: System monitors flow and pressure continuously

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn the unit off.
  • Wait 30–60 seconds.
  • Restart and attempt a new cycle.

Expected: Normal cycle operation resumes
Why it matters: Clears transient system faults

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and cycles continue to interrupt, the issue is likely internal (pump failure, sensors, valves, or control system).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents wasted time and ensures safe equipment use.

Clinical Use Tip

Never assume a partially completed cycle is safe. Always reprocess the scope using a verified working AER or approved backup method before patient use.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"AER cycle stopping mid-process and not completing disinfection."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Low disinfectant level causing cycle abort during dosing phase."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced disinfectant container, verified proper dosing, and confirmed successful full cycle completion."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Stage of cycle where interruption occurs
  • Alarm messages or error codes displayed
  • Water supply and pressure verified
  • Chemical levels checked and replaced
  • Scope connections verified
  • Drain and filters inspected
  • Unit restarted and retested
  • Final operational status

Final Thought

Cycle interruptions are most often caused by external factors like door sealing, water supply, or chemical levels. Systematically ruling these out ensures safe operation and prevents unnecessary repairs. Always prioritize patient safety and proper documentation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
