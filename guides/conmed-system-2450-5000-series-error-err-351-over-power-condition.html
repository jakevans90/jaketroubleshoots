CONMED System 2450 / 5000 Series

Error: Err 351 Over-Power Condition

Asset Type

Electrosurgical Unit (ESU)

Manufacturer

CONMED

Model

System 2450 / 5000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Err 351 on the CONMED System 2450 / 5000 Series electrosurgical unit. Err 351 indicates the monitor sensed an over-power condition for a 300 ms running average. This may affect safe cutting or coagulation delivery and should be treated as a serious RF output or sensing-related fault until proven otherwise.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the ESU from patient use if Err 351 appears during setup, activation, or clinical use.
  • Stop use of the generator.
  • Have staff switch to another ESU if electrosurgery is still needed.
  • Do not continue using the unit with an unresolved over-power fault.
  • Label the device for Clinical Engineering evaluation.

Expected outcome: Patient care continues without relying on an ESU that may deliver incorrect RF output.

Why it matters: An over-power condition can create a real risk of unintended tissue effect, burns, or unsafe energy delivery.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact displayed error is Err 351.
  • Ask when the error occurred: during power-on self-test, when activating cut, when activating coag, intermittently during use, or only with a specific accessory or mode.
  • Cycle power once and observe whether the error returns.
  • Note whether the unit allows activation or locks out operation.

Expected outcome: Err 351 is confirmed and the behavior is reproducible or clearly documented.

Why it matters: This confirms a true system-reported over-power condition instead of a user report, accessory issue, or one-time event.

Check External and Simple Causes First

  • Inspect the power cord, plug, and outlet condition.
  • Verify the ESU is connected to a proper hospital-grade outlet.
  • Check for obvious physical damage, liquid intrusion, burnt smell, overheating, or loose external connectors.
  • Inspect the active electrode, return electrode cable, footswitch, handpiece, and any connected accessories.
  • Remove questionable accessories and retest with known-good test accessories.

Expected outcome: No external damage, accessory fault, or setup issue is causing abnormal activation behavior.

Why it matters: External accessory problems can create unusual loading conditions or activation complaints that may appear as generator output issues.

Verify Settings and Load Conditions

  • Confirm the selected mode and power setting match the reported use condition.
  • Make sure the ESU is not being tested into an incorrect or unstable load.
  • Use an appropriate ESU analyzer and fixed load for output testing.
  • Avoid judging output power based only on clinical complaint or tissue effect.

Expected outcome: The test setup is controlled, repeatable, and appropriate for measuring actual ESU output power.

Why it matters: Err 351 relates to sensed output power. A controlled load is needed before deciding whether the generator is actually overpowering or sensing incorrectly.

Configure for Test Mode and Retest

  • Configure the system for test mode according to facility-approved service procedures.
  • Cycle system power.
  • Observe whether Err 351 appears during startup or only during RF activation.
  • Record the mode, power setting, load, and activation method used during testing.

Expected outcome: The fault either clears, repeats during POST/startup, or repeats during controlled RF output testing.

Why it matters: Test mode helps separate a random clinical complaint from a reproducible generator fault.

Measure Actual Output Power Into a Fixed Load

  • Connect the ESU to an appropriate ESU analyzer.
  • Use a known fixed load suitable for the mode being tested.
  • Activate the generator in the affected mode.
  • Compare measured output power against the selected setting and expected performance range.
  • Repeat in more than one mode if safe and appropriate.

Expected outcome: If measured output power is excessive, the unit likely has a true RF output regulation problem. If measured output power is correct, the fault may be related to voltage/current sensing rather than actual over-output.

Why it matters: Err 351 may indicate real excessive output power or a sensing/monitoring circuit problem.

Determine Whether the Issue Is Output-Related or Sensing-Related

  • If actual output power is high, suspect the RF output control path, HVPS, RF Amplifier, RF Transformer board, RF Output board, or Controller assembly.
  • If actual output power is normal but Err 351 still appears, suspect the Monitor voltage/current sensing circuits or RF Controller voltage/current sensing circuits.
  • Check for loose internal harness connections only as allowed by facility procedure and your service level.
  • Do not continue deep board-level troubleshooting unless trained and equipped to do so.

Expected outcome: The issue is narrowed to either excessive RF output or incorrect power sensing/feedback.

Why it matters: Correct isolation prevents replacing the wrong assembly and helps determine whether the ESU is unsafe or incorrectly reporting power.

Stop When Resolved

  • If the error clears after correcting an external issue, accessory issue, setup issue, or test configuration problem, retest output using the ESU analyzer.
  • Confirm the unit passes required performance and safety checks.
  • Document the correction clearly.
  • Return the ESU to service only after it operates normally and Err 351 does not return.

Expected outcome: The ESU completes testing without Err 351 and output power is verified.

If the Problem Persists

If Err 351 persists after external checks, controlled load testing, accessory checks, and power cycling, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent Err 351 strongly indicates an internal RF output regulation or voltage/current sensing failure.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Likely repair areas may include the Monitor voltage/current sensing circuits, RF Controller voltage/current sensing circuits, HVPS, RF Amplifier, RF Transformer board, RF Output board, or Controller assembly.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. An ESU with a possible over-power fault should not be returned to patient use without verified output testing and repair.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot an over-power ESU fault on an active patient. If electrosurgery is needed, move the patient to a backup device first by having staff switch to another verified generator. Never rely on an ESU with an unresolved over-power error.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"CONMED System 2450 / 5000 Series ESU reported Err 351 over-power condition during activation. Unit removed from service for Clinical Engineering evaluation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Output power measured into fixed test load was within expected range, but Err 351 returned during controlled testing. Monitor or RF Controller voltage/current sensing fault suspected."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ESU from service and labeled Out of Service. Sent for bench repair evaluation of monitor sensing, RF Controller sensing, HVPS, RF Amplifier, RF Transformer board, RF Output board, and Controller assembly."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Err 351 should be treated seriously because it involves possible excessive RF output power. Start with patient safety, confirm the exact error, rule out external causes, then verify actual output with a controlled ESU analyzer setup. If the error persists or output is abnormal, escalate for internal repair rather than guessing. Clear documentation protects the patient, the technician, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
