Philips IntelliVue MP Series

Alarm: CO2 EQUIP MALF

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

IntelliVue MP Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot CO2 EQUIP MALF INOPs on Philips IntelliVue MP Series patient monitors when CO2 monitoring is unavailable due to a possible CO2 module, MMS, parameter device, accessory, connection, or monitor-side interface fault.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the IntelliVue MP monitor is not the only active source of respiratory or ventilation monitoring before troubleshooting the CO2 parameter.
  • If the patient requires continuous CO2 monitoring, have clinical staff transfer CO2 monitoring to another working monitor, module, or approved alternate device first.
  • Expected: Patient monitoring remains active and safe.
  • Why it matters: A CO2 equipment malfunction can remove an important ventilation monitoring parameter.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact INOP displayed is CO2 EQUIP MALF.
  • Observe whether the INOP appears at startup, after connecting the CO2 module, during monitoring, intermittently, or only with a specific module or MMS.
  • Check whether the CO2 waveform, numeric value, and related alarms are missing or unavailable.
  • Expected: The CO2 EQUIP MALF INOP is confirmed and its behavior is documented.
  • Why it matters: This confirms a true parameter equipment malfunction instead of a simple setup or accessory issue.

Check the Patient Monitor Status

  • Verify the IntelliVue MP monitor powers on normally and other parameters function correctly.
  • Check ECG, SpO2, NIBP, or other available parameters to confirm whether the issue is isolated to CO2 or affects multiple functions.
  • Expected: Other monitor functions operate normally.
  • Why it matters: If multiple parameters are failing, the problem may involve the monitor, MMS, rack, or power and communication path rather than only CO2.

Check the CO2 Module or MMS Connection

  • Remove the CO2 module, MMS, or parameter device from the monitor connection point and reconnect it securely.
  • Inspect the module seating, connector alignment, and locking mechanism.
  • Do not force the module into place.
  • Expected: Module seats properly and the INOP clears after reconnection.
  • Why it matters: Poor seating or incomplete connection can appear as a parameter equipment malfunction.

Inspect External Physical Condition

  • Check the CO2 module, MMS, connector, and cable path for cracked housing, bent or damaged pins, liquid contamination, drop damage, loose connector fit, missing labels, or obvious abuse.
  • Expected: No visible damage or contamination is found.
  • Why it matters: Physical damage can cause the monitor to report a true equipment malfunction.

Check CO2 Accessories Before Replacing Hardware

  • Verify that the airway adapter, sampling line, FilterLine, or CO2 accessory is compatible, connected correctly, and not visibly damaged or contaminated.
  • Replace the external CO2 accessory with a known-good compatible accessory if available.
  • Expected: CO2 accessory replacement clears accessory-related problems.
  • Why it matters: Although CO2 EQUIP MALF points toward equipment failure, external accessories should still be ruled out first.

Power Cycle the Monitor Safely

  • Remove the device from patient use before power cycling.
  • Turn the monitor off, disconnect external power if appropriate, wait briefly, then power the monitor back on and reconnect the CO2 module or MMS.
  • Expected: Monitor restarts normally and the CO2 EQUIP MALF INOP clears.
  • Why it matters: A temporary parameter communication fault may clear after a controlled restart.

Test the CO2 Parameter in Another Compatible Device

  • Move the suspect CO2 module, MMS, or indicated parameter device to another compatible Philips IntelliVue monitor or known-good test setup.
  • Confirm whether CO2 EQUIP MALF follows the module, MMS, or indicated parameter device.
  • Expected: If the INOP follows the parameter device, the module, MMS, or indicated device is likely faulty.
  • Why it matters: This is the key isolation step for this alarm.

Test a Known-Good CO2 Module or MMS on the Original Monitor

  • Install a known-good compatible CO2 module, MMS, or parameter device on the original IntelliVue MP monitor.
  • Verify whether CO2 becomes available and the INOP clears.
  • Expected: Known-good CO2 hardware works correctly on the original monitor.
  • Why it matters: This helps separate a failed CO2 parameter device from a monitor-side interface issue.

Determine the Failure Location

  • If the alarm follows the CO2 module, suspect the CO2 module.
  • If the alarm follows the MMS, suspect the MMS.
  • If all CO2 modules fail only on one monitor, suspect the monitor-side interface or related hardware.
  • If the issue occurs only with one accessory, suspect the external CO2 accessory.
  • If the issue is intermittent with movement, suspect connector damage or poor module seating.
  • Expected: The failure is narrowed to accessory, module, MMS, or monitor-side hardware.

Stop When Resolved

  • If reseating, accessory replacement, restart, or swapping to a known-good module clears the INOP, perform a functional check of CO2 operation before returning the monitor to service.
  • Confirm CO2 waveform and numeric display are available if using an appropriate simulator or approved test setup.
  • Expected: CO2 monitoring functions normally and the CO2 EQUIP MALF INOP does not return.

If the Problem Persists

If CO2 EQUIP MALF remains after external accessories, connections, restart, and module or MMS swap testing have been ruled out, the condition strongly indicates a failed CO2 parameter module, MMS, indicated device, or monitor-side interface.

Remove the affected device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Do not continue swapping parts repeatedly on an active clinical unit. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a CO2 equipment malfunction on an actively monitored patient. Move the patient to a backup device first, or have clinical staff confirm that alternate ventilation monitoring is in place before troubleshooting begins.

Maintain therapy and monitoring continuity, especially in areas where capnography is required for sedation, ventilation, transport, or respiratory monitoring.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Philips IntelliVue MP Series monitor displayed CO2 EQUIP MALF INOP. CO2 waveform and numeric value were unavailable during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"CO2 parameter equipment malfunction confirmed. External CO2 accessory and module seating were checked. INOP followed the suspect CO2 parameter module or MMS during swap testing, indicating likely parameter hardware failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed failed CO2 module or MMS from service and replaced with known-good compatible device. Verified CO2 parameter became available and CO2 EQUIP MALF INOP cleared. Monitor returned to service after functional check."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Exact alarm behavior, including whether CO2 EQUIP MALF appeared at startup, during use, intermittently, or after module connection.
  • Accessories swapped, including airway adapter, sampling line, FilterLine, or other CO2 accessory.
  • Power behavior, including whether the monitor powered on normally and whether power cycling changed the INOP.
  • Environmental factors, including liquid contamination, drop damage, connector stress, or movement-related failures.
  • Indicator lights, missing CO2 waveform, missing numeric value, related INOPs, or other unavailable parameters.
  • Final device status, including whether the module, MMS, accessory, or monitor was returned to service or removed for repair.

Final Thought

A CO2 equipment malfunction INOP should be treated as a real loss of parameter function until proven otherwise. Start with patient safety, verify the alarm, check accessories and connections, then isolate the problem by moving the parameter device to another compatible monitor. If the alarm follows the module or MMS, remove it from service and document the failure clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
