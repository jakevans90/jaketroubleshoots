Mindray BeneVision N Series

Alarm: XX V OVERVOLTAGE

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

BeneVision N Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Mindray BeneVision N Series XX V OVERVOLTAGE alarm. This alarm indicates that a voltage rail is above the expected range. It may be caused by a temporary startup condition, unstable external power, battery or charging behavior, connected accessories, power supply board failure, main control board failure, or another internal power regulation issue.

Because abnormal voltage conditions can affect monitor stability, parameter accuracy, battery charging, display behavior, and alarm reliability, the monitor should not be trusted for active patient monitoring until the condition has been evaluated.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Check the patient and confirm monitoring is still clinically appropriate.
  • If the monitor is alarming, restarting, freezing, losing parameters, flickering, overheating, or behaving abnormally, remove it from patient use.
  • Transfer the patient to another verified monitor if continued monitoring is required.
  • Do not continue relying on a patient monitor with an unresolved voltage-related alarm.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact alarm shown on the BeneVision N Series monitor.
  • Check whether the displayed alarm reads XX V OVERVOLTAGE.
  • Note whether the alarm appears during startup, after running for several minutes, on AC power, on battery power, during charging, or intermittently during normal use.
  • Look for related symptoms such as unexpected restart, failure to boot, screen flicker, parameter loss, battery charging issue, fan abnormality, heat, burning smell, or unusual noise.

Restart the Monitor

  • Remove the monitor from patient use before restarting.
  • Power the monitor off.
  • Disconnect AC power.
  • Wait briefly, then reconnect AC power.
  • Power the monitor back on and allow it to complete startup.
  • Observe whether the XX V OVERVOLTAGE alarm returns.

Check the AC Power Source

  • Verify that the monitor is connected to a proper hospital-grade AC outlet.
  • Inspect the outlet for looseness, damage, or switch-controlled power.
  • Try a known-good outlet.
  • Avoid damaged power strips, questionable extension cords, or non-approved adapters.
  • Confirm the monitor powers normally from a verified AC source.

Inspect the Power Cord and External Power Path

  • Inspect the power cord for cuts, bent prongs, loose strain relief, or signs of overheating.
  • Confirm the cord seats fully into the monitor, dock, or power connection.
  • Swap with a known-good compatible power cord if available.
  • Do not assume an internal failure until visible and replaceable external power-path issues have been ruled out.

Check Battery-Related Behavior

  • Determine whether the alarm appears on AC power, battery power, or during charging.
  • Check for battery warnings, charging failure, swelling, heat, leakage, or poor runtime.
  • If available and approved by facility process, test with a known-good battery.
  • Do not use any battery that is swollen, overheating, leaking, or physically damaged.

Inspect for Physical Damage or Contamination

  • Inspect the monitor exterior for impact damage, cracked housing, damaged connectors, or fluid intrusion.
  • Check around power ports, module connections, docking areas, and accessory ports for sticky residue or corrosion.
  • Smell for burning odor and feel for unusual heat without opening the device.
  • If liquid damage, impact damage, overheating, or burning smell is present, remove the monitor from service.

Remove Accessories and Modules for Isolation

  • Remove nonessential accessories, parameter modules, external displays, USB devices, network accessories, and docking accessories if applicable.
  • Restart the monitor with only the basic required power connection.
  • Observe whether the alarm clears with accessories removed or remains present on the base monitor.
  • If the alarm clears after removing an accessory or module, reconnect one item at a time to identify the cause.

Perform Basic Functional Verification

  • If the alarm clears, confirm normal startup.
  • Verify the alarm does not return during observation.
  • Check display function, touchscreen or buttons, speaker, battery status, charging status, and network connection if applicable.
  • Connect approved test accessories or simulator equipment according to department practice.
  • Confirm expected parameter function before returning the monitor to service.

Determine Whether the Fault Is Internal

  • If the XX V OVERVOLTAGE alarm returns after restart, verified AC power, power cord inspection, accessory isolation, and battery-related checks, suspect an internal fault.
  • Likely areas include the power supply board, main control board, internal power regulation circuitry, or internal board interconnection issue.
  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send it for repair or bench evaluation according to facility process.

If the Problem Persists

If the XX V OVERVOLTAGE alarm continues after restart, verified AC power, power cord inspection, accessory isolation, battery checks, and external damage inspection, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent voltage rail alarm strongly suggests an internal power or board-level fault. Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation. Do not repeatedly restart a patient monitor with a recurring overvoltage alarm and place it back into clinical use.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a voltage-related monitor alarm while the device is connected to an active patient. Move the patient to a backup monitor first, then evaluate the monitor. A patient monitor with unresolved internal power alarms should not be trusted for continuous monitoring, alarm notification, or parameter accuracy.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor displayed XX V OVERVOLTAGE alarm during startup and was removed from patient use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Alarm cleared after controlled restart. AC outlet, power cord, battery status, and external accessories checked with no abnormal findings. Monitor passed functional verification and alarm did not return during observation."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Restarted monitor, verified external power path, checked battery status, removed and reconnected accessories, and completed functional test. Monitor returned to service after no recurrence."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A Mindray BeneVision N Series XX V OVERVOLTAGE alarm should be treated as a real internal power warning until proven otherwise. Start with patient safety, confirm the alarm, check external power and accessories first, then escalate when the alarm persists. Proper troubleshooting protects the patient, the technician, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
