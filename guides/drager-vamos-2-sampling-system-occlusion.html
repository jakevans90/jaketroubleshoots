Drager Vamos 2

Sampling System Occlusion

Asset Type

Anesthesia Monitor

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Vamos 2

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting a Vamos 2 sampling system occlusion caused by blocked sample lines, water traps, filters, or exhaust restrictions.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

Do not troubleshoot the sampling system on an active patient unless clinical staff confirm it is safe.

Action:

  • Confirm anesthesia gas, oxygenation, ventilation, and patient monitoring are being maintained.
  • If gas monitoring is required and the Vamos 2 is not providing reliable readings, have clinical staff use another verified gas monitor or anesthesia system module.
  • Do not delay patient care while investigating the sampling occlusion.

Expected outcome: The patient remains safely monitored while the sampling issue is evaluated.

Why it matters: A sampling occlusion may prevent accurate CO₂, O₂, N₂O, or anesthetic agent readings.

If this resolves the safety concern, continue troubleshooting only when safe.

2. Verify the Reported Occlusion

Confirm the actual message, alarm, or behavior reported.

Action:

  • Check whether the Vamos 2 displays a sampling system occlusion, sample line blocked condition, low/no sample flow, or missing gas readings.
  • Observe whether CO₂ and agent values are absent, delayed, unstable, or not updating.
  • Listen for abnormal pump sounds, repeated cycling, or unusual restriction noises.

Expected outcome: The issue is confirmed as a sampling flow problem rather than a display, communication, or sensor interpretation issue.

3. Check the Sample Line for Kinks or Blockage

Inspect the full length of the gas sample line.

Action:

  • Look for kinks, pinched tubing, crushed sections, liquid in the line, secretions or contamination, and a blocked Luer connection.
  • Replace the sample line with a known-good compatible line if any restriction is found.

Expected outcome: Sample flow returns and gas readings stabilize.

If the occlusion clears, document the blocked or damaged sample line and stop.

4. Verify the Sample Line Is Connected Correctly

Check the connection between the patient circuit, sample line, water trap, and Vamos 2 inlet.

Action:

  • Confirm the sample line is connected to the correct sampling port.
  • Confirm the Luer fitting is tight but not cross-threaded.
  • Check that the sample line is not connected to a capped or blocked adapter.
  • Verify the sampling point on the airway adapter is open and positioned correctly.

Expected outcome: The monitor can draw gas from the breathing circuit without restriction.

If reconnection restores readings, document the connection issue and stop.

5. Check the Airway Adapter or Sampling Port

Inspect the airway adapter or gas sampling connection at the breathing circuit.

Action:

  • Look for moisture, secretions, condensation, or debris blocking the sampling port.
  • Replace the airway adapter or sampling connector if contamination is present.
  • Confirm the replacement part is compatible and properly installed.

Expected outcome: Gas sample flow is restored through the patient circuit sampling point.

If the issue clears after replacing the airway adapter, stop troubleshooting.

6. Inspect the Water Trap

Check the water trap for fullness, blockage, improper seating, or contamination.

Action:

  • Remove the water trap only when safe and appropriate.
  • Check whether it is full, wet, clogged, cracked, or contaminated.
  • Empty or replace it according to facility procedure.
  • Reseat the water trap firmly and confirm it locks into place.

Expected outcome: The sampling system recognizes the water trap and draws normally.

Why it matters: A full or blocked water trap is one of the most common external causes of sampling occlusion.

If the occlusion clears after water trap service, document the finding and stop.

7. Check for Incorrect or Non-Compatible Consumables

Verify the sample line, water trap, filter, and airway adapter are compatible with the Vamos 2 sampling system.

Action:

  • Look for recently changed accessories.
  • Compare the installed consumables with approved hospital stock.
  • Replace questionable accessories with known-good compatible parts.

Expected outcome: The sampling path is restored using parts that allow proper flow and moisture management.

If replacing consumables resolves the issue, document the incompatible or defective accessory and stop.

8. Check for Moisture or Liquid Contamination

Look for signs that fluid has entered the sample path.

Action:

  • Inspect the sample line, water trap, and inlet area for visible liquid.
  • Replace wet sample lines and contaminated disposable components.
  • Allow the external inlet area to dry if only minor external moisture is present.
  • Do not blow compressed air into the device unless directed by approved service procedure.

Expected outcome: External fluid contamination is corrected without forcing liquid deeper into the monitor.

If fluid appears to have entered the internal sampling system, remove the device from service.

9. Check the Exhaust Path

Verify the gas exhaust path is not blocked.

Action:

  • Inspect the exhaust tubing if connected.
  • Check for kinked, capped, crushed, or liquid-filled exhaust tubing.
  • Confirm scavenging or exhaust connections are not creating excessive back pressure.
  • Disconnect or replace questionable exhaust tubing if safe and appropriate.

Expected outcome: The Vamos 2 can draw and exhaust sample gas normally.

If clearing the exhaust restriction resolves the occlusion, document the blocked exhaust path and stop.

10. Power Cycle and Recheck

After external restrictions are corrected, restart the monitor if the occlusion message remains.

Action:

  • Remove the device from active patient use first.
  • Power the Vamos 2 down.
  • Wait briefly, then power it back on.
  • Allow the monitor to complete startup and sampling checks.
  • Reconnect a known-good sample line and water trap.

Expected outcome: The occlusion alarm clears and gas readings return.

If the alarm returns immediately with known-good external components, suspect an internal sampling system issue.

11. Compare With Known-Good Accessories

Use a known-good complete sampling setup before condemning the monitor.

Action:

  • Install a known-good sample line.
  • Install a known-good water trap.
  • Use a known-good airway adapter or test sampling source if available.
  • Confirm the exhaust path is open.

Expected outcome: The Vamos 2 samples normally with verified accessories.

If the occlusion remains with all external parts replaced, external causes have likely been ruled out.

If the Problem Persists

If the sampling system occlusion continues after checking the sample line, airway adapter, water trap, consumables, moisture, and exhaust path, the issue is likely internal.

The device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Possible internal causes may include a restricted internal sample path, failed pump, internal valve issue, flow sensor problem, contamination, or internal moisture damage.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not keep returning the monitor to use if the sampling system cannot be trusted.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a gas sampling occlusion while the monitor is the only source of CO₂ or anesthetic agent monitoring for an active patient. Move monitoring to another verified device first, then troubleshoot the Vamos 2 only when it is safe. Maintain therapy continuity and do not interrupt patient monitoring while investigating the device.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Drager Vamos 2 anesthesia monitor reported sampling system occlusion and was not displaying reliable gas readings."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found the gas sample line kinked near the airway adapter, restricting sample flow to the monitor."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced the sample line, verified the water trap was seated correctly, confirmed normal sample flow and gas readings, and returned the monitor to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested if power was questioned
  • Sample line inspected or replaced
  • Water trap condition
  • Airway adapter condition
  • Exhaust tubing checked
  • Alarm message observed
  • CO₂ or agent readings present or absent
  • Any moisture, secretions, or contamination found
  • Pump sound normal or abnormal
  • Whether known-good accessories were tested
  • Environmental factors near the device or sampling setup
  • Indicator lights, startup messages, or sampling status behavior
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Sampling system occlusions should be handled logically and safely. Start with the external sample path, because most problems come from kinked tubing, full water traps, blocked adapters, or exhaust restrictions. If those causes are ruled out and the occlusion remains, remove the device from service and document the findings clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
