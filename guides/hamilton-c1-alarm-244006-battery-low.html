Hamilton C1

Alarm: 244006 Battery low

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm: 244006 Battery low on the Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates battery runtime may be limited and the ventilator may lose power if external power is not restored or if the battery cannot reliably charge and hold capacity.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Treat a low battery alarm on a ventilator as a patient safety issue.
  • If the ventilator is in active use, connect external AC power immediately.
  • Confirm ventilation continues normally.
  • Have an alternate ventilator or approved backup ventilation method available if the alarm persists.
  • Do not continue relying on battery power unless remaining runtime is clearly adequate for safe patient care.
  • Remove the ventilator from patient use if power stability cannot be immediately confirmed.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the display shows Alarm: 244006 Battery low.
  • Note whether the alarm occurs during transport, after unplugging from AC power, immediately after startup, after sitting unused, while connected to AC power, or intermittently during movement.
  • Check for associated symptoms such as a low battery icon, battery not charging, short battery runtime, power supply indicator not lit, alarm returning after charging, or shutdown warnings.
  • Confirm whether the issue is reproducible or was clearly reported by clinical staff.

3. Connect External Power Immediately

  • Plug the ventilator into a known working AC outlet.
  • Confirm the power cord is fully seated at the ventilator and wall outlet.
  • Verify the external power indicator shows AC power is present.
  • Allow the ventilator to begin charging.
  • If the alarm clears and the battery charges normally, continue monitoring charge status before returning the ventilator to service.

4. Verify the Wall Outlet and Power Source

  • Do not assume the ventilator is receiving power just because it is plugged in.
  • Test the outlet with another approved device or outlet tester.
  • Check whether the outlet is controlled by a switch, emergency power circuit, or power strip.
  • Move the ventilator to a known good outlet if needed.
  • Avoid using damaged extension cords or questionable power strips.
  • If the outlet is the cause, document the failed outlet and notify facilities if needed.

5. Inspect the Power Cord and Connection

  • Inspect the AC power cord for cuts, loose strain relief, bent pins, damaged plug, intermittent connection, heat damage, or signs of arcing.
  • Reseat the power cord firmly.
  • Try a known good compatible power cord if available.
  • Confirm the ventilator detects external power consistently.

6. Check Battery Charging Status

  • With AC power connected, observe the battery charge indicator.
  • Allow the ventilator to charge for an appropriate period.
  • Confirm the battery percentage or charge icon increases.
  • Check whether the alarm clears after charging.
  • If the alarm does not improve while connected to AC power, suspect a battery, charging, contact, or internal power issue.

7. Check Battery Seating and External Condition

  • Remove the ventilator from clinical use before handling the battery.
  • Inspect the battery compartment area if accessible without deep disassembly.
  • Confirm the battery is properly seated and latched.
  • Check for visible damage, swelling, contamination, corrosion, or loose contacts.
  • Do not use a swollen, damaged, leaking, or overheated battery.

8. Check Battery Age and Runtime History

  • Review battery age, service history, and prior runtime complaints.
  • Ask staff whether the ventilator recently lost runtime during transport.
  • Check whether the battery drops quickly after being unplugged.
  • Compare runtime against expected clinical use requirements.
  • If the battery is old, fails quickly, or will not hold charge, replace the battery according to facility procedure.

9. Replace the Battery if It Will Not Hold Charge

  • Install a known good compatible battery if available.
  • Charge the ventilator.
  • Confirm the low battery alarm clears.
  • Verify the ventilator runs on battery for an acceptable period during bench testing.
  • If battery replacement resolves the issue, return the ventilator to service after completing required checks.

10. Isolate Charging or Internal Power Issues

  • Confirm a known good outlet was used.
  • Confirm a known good power cord was used.
  • Confirm the battery is seated correctly.
  • Confirm the battery is not damaged or expired.
  • Test with a known good battery if available.
  • If charging status remains abnormal, suspect an internal charging circuit, battery interface, power supply, or battery monitoring issue.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm: 244006 Battery low remains after external power, outlet, cord, battery seating, charging status, and battery condition have been checked, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent low battery alarm may indicate battery failure, charging circuit failure, battery contact issues, internal power supply problems, or battery monitoring failure.

The ventilator should be removed from patient use, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

A ventilator with unresolved battery or power alarms should not be trusted for patient care or transport.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first.

For transport use, verify battery charge and expected runtime before disconnecting the Hamilton C1 from AC power.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator reported Alarm: 244006 Battery low. Staff stated battery runtime was limited and the ventilator alarmed after being unplugged from AC power."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery charge was low. External AC power, outlet, and power cord were checked. Battery charging status and seating were inspected. Battery condition suggested reduced capacity and unreliable runtime."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Connected ventilator to verified AC power and confirmed charging status. Battery was reseated and evaluated. Battery was replaced after failing to hold adequate charge. Alarm cleared after charging with replacement battery. Ventilator passed operational checks and was returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A low battery alarm on a ventilator should always be treated as a patient safety concern. Start with external power, verify the outlet and cord, confirm the battery is charging, and check battery condition before suspecting internal failure. If the battery cannot reliably charge or hold runtime, remove the ventilator from service and document the findings clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
