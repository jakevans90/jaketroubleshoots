Hamilton C1

Alarm: 244008 Battery 1: Wrong battery

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm 244008 Battery 1: Wrong battery on the Hamilton C1 ventilator. This condition means the ventilator does not recognize Battery 1 as an expected compatible battery, which may affect backup power, transport readiness, and safe operation during external power loss.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot a battery compatibility alarm while the ventilator is supporting a patient if backup power reliability may be needed.
  • Confirm the patient is safely supported on another ventilator or approved backup ventilation method if the device is in clinical use.
  • Do not rely on the ventilator for transport or backup operation until the battery issue is corrected.
  • Keep the ventilator connected to stable AC power while evaluating the battery alarm, if safe to do so.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Power the ventilator on and confirm Alarm 244008 is present.
  • Confirm the displayed message reads: Battery 1: Wrong battery.
  • Note whether the alarm appears at startup, after battery insertion, during charging, or intermittently.
  • Check whether Battery 2 is recognized normally, if installed.
  • Confirm whether the ventilator is charging, running on AC power, or refusing battery operation.

Confirm AC Power Is Stable

  • Connect the ventilator to a known-good AC outlet.
  • Confirm the power cord is fully seated at the wall outlet and ventilator power input.
  • Verify AC power indicators are present.
  • Avoid evaluating battery recognition while the device is on unstable or questionable external power.

Inspect Battery 1 Installation

  • Power down the ventilator according to facility practice.
  • Remove Battery 1.
  • Inspect the battery compartment for poor seating, obstruction, debris, or physical damage.
  • Reinstall Battery 1 fully and confirm it seats or latches correctly.
  • Power the ventilator back on and check whether the alarm clears.

Verify Battery Compatibility

  • Confirm the installed battery is the correct Hamilton-compatible battery for the C1.
  • Compare Battery 1 against facility-approved replacement battery information.
  • Check the battery label, part number, voltage rating, and physical fit.
  • Confirm the battery is not from another Hamilton model or another ventilator family unless approved as compatible.
  • Do not force a battery that does not fit correctly or does not match expected labeling.

Swap With a Known-Good Compatible Battery

  • Remove the suspect Battery 1.
  • Install a known-good approved Hamilton-compatible battery in the Battery 1 position.
  • Power the ventilator on.
  • Confirm whether Battery 1 is recognized and charging normally.
  • If the alarm clears, remove the original battery from service as incorrect, incompatible, damaged, or not approved for this unit.
  • If the alarm remains with a known-good compatible battery, suspect the Battery 1 contacts, battery detection path, or internal power management hardware.

Inspect Battery Contacts

  • Remove Battery 1.
  • Inspect the battery contacts for bent pins, corrosion, contamination, fluid residue, or physical damage.
  • Inspect the contacts inside the battery bay using proper lighting.
  • Clean only according to facility-approved methods.
  • Do not scrape, bend, or modify contacts.

Check Charging and Recognition Status

  • Install the approved compatible battery.
  • Connect the ventilator to AC power.
  • Check the battery status screen or battery indicators.
  • Confirm Battery 1 is detected.
  • Confirm Battery 1 shows charging or normal charge status.
  • Allow appropriate charging time if the battery is depleted.

Determine Whether the Issue Is Battery-Related or Device-Related

  • If only one battery causes the alarm, remove that battery from service.
  • If multiple known-good compatible batteries cause the same Battery 1 alarm, suspect a device-side issue.
  • If Battery 2 works normally but Battery 1 does not, focus on the Battery 1 bay, contacts, or related battery detection path.
  • If both battery positions show abnormal behavior, suspect a broader battery management or power system issue.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm 244008 remains after installing an approved compatible battery, reseating Battery 1, checking contacts, and confirming stable AC power, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent Battery 1: Wrong battery alarms may indicate a Battery 1 bay contact problem, battery identification issue, or internal battery management fault.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Do not use it for patient transport or backup power-dependent applications.
  • Send the ventilator for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot battery compatibility alarms on an active patient if loss of backup power could affect care. Move the patient to a backup ventilator first, especially before transport or use in areas where external power may be interrupted.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator reported Alarm 244008 Battery 1: Wrong battery. Battery 1 was not recognized as a compatible battery and backup power reliability could not be confirmed."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery 1 was not identified by the ventilator as an approved compatible battery. Battery seating, contacts, compatibility, and charging status were checked. Known-good battery testing was used to determine whether the issue was battery-related or device-related."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Installed approved Hamilton-compatible battery in Battery 1 position, inspected contacts, confirmed battery was recognized, and verified charging status. If alarm persisted with known-good battery, ventilator was removed from service and sent for repair evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery compatibility alarms should be treated as patient safety concerns because they affect backup power reliability. Start with simple checks, confirm the battery is approved and seated correctly, inspect contacts, and use a known-good battery to isolate the issue. If the alarm remains, remove the ventilator from service and escalate appropriately.

That is successful troubleshooting.
