B. Braun Space Infusion Pump System

Pump Not Recognized by Space Docking Station

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

B. Braun

Model

Space Infusion Pump System

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a B. Braun Space infusion pump is inserted into a Space docking station but is not recognized by the dock. Symptoms may include the pump not powering from the dock, no communication with the system, or the docking station not displaying the pump as connected.

Many recognition issues are caused by poor seating in the dock, dirty contacts, or docking station power problems rather than internal pump failures.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Confirm the Docking Station Has Power

Verify the Space docking station is powered.

  • Power indicator lights on the docking station
  • AC power cable securely connected to the wall outlet
  • Outlet tested with another device if needed

If the dock itself has no power, the pump cannot be recognized.

Remove and Reseat the Pump

Remove the infusion pump from the docking station and reseat it firmly.

Ensure:

  • The pump slides fully into the docking rails
  • The latch engages properly
  • The pump sits flush with the dock

Partial seating is one of the most common causes of recognition failures.

Observe Pump Behavior When Docked

Watch the pump screen and indicators when inserting it into the dock.

Look for:

  • Charging indicators
  • Communication symbols
  • Any alert messages

If nothing changes when the pump is inserted, the dock may not be making electrical contact.

Inspect Docking Contacts

Visually inspect the metal contacts on both:

  • The pump rear connector
  • The docking station connector

Look for:

  • Dust or debris
  • Dried IV fluid contamination
  • Bent or recessed contacts

If contamination is present, clean gently using approved wipes (per facility policy).

Test the Pump in Another Dock

Place the pump into a different docking station that is known to be working.

Results help isolate the problem:

  • Pump works in another dock: Original docking station likely faulty
  • Pump not recognized in any dock: Pump docking interface may be defective

This step helps determine whether the issue is dock-related or pump-related.

Test Another Pump in the Same Dock

Insert a known-good Space infusion pump into the same docking station.

Possible outcomes:

  • Second pump works normally: Original pump likely has a docking interface issue
  • Second pump also fails: Docking station may be defective

Inspect for Physical Damage

Check the pump and dock for mechanical issues.

Look for:

  • Cracked housings
  • Loose docking rails
  • Bent connectors
  • Signs of fluid intrusion

Mechanical misalignment can prevent proper docking connection.

If the Problem Persists

If the pump is still not recognized after verifying power, reseating the pump, cleaning contacts, and testing with alternate docks and pumps, common external causes have been ruled out.

The issue is likely related to:

  • Internal docking connector failure
  • Communication interface fault
  • Docking station electronics failure

At this point:

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Send the pump or docking station for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop external troubleshooting prevents unnecessary risk and protects patient care equipment.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot docking issues while the pump is actively infusing medication on a patient. Move the patient to another functioning infusion pump first, then remove the suspect pump or docking station from service for safe troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nursing reported the B. Braun Space infusion pump was not recognized when inserted into the docking station and would not charge or communicate with the system.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Docking station contacts contaminated with dried IV fluid preventing proper electrical connection.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Cleaned docking contacts and reseated pump. Dock recognized pump and charging indicator appeared. Pump returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Dock power indicator status
  • Outlet tested
  • Pump reseated
  • Alternate dock tested
  • Alternate pump tested
  • Connector condition
  • Any contamination observed
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Many docking issues are caused by simple problems such as incomplete seating, dirty contacts, or docking station power loss. A structured troubleshooting approach helps quickly isolate whether the problem lies with the pump or the docking station. Just as important is recognizing when the issue is likely internal and removing the device from service to maintain patient safety and equipment reliability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
