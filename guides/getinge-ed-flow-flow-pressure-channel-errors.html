Getinge ED-Flow

Flow or Pressure-Related Channel Errors

Asset Type

Endoscope Reprocessor (AER)

Manufacturer

Getinge

Model

ED-Flow

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting flow or pressure errors preventing proper channel flushing, often caused by blockages, connection issues, or supply restrictions.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot during an active reprocessing cycle with a contaminated scope
  • Remove the scope and follow proper infection control procedures

Expected: No patient risk and safe handling of contaminated equipment
Why it matters: Prevents exposure and ensures proper reprocessing workflow

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact error message and when it occurs
  • Note if error occurs during fill, rinse, or channel flush phase
  • Observe if cycle aborts or continues with alarms

Expected: Clear understanding of when flow/pressure failure occurs
Why it matters: Helps isolate whether issue is supply, connection, or internal restriction

Check Scope Channel Connections (Most Common Cause)

  • Verify all channel connectors are attached to correct ports
  • Ensure fittings are fully seated and locked
  • Confirm correct connector set is used for the specific scope

Expected: Secure, correct connections with no looseness
Why it matters: Misconnections prevent proper flow through channels

Inspect for Kinked or Obstructed Tubing

  • Trace all external tubing for kinks or pinches
  • Straighten any compressed lines
  • Ensure scope is not positioned in a way that restricts flow

Expected: Unrestricted, open tubing path
Why it matters: Even minor kinks can trigger pressure alarms

Check for Channel Blockage in the Scope

  • Confirm manual pre-cleaning was completed
  • Flush channels manually (if protocol allows)
  • Look for resistance when flushing

Expected: Free flow through all channels
Why it matters: Blocked channels will cause pressure buildup or flow failure

Verify Water Supply and Inlet Pressure

  • Confirm water supply valve is fully open
  • Check facility water pressure is within normal range
  • Inspect inlet filters/screens for debris

Expected: Stable and adequate water flow into the unit
Why it matters: Low inlet pressure prevents proper channel flushing

Inspect External Filters and Strainers

  • Check and clean inlet strainers if accessible
  • Inspect any external filtration system connected to the AER

Expected: Clean filters with unrestricted flow
Why it matters: Partial blockage reduces system flow and increases pressure errors

Check for Air Traps or Improper Loading

  • Ensure scope is properly positioned in the basin
  • Avoid loops that trap air in channels
  • Re-seat the scope and reconnect

Expected: Proper positioning with consistent flow
Why it matters: Air pockets can mimic pressure or flow issues

Restart the Cycle

  • Power cycle the unit if needed
  • Restart a new cycle with correct setup

Expected: Cycle runs without flow/pressure alarms
Why it matters: Confirms issue was resolved through external correction

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes (connections, tubing, filters, supply, and scope condition) have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal (pump, pressure sensor, or internal flow path restriction).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and wasted troubleshooting time.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot flow or pressure issues with a contaminated scope actively connected. Always remove and reprocess safely before testing to avoid cross-contamination.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"AER reporting flow/pressure error during channel flush phase, cycle aborting."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Kinked scope channel tubing restricting flow and causing pressure alarm."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Corrected tubing routing, verified proper connections, and confirmed normal cycle operation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Error message text and cycle phase
  • Water supply verified
  • Tubing inspected for kinks
  • Filters checked/cleaned
  • Scope pre-cleaning status
  • Alarm recurrence after restart
  • Final operational status of AER

Final Thought

Flow and pressure errors are most often caused by simple external restrictions or setup issues. A structured approach ensures patient safety, avoids unnecessary downtime, and supports efficient escalation when needed.

That is successful troubleshooting.
