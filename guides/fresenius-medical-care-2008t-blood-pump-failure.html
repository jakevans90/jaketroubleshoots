Fresenius Medical Care 2008T

Blood Pump Motor or Flow Rate Failure

Asset Type

Hemodialysis (HD) Machine

Manufacturer

Fresenius Medical Care

Model

2008T

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting blood pump motor or flow rate failures on a Fresenius 2008T HD machine. Symptoms may include alarms related to blood pump speed, erratic flow readings, inability to reach target blood flow rates, or unusual noises from the pump. The goal is to verify external and operational causes before considering internal repair or motor replacement.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power and Machine Readiness

  • Ensure the machine is properly connected to a verified power source.
  • Confirm the machine has completed self-checks and displays no other critical errors.

Why: Blood pump motors rely on stable voltage and startup sequences to function correctly.

Inspect Blood Lines and Connections

  • Check arterial and venous lines for kinks, clots, or air bubbles.
  • Confirm all connectors are seated properly in the pump segment.

Why: Obstructions or poor line placement can prevent the pump from reaching the set flow rate.

Check Pump Segment Installation

  • Verify the blood pump segment (cassette) is correctly installed and not worn or damaged.
  • Look for wear, cracking, or misalignment that could affect flow.

Why: Improper installation or worn segments can cause flow errors without motor failure.

Assess Priming and Blood Flow Settings

  • Ensure priming was completed successfully.
  • Confirm the blood flow rate set on the machine is within safe operating limits.

Why: High target flow rates or incomplete priming can trigger alarms or prevent the motor from achieving set speed.

Observe Pump Operation During Test Mode

  • If safe, run the pump in a test or maintenance mode without patient connection.
  • Listen for unusual noises, vibration, or erratic rotation.

Why: Motor noise or irregular movement can indicate mechanical failure or pump resistance.

Check Machine Logs and Alarms

  • Review error codes and event logs for indications of motor failure or flow restrictions.

Why: Provides diagnostic insight without disassembly.

If the Problem Persists

Common external issues have been ruled out.

The blood pump motor, drive system, or internal flow sensors may be failing.

  • Remove the machine from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for vendor repair or bench evaluation.

Why: Continuing to use a machine with potential motor failure risks patient safety and may cause further damage.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not run blood pump troubleshooting on an active patient.

Move the patient to a functional HD machine before any test or maintenance.

Always ensure lines are clamped or disconnected to prevent blood loss during troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Blood pump unable to reach prescribed flow rate; alarm triggered.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Pump segment properly installed; lines free of kinks; test run shows irregular pump rotation and motor noise.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Machine removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for vendor bench repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Blood line type and installation verified
  • Pump segment condition
  • Target and observed blood flow rate
  • Error codes and alarm messages
  • Motor noise or vibration observed

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount; never troubleshoot a blood pump motor with a patient connected. Logical, stepwise checks help rule out external causes and prevent unnecessary repairs. Thorough documentation ensures accountability and supports safe escalation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
