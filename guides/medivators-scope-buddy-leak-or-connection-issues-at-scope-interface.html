Medivators Scope Buddy

Leak or Connection Issues at Scope Interface

Asset Type

Endoscope Flushing System

Manufacturer

Medivators

Model

Scope Buddy

What This Guide Helps With

Leaks or poor connections at the scope interface causing fluid loss, poor flushing performance, or inability to maintain proper pressure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the scope from patient use and ensure it is not connected to a patient during troubleshooting
  • Confirm safe environment for equipment inspection

Expected: Safe environment for equipment inspection
Why it matters: Prevents contamination risk and ensures no patient exposure to faulty equipment

Inspect Scope Connection Setup

  • Disconnect and reconnect the scope tubing
  • Ensure correct port matching (channel-to-channel)
  • Confirm connections are fully seated

Expected: Secure, tight connections with no visible gaps
Why it matters: Improper seating is the most common cause of leaks

Check for Damaged or Worn Interface Fittings

  • Look for cracks, worn O-rings, or deformation
  • Check for loose or stripped fittings

Expected: No visible damage or wear
Why it matters: Even minor damage can cause leaks under pressure

Verify Correct Adapter Usage

  • Confirm adapter matches the scope model
  • Swap with a known-good adapter if available

Expected: Proper fit without forcing or looseness
Why it matters: Incorrect adapters lead to poor sealing and leakage

Inspect Tubing and Lines for Leaks

  • Look for cracks, kinks, or loose connections
  • Gently manipulate tubing while observing for leaks

Expected: No visible fluid escape or pressure loss
Why it matters: Leaks may originate upstream but appear at the interface

Check for Proper Tightening (Do Not Overtighten)

  • Hand-tighten fittings only
  • Avoid using tools unless specified

Expected: Leak-free seal without stressing components
Why it matters: Overtightening can damage seals and threads

Run a Test Flush Cycle

  • Operate the Scope Buddy with the scope connected
  • Observe the interface during operation
  • Look for active leaking or pressure drop

Expected: No leaks and consistent flow
Why it matters: Confirms issue under real operating conditions

Swap with Known-Good Components

  • Try a different scope
  • Try different tubing or adapters

Expected: Leak resolves when faulty component is removed
Why it matters: Helps identify whether issue is scope, adapter, or system-related

If the Problem Persists

If all external connections, adapters, and tubing have been verified and leaks continue, the issue is likely internal to the interface assembly or pressure system.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot flushing or leak issues while a scope is intended for patient use. Always remove and reprocess the scope before testing.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported leaking at the Scope Buddy interface during flushing cycle."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Worn O-ring on scope adapter causing poor seal under pressure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced adapter with known-good unit and verified no leaks during test cycle."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Adapter type and scope model used
  • Whether leak occurs immediately or under pressure
  • Condition of O-rings and fittings
  • Tubing condition and routing
  • Results of component swaps
  • Final leak status after testing

Final Thought

Connection and leak issues at the scope interface are most often caused by simple external factors like worn seals or incorrect adapters. A structured approach ensures quick resolution while protecting both equipment and patient safety. Proper escalation and documentation are key when internal faults are suspected.

That is successful troubleshooting.
