GE Healthcare CARESCAPE R850 Ventilator

Flow Sensor Error

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE R860

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the CARESCAPE R860 ventilator displays a Flow Sensor Error. Symptoms may include alarms related to inaccurate tidal volume, unusual patient breathing patterns, or failure to detect patient effort. Many issues originate from external factors such as dislodged sensors, blocked tubing, or improper sensor installation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Move the patient to another ventilator or manual ventilation device before troubleshooting.
  • This prevents interruption of therapy during testing.

Check Ventilator Power

  • Confirm the ventilator is powered on and not in standby mode.
  • Verify the battery is charged if in portable use.

Inspect Flow Sensor Connection

  • Ensure the flow sensor is properly seated in the patient circuit.
  • Check for loose or misaligned connectors; even slight misalignment can trigger errors.

Examine the Patient Circuit for Obstructions

  • Inspect tubing, filters, and humidifiers for kinks, water buildup, or secretions.
  • Replace or clear any obstructions; flow sensors require unobstructed airflow to function properly.

Check Sensor Orientation and Installation

  • Ensure the flow sensor is installed in the correct orientation (follow the arrow markings on the sensor).
  • Improper insertion may cause inaccurate readings.

Inspect Sensor Condition

  • Look for visible damage, moisture, or contamination on the sensor.
  • Clean or replace according to infection control and manufacturer guidelines.

Perform a Quick Self-Test (if available)

  • Many R860 ventilators allow a sensor check via the menu.
  • Follow the on-screen prompts to verify sensor functionality.

If the Problem Persists

All external checks and routine troubleshooting have been completed. Likely causes now involve internal flow sensor failure or board-level issues.

  • Remove ventilator from patient care.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send to biomedical repair or manufacturer service for bench evaluation.

Knowing when to escalate is proper troubleshooting and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot flow sensors while the patient is connected and relying on the ventilator. Always have an alternative ventilation plan ready to maintain uninterrupted therapy.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator displayed “Flow Sensor Error” during patient ventilation.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Flow sensor misaligned or blocked, preventing accurate airflow measurement.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Verified sensor seating and orientation, cleared tubing obstructions, performed self-test; if error persists, removed ventilator from service for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Check filter condition
  • Circuit connections tight and oriented correctly
  • No condensation or secretions on sensor
  • Ventilator self-test results
  • Alarm logs and error codes

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting focuses on patient safety, methodical logic, and knowing when external fixes are insufficient. Documentation of steps taken not only supports future troubleshooting but also ensures accountability and safety compliance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
