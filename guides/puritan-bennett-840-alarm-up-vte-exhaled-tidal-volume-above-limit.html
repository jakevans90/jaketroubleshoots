Puritan Bennett 840

Alarm: ↑VTE Exhaled tidal volume above limit

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Puritan Bennett

Model

840

What This Guide Helps With

Alarm: ↑VTE indicating exhaled tidal volume exceeds set limits, which can result in over-ventilation and increase the risk of lung injury or barotrauma.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient for signs of over-ventilation or distress
  • Be prepared to provide alternate ventilation if needed
  • Confirm adequate oxygenation and ventilation status

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ↑VTE alarm is displayed
  • Observe exhaled tidal volume compared to set tidal volume
  • Check if alarm occurs continuously or intermittently
  • Review waveforms for unusually large breaths

3. Check Ventilator Settings (Most Common Cause)

  • Verify set tidal volume (VT)
  • Check high VTE alarm limit settings
  • Confirm correct ventilation mode (volume vs pressure control)
  • Ensure settings match patient size and clinical condition

4. Assess Patient Condition

  • Evaluate for increased lung compliance
  • Assess for strong spontaneous breathing efforts
  • Review sedation level or patient-ventilator synchrony
  • Check for coughing or agitation

5. Inspect Patient Circuit and Sensors

  • Check flow sensor for moisture or contamination
  • Inspect circuit for leaks or improper setup
  • Ensure all tubing and components are properly connected

6. Run Ventilator Self Tests

  • Run SST (Short Self Test)
  • Run EST (Extended Self Test)
  • Verify all tests pass without errors

7. Stop When Resolved

  • Confirm alarm clears after adjustments
  • Verify stable ventilation parameters
  • Return device to service if functioning properly

If the Problem Persists

If the ↑VTE alarm continues after all external checks and testing:

  • External causes have been ruled out
  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label the device Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilator alarms while the device is actively supporting a patient. Always transition the patient to a backup ventilation method before troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming ↑VTE with higher-than-set exhaled tidal volumes during operation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Ventilator settings and circuit verified; SST/EST performed. Persistent ↑VTE alarm indicates likely internal sensing or control issue."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service, labeled out of service, and sent for repair evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

High tidal volume alarms must be addressed quickly to protect patient lung safety. Focus on settings, patient factors, and sensor accuracy first, and escalate appropriately when external causes are ruled out.

That is successful troubleshooting.
