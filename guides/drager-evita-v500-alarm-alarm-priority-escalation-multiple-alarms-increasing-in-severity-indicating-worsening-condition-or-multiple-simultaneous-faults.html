Alarm: Alarm priority escalation Multiple alarms increasing in severity indicating worsening condition or multiple simultaneous faults

Ventilator

Drager

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses the Alarm priority escalation condition, where multiple alarms increase in severity, indicating worsening conditions or simultaneous faults that may compromise safe ventilation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s ventilation status
  • If ventilation is compromised, remove from ventilator
  • Provide alternate ventilation (bag-valve mask or backup ventilator)

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm multiple alarms are active simultaneously
  • Observe escalation from yellow (medium) to red (high priority)
  • Note increased audible alarm intensity
  • Assess for changes in ventilation performance

3. Identify and Address Highest Priority Alarm First

  • Focus on red (high priority) alarms first
  • Read and identify the highest priority alarm displayed
  • Follow troubleshooting steps specific to that alarm
  • Avoid attempting to resolve all alarms at once

4. Check for Common Shared External Causes

  • Inspect breathing circuit for disconnections, leaks, or obstruction
  • Verify air and oxygen gas supply connections and pressure
  • Check power source stability
  • Ensure proper connection of accessories (flow sensor, expiratory valve)

5. Evaluate Ventilator Settings and Configuration

  • Review ventilation mode and parameters
  • Check alarm limits for appropriateness
  • Confirm tube type and system configuration are correct

6. Observe Alarm Reduction as Issues Are Resolved

  • Confirm alarms decrease in number and severity
  • Verify stable ventilation parameters return
  • Ensure system stabilizes after corrective actions

7. Stop When Resolved

  • Return device to service once alarms normalize
  • Continue monitoring for recurrence

If the Problem Persists

If alarm escalation continues after external checks, multiple or worsening conditions may indicate internal system faults or control failures.

  • Remove device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot escalating alarms on an active patient. Always move the patient to a backup device before continuing troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator displaying multiple alarms escalating from yellow to red with unstable ventilation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Multiple simultaneous issues identified including circuit connection problem and gas supply instability causing alarm escalation."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Addressed highest priority alarms first and corrected external issues. Device removed from service when escalation persisted."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Alarm priority escalation indicates worsening or multiple system failures. Always prioritize the highest severity alarm, rule out common external causes, and escalate appropriately when conditions do not resolve.

That is successful troubleshooting.
