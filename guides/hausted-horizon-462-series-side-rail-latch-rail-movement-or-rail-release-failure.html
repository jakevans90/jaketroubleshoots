Hausted Horizon 462 Series

Side rail latch, rail movement, or rail release failure

Asset Type

Stretcher

Manufacturer

Hausted

Model

Horizon 462 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Side rail will not latch, release, raise, lower, or stay securely locked during normal stretcher use.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Remove the stretcher from patient use if the side rail does not lock securely or moves unexpectedly. Transfer the patient to another safe surface if needed.

Expected outcome: No patient remains dependent on an unsafe rail.

If this resolves the immediate safety risk, stop and document the action.

Verify the Reported Side Rail Issue

Identify which side rail is affected and what failure is occurring: will not raise, will not lower, will not latch, releases unexpectedly, binds, or feels loose.

Expected outcome: The exact symptom and affected rail are confirmed.

Why it matters: A latch failure, movement obstruction, and damaged rail assembly may look similar at first.

Inspect for Obvious Damage

Check the rail, latch handle, release lever, hinge points, pivot brackets, mounting hardware, and surrounding frame area for bending, cracks, missing parts, or impact damage.

Expected outcome: No visible damage or missing hardware is found.

If damaged components are found, remove the stretcher from service and send for repair.

Check for Obstructions Around the Rail Path

Look for bedding, straps, IV lines, patient belongings, debris, tape, or accessory mounts interfering with rail travel or latch engagement.

Expected outcome: The rail moves freely through its normal range without anything blocking it.

If an obstruction is removed and the rail latches normally, return the stretcher to service only after confirming safe operation.

Operate the Rail Slowly Through Its Full Motion

Raise and lower the rail using normal operation. Do not force it. Listen and feel for binding, grinding, uneven motion, or sticking.

Expected outcome: The rail moves smoothly and locks positively in the intended positions.

If the rail binds or requires force, remove the stretcher from service.

Confirm the Latch Fully Engages

Raise the side rail into the locked position and gently apply pressure in the expected direction of use. Verify that the latch clicks or seats fully and does not slip.

Expected outcome: The rail remains locked and does not partially release.

If the latch does not fully engage, the stretcher should not be used.

Check the Release Handle or Lever Action

Operate the release mechanism and confirm it returns to its normal resting position after being released. Look for looseness, sticking, broken plastic, bent linkage, or failure to spring back.

Expected outcome: The release mechanism moves normally and returns fully.

If the release mechanism sticks or does not return, remove the stretcher from service.

Inspect Mounting Hardware and Rail Alignment

Check for loose fasteners, shifted brackets, or rail misalignment. A rail that is slightly bent or out of alignment may not allow the latch to seat correctly.

Expected outcome: Hardware appears secure and the rail lines up correctly with the latch points.

Do not continue using the stretcher if alignment or hardware security is questionable.

Compare to the Opposite Side Rail

If the opposite rail is functioning normally, compare latch feel, movement, handle return, rail height, and locking position.

Expected outcome: The suspect rail behaves the same as the functioning rail.

Why it matters: A side-to-side comparison can quickly reveal abnormal movement or incomplete latch engagement.

Perform a Final Safety Check

After any minor external correction, raise and lower the rail several times and confirm it locks securely every time.

Expected outcome: Side rail operation is repeatable, secure, and safe.

If the rail passes consistently, document the check and return the stretcher to service.

If the Problem Persists

If the side rail still does not latch, release, move smoothly, or stay locked after external checks, common user-correctable causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely related to the rail latch mechanism, linkage, hinge assembly, mounting hardware, or structural alignment.

The stretcher should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A side rail that does not lock securely is a patient safety risk and should not be treated as a minor convenience issue.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a questionable side rail while a patient is relying on it for positioning or fall prevention. Move the patient to another stretcher first, then evaluate the rail only when it is safe to do so. Maintaining patient safety and care continuity comes before troubleshooting the equipment.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported the left side rail on the Hausted Horizon 462 stretcher would not stay latched in the raised position."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Inspection found the side rail latch did not fully engage after repeated raise/lower testing, with no bedding or accessory obstruction present."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed stretcher from service, labeled Out of Service, and routed for repair evaluation due to unreliable side rail latch operation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Which side rail was affected
  • Whether the rail would not raise, lower, latch, or release
  • Whether the latch clicked or failed to seat
  • Any visible bending, cracks, missing hardware, or loose fasteners
  • Whether bedding, straps, or accessories were obstructing movement
  • Whether the release handle returned normally
  • Whether the opposite rail operated normally
  • Whether the rail failed during repeated testing
  • Any alarm behavior or staff-reported safety concern
  • Any accessories swapped, removed, or found interfering with rail movement
  • Any power behavior or powered accessory involvement, if applicable
  • Environmental factors such as tight room spacing, transport damage, wet floors, or repeated impact
  • Indicator lights or labels present on the stretcher, if applicable
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Side rail failures should be handled as patient safety concerns, not just mechanical complaints. Start with simple external causes such as obstructions, visible damage, and latch engagement, but remove the stretcher from service when the rail cannot be proven reliable. Clear CCR documentation helps show what was reported, what was checked, and why the stretcher was either returned to use or escalated for repair.

That is successful troubleshooting.
