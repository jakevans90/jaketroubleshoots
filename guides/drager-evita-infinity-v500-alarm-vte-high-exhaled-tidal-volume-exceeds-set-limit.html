Drager Evita V500

Alarm: VTE high exhaled tidal volume exceeds set limit

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

Alarm: VTE high indicating excessive exhaled tidal volume causing potential overventilation or unsafe delivery conditions.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s respiratory status
  • Confirm adequate ventilation and oxygenation
  • Transition to a backup ventilator or manual ventilation if needed

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the “VTE high” alarm is present on the display
  • Observe if it occurs during mandatory or spontaneous breaths
  • Check for higher-than-expected tidal volume readings
  • Note if the alarm repeats consistently with each breath

Check Ventilation Settings (Most Common Cause)

  • Review set tidal volume (VT)
  • Check pressure support or inspiratory pressure levels
  • Verify alarm limits are appropriately configured

Check for Circuit Leaks or Compensation Issues

  • Inspect all breathing circuit connections
  • Look for loose fittings or partial disconnections
  • Verify proper placement of filters and humidifier components

Assess Patient Condition and Interaction

  • Check for strong spontaneous breathing efforts
  • Observe for coughing or ventilator asynchrony
  • Coordinate with clinical staff for patient assessment

Run System Self Test (SST)

  • Perform the System Self Test per manufacturer procedure
  • Ensure the test completes without errors

Run Extended System Test (EST) if Needed

  • Perform Extended System Test if the issue persists after SST
  • Evaluate for deeper system or sensor-related faults

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm continues after external causes have been ruled out and system tests have been completed, this indicates a likely internal sensing or control issue.

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label the device Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a ventilator actively delivering excessive tidal volumes on a patient. Always move the patient to a safe, functioning device first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming VTE high with elevated tidal volumes observed during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Settings verified and circuit checked with no external issues found; system tests completed with persistent alarm indicating internal issue."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service, labeled out of service, and sent for further evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

High tidal volume alarms represent a direct patient safety concern. Always verify settings and external factors first, then escalate appropriately when the condition persists.

That is successful troubleshooting.
