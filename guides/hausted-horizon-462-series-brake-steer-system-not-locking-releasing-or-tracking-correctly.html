Hausted Horizon 462 Series

Brake/steer system not locking, releasing, or tracking correctly

Asset Type

Stretcher

Manufacturer

Hausted

Model

Horizon 462 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting brake, steer, caster lock, pedal linkage, or tracking issues that affect safe stretcher movement or parking.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Remove the stretcher from patient use if the brake/steer system is unreliable, hard to control, or will not hold position. Transfer the patient to another safe surface before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: The stretcher is evaluated without risk of unintended movement.

If this resolves the immediate safety concern, stop and document the action.

Place the Stretcher on a Flat, Clean Floor

Test the brake and steer system on a level surface free of debris, cords, thresholds, floor mats, or uneven flooring.

Expected outcome: Floor conditions are ruled out as the cause of poor tracking or brake performance.

Why it matters: A stretcher may appear to pull, drift, or fail to lock if tested on an uneven or contaminated floor.

Verify the Reported Condition

Cycle the brake/steer pedal through each position: brake, neutral, and steer if equipped. Confirm whether the issue is failure to lock, failure to release, poor tracking, or a pedal that does not fully engage.

Expected outcome: The failure mode is clearly identified before adjustments or repair decisions are made.

Check for Obvious Pedal Obstruction or Incomplete Pedal Travel

Inspect around the brake/steer pedals for debris, dropped supplies, damaged covers, bent pedal arms, or anything preventing full movement.

Expected outcome: Pedal moves fully into each position with a positive feel.

If debris or obstruction is found and removed, retest. If normal operation returns, stop.

Inspect All Casters and Wheels Externally

Check for flat spots, missing tread, wrapped string, tape, hair, floor wax buildup, damaged bearings, loose hardware, or uneven wheel movement.

Expected outcome: All wheels rotate and swivel freely when released.

Why it matters: A caster problem can mimic a brake/steer failure by causing dragging, pulling, or poor tracking.

Confirm Brake Engagement at Each Caster

With the stretcher unloaded and on a flat surface, place the pedal in the brake position and gently attempt to move the stretcher. Observe whether one or more casters still roll or swivel.

Expected outcome: The stretcher should resist movement and remain parked.

If one caster does not lock while others do, suspect caster brake linkage, adjustment, or caster assembly damage.

Confirm Brake Release at Each Caster

Move the pedal to neutral/released position and push the stretcher forward, backward, and side-to-side.

Expected outcome: The stretcher should roll freely without dragging.

If a wheel remains partially locked or drags, inspect that caster area for linkage binding, brake pad contact, or wheel damage.

Check Steer Function and Tracking

Place the pedal in steer mode and push the stretcher in a straight path. Confirm whether the designated steer caster locks directionally and whether the stretcher tracks straight without excessive pulling.

Expected outcome: The stretcher should track predictably with the steer caster engaged.

If steer does not engage or the stretcher wanders, suspect steer lock engagement, caster alignment, worn caster components, or linkage issues.

Inspect Visible Brake/Steer Linkage

Without deep disassembly, look underneath for bent rods, disconnected linkage, missing hardware, loose fasteners, broken springs, or obvious damage.

Expected outcome: Linkage should move consistently when the pedal is cycled.

If linkage is disconnected, bent, or damaged, remove the stretcher from service for repair.

Listen and Feel for Abnormal Engagement

Cycle the pedal slowly and note grinding, popping, excessive looseness, no resistance, or failure to detent into position.

Expected outcome: Pedal should have controlled movement and clear position changes.

Why it matters: Abnormal pedal feel often points to mechanical wear or linkage failure rather than user error.

Check for Frame or Caster Mount Damage

Inspect caster mounting points, frame corners, and lower structure for impact damage, cracks, deformation, loose bolts, or signs the stretcher was struck or dropped.

Expected outcome: Caster mounts and frame structure should be secure and aligned.

If frame or caster mount damage is found, remove from service. Do not return to use until repaired and verified.

Perform a Final Functional Check if No Damage Is Found

Cycle through brake, neutral, and steer several times. Test locking, releasing, turning, and straight tracking on a flat surface.

Expected outcome: Brake locks securely, neutral rolls freely, and steer tracks correctly.

If the stretcher passes consistently, return to service according to facility policy.

If the Problem Persists

If the brake/steer system still does not lock, release, or track correctly after external checks, common surface, caster, and obstruction causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely internal mechanical linkage wear, caster assembly failure, brake mechanism damage, or frame/caster alignment problems.

The stretcher should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A stretcher with unreliable brakes or steering is a patient and staff safety risk and should not remain available for clinical use.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot brake or steer problems while the stretcher is occupied unless there is an immediate safety need. Move the patient to another safe surface first. A stretcher that will not brake reliably can roll during transfer, transport, cleaning, or patient positioning.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported the Hausted Horizon 462 Series stretcher brake/steer pedal would not lock the stretcher securely and the unit pulled during transport."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found debris wrapped around one caster and brake engagement inconsistent at the right foot-end caster during functional testing."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed debris, inspected caster movement, cycled brake/neutral/steer positions, and verified stretcher locked, released, and tracked correctly before returning to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Whether the issue involved brake, neutral, steer, or all positions
  • Which caster failed to lock or release
  • Whether the stretcher pulled left or right during transport
  • Floor surface where the issue was reported
  • Pedal position and feel
  • Caster condition
  • Any wrapped debris, buildup, or flat spots
  • Visible linkage condition
  • Loose, bent, or missing hardware
  • Alarm behavior or reported safety concern
  • Accessories swapped or removed during testing
  • Power behavior, if any powered accessory or powered stretcher feature was involved
  • Environmental factors such as flooring, thresholds, cords, mats, wet floors, or debris
  • Indicator lights, if any powered accessory or related control indicator was involved
  • Final device status: returned to service or removed from service

Final Thought

Brake and steer issues should always be treated as safety concerns first. Start with the floor, pedal travel, casters, wheels, and visible linkage before assuming deeper mechanical failure. If the stretcher cannot be proven safe and predictable, remove it from service and document the findings clearly using the CCR method.

That is successful troubleshooting.
