Stryker System 7 Power Tools

Motor failure or reduced torque

Asset Type

Surgical Power Tool System

Manufacturer

Stryker

Model

System 7 Power Tools

What This Guide Helps With

Identifies causes when the motor operates weakly or intermittently, impacting surgical performance and procedural efficiency.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm no active surgery is dependent on the affected tool. Move patient to an alternate power tool to prevent procedural delay.

Verify Power Source

  • Check battery charge level; ensure fully charged and seated correctly.
  • Swap with a known good battery if available.
  • Expected Outcome: Motor should operate at full torque with a good battery.

Inspect Handpiece Connection

  • Remove and reconnect handpiece to console, ensuring pins align and no debris is present.
  • Check for corrosion, bent pins, or damaged connectors.
  • Expected Outcome: Proper connection allows full motor function.

Check Motor Function Without Load

  • Activate motor in air (no surgical load) to observe speed and torque.
  • Expected Outcome: Motor should run smoothly; stuttering indicates possible motor or handpiece issue.

Test Alternate Handpiece or Console Port

  • Swap with a different handpiece or console port to isolate the problem.
  • Expected Outcome: If torque returns to normal, original handpiece or port may be faulty.

Examine Settings and Mode Selection

  • Verify torque, speed, and mode settings on the console match intended surgical requirements.
  • Expected Outcome: Misconfigured settings can mimic reduced torque.

Observe for Unusual Sounds or Heat

  • Listen for grinding, clicking, or abnormal vibration.
  • Check for excessive warmth after brief use.
  • Expected Outcome: Unusual behavior can indicate internal motor wear or damage.

If the Problem Persists

After external causes are ruled out, the motor issue is likely internal.

  • Remove the tool from service.
  • Label Out of Service.
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation by qualified personnel.

Knowing when to stop prevents further damage and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot the motor while the tool is in active surgery. Always move the patient to an alternate device first. Use caution when testing spinning components.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Motor on System 7 handpiece operates weakly or stalls under load."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery and handpiece connection verified; motor performance remains below expected torque."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Tool removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Battery charge levels checked
  • Handpiece swapped/tested
  • Console port tested
  • Abnormal sounds or heat noted
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety comes first. Follow logical, stepwise troubleshooting from simple external checks to more involved diagnostics. Proper documentation and escalation preserve tool integrity and surgical readiness.

That is successful troubleshooting.
