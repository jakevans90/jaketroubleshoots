Getinge GSS Series Washer-Disinfector

Heating Failure Preventing Disinfection Temperature

Asset Type

Washer-Disinfector

Manufacturer

Getinge

Model

GSS Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the GSS Series washer-disinfector fails to reach the required disinfection temperature. This prevents proper thermal disinfection and may trigger alarms. The focus is on external, verifiable checks before assuming internal heater failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Only troubleshoot the heater when the chamber is empty and the device is off.
  • Do not attempt disinfection on active patient instruments if temperature cannot be verified.

Verify Power Supply

  • Check the device is plugged into a dedicated outlet with proper voltage and grounding.
  • Ensure no tripped breakers or blown fuses are present.

Why: Heater elements require full power; low or unstable voltage can prevent heating.

Check Water Supply and Inlet Temperature

  • Ensure the inlet water valve is open and supply pressure is within specifications.
  • Verify incoming water temperature is not excessively low.

Why: Some washer-disinfectors adjust heater load based on inlet water temperature; extremely cold water can delay or prevent reaching setpoint.

Inspect for Water Flow Obstructions

  • Check filters, strainers, and inlet hoses for clogs.
  • Ensure spray arms move freely and no debris is blocking circulation.

Why: Poor circulation reduces heat transfer, causing slow or incomplete heating.

Confirm Cycle and Program Settings

  • Verify the selected program requires thermal disinfection and is not a cold or pre-rinse cycle.
  • Check that any optional “eco” or “low temp” modes are not active.

Why: Incorrect program selection may prevent heaters from activating fully.

Check Heater Indicators and Alarms

  • Observe any heater fault indicators or error codes on the display.
  • Document exact messages for work order and potential vendor support.

Why: Built-in diagnostics can help determine whether the heater circuit or control system is at fault.

Inspect External Connections

  • Check terminal connections to the heater assembly for looseness or corrosion.

Why: Poor connections can prevent power delivery to heaters.

If the Problem Persists

External causes have been ruled out; likely causes include:

  • Heater element failure
  • Heater relay or controller malfunction
  • Temperature sensor (thermocouple/RTD) failure

Action: Remove the washer-disinfector from service. Label it Out of Service. Send for repair or bench evaluation by trained service personnel. Avoid attempting internal heater replacement or control board repair on-site.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not troubleshoot on an active patient.
  • Move instruments to another functioning washer-disinfector to maintain patient safety.
  • Only troubleshoot the heater when the chamber is empty and the device is off.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reports that the washer-disinfector does not reach disinfection temperature and cycle alarms for ‘temperature not reached.’”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Verified power supply, water supply, circulation, and program selection. Heater failed to reach setpoint; no external faults identified.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair to vendor service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet voltage and test results
  • Inlet water temperature and pressure
  • Observed error codes
  • Heater indicator lights status
  • Spray arm circulation status

Final Thought

Patient safety depends on proper disinfection; logic-based troubleshooting ensures external causes are ruled out before escalating to repair. Accurate documentation provides accountability and facilitates timely service.

That is successful troubleshooting.
