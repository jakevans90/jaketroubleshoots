Midmark M9 UltraClave Series

Control Board or Keypad Failure

Asset Type

Sterilizer

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

M9 UltraClave Series

What This Guide Helps With

Unresponsive keypad, incorrect inputs, display issues, or inability to start cycles caused by power, connection, or interface-related problems.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the sterilizer from active use and confirm alternate sterilization is available.
  • Expected: No impact to patient care workflow.
  • Why it matters: A non-functional sterilizer compromises instrument sterility.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Power on the unit and attempt multiple keypad inputs. Observe display behavior and response time.
  • Expected: No response, delayed response, or incorrect selections.
  • Why it matters: Confirms true keypad/control issue vs user error.

Check Power Source

  • Verify the unit is plugged into a known working outlet. Test the outlet with another device.
  • Expected: Stable power and consistent display illumination.
  • Why it matters: Low or unstable power can cause keypad or display malfunction.
  • If no power → restore power and retest

Perform a Power Reset

  • Turn the unit off, unplug it for 30–60 seconds, then reconnect and power back on.
  • Expected: System reboots and keypad responds normally.
  • Why it matters: Resets minor control board or software glitches.
  • If resolved → return to service

Inspect Display and Indicator Behavior

  • Observe for flickering, partial display, dim screen, or error messages.
  • Expected: Clear, stable display with normal brightness.
  • Why it matters: Display issues can indicate power supply or control board instability.

Check Keypad Surface Condition

  • Inspect the keypad for damage, stuck buttons, worn membrane, or debris/liquid intrusion.
  • Expected: Buttons move freely with no sticking or visible damage.
  • Why it matters: Physical keypad failure is a common external cause.
  • If stuck/damaged → clean or replace keypad and retest

Inspect for Moisture or Contamination

  • Check around the keypad and control panel for signs of water intrusion, residue, or cleaning fluid buildup.
  • Expected: Clean, dry control surface.
  • Why it matters: Moisture can short keypad contacts or affect responsiveness.
  • If contamination found → clean, dry thoroughly, and retest

Check for Loose External Connections (If Accessible)

  • Inspect any accessible external connectors to the control panel (without disassembly).
  • Expected: All visible connections secure.
  • Why it matters: Loose connections can interrupt keypad signals.

Observe for Error Codes or Boot Issues

  • Watch startup behavior for abnormal delays, frozen screens, or recurring errors.
  • Expected: Normal boot sequence with responsive interface.
  • Why it matters: Helps differentiate between keypad failure and control board failure.

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes (power, keypad condition, contamination, and reset) have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal to the control board or keypad circuitry.

  • Remove the unit from service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and aligns with proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot while instruments are needed for active procedures. Always transition to a backup sterilizer before beginning troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Sterilizer keypad not responding and unable to start cycle."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Failed keypad membrane due to wear and contamination."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleaned and tested keypad; replaced keypad assembly when issue persisted."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Power reset performed
  • Keypad condition (stuck, worn, intact)
  • Display behavior (normal, dim, flickering)
  • Presence of moisture or residue
  • Error codes observed
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple, external checks before assuming internal failure. Protect patient safety, avoid unnecessary disassembly, and document clearly. Recognizing when escalation is needed is just as important as resolving the issue.

That is successful troubleshooting.
