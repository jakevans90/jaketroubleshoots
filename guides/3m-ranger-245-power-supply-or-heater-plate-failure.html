3M Ranger 245

Power Supply or Heater Plate Failure

Asset Type

Fluid & Blood Warmer

Manufacturer

3M

Model

Ranger 245

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a 3M Ranger 245 fluid and blood warmer does not power on, shuts down unexpectedly, or fails to generate heat due to potential power supply or heater plate problems. The goal is to rule out common external causes such as power source issues, cord problems, environmental conditions, or disposable set issues before assuming an internal power or heating component failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify the AC power source

  • Confirm the unit is plugged into a known-good hospital-grade outlet. Test the outlet with another device or outlet tester.
  • Power issues are a common cause of devices appearing completely inoperative.

Inspect the power cord and plug

  • Check the Ranger 245 power cord for:
    • Loose connections
    • Bent prongs
    • Frayed or damaged insulation
  • If the cord is removable and damage is suspected, replace it with a known-good cord if available.

Confirm the power switch operation

  • Turn the unit on and observe the front panel indicators or display.
  • Watch for:
    • No lights at all
    • Lights that briefly illuminate and then shut off
    • Fault or temperature alarms
  • These symptoms may help differentiate between power input issues and heating system faults.

Check for system fault or temperature alarms

  • If the unit powers on but reports temperature or system faults, note the alarm behavior and messages displayed.
  • Some alarms may occur if the heater cannot properly regulate temperature or if the system detects abnormal heating conditions.

Verify proper disposable cassette installation

  • Ensure a Ranger disposable warming cassette is properly installed and fully seated.
  • The heater plate relies on correct cassette placement to transfer heat effectively. Improper installation may cause heating issues or fault conditions.

Inspect airflow around the heater unit

  • Confirm the heater unit ventilation areas are not blocked by drapes, equipment, or debris.
  • Restricted airflow can cause overheating protection circuits to activate, resulting in shutdown or fault alarms.

Check for unusual heat, smell, or noise

  • During power-up, observe for:
    • Burning smell
    • Excessive heat from the unit
    • Clicking or abnormal internal sounds
  • These symptoms may indicate internal power supply or heater component failure.

Power cycle the device

  • Turn the unit off, unplug it from AC power for approximately 30 seconds, then reconnect and power it back on.
  • This can reset certain fault conditions and confirm whether the issue is persistent.

Observe temperature behavior during operation

  • If the unit powers on but fails to warm fluids properly, monitor the temperature indicators or alarms during operation.
  • Failure to reach or maintain temperature after external checks may indicate a heater plate or temperature control failure.

If the Problem Persists

If power supply problems or heating failures continue after verifying the outlet, power cord, cassette installation, and airflow, the issue is likely internal.

At this point:

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label the unit Out of Service
  • Send the device for bench evaluation or manufacturer repair

Internal power supply components or heater plate assemblies require controlled repair procedures and should not be serviced during routine clinical troubleshooting.

Knowing when to escalate the repair is an important part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Always ensure the device is not connected to an active patient infusion before troubleshooting heating or power issues. If warming therapy is required, transfer the patient to another approved warming device before removing the Ranger unit from service.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nursing reported the Ranger 245 fluid warmer would not power on during setup for a blood transfusion.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Outlet verified functional. Power cord intact. Unit showed no power indicators after multiple attempts to start. Likely internal power supply failure.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service and labeled Out of Service. Unit sent to Clinical Engineering shop for further evaluation and repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified working
  • Power cord inspected or swapped
  • Disposable cassette installed correctly
  • Alarm messages or indicators observed
  • Airflow around heater checked
  • Any unusual heat, smell, or sounds noted
  • Final device status (returned to service or removed)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with verifying simple external factors before assuming internal failure. By checking power sources, connections, disposable components, and airflow first, Clinical Engineering can quickly rule out common issues and ensure patient care is not delayed. When internal heating or power faults are suspected, removing the device from service and documenting findings properly protects both patient safety and equipment reliability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
