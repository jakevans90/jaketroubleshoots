Drager Oxylog 3000 Plus

Battery Not Charging or Shortened Runtime

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Oxylog 3000 Plus

What This Guide Helps With

Ventilator not charging, losing battery quickly, or shutting down early due to power source, battery condition, or connection issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the ventilator is in use, immediately transition the patient to another ventilator before troubleshooting.

Expected: Patient ventilation continues without interruption.

Why it matters: Power failure on a ventilator is a critical patient safety risk.

Verify External Power Source

  • Plug in a known working device to confirm outlet power
  • Try a different outlet
  • If using ambulance/DC power, verify vehicle power is active

Expected: Confirmed stable external power source.

Why it matters: Loss of input power prevents charging and mimics battery failure.

Inspect AC/DC Power Adapter and Cable

  • Ensure connections are fully seated at ventilator and outlet
  • Inspect for frayed wires, bent pins, or loose connectors
  • Swap with a known good adapter if available

Expected: Secure, undamaged power connection.

Why it matters: Damaged adapters are a common cause of charging failure.

Check Charging Indicators on the Ventilator

  • Confirm charging icon or LED is present when plugged in
  • Note any error messages or absence of charging indication

Expected: Charging indicator activates when external power is connected.

Why it matters: Helps determine if the issue is input power vs internal charging circuit.

Verify Battery Installation and Seating

  • Remove and reseat the battery pack
  • Ensure contacts are clean and aligned
  • Inspect for corrosion or debris on contacts

Expected: Battery is firmly seated with clean contact points.

Why it matters: Poor contact prevents charging and proper power transfer.

Evaluate Battery Condition and Age

  • Review battery age and service history
  • Note if runtime is significantly shorter than expected
  • If available, test with a known good battery

Expected: Normal runtime with a functional battery.

Why it matters: Batteries naturally degrade and lose capacity over time.

Perform Functional Runtime Test

  • Fully charge (if possible), then disconnect AC power
  • Run the ventilator under normal settings
  • Monitor runtime duration

Expected: Runtime meets manufacturer expectations.

Why it matters: Confirms whether issue is charging failure or reduced battery capacity.

Check for Overheating or Environmental Factors

  • Ensure vents are not blocked
  • Check for excessive heat during charging or use

Expected: Normal operating temperature.

Why it matters: Heat can reduce charging efficiency and battery lifespan.

If the Problem Persists

If external power, cables, and battery condition have been verified, the issue is likely internal (charging circuit or power management system).

Remove the ventilator from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and ensures safe equipment use.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot battery or power issues while a patient is actively ventilated. Always transition to a backup ventilator before beginning any checks.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator not charging and losing power quickly during transport use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Degraded battery with significantly reduced runtime confirmed during functional testing."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced battery and verified normal charging and runtime operation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Power adapter swapped
  • Charging indicator status
  • Battery age and condition
  • Runtime test results
  • Any overheating observed
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Ventilator power issues must be approached with urgency and caution. Always rule out external power and battery condition first, and escalate quickly when internal faults are suspected. Proper documentation ensures reliability and patient safety.

That is successful troubleshooting.
