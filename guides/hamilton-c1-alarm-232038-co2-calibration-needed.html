Hamilton C1

Alarm: 232038 CO2 calibration needed

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm: 232038 CO2 calibration needed on a Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates the CO2 sensor or CO2 monitoring system requires calibration before CO2 readings should be considered reliable.

This condition can affect displayed CO2 values, waveform reliability, trending, alarm confidence, and staff trust in the ventilator monitoring setup.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot or calibrate the CO2 sensor on an active patient if reliable ventilation or monitoring could be affected.
  • Remove the ventilator from patient use if the CO2 alarm affects clinical monitoring, alarm reliability, or staff confidence in the device.
  • If the patient is actively ventilated, transfer the patient to another ventilator or approved backup monitoring method before troubleshooting.
  • Do not rely on CO2 values from a ventilator that is actively requesting CO2 calibration.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the Hamilton C1 is displaying Alarm: 232038 CO2 calibration needed.
  • Observe whether the alarm appears during startup, after connecting the CO2 sensor, after replacing the airway adapter, during patient setup, or intermittently during use.
  • Check whether CO2 values are missing, unstable, incorrect, or unavailable.
  • Confirm the issue is reproducible before assuming an internal ventilator failure.

Confirm the CO2 Sensor Is Connected Properly

  • Inspect the CO2 sensor connection at the ventilator and sensor cable.
  • Check for a loose connector, bent pins, damaged cable, intermittent connection, sensor not fully seated, or wrong sensor type.
  • Reconnect the sensor securely.
  • Verify the ventilator recognizes the CO2 sensor consistently.

Inspect the Airway Adapter

  • Check the CO2 airway adapter for cracks, moisture, secretions, contamination, incorrect adapter type, poor sensor fit, or obstruction in the optical path.
  • Replace the airway adapter if it is dirty, wet, cracked, damaged, or questionable.
  • Confirm the sensor fits securely on a clean, dry, correct airway adapter.
  • A dirty or wet optical path can prevent reliable CO2 calibration and measurement.

Remove the Sensor From Patient or Exhaled CO2 Before Calibration

  • Make sure CO2 calibration is performed away from the patient circuit, exhaled gas, and any CO2 source.
  • Use room air conditions as required by the approved calibration procedure.
  • Do not perform calibration while the sensor is exposed to patient breath or recently exhaled CO2.
  • Calibrating in the presence of CO2 can cause inaccurate readings after calibration.

Perform CO2 Calibration

  • Perform CO2 calibration according to the approved Hamilton C1 procedure.
  • Allow the sensor to complete the calibration process without disturbing the cable, sensor, or adapter.
  • Confirm calibration completes successfully and the alarm clears.
  • If calibration clears the alarm, confirm stable CO2 readings before returning the ventilator to service.

Repeat With a Known-Good CO2 Sensor or Adapter

  • If calibration fails or the alarm returns, test with a known-good CO2 sensor and airway adapter if available.
  • Swap only one item at a time when possible.
  • Replace the airway adapter first if it is wet, dirty, cracked, or suspect.
  • Replace the CO2 sensor or cable assembly if the adapter is known good and the alarm remains.

Check for Environmental or Setup Issues

  • Confirm the calibration area is appropriate and stable.
  • Check whether calibration was attempted near exhaled gas, moisture, cleaning residue, nebulized medication, secretions, or other contamination.
  • Allow the sensor to dry fully if it was recently cleaned.
  • Avoid handling, moving, or disturbing the sensor during the calibration process.

Confirm Alarm Resolution

  • Reconnect the CO2 sensor and airway adapter as appropriate after calibration.
  • Verify Alarm 232038 is cleared.
  • Confirm CO2 values display normally.
  • Confirm the CO2 waveform or trend appears stable if available.
  • Check that no related CO2 sensor alarms appear.
  • Complete any required pre-use check or facility verification before returning the ventilator to service.

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm continues after checking the sensor connection, airway adapter, calibration environment, and known-good accessories, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent CO2 calibration needed alarm may indicate a failing CO2 sensor, damaged sensor cable, accessory recognition issue, or internal CO2 monitoring/interface fault.

The ventilator should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Do not keep a ventilator in clinical use if CO2 monitoring remains unreliable and the alarm cannot be cleared.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot or calibrate CO2 monitoring on an active patient if it could affect care, monitoring reliability, or staff confidence in the device. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or alternate approved monitoring method first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator displayed Alarm 232038 CO2 calibration needed. Staff reported CO2 monitoring was unavailable or not considered reliable."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"CO2 sensor/system required calibration. CO2 sensor connection and airway adapter were inspected. Calibration was performed away from patient circuit and exhaled CO2 source."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Performed CO2 calibration per procedure. Verified CO2 sensor connection and airway adapter condition. Alarm cleared, CO2 monitoring displayed normally, and ventilator was returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Alarm 232038 should be treated as a reliability warning for CO2 monitoring. Start with patient safety, verify the alarm, check the external CO2 sensor and airway adapter, perform calibration correctly, and escalate if the alarm persists after common causes are ruled out. Clear documentation helps show whether the issue was calibration-related, accessory-related, or likely internal.

That is successful troubleshooting.
