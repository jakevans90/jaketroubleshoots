GE Healthcare Panda Warmer

Alarm: Temp Probe Failure

Asset Type

Infant Warmer

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Panda Warmer

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Temp Probe Failure alarm when patient temperature input is unavailable, abnormal, or inconsistent. This condition may prevent reliable patient temperature monitoring or servo-controlled warming.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the warmer is being used for an infant, notify clinical staff immediately.
  • Move the patient to an appropriate alternate warming and temperature-monitoring method before troubleshooting.
  • Do not rely on patient temperature readings or servo-controlled warming while Temp Probe Failure remains active.

Expected outcome: The patient is no longer dependent on equipment with unreliable patient-temperature feedback.

Why it matters: A failed or mismatched patient probe signal can prevent accurate temperature monitoring and may compromise safe servo-controlled warming.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • With the warmer removed from patient use, confirm that the display shows Temp Probe Failure.
  • Document whether the alarm occurs at startup, when the patient probe is connected, when servo control is selected, during operation, or when the probe cable is moved.
  • Observe for missing or abnormal patient temperature display, inability to maintain servo operation, or repeated alarm activation after reconnecting the probe.

Expected outcome: The alarm is confirmed and its relationship to the patient probe input path is documented.

Why it matters: Confirming repeatable behavior helps distinguish a true probe-path failure from an isolated connection issue.

3. Inspect the Patient Probe and External Connection

  • Confirm that the correct compatible patient temperature probe is being used.
  • Verify that the probe connector is fully inserted into the patient probe connection.
  • Inspect the probe and accessible cable for cuts, pinching, fraying, exposed conductors, contamination, fluid intrusion, bent contacts, or other visible damage.
  • Disconnect and reconnect the probe securely, then check whether the alarm clears or returns.
  • Observe whether gently repositioning the external cable causes intermittent alarm behavior.

Expected outcome: A loose or contaminated connection is corrected, or visible probe damage is identified.

Why it matters: A disconnected, damaged, or intermittent probe is an accessible cause of unreliable patient temperature input.

4. Test With a Known-Good Compatible Patient Probe

  • Replace the suspected probe with a known-good compatible patient temperature probe, when available.
  • Reconnect the replacement probe securely and observe whether Temp Probe Failure returns.
  • If the alarm clears, remove the failed probe from use and document the replacement.
  • If the alarm remains, do not return the warmer to clinical use.

Expected outcome: The probe is identified as the cause, or the warmer is determined to require further bench evaluation.

Why it matters: Substituting a known-good probe is a safe external isolation step for determining whether the accessory is defective.

5. Inspect Accessible External Conditions

  • Confirm that the probe connector area is clean and dry.
  • Check that the probe cable is not trapped, stretched, tightly bent, or routed where movement could interrupt the connection.
  • Check for recent cleaning, fluid exposure, transport damage, or accessory changes that may be associated with the alarm.
  • Document whether the alarm is constant or intermittent after external checks are completed.

Expected outcome: External contributors are corrected or ruled out before escalation.

Why it matters: Environmental or accessory-related factors can cause intermittent probe input failures without requiring internal evaluation.

6. Verify Alarm Status After External Troubleshooting

  • After reseating the probe or installing a known-good compatible probe, confirm whether Temp Probe Failure remains present.
  • If the alarm has cleared, complete required operational verification according to facility procedure before considering return to service.
  • If the alarm returns or remains active, discontinue troubleshooting at the clinical-use level and escalate for repair evaluation.

Expected outcome: The warmer either passes external correction and verification or is appropriately removed from service for further evaluation.

Why it matters: Persistent temperature-probe failure after accessory checks may indicate an internal probe-input or control-related failure.

If the Problem Persists

If Temp Probe Failure remains after confirming the correct probe, inspecting the external probe and connector, reseating the connection, and testing with a known-good compatible patient probe, external and common accessory causes have been ruled out.

A persistent alarm may indicate a device-level patient temperature input or control failure.

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label the device Out of Service.
  • Send the warmer for repair or bench evaluation according to facility procedure.
  • Return the warmer to clinical use only after successful repair and complete operational verification.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup warming and temperature-monitoring method first, then evaluate the warmer and temperature probe away from clinical use.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"GE Healthcare Panda Warmer displayed Temp Probe Failure alarm during attempted servo-controlled warming. Patient temperature input was unavailable or inconsistent."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Confirmed Temp Probe Failure alarm. Original patient temperature probe showed visible cable damage and alarm remained associated with the probe until replaced with a known-good compatible probe."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed warmer from patient use, replaced defective patient temperature probe with a known-good compatible probe, verified alarm cleared and temperature input operated normally, completed operational verification, and returned unit to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The Temp Probe Failure alarm means the Panda Warmer cannot be trusted for reliable patient temperature input until the cause is identified and corrected. Safe troubleshooting protects the patient first, evaluates the external probe path logically, escalates persistent failures appropriately, and clearly documents the outcome.

That is successful troubleshooting.
