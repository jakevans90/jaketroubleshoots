Invacare Perfecto2

Dirty Or Restricted Cabinet / Intake Filters

Asset Type

Oxygen Concentrator

Manufacturer

Invacare

Model

Perfecto2

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting poor airflow, overheating, alarms, or reduced performance caused by dirty or restricted external cabinet and intake filters.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

If the Perfecto2 is in use on a patient and an alarm, low oxygen concern, or airflow issue is reported, have clinical staff move the patient to an alternate oxygen source before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: The patient is supported by a verified oxygen source before the concentrator is inspected.

If patient therapy is restored with another device, continue troubleshooting only when the concentrator is safe to remove from service.

Verify the Reported Issue

Confirm the complaint with the reporting staff.

Ask whether the unit had:

  • Reduced flow output
  • Low oxygen alarm
  • Excessive noise
  • Overheating
  • Poor cooling airflow
  • Visible dust buildup
  • Frequent filter-related service concerns

Expected outcome: The issue is confirmed and tied to airflow restriction, contamination, or poor cooling.

Inspect the Exterior Cabinet

Look over the cabinet vents, rear intake area, side panels, and lower cabinet openings.

Check for dust mats, lint, hair, tape, plastic bags, bedding, or wall obstruction blocking airflow.

Expected outcome: Cabinet vents are open and unobstructed.

If a visible obstruction is found, remove it, clean the exterior, retest the unit, and stop if normal operation returns.

Check Unit Placement

Verify the concentrator is not pushed tightly against a wall, curtain, bed, or furniture.

Oxygen concentrators need open airflow for cooling and compressor operation.

Expected outcome: The unit has adequate clearance around air intake and exhaust areas.

If poor placement caused the restriction, reposition the unit, run the device, and confirm normal operation.

Inspect the Cabinet Filter

Remove and inspect the external cabinet filter according to facility procedure.

Look for:

  • Heavy dust buildup
  • Lint
  • Hair
  • Discoloration
  • Moisture
  • Physical damage
  • Filter material breakdown

Expected outcome: The filter is clean, dry, intact, and seated correctly.

If the filter is dirty, clean or replace it per facility policy. If the issue resolves after filter service, document the corrective action and stop.

Check for Missing or Incorrect Filter Installation

Confirm the filter is installed in the correct location and fully seated.

A missing, incorrectly seated, or wrong filter may allow contamination into the cabinet or restrict airflow.

Expected outcome: The correct filter is installed properly.

If the filter was missing or incorrect, replace it with the correct approved filter and inspect for excessive internal dust contamination through visible openings only.

Inspect Intake Path for External Restriction

Without opening the concentrator beyond normal user-serviceable access, inspect the intake area for lint, dust mats, labels, or debris.

Expected outcome: Intake path is visually clear from the outside.

If debris is removable from the outside, clean it carefully and retest the unit.

Power On and Observe Operation

Plug the Perfecto2 into a known-good outlet and turn it on.

Observe for:

  • Normal startup
  • Cooling fan operation
  • Abnormal noise
  • Alarm condition
  • Airflow restriction
  • Unusual heat
  • Burning smell

Expected outcome: The unit starts normally without airflow-related alarms or overheating.

If the device operates normally after filter cleaning, return it to service only after performance checks required by facility policy are completed.

Verify Flow Output Setting and Delivery Path

Check that the flowmeter is set appropriately for testing and that tubing or accessories are not kinked or blocked.

A filter issue may be reported as low output, but tubing, humidifier bottles, or cannulas can also restrict flow.

Expected outcome: Flow path is open and the flowmeter responds normally.

If an accessory restriction is found, replace the accessory and retest.

Check for Recurring Alarm or Overheating After Cleaning

Allow the concentrator to run long enough to confirm the alarm does not return.

Monitor cabinet temperature, airflow noise, and any visual or audible alarm behavior.

Expected outcome: Unit runs normally without recurring airflow, temperature, or oxygen-related alarms.

If alarms return after filters and external restrictions are addressed, remove the unit from service.

If the Problem Persists

If the Perfecto2 continues to alarm, overheat, run loudly, or show reduced output after cabinet filter cleaning, placement correction, and external airflow checks, common external causes have been ruled out.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Possible internal concerns may include restricted internal filters, compressor wear, fan problems, sieve bed performance issues, internal contamination, or oxygen sensing concerns. Clinical Engineering should stop at the appropriate troubleshooting boundary and escalate for repair or full performance evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot an oxygen concentrator while a patient is dependent on it for oxygen therapy. Move the patient to another verified oxygen source first, then evaluate the concentrator in a controlled setting.

Maintain oxygen therapy continuity by using a backup oxygen source before any testing, cleaning, or troubleshooting begins.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Nursing reported the Invacare Perfecto2 was alarming and appeared dusty around the cabinet intake area."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External cabinet filter was heavily restricted with lint and dust, reducing cooling airflow."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed unit from service, cleaned/replaced cabinet filter, cleared exterior intake area, verified normal startup and operation, and returned unit to service after required checks."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested
  • Cabinet filter condition
  • Filter cleaned or replaced
  • Intake area inspected
  • Cabinet vents checked
  • Unit placement verified
  • Alarm behavior before and after cleaning
  • Flow setting during test
  • Tubing or humidifier bottle checked
  • Unusual heat, smell, or noise
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Dirty or restricted filters are a common but important oxygen concentrator issue. Clinical Engineering should start with patient safety, confirm external airflow problems first, clean or replace accessible filters, and escalate if alarms or performance concerns continue. Clear CCR documentation helps show that the device was evaluated logically and safely.

That is successful troubleshooting.
