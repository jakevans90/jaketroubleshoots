Hamilton C1

Alarm: 231017 Blower service required

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm: 231017 Blower service required on the Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates the ventilator has detected a blower service condition, airflow-related issue, service threshold, or internal blower-related fault that may affect ventilation performance.

Because the blower is part of the ventilator air delivery system, this alarm should be handled carefully. External causes such as blocked air intake, dirty filters, poor cooling airflow, power instability, or circuit setup issues should be checked first before assuming an internal blower failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot a blower-related ventilator alarm on an active patient unless the patient is already safely supported.
  • Remove the Hamilton C1 from patient use if ventilation could be affected.
  • Place the patient on an alternate ventilator or approved manual ventilation support per clinical protocol.
  • Notify respiratory therapy that the ventilator has reported a blower service alarm.
  • Do not return the ventilator to patient use until the alarm is understood and required testing has passed.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Power on the ventilator in a safe, non-patient-use environment.
  • Confirm that the displayed alarm is exactly Alarm: 231017 Blower service required.
  • Observe whether the alarm appears during startup, pre-use check, active ventilation, after warm-up, or only during high-demand settings.
  • Check for associated symptoms such as low delivered flow, pressure instability, failed pre-use check, abnormal blower noise, vibration, unusual smell, or overheating.
  • Document when the alarm occurs and whether it clears after restart.

Remove the Device From Clinical Use Until Evaluated

  • Treat this as a service-related ventilator condition.
  • Do not return the ventilator directly to patient care just because the alarm clears after restart.
  • Label the device Out of Service if the alarm is reproducible, unexplained, or associated with abnormal performance.
  • Move the ventilator to a controlled bench area for evaluation.

Check Power Source and Startup Behavior

  • Connect the ventilator to a known-good AC outlet.
  • Confirm the power cord is fully seated at the outlet and ventilator.
  • Inspect for damaged cord, loose plug, unstable power connection, or power interruption.
  • Power cycle the ventilator and observe whether startup is normal.
  • Do not dismiss a repeated blower service alarm as a simple power issue unless the device passes required checks afterward.

Check the Air Inlet Path

  • Inspect the external air intake area for blockage.
  • Confirm the ventilator is not pushed against a wall, blanket, cart surface, equipment, or other object that restricts airflow.
  • Check for dust buildup, lint, debris, or contamination around the air intake.
  • Make sure the ventilator has adequate clearance for air movement and cooling.
  • Correct any external restriction before repeating the check.

Inspect External Filters

  • Inspect the air inlet filter condition.
  • Look for dirt, clogging, moisture, discoloration, damage, or incorrect installation.
  • Replace filters if they are dirty, blocked, wet, damaged, or past their maintenance interval.
  • Use only the correct filter type for the Hamilton C1.
  • After filter replacement, repeat the approved pre-use check or system test if required by facility procedure.

Check Cooling Airflow Around the Device

  • Inspect cooling vents for blockage or dust buildup.
  • Verify the ventilator is not covered by linens or placed in a confined area.
  • Confirm the device is not operating near heat sources or in a high-temperature environment.
  • Listen for abnormal fan or blower noise.
  • Check whether the alarm appears only after the device warms up.

Review Service History and Blower Operating Hours

  • Check preventive maintenance records.
  • Review any prior blower, fan, inlet filter, cooling, or airflow-related repairs.
  • Check blower-related operating hours or service counters if available through approved service access.
  • Determine whether the alarm may be related to a maintenance interval or internal blower service threshold.

Run Approved Pre-Use Check or Self-Test

  • Install a known-good patient circuit and required test lung if appropriate.
  • Perform the approved pre-use check or system test.
  • Observe whether Alarm: 231017 Blower service required returns.
  • Document any failed test step, related technical alarm, abnormal airflow behavior, or pressure instability.
  • Do not return the ventilator to service unless it passes required testing and the alarm does not recur.

Isolate External Versus Device-Related Causes

  • Confirm known-good AC power has been tested.
  • Confirm the air inlet path is clear.
  • Confirm filters are clean, correct, and properly installed.
  • Confirm cooling airflow is not blocked.
  • Confirm a known-good patient circuit has been used.
  • Confirm no obvious external obstruction or setup problem remains.
  • If the alarm persists after these checks, suspect an internal blower service requirement, blower wear, blower control issue, airflow sensing issue, cooling issue, or related internal fault.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm: 231017 Blower service required continues after checking power, inlet airflow, filters, cooling airflow, service history, circuit setup, and pre-use testing, external causes have been ruled out.

Remove the ventilator from service. Label it Out of Service. Do not use it for patient ventilation.

Send the device for repair or bench evaluation. Include the alarm code, displayed message, when it occurs, pre-use check results, service history findings, and all external checks already completed.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or approved manual ventilation method first.

Do not rely on a ventilator with an unresolved blower service alarm, especially if airflow delivery, pressure stability, cooling, or pre-use testing is affected.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator displayed Alarm: 231017 Blower service required during startup/pre-use check. Staff removed device from patient use due to possible blower service condition."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External checks completed. AC power, air inlet path, filter condition, cooling airflow, patient circuit, and service history were reviewed. Alarm persisted, indicating likely internal blower service threshold or blower-related device fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for service-level evaluation/repair. Documented Alarm: 231017 Blower service required, alarm timing, pre-use check result, and completed external troubleshooting checks."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A blower service alarm on a ventilator should be handled with patient safety first, then logical external checks. Confirm the exact alarm, inspect power, air intake, filters, cooling airflow, circuit setup, and service history before escalating.

If the alarm persists after the common external causes are ruled out, stopping and sending the ventilator for service-level evaluation is the correct action. Clear documentation protects the patient, the technician, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
