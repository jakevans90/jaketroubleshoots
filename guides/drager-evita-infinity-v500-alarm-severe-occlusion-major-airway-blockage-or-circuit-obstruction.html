Drager Evita V500

Alarm: Severe occlusion major airway blockage or circuit obstruction

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

Severe occlusion alarm indicating blocked airway or circuit causing little to no airflow to or from the patient.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient
  • Remove from ventilator support if necessary
  • Provide manual ventilation using a bag-valve mask or backup ventilator

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ventilator is displaying a severe occlusion alarm
  • Observe for very high airway pressures
  • Check for minimal or no delivered tidal volume
  • Determine if alarm occurs during inspiration

Check Patient Airway (Most Critical Step)

  • Assess for kinked or blocked endotracheal tube
  • Check for mucus plugs or secretions
  • Evaluate for biting or patient-induced obstruction

Check Breathing Circuit for Obstruction

  • Inspect tubing for kinks or compression
  • Check for fluid or condensation buildup
  • Inspect filters for blockage
  • Ensure no restrictions in tubing path

Check Expiratory Valve and Components

  • Remove and inspect expiratory valve
  • Check for contamination or sticking
  • Ensure proper seating and reinstallation

Isolate Circuit vs Device Issue

  • Replace breathing circuit with a known-good circuit
  • Test ventilator without a patient if safe
  • Observe if alarm clears with new components

Stop When Resolved

  • Confirm normal airway pressures and tidal volumes
  • Clear alarms
  • Return device to service if functioning properly

If the Problem Persists

If airway and circuit are confirmed clear and the alarm continues:

  • External causes have been ruled out
  • Remove the device from service
  • Label the device Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot an occlusion alarm on an active patient. Always move the patient to a backup ventilation source before investigating the device.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming 'Severe occlusion,' no tidal volume delivered during use"

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Kinked breathing circuit causing airflow obstruction (or) no external issue found, suspected internal valve or sensor failure"

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleared obstruction and restored ventilation (or) removed ventilator from service and sent for repair"

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Severe occlusion alarms indicate a high-risk condition where airflow is significantly restricted or completely blocked. Always prioritize patient safety, systematically check external components, and escalate quickly when the issue is not resolved.

That is successful troubleshooting.
