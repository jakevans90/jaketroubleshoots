Hamilton C1

Alarm: 246001 Preventive maintenance required

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm 246001 Preventive maintenance required on a Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates scheduled maintenance is due, which may affect reliability, battery readiness, blower status, filter condition, or service compliance.

A preventive maintenance alarm may not mean immediate device failure, but it should not be ignored or cleared without completing the required maintenance process.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not perform preventive maintenance checks on a ventilator that is actively supporting a patient.
  • Remove the ventilator from patient use if the alarm appears during clinical operation and another ventilator is available.
  • Transfer the patient to another approved ventilator if continued ventilation support is required.
  • Do not reset or clear maintenance status just to silence the alarm.
  • Confirm patient ventilation continues safely on reliable equipment before continuing troubleshooting.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ventilator displays Alarm: 246001 Preventive maintenance required.
  • Observe whether the alarm appears during startup, pre-use check, normal operation, after service interval expiration, or continuously.
  • Check for associated issues such as failed pre-use check, battery warnings, filter warnings, blower-related service messages, reduced runtime, abnormal fan noise, overheating, unusual sounds, or unusual smells.
  • Confirm the preventive maintenance alarm is not being confused with a separate technical fault.

Check Whether the Ventilator Is Due for Scheduled PM

  • Review the equipment maintenance record, CMMS history, asset tag, and last PM completion date.
  • Confirm whether the device is past its scheduled preventive maintenance interval.
  • Check whether the alarm appeared because the PM interval expired, the blower service interval is due, filters are due for replacement, battery checks are due, or a prior PM was completed but not properly documented or reset.
  • Confirm maintenance status matches the service history before returning the ventilator to use.

Inspect External Condition Before Service

  • Inspect the ventilator for physical damage, cracked housing, loose connectors, blocked vents, dust buildup, contamination, missing covers, damaged power cord, damaged oxygen inlet or gas fittings, and damaged patient circuit connections.
  • Do not treat preventive maintenance as only a checklist reset.
  • Address visible external damage, contamination, or blocked airflow before clearing the device for clinical use.

Check Power Source and Charging Status

  • Connect the ventilator to a known-good AC outlet.
  • Confirm external power is recognized.
  • Inspect the AC power cord, power adapter, and strain relief.
  • Check battery charging indicators.
  • Review battery condition if available.
  • Look for weak batteries, poor charging, damaged cords, or unreliable power connections.

Check Battery Condition

  • Confirm the batteries are recognized by the ventilator.
  • Look for battery-related alarms or technical faults.
  • Inspect battery seating and contacts if accessible without deep disassembly.
  • Confirm battery age and replacement history.
  • Perform runtime testing only if required by facility procedure.
  • Do not return the ventilator to transport or patient use if batteries are not recognized, not charging normally, or reporting replacement or defective conditions.

Check Filters, Airflow, and Cooling Path

  • Check external filters according to facility procedure.
  • Inspect air intake and exhaust areas for blockage.
  • Look for dust buildup around vents.
  • Confirm the ventilator is not being used near a heat source or in a restricted airflow area.
  • Listen for abnormal fan or blower noise.
  • Replace or service filters as required by the approved PM procedure.

Check Blower and Service Status

  • Review whether blower-related service is due.
  • Check the maintenance interval and any available service counters.
  • Look for additional blower-related messages or technical events.
  • Listen for abnormal blower noise during startup and operation.
  • Do not open the device or perform internal blower service unless authorized and trained.

Perform the Approved Preventive Maintenance Procedure

  • Complete preventive maintenance using the approved facility checklist and manufacturer-based procedure.
  • Perform required PM checks, which may include visual inspection, electrical safety testing if required, battery condition check, filter replacement, alarm function checks, ventilation performance checks, oxygen-related checks, flow or pressure verification, pre-use check, documentation of parts replaced, and review of service counters.
  • Confirm the ventilator passes the required preventive maintenance checklist before returning it to service.
  • Resolve the alarm by completing the actual maintenance requirement, not by clearing the message alone.

Reset Service Status Only After PM Completion

  • Use only the approved reset method according to facility and manufacturer procedure.
  • Do not reset the interval if the PM was not completed.
  • Do not reset the interval if the ventilator failed any required checks.
  • Do not reset the interval if parts are still needed.
  • Do not reset the interval if battery, blower, filter, or alarm issues remain unresolved.
  • Confirm preventive maintenance status is reset only after documented completion.

Run Final Operational Checks

  • Power cycle the ventilator.
  • Confirm Alarm 246001 does not return.
  • Run the required pre-use check or self-test.
  • Confirm audible and visual alarms function.
  • Verify external power and battery status.
  • Confirm no new alarms are present.
  • Return the ventilator to service only after final checks pass.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm 246001 remains after preventive maintenance has been completed and service status has been reset according to approved procedure, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent preventive maintenance alarm conditions may indicate a service counter reset failure, incorrect PM reset procedure, software or service configuration issue, internal service tracking fault, control system issue, memory-related issue, or an additional unresolved maintenance condition.

The ventilator should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation or manufacturer/service-level repair.

Do not repeatedly reset or bypass a service alarm without confirming why it remains active.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot or perform preventive maintenance on a ventilator while it is connected to an active patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator first, then complete the required service work on the removed device.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator displayed Alarm 246001 Preventive maintenance required during startup."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Scheduled preventive maintenance interval was due. PM history confirmed the device was past its required maintenance date."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service, completed required preventive maintenance checklist, inspected filters, verified battery charging, completed operational checks, reset service status per approved procedure, and returned unit to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Alarm 246001 should be treated as a service-due condition, not just a message to clear. Clinical Engineering should confirm the alarm, verify PM history, check external condition, complete the approved preventive maintenance checklist, and reset service status only after the work is properly performed. Patient safety, accurate interpretation of the alarm, and clear documentation are what make the troubleshooting complete.

That is successful troubleshooting.
