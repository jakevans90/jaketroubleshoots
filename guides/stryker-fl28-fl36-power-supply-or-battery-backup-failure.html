Stryker FL28 / FL36

Power Supply or Battery Backup Failure

Asset Type

Hospital Bed

Manufacturer

Stryker

Model

FL28 / FL36

What This Guide Helps With

Identifies causes of bed power loss or battery backup failure, including outlet, cabling, battery, and fuse issues before assuming internal electronics failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety

Confirm the patient is safely transferred or the bed is unoccupied. Never work on power systems with a patient in the bed.

Check Main Power Source

  • Verify the wall outlet is live using a known working device.
  • Ensure the bed’s power cord is fully seated in the outlet.

Expected outcome: Bed powers on when plugged into a confirmed live outlet.

Inspect Power Cord and Connections

  • Examine the power cord for cuts, frays, or damage.
  • Check connections at the bed and at the control module.

Expected outcome: No visible damage; cord firmly connected.

Verify On-Board Fuses or Circuit Breakers

  • Locate the bed’s power module fuses/circuit breakers.
  • Replace blown fuses or reset tripped breakers.

Expected outcome: Bed powers on or shows normal status indicators.

Check Battery Backup

  • If bed has battery backup, verify battery is installed and properly seated.
  • Observe battery charge status indicators if available.

Expected outcome: Bed operates on battery when unplugged; indicator shows charge.

Swap or Test Battery

  • If bed still fails to power on, try a known good battery (if available).
  • Observe if bed powers on normally.

Expected outcome: Bed powers on with a known good battery.

Observe Bed Behavior

  • Power on/off cycles should be normal; no unexpected shutdowns or indicator lights signaling faults.
  • Note any unusual sounds or heat from the power module.

If the Problem Persists

External power sources, cords, fuses, and batteries have been ruled out. The bed should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for vendor repair or bench evaluation

Stopping here prevents unsafe troubleshooting and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt troubleshooting while a patient remains in bed. Move patient to another bed first. Confirm power loss isn’t intermittent during patient care.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Bed loses power intermittently, fails to operate on battery."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery was discharged and power cord had a loose connection."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Secured power cord and replaced battery; bed powers on normally."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and confirmed live
  • Power cord condition and seating
  • Battery presence, seating, and charge status
  • Fuse or breaker status
  • Indicator lights behavior
  • Any unusual sounds or heat

Final Thought

Patient safety is the top priority. Systematic, logical troubleshooting ensures external causes are ruled out before escalating. Proper documentation and timely escalation preserve safety and minimize downtime.

That is successful troubleshooting.
