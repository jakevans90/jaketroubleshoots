Philips SureSigns VM Series

Recorder / Printer Failure

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

SureSigns VM Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting SureSigns VM recorder paper feed, print quality, door, paper, and printer response issues before repair escalation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Confirm the Philips SureSigns VM monitor is not the only active monitor being relied on for patient care before removing paper, opening the recorder door, or restarting the monitor.

If monitoring is required, have staff use another verified monitor or continue observation while troubleshooting is performed.

Expected outcome: Patient monitoring is maintained while the recorder issue is evaluated safely.

Confirm the Exact Recorder Complaint

Ask clinical staff what failed. Determine whether the issue is:

  • Recorder does not print
  • Paper does not feed
  • Paper jam
  • Recorder door will not close
  • Blank printout
  • Faded or unreadable printout
  • Intermittent printing
  • Error message related to recorder, paper, or printer
  • Monitor works normally except for printing

Expected outcome: The issue is narrowed to paper, door, print command, recorder mechanism, or internal printer failure.

Verify the Monitor Is Powered On and Functioning Normally

Confirm the SureSigns VM powers on, displays parameters, and responds to normal user input.

If the entire monitor is frozen, blank, or unresponsive, troubleshoot the monitor power or display issue first.

Expected outcome: The problem is confirmed to be recorder-specific, not a full monitor failure.

Check for Recorder or Paper Messages

Look for any message indicating printer error, paper out, recorder door open, paper jam, or recorder unavailable.

Clear only external conditions that can be safely corrected.

Expected outcome: On-screen messages guide whether the issue is paper, door, jam, or recorder hardware related.

Inspect the Recorder Door

Open and close the recorder door fully. Confirm it latches securely and is not cracked, loose, or blocked by paper.

A door that is not fully closed may prevent printing even if paper is loaded.

Expected outcome: Door closes normally and any door-open condition clears.

Check Paper Roll Installation

Verify the correct recorder paper is installed and that the roll is seated properly. Confirm the paper feeds from the correct direction for thermal printing.

If the paper is backwards, the recorder may feed paper but produce a blank strip.

Expected outcome: Paper is loaded correctly and positioned for printing.

Replace the Paper Roll

Install a known-good compatible thermal paper roll. Do not assume the existing roll is usable based only on appearance.

Old, incorrect, or non-thermal paper can cause blank or poor-quality printouts.

Expected outcome: Recorder prints normally with verified paper. If resolved, return the device to service.

Check for Paper Jam or Torn Paper

Inspect the paper path for torn paper, folded edges, stuck labels, debris, or pieces of paper caught near the feed path.

Remove only loose, visible obstructions without forcing the mechanism or disassembling the recorder.

Expected outcome: Paper path is clear and paper feeds smoothly.

Test Manual Paper Feed

Use the recorder feed function, if available, to confirm paper advances.

If the motor runs but paper does not move, suspect paper loading, roller contact, door latch, or feed mechanism failure.

If there is no sound or movement, suspect recorder power/control failure.

Expected outcome: Paper advances consistently during feed testing.

Test a Print Function

Initiate a simple printout such as a strip recording, alarm recording, trend print, or available recorder test function depending on unit configuration.

Expected outcome: Recorder starts, feeds paper, and produces readable output.

Inspect Print Quality

Review the printout for fading, missing sections, uneven darkness, blank output, or intermittent lines.

Confirm the issue is not caused by reversed paper or poor paper quality before suspecting the thermal printhead.

Expected outcome: Print is readable and consistent across the strip.

Check Recorder Configuration or Print Settings

Confirm printing has not been disabled, redirected, or limited by configuration. Verify staff are using the correct print command and not expecting a function that is unavailable on that unit.

Expected outcome: Recorder settings and user command match the expected print behavior.

Power Cycle Only When Safe

If the monitor is not actively monitoring a patient, power cycle the SureSigns VM and retest the recorder.

A temporary software lockup can prevent recorder response even when the monitor otherwise appears normal.

Expected outcome: Recorder function returns after restart. If resolved, document and monitor for recurrence.

Compare With a Known-Good Unit if Available

Use the same type of paper and same print command on another SureSigns VM monitor.

If the paper and workflow work on another monitor, the issue likely remains with the suspect unit.

Expected outcome: External paper and user process are ruled out.

Check for Physical Damage or Contamination

Inspect the recorder door, hinge, latch, paper tray, and visible feed area for cracks, fluid residue, impact damage, melted plastic, unusual smell, heat, or binding.

Do not continue using a device with signs of fluid intrusion, overheating, or mechanical damage.

Expected outcome: Device is confirmed safe for continued evaluation or removed from service.

If the Problem Persists

If the recorder still does not print, feed paper, latch correctly, or produce readable output after paper, door, settings, jam, and restart checks have been completed, common external causes have been ruled out.

The monitor should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Likely internal causes may include a failed recorder assembly, feed motor issue, door/latch sensor failure, thermal printhead failure, internal connection problem, or recorder control circuitry fault. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting and prevents unnecessary damage to the recorder mechanism.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot the recorder while the monitor is the only active source of patient monitoring. A failed printer does not always mean the monitor is unsafe for monitoring, but printed records should not be relied on until recorder function is verified. Move the patient to another device first if recorder output is clinically required.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported the Philips SureSigns VM monitor would not print patient strips from the recorder."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Troubleshooting found the recorder door latched properly and a known-good paper roll was installed, but the recorder would not feed or print during test operation."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Monitor was removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation for suspected recorder assembly failure."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Whether the monitor otherwise powered on normally
  • Exact recorder message or error displayed
  • Whether paper was missing, incorrect, reversed, or jammed
  • Whether a known-good thermal paper roll was installed
  • Whether the recorder door latched securely
  • Whether manual feed worked
  • Whether the recorder motor made noise
  • Whether output was blank, faded, or incomplete
  • Whether the issue occurred during strip print, trend print, or alarm recording
  • Any signs of physical damage, fluid residue, unusual heat, or smell
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Recorder failures should be approached logically by checking patient safety, paper, door closure, jams, settings, and print commands before assuming internal failure. If the recorder remains nonfunctional after external checks, removing the monitor from service and documenting the findings clearly protects patients, staff, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
