Drager Evita V300 Ventilator

Internal Battery Failure

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V300

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Drager Evita V300 ventilator reports an Internal Battery Failure or similar battery-related alarm. The goal is to first verify external power conditions and user setup before assuming the internal battery has failed. Internal batteries support the ventilator during transport or power loss, so proper function is important for patient safety.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the ventilator is not actively supporting a patient before troubleshooting.
  • If the ventilator is in use, transition the patient to another ventilator before beginning any checks.
  • This prevents interruption of respiratory therapy.

Confirm the Device Is Connected to AC Power

  • Verify that the ventilator is plugged into a functioning hospital-grade outlet.
  • Check that the power cord is fully inserted into both the ventilator and the wall outlet.
  • Ensure the outlet has power (test with another device if necessary).
  • If the ventilator is running on internal battery only, it may trigger battery-related warnings.

Check the Power Indicator Lights

  • Look at the ventilator’s power indicators.
  • Confirm the device shows that AC power is present.
  • If the device does not recognize AC power, inspect the power cable and outlet.
  • A ventilator that cannot detect AC power may continue running on battery and falsely appear to have a battery issue.

Inspect the Power Cord and External Connections

  • Check for damaged or frayed power cord.
  • Check for loose connectors.
  • Check for bent pins on the AC input connector.
  • If damage is observed, remove the ventilator from service and replace the power cable if applicable.

Check the Battery Status in the System Menu

  • Navigate to the system information or battery status area on the ventilator.
  • Look for battery charge level.
  • Look for battery health warnings.
  • Look for messages indicating battery not detected or battery fault.
  • A battery showing very low capacity or not detected may indicate end-of-life.

Allow the Ventilator to Charge

  • If the ventilator was recently unplugged or stored, leave the ventilator connected to AC power for at least 30–60 minutes.
  • Observe whether the battery begins charging.
  • Sometimes a deeply discharged battery can trigger a temporary warning until it begins charging.

Restart the Ventilator

  • Power cycle the ventilator when it is safe to do so and not connected to a patient.
  • Turn the ventilator off.
  • Wait approximately 10 seconds.
  • Turn it back on and observe if the battery alarm clears.
  • This helps rule out a temporary system error.

Verify Alarm Behavior

  • Observe whether the ventilator continues to report Internal battery failure.
  • Observe whether the ventilator reports Battery not charging.
  • Observe whether the ventilator reports Battery not detected.
  • Persistent alarms after the above checks usually indicate battery degradation or internal battery circuitry issues.

If the Problem Persists

If the internal battery failure alarm continues after verifying:

  • AC power is present
  • Power cord is functioning
  • Device has been allowed to charge
  • The ventilator has been restarted

Then common external causes have been ruled out.

At this point the issue is likely due to:

  • Internal battery end-of-life
  • Battery pack failure
  • Internal charging circuitry issue

The ventilator should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop external troubleshooting and escalate for repair is proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a ventilator battery issue while it is actively supporting a patient.

If a battery alarm occurs during clinical use:

  • Immediately transition the patient to another ventilator if necessary.
  • Only begin troubleshooting once the patient is safely supported by another device.

Patient safety must always come first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator displayed Internal Battery Failure alarm during equipment check.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Ventilator confirmed connected to AC power; battery status menu indicated battery fault and battery would not charge.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Ventilator removed from service and sent to Clinical Engineering for battery replacement and evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified functional
  • Power cord inspected
  • AC power indicator status
  • Battery charge percentage displayed
  • Any battery-related alarms observed
  • Whether the alarm cleared after restart
  • Final device status (removed from service or repaired)

Final Thought

Ventilators rely on internal batteries to maintain operation during power interruptions and patient transport. External checks such as confirming AC power, inspecting the power cord, and verifying battery status can quickly identify common issues. When alarms persist after these checks, escalation for repair is appropriate and protects both patient safety and equipment reliability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
