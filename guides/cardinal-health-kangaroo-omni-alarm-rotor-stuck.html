Cardinal Health Kangaroo OMNI

Alarm: Rotor Stuck

Asset Type

Feeding Pump

Manufacturer

Cardinal Health

Model

Kangaroo OMNI

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Rotor Stuck alarm on the Cardinal Health Kangaroo OMNI feeding pump. This alarm may be caused by rotor resistance, fluid buildup, contamination, a misloaded feeding set, or rotor movement issues affecting feed or flush delivery.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the Kangaroo OMNI is connected to a patient and the Rotor Stuck alarm is active, do not continue relying on the pump until the alarm is corrected.
  • Pause feeding or flushing according to clinical policy and notify clinical staff.
  • If feeding must continue, transfer the patient to a known-good feeding pump.
  • Expected: Patient nutrition or flushing is maintained safely with a reliable pump.
  • Why it matters: A rotor resistance condition may prevent accurate delivery or stop feeding entirely.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the display shows Rotor Stuck.
  • Ask staff when the alarm occurred, such as during startup, after loading the feeding set, during active feeding or flushing, after a spill or fluid exposure, or intermittently during operation.
  • Observe whether the alarm can be reproduced under facility-approved test conditions.
  • Expected: The Rotor Stuck alarm is clearly confirmed or reproduced.
  • Why it matters: This confirms a true pump-detected rotor resistance condition rather than a general setup or feeding alarm.

Understand What the Alarm Means

  • The Rotor Stuck alarm means the pump detected unusual resistance on the rotor.
  • This may be caused by enteral formula, water, medication residue, or other contaminants building up around the rotor area.
  • Detection may take up to 2 minutes.
  • Expected: Troubleshooting focuses first on contamination, feeding set loading, and rotor movement before assuming internal pump failure.

Remove Power Before Cleaning

  • Before cleaning the rotor area, unplug the pump from the electrical outlet.
  • Do not clean the pump while it is connected to AC power.
  • Expected: The pump is disconnected from wall power before cleaning is performed.
  • Why it matters: This reduces electrical safety risk while working around a potentially contaminated pump surface.

Inspect the Rotor Area

  • Remove the feeding set from the pump.
  • Inspect the rotor area for dried formula, sticky residue, medication buildup, contamination, visible obstruction, or damage around the rotor path.
  • Confirm the rotor area is clean, clear, and free of visible buildup.
  • Why it matters: Contaminant buildup can create mechanical drag and cause the pump to detect abnormal rotor resistance.

Clean the Rotor

  • Wipe the rotor with a wet cloth.
  • Remove visible enteral fluid, residue, or contaminant buildup from the rotor surface and surrounding area.
  • Do not spray liquid directly into the pump.
  • Allow the area to dry before reloading the feeding set.
  • Expected: The rotor is clean and able to move without visible obstruction.
  • Why it matters: Cleaning the rotor removes a common external cause of the Rotor Stuck alarm.

Reload the Feeding Set

  • Install the feeding set correctly and confirm it is seated properly in the pump.
  • Check that the tubing is not pinched, misrouted, pulled tight, or interfering with rotor movement.
  • Expected: The feeding set loads smoothly and does not create abnormal resistance at the rotor.
  • Why it matters: A misloaded or improperly seated set can mimic a rotor resistance issue.

Power On and Recheck Operation

  • Reconnect the pump to power if needed.
  • Power on the pump and attempt normal setup using facility-approved test conditions.
  • Observe whether the Rotor Stuck alarm returns.
  • Expected: The alarm clears after cleaning and proper set loading.
  • If the alarm clears, complete functional verification, clean the exterior as needed, document the correction, and return the pump to service.

Try a Known-Good Feeding Set if Needed

  • If the alarm continues, remove the current feeding set and install a known-good compatible Kangaroo OMNI feeding set.
  • Check for stiff tubing, damaged cassette or set components, or anything that may place extra load on the rotor.
  • Expected: A good feeding set operates without triggering Rotor Stuck.
  • Why it matters: This helps separate a set-related resistance problem from a pump-related rotor problem.

Isolate the Pump

  • If the Rotor Stuck alarm continues after cleaning and using a known-good feeding set, power off the pump.
  • Use a different Kangaroo OMNI pump for patient care.
  • Expected: The questionable pump is removed from clinical use and a functional pump supports feeding.
  • Why it matters: Persistent rotor resistance alarms may indicate a mechanical or internal pump issue that should not be trusted for patient feeding.

If the Problem Persists

If the Rotor Stuck alarm remains after the rotor is cleaned, the feeding set is properly loaded, and external contamination or set issues have been ruled out, remove the pump from service.

Label the pump Out of Service and send it for bench evaluation or repair.

Do not continue repeated clinical resets or use the pump on a patient with an unresolved Rotor Stuck alarm.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot or clean the Kangaroo OMNI while it is actively supporting a patient. Move the patient to a backup device first, then continue troubleshooting once patient feeding or flushing is safely supported.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Kangaroo OMNI feeding pump reported with Rotor Stuck alarm during feeding. Staff stated pump stopped and alarm returned after setup."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Rotor area had visible enteral fluid residue or contaminant buildup causing increased rotor resistance. Alarm cleared after cleaning and reloading feeding set."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed pump from patient use for evaluation. Unplugged pump before cleaning. Cleaned rotor with wet cloth, allowed area to dry, reloaded feeding set, and verified operation without Rotor Stuck alarm. Returned pump to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The Rotor Stuck alarm should be treated as a real delivery-related fault until proven otherwise. Start with patient safety, remove power before cleaning, check for contamination and feeding set issues, and escalate when the alarm continues after basic external causes are ruled out. Clear documentation supports safe return to service or proper repair follow-up.

That is successful troubleshooting.
