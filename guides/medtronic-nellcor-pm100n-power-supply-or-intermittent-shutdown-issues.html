Medtronic Nellcor PM100N

Power Supply or Intermittent Shutdown Issues

Asset Type

Pulse Oximeter

Manufacturer

Medtronic

Model

Nellcor PM100N

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Nellcor PM100N experiences power supply problems or shuts down intermittently. Such issues are often caused by external factors such as AC power instability, battery degradation, loose connections, or environmental factors before assuming internal device failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify AC Power Source

  • Ensure the wall outlet is providing stable power.
  • Use a known working outlet or test with a meter.
  • Why: Fluctuating or low voltage can cause intermittent shutdowns.

Check Power Cord and AC Adapter

  • Inspect for damage, fraying, or loose connectors.
  • Reseat both ends securely.
  • Why: Poor connections can interrupt power supply even if the outlet is fine.

Test Device on Battery Power Alone

  • Fully charge the battery and run the unit without AC power.
  • Observe for normal operation duration.
  • Why: Confirms whether the battery can hold charge or is causing shutdowns.

Inspect Battery Contacts and Compartment

  • Remove the battery and check for corrosion or debris.
  • Reseat or clean contacts as needed.
  • Why: Poor contact can mimic power supply failures.

Monitor for Overheating or Environmental Factors

  • Ensure the device is not in direct sunlight, near heaters, or in excessive humidity.
  • Why: Thermal protection may cause shutdowns if overheating is detected.

Check for Software or Firmware Alerts

  • Observe display for error codes or messages during shutdown.
  • Document any patterns (e.g., after warm-up, during charging).
  • Why: Some shutdowns are device-protection responses.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out.

  • Device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Clinical Engineering should avoid further internal troubleshooting to maintain patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot on a patient. Move the patient to a working unit first.
  • Only power cycle or test the device in a safe environment.
  • Ensure the patient is monitored continuously while the device is offline.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Device shuts down intermittently and sometimes won’t power on.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Battery tested; AC power stable; cord and contacts checked. No external cause found.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Device labeled Out of Service and sent for repair. Battery replacement recommended if internal test confirms degradation.

Helpful Details to Include

  • AC outlet tested and verified
  • Power cord and adapter checked
  • Battery charge and runtime observed
  • Shutdown patterns noted
  • Device temperature and environment recorded
  • Any error codes captured

Final Thought

Systematic external checks help rule out the most common causes before escalating. Patient safety is paramount; always ensure the patient is on a working monitor before troubleshooting. Proper CCR documentation supports repair and prevents repeated issues.

That is successful troubleshooting.
