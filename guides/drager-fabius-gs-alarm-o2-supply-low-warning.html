Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: O2 SUPPLY LOW !!!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

O2 SUPPLY LOW !!! alarm indicating oxygen supply pressure below minimum (~20 psi), risking inadequate oxygen delivery and unsafe ventilation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess oxygen delivery
  • Switch to backup ventilation if needed
  • Confirm patient is receiving oxygen from a reliable source

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the O2 SUPPLY LOW !!! alarm is present
  • Check displayed O2 pipeline pressure
  • Observe when the alarm occurs (startup or during use)
  • Check for additional gas supply alarms

Check Wall Oxygen Supply

  • Ensure O2 hose is connected to the correct outlet
  • Verify secure connections at wall and machine
  • Test outlet using another device if available

Inspect O2 Hose and Connections

  • Check for kinks, compression, or damage
  • Ensure fittings are fully seated
  • Try a known-good O2 hose

Check Backup O2 Cylinder

  • Ensure cylinder is installed and valve is open
  • Check cylinder pressure gauge
  • Replace cylinder if empty or low

Verify Cylinder Regulator Function

  • Confirm regulator is properly attached
  • Check for leaks
  • Observe pressure when switching to cylinder supply

Isolate Supply Source

  • Try a different wall outlet
  • Try a different hose
  • Test cylinder-only operation

Stop When Resolved

  • Confirm O2 pressure returns to normal range
  • Verify alarm clears
  • Return device to service

If the Problem Persists

If all external sources (wall supply, hose, cylinder, regulator) are confirmed good and the alarm remains:

  • Remove device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot oxygen supply issues on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Unit alarming O2 SUPPLY LOW !!! with low displayed pipeline pressure and concern for inadequate oxygen delivery."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Kinked O2 supply hose restricting flow."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced damaged O2 hose; pressure restored and alarm cleared."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Oxygen supply alarms must always be treated as critical. Start with simple supply checks, confirm true pressure loss, and escalate appropriately to protect patient safety and ensure reliable gas delivery.

That is successful troubleshooting.
