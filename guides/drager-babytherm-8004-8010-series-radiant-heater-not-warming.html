Drager Babytherm 8004 / 8010 Series

Radiant Heater Not Warming

Asset Type

Infant Warmer

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Babytherm 8004 / 8010 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting absent or reduced radiant heat caused by power, heater enablement, operating mode, temperature sensor, alarm timeout, or heater-element failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

If the warmer is in use with an infant, immediately notify clinical staff and move the patient to an approved functioning warmer or other appropriate thermal-support device before troubleshooting.

Do not attempt prolonged heater testing while a patient depends on the unit for warmth.

Expected outcome: The infant is safely supported on working equipment before troubleshooting begins.

Why it matters: A warmer that is not producing reliable heat can allow rapid neonatal heat loss.

2. Inspect the Unit for Immediate Safety Concerns

Visually inspect the radiant heater head, protective screen, control panel, power cord, plug, and frame for:

  • Physical damage
  • Liquid contamination
  • Discoloration or signs of overheating
  • Burning odor
  • Loose or damaged power cord
  • Objects blocking or covering the radiant heater

Do not touch the radiant heater screen or heating surface during or immediately after operation.

Expected outcome: No visible damage, obstruction, unusual odor, or unsafe condition is present.

If damage, burning odor, or overheating is found: Remove the unit from service immediately and stop troubleshooting.

3. Verify Incoming Power and Controller Operation

Confirm the warmer is connected to a known-good hospital-grade outlet. Verify the power cord is fully seated and inspect for signs of damage.

Turn the controller on and observe the control panel:

  • Green power indicator should illuminate during normal power operation.
  • A red power-failure indication, continuous alarm, or blank/nonresponsive display may indicate an incoming power or device failure condition.

The Babytherm operating instructions identify loss of power as a possible cause of alarm behavior and direct that the unit be checked for connection to power and removed from service when appropriate.

Expected outcome: The unit powers on normally, the display is functional, and no unresolved power-related alarm remains.

If the outlet, cord, or power-up behavior is abnormal: Correct the external power issue if possible. If the unit still does not operate normally, remove it from service.

4. Confirm the Radiant Heater Is Turned On

Verify that the radiant heater function is actually enabled at the control panel, not only the mattress heater or examination light.

Confirm that the radiant heater on/off control has been activated and that the radiant heater output-level display is illuminated.

For the Babytherm 8010, remember that the heated gel mattress is a separate warming function and does not confirm that the overhead radiant heater is operating.

Expected outcome: The radiant heater is enabled and a heater output level is displayed.

If the radiant heater was off: Turn it on, confirm the selected output level, and recheck for heat. If warming is restored, document the finding and return the device only after functional verification.

5. Check for Active Alarms or Device-Failure Indications

Observe the controller for:

  • Red Inop. LED
  • Red or yellow alarm LED
  • Central alarm light
  • Audible alarm
  • Text display messages
  • Skin temperature sensor warnings
  • Heater-related warning behavior

A red Inop. indicator identifies a device fault and requires removal from service rather than continued use.

Expected outcome: No unresolved device-failure indication or alarm is preventing dependable heater operation.

If an Inop. condition or persistent fault message is present: Record the message exactly, remove the unit from service, and proceed to repair evaluation.

6. Verify Operating Mode and Heater Output Setting

With no patient on the device, place the radiant heater in manual mode for a controlled functional check.

Confirm:

  • The yellow man. indicator is illuminated.
  • A heater output level is selected and confirmed.
  • The output is not unintentionally set at a minimal level that may be difficult to perceive during a brief check.

The Babytherm manual mode provides a selected heater output independent of skin temperature, while skin mode automatically adjusts heater output according to the connected skin temperature sensor and setpoint.

Expected outcome: Manual mode activates normally and the selected radiant heater output remains displayed.

If the unit was in skin mode with low output: Manual-mode testing may show the radiant heater is functional and the complaint was related to operating mode, setpoint, or sensor feedback. Document the finding and continue verification before returning to service.

7. Check for Warmth From Both Radiant Heating Elements

Allow a brief warm-up period in manual mode. Carefully place your hand at a safe distance below the radiant heater without touching the protective screen or heater assembly.

Determine whether both infrared heating elements provide noticeable warmth.

The Babytherm operating instructions specifically direct checking for warmth from both infrared heating elements at a safe distance beneath the radiant heater.

Expected outcome: Both radiant heating elements produce warmth.

If neither element warms: Continue to the alarm-timeout and control checks below, then remove from service if heat is still absent.

If only one element appears to warm: Remove the unit from service for repair evaluation; radiant heat output may be incomplete or unreliable.

8. Determine Whether the Heater Shut Off Following an Unacknowledged Temperature-Check Alarm

Ask clinical staff whether the heater initially warmed and later stopped.

When operating in manual mode at heater levels 4 and above, the Babytherm generates a timed temperature-check alarm. If that alarm is not acknowledged, the heater switches off after the alarm period and remains off until the alarm is acknowledged.

With no patient present and only as part of controlled equipment testing, review the displayed message and alarm state. If the display indicates the required temperature check alarm condition, acknowledge it and verify whether radiant heat resumes.

Expected outcome: If heater shutdown was caused by the timed alarm, acknowledging the alarm restores heater operation.

If heat returns after acknowledgment: The heater may be functioning normally. Document that the reported no-heat condition was associated with the unacknowledged timed alarm and verify normal operation before returning the device to service.

9. Inspect Skin Temperature Sensor Connection When the Complaint Occurred in Skin Mode

If the issue was reported while operating in skin mode, inspect the skin temperature sensor and connection:

  • Confirm the sensor connector is fully seated in the correct socket.
  • Inspect the sensor and cable for damage.
  • Review the display for messages such as Plug in skin temp. sensor or Skin temp. sensor fault.
  • Use a known-good compatible sensor if available and approved by facility practice.

The Babytherm troubleshooting information identifies a disconnected or faulty skin temperature sensor as a cause of skin-mode alarms and directs checking the connection and replacing the sensor as appropriate.

Expected outcome: The skin temperature sensor is intact, connected correctly, and does not generate an unresolved fault.

If a known-good sensor corrects the issue: Complete functional verification and document the failed accessory replacement.

If sensor faults continue: Remove the device from service for bench evaluation.

10. Verify Basic Function Only; Do Not Perform Internal Heater Repair at the Point of Use

If power is present, the heater is enabled, manual mode is confirmed, alarm timeout is not the cause, sensor issues have been ruled out, and one or both radiant elements still do not heat properly, do not open the warmer or attempt internal repair in the care area.

Expected outcome: External and readily verifiable causes have been ruled out.

If the issue remains unresolved: Remove the device from service for qualified repair evaluation.

If the Problem Persists

If the radiant heater still does not warm after checking power, heater enablement, operating mode, output setting, alarm timeout behavior, sensor connection, and visible external condition, the fault is likely internal to the heater elements, control circuitry, wiring, or temperature-control system.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Do not return the warmer to clinical use based only on partial warming, intermittent operation, or a temporarily cleared alarm. Knowing when to stop troubleshooting is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a suspected no-heat condition while an infant is dependent on the warmer. Transfer the patient to functioning thermal support first, then evaluate the warmer without patient risk. In manual heater operation, patient temperature monitoring remains essential because heater output does not automatically respond to the infant’s temperature.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"NICU staff reported that the Drager Babytherm 8010 overhead radiant heater was turned on but was not producing noticeable warmth."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified normal incoming power and controller operation; radiant heater remained nonfunctional in manual mode with no warmth detected from either infrared heating element."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the infant warmer from service, applied an Out of Service label, and routed the unit for bench evaluation of the radiant heater system."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and result
  • Power indicator and display behavior
  • Radiant heater on/off indicator status
  • Manual or skin operating mode at time of failure
  • Heater output level selected
  • Alarm messages or Inop. indicator observed
  • Timed temperature-check alarm acknowledged or not applicable
  • Skin temperature sensor condition and whether a known-good sensor was tested
  • Whether one, both, or neither radiant element produced warmth
  • Any unusual odor, discoloration, heat, or physical damage
  • Final device status and repair routing

Final Thought

A neonatal warmer with unreliable radiant heat should be treated as a patient safety concern. Start with power, controls, settings, alarms, and accessories, then escalate when reliable heat cannot be confirmed. Thorough CCR documentation ensures the failure is clearly communicated and appropriately repaired.

That is successful troubleshooting.
