Branson Bransonic Series

Unit Operates But Solution Does Not Heat

Asset Type

Ultrasonic Cleaner

Manufacturer

Branson

Model

Bransonic Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting a Bransonic ultrasonic cleaner that powers on and runs sonics, but the bath solution does not heat properly.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Safety First

Confirm the unit is not being used for active instrument reprocessing before troubleshooting.

Allow the tank and solution to cool before handling, draining, or moving the cleaner.

Expected outcome: Troubleshooting is performed without burn risk, splash exposure, or interruption to clinical workflow.

Verify the Reported Heating Issue

Ask staff what they observed:

  • Unit powers on
  • Timer or cycle runs
  • Sonics appear to operate
  • Solution remains cold or only slightly warm
  • Heat indicator does or does not illuminate
  • Issue occurs every use or intermittently

Expected outcome: The complaint is confirmed as a heating problem, not a general power or cycle-start issue.

Check That the Heat Function Is Actually Enabled

Verify the temperature control or heat setting is turned on and set above the current solution temperature.

Some ultrasonic cleaners can operate sonics without heating if heat is off or set too low.

Expected outcome: Heat is selected and the set temperature is high enough to call for heating.

If the heat setting was off or set too low, correct the setting, allow the unit time to warm, verify heating, document the finding, and stop.

Allow Adequate Warm-Up Time

Confirm the unit was given enough time to heat the solution before staff reported the failure.

Ultrasonic baths do not heat instantly, especially when filled with cold water or a high solution volume.

Expected outcome: The solution temperature should gradually rise over time when heat is enabled.

If the unit heats normally after adequate warm-up time, return it to service and document user education.

Check Fill Level

Inspect the tank fill level.

The solution should be within the manufacturer’s normal operating range and not below the minimum fill line.

Expected outcome: The tank contains enough solution for safe heating and ultrasonic operation.

If the tank is underfilled, correct the fill level, retest heating, and stop if the issue is resolved.

Check for Excessive Solution Volume or Cold Starting Conditions

Determine whether staff filled the tank with very cold water, overfilled the tank, or used a large volume of solution immediately before use.

Expected outcome: Heating expectations are realistic based on starting temperature and fill volume.

If the cleaner heats slowly but reaches temperature, document normal operation and educate staff.

Verify Power Source

Check that the ultrasonic cleaner is plugged directly into a known-good hospital-grade outlet, unless facility policy allows another approved configuration.

Avoid power strips, loose outlet connections, damaged cords, or shared circuits when evaluating heating complaints.

Expected outcome: The unit receives stable power.

If the outlet is defective or unstable, move the unit to a verified outlet, retest, document the outlet issue, and stop.

Inspect the Power Cord and Plug

Look for:

  • Bent plug blades
  • Damaged insulation
  • Loose strain relief
  • Burn marks
  • Heat damage
  • Intermittent power when the cord is gently repositioned

Expected outcome: Cord and plug are intact with no visible damage.

If cord damage is found, remove the unit from service and send it for repair or cord replacement evaluation.

Inspect the Tank and Exterior Condition

Check for signs that may indicate unsafe operation:

  • Leaking tank
  • Corrosion
  • Cracked housing
  • Liquid under the unit
  • Unusual odor
  • Discoloration from overheating
  • Excessive external heat

Expected outcome: The unit is physically safe to operate.

If leakage, burning smell, or abnormal heat is present, unplug the unit, label it Out of Service, and escalate for repair.

Confirm the Lid and Normal Use Conditions

Verify staff are using the lid if required by department practice.

An open tank can lose heat faster and may appear to heat poorly, especially in cooler rooms.

Expected outcome: The unit is tested under normal operating conditions.

If heating improves with proper lid use and normal setup, document the finding and return the unit to service.

Check for Heater Indicator Behavior

Observe whether the heat indicator light or display icon activates when heat is enabled.

Expected outcome: The unit shows that it is calling for heat.

If the heater indicator does not activate despite proper settings, suspect a control, thermostat, sensor, or heater circuit issue.

Measure Solution Temperature Externally

Use an appropriate external thermometer to compare actual bath temperature to the expected heating behavior.

Do not rely only on touch.

Expected outcome: Temperature should rise over time when heat is enabled.

If the temperature rises normally, the unit may be functioning correctly and staff expectations or warm-up time may be the issue.

Run a Controlled Bench Test

With the unit off patient-care workflow, fill to the proper level with room-temperature water or approved test solution.

Turn heat on, set the temperature appropriately, and monitor temperature rise over a reasonable interval.

Expected outcome: The bath temperature increases steadily.

If the unit operates but the solution temperature does not rise at all, external causes have likely been ruled out.

Check for Intermittent Heating

If heating works sometimes but not consistently, gently observe for changes related to:

  • Cord movement
  • Control setting movement
  • Display changes
  • Heat indicator flickering
  • Unit vibration during sonics

Expected outcome: Heating remains stable during operation.

If heating is intermittent, remove the device from service for bench evaluation.

Do Not Perform Internal Heater Repair During Field Troubleshooting

Do not open the unit for board-level diagnosis, heater replacement, thermostat replacement, or internal wiring repair during basic troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: Clinical Engineering stops once external causes and safe operational checks are complete.

If the Problem Persists

If the Bransonic unit operates but the solution still does not heat after checking settings, fill level, warm-up time, outlet power, cord condition, and controlled bench testing, the likely cause is internal.

Possible internal causes include a failed heater element, thermostat, temperature sensor, control board, relay, wiring connection, or heater safety device.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair, bench evaluation, or manufacturer/service-provider assessment

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A unit that runs sonics but does not heat should not be returned to service unless heating performance is verified.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot an ultrasonic cleaner while staff are relying on it for active instrument reprocessing. Provide another working cleaner or redirect workflow first. Heating problems can affect cleaning performance and reprocessing consistency.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported the Branson Bransonic ultrasonic cleaner powered on and completed cycles, but the solution did not heat."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified proper outlet power, fill level, and heat setting; bath temperature failed to rise during controlled bench testing."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed unit from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for repair evaluation for suspected internal heater or temperature-control failure."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested
  • Power cord condition
  • Heat setting verified
  • Fill level verified
  • Starting solution temperature
  • Final measured solution temperature
  • Warm-up time allowed
  • Heater indicator behavior
  • Sonics still operating or not
  • Any unusual odor, heat, leakage, or sound
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Heating complaints should be approached logically: verify safety, confirm the setting, check fill level and power, then measure actual temperature. Once external causes are ruled out, escalation is appropriate. Clear CCR documentation helps the next technician understand what was tested and why the device was removed from service.

That is successful troubleshooting.
