GE Healthcare Giraffe Warmer

Alarm Silence Sensor or Alarm Fault

Asset Type

Infant Warmer

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Giraffe Warmer

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting hands-free alarm silence failure, alarm acknowledgement problems, abnormal alarm operation, or alarm-related system fault indications.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

If the warmer is in use with an infant, notify clinical staff and transfer the patient to another appropriate warming device before troubleshooting.

Do not continue using a warmer with unreliable alarm behavior, an active system failure, or an alarm that cannot be properly acknowledged.

Expected outcome: The infant is safely supported on alternate equipment before testing begins.

Why it matters: The Giraffe Warmer uses audible and visual alarms to notify staff of unsafe conditions. Alarm failure can delay response to a clinically significant event.

2. Confirm the Reported Alarm Problem

Power on the warmer while it is not in patient use and identify the reported symptom:

  • Hands-free alarm silence sensor does not respond.
  • Alarm will not silence when expected.
  • Alarm silences intermittently or without intentional activation.
  • Audible alarm is weak, absent, abnormal, or continuous.
  • A system failure message or alarm-related error is displayed.

Record the exact alarm message, displayed fault, and whether the issue occurs at startup or during operation.

Expected outcome: The complaint is verified and clearly distinguished as a sensor-response issue, alarm-output issue, or system fault.

3. Inspect the Hands-Free Alarm Silence Sensor Area

Inspect the alarm light/sensor area for tape, stickers, residue, dust, dried cleaning solution, cracks, or physical damage.

Clean the external sensor surface according to approved cleaning procedures, then allow it to dry completely.

Expected outcome: The sensor area is unobstructed, clean, dry, and free of obvious damage.

Why it matters: The Giraffe Warmer uses a hands-free alarm silence sensor near the alarm light; contamination or obstruction can interfere with infrared sensing. GE describes alarm silencing by waving a hand in front of the sensor.

4. Test the Hands-Free Alarm Silence Response

Create or observe a safe test alarm condition only according to facility practice and approved equipment testing methods. Pass a hand slowly within approximately six inches in front of the alarm light/sensor area.

Observe whether the alarm acknowledges or silences as intended.

Expected outcome: The hands-free sensor consistently responds when a hand is passed in front of the sensor area.

If the sensor works normally: The issue may have been caused by obstruction or contamination. Complete required operational verification and document the finding before returning the warmer to service.

If the sensor does not respond: Continue troubleshooting.

Why it matters: GE training material identifies the hands-free alarm silence function as operating when a hand is passed within six inches in front of the alarm light.

5. Check for Unintentional Sensor Activation

Verify that blankets, supply bags, cables, equipment covers, hanging accessories, or staff movement are not repeatedly passing in front of the sensor area.

Observe the unit during an alarm test to determine whether alarms silence unexpectedly without intentional hand movement.

Expected outcome: No nearby object or workflow condition is unintentionally activating the hands-free sensor.

If the problem is resolved: Reposition or remove the interfering item, complete operational verification, and document the correction.

6. Verify Alarm Audio and Visual Indication

During a safe test alarm condition, confirm that:

  • The audible alarm is clearly heard.
  • The alarm light activates appropriately.
  • The displayed alarm message corresponds to the condition.
  • Alarm acknowledgement does not prevent recognition of a continuing alarm condition.

Expected outcome: Audible, visual, and displayed alarm indications operate consistently.

If alarm audio is absent, distorted, unusually quiet, continuous, or inconsistent: Remove the warmer from service for bench evaluation.

7. Check Basic Power Conditions

Inspect the power cord, plug, strain relief, and outlet connection for looseness or damage. Confirm the warmer is connected to a known-good hospital-grade outlet and powers on normally without intermittent resets.

Expected outcome: Stable power is present and the warmer completes normal startup without alarm-system symptoms caused by interrupted power.

If the issue resolves after correcting an external power problem: Complete functional testing and document the cause and resolution.

8. Review for System Failure or Startup Fault Behavior

Observe the display during startup and alarm testing for any system failure message, blank display with high-priority alarm, repeated restart, or non-silenceable alarm.

The Giraffe/Panda warmer service documentation describes abnormal system operation as capable of generating a high-priority alarm and system failure indication; a detected startup failure is recorded and initiates a high-priority alarm.

Expected outcome: No system failure indication or unresolved high-priority alarm is present.

If a system failure or unresolved high-priority alarm occurs: Stop troubleshooting, record the displayed information, and remove the device from service.

9. Determine Whether the Issue Is External or Requires Repair

If cleaning the sensor area, removing nearby obstructions, verifying power, and performing a safe alarm-function test do not restore reliable operation, do not proceed with internal component replacement at the clinical troubleshooting level.

Expected outcome: A device with unresolved alarm silence sensor or alarm-function failure is identified for repair evaluation rather than returned to patient use.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes such as contamination, obstruction, nearby unintended activation, unstable power, and basic functional setup have been ruled out. The problem is likely related to the alarm light/sensor assembly, alarm circuitry, control electronics, internal cabling, or another internal fault.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service.
  • Labeled Out of Service.
  • Sent for repair or qualified bench evaluation.
  • Accompanied by documentation of the exact symptom, alarm behavior, displayed messages, and testing performed.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. An infant warmer with unreliable alarm function should not be returned to clinical use until repaired and verified.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot an alarm or alarm-silence failure while the warmer is supporting an infant. Transfer the patient to a verified working warmer first, because unreliable alarms can delay recognition of unsafe thermal conditions.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the GE Healthcare Giraffe Warmer alarm would not silence when a hand was passed in front of the hands-free alarm silence sensor."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified that the alarm activated normally, but the hands-free alarm silence sensor did not respond after the sensor window was cleaned and external obstructions were removed."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the Giraffe Warmer from service, applied an Out of Service label, documented the failed alarm-silence test, and routed the unit for bench evaluation of the alarm sensor/alarm system."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Patient removed from device before troubleshooting
  • Exact alarm message or system failure indication
  • Whether failure occurred at startup or during operation
  • Alarm audio present, absent, weak, or continuous
  • Alarm light behavior
  • Hands-free sensor area cleaned and inspected
  • Obstructions or nearby items removed
  • Sensor response when hand passed within approximately six inches
  • Outlet tested or known-good outlet used
  • Power cord condition
  • Any unusual sounds, heat, odor, or intermittent resets
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Alarm reliability is a patient safety function. A logical Clinical Engineering approach begins with safe removal from patient use, verifies simple external causes, confirms alarm behavior, and escalates unresolved faults without unnecessary internal intervention. Clear CCR documentation helps ensure the device receives appropriate follow-up and prevents unsafe return to service.

That is successful troubleshooting.
