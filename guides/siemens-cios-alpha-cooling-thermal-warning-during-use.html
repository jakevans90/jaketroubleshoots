Siemens Healthineers Cios Alpha

Cooling / Thermal Warning During Use

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

Siemens Healthineers

Model

Cios Alpha

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting cooling or thermal warnings during C-arm use caused by heat buildup, airflow restriction, workload, or environmental conditions.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

Before troubleshooting, confirm the procedure can continue safely.

Action:

  • Confirm with the clinical team whether imaging is urgently needed.
  • If the warning affects imaging availability, dose delivery, or system operation, have another imaging option available if possible.
  • Do not power cycle, move, or service the C-arm while it is actively needed for patient care unless the clinical team confirms it is safe.

Expected outcome: Patient care is protected before the thermal issue is investigated.

Why it matters: A cooling or thermal warning may progress to limited imaging, automatic shutdown, or interrupted fluoroscopy.

2. Verify the Reported Warning

Confirm the exact warning message displayed on the Cios Alpha.

Action:

  • Check whether the warning appears on the main system display, monitor cart, generator interface, or system status area.
  • Ask staff when the warning appeared:
  • During continuous fluoro
  • After repeated exposures
  • During startup
  • After the unit was moved
  • After being stored in a warm area

Expected outcome: The thermal warning is confirmed and the operating conditions are understood.

If the message clears and does not return after normal cool-down, document the event and return the unit to service if all functions pass.

3. Stop Imaging Long Enough to Allow Cooling

Pause imaging if clinically acceptable.

Action:

  • Ask staff to stop fluoroscopy or exposure use while keeping the system powered as appropriate.
  • Allow the system time to cool with normal fans operating.
  • Observe whether the warning changes, clears, or escalates.

Expected outcome: A workload-related temperature warning should improve after reduced use.

If the warning clears and imaging resumes normally, the issue was likely related to heat buildup from heavy use. Document and stop.

4. Check for Blocked Airflow

Inspect the C-arm and monitor cart for blocked vents or restricted airflow.

Action:

  • Look for drapes, covers, linens, bags, wall clearance issues, or equipment carts blocking ventilation openings.
  • Make sure the system is not pushed tightly against a wall or cabinet.
  • Verify that the system has enough space around cooling vents.

Expected outcome: Airflow paths are open and unrestricted.

If blocked airflow is found, remove the obstruction, allow the system to cool, and retest operation.

5. Check Room Conditions

Evaluate whether the room environment may be contributing to overheating.

Action:

  • Check if the room is unusually warm.
  • Confirm the unit is not positioned near warming devices, direct sunlight, heat exhaust, or other equipment generating heat.
  • Ask whether the system was recently moved from a hot storage area into the procedure room.

Expected outcome: The system is operating in a reasonable clinical environment with no obvious external heat source.

If environmental heat appears to be the cause, allow the system to stabilize in the room before returning it to active use.

6. Listen for Cooling Fan Operation

Without opening covers, listen for normal fan operation.

Action:

  • Stand near the system base, generator area, and monitor cart.
  • Listen for normal fan noise.
  • Check for unusual sounds such as grinding, rattling, intermittent fan cycling, or no fan operation when the unit is warm.

Expected outcome: Cooling fans sound normal and consistent.

If fan noise is abnormal, absent, or unusually loud, remove the device from service for repair evaluation.

7. Check for Dust or External Contamination

Inspect accessible exterior vents and filters, if externally accessible.

Action:

  • Look for dust buildup, lint, plastic wrap, tape, or debris on exterior vent areas.
  • Do not remove internal covers unless permitted by your facility procedure and service documentation.
  • Clean only external surfaces using approved methods.

Expected outcome: Exterior cooling paths are visibly clear.

If vents are heavily contaminated or the warning returns after cleaning, remove the unit from service for bench or vendor evaluation.

8. Review Use Pattern With Clinical Staff

Determine whether the warning occurred during unusually demanding imaging use.

Action:

  • Ask whether the case involved long fluoro time, repeated high-dose imaging, large patient body habitus, steep angles, or extended continuous operation.
  • Check whether staff noticed the warning after a specific exposure pattern.

Expected outcome: You can determine whether the warning is likely due to normal thermal protection during heavy use.

If the unit only warns after unusually heavy use and recovers normally, document the workload condition and educate staff to allow cooling breaks when clinically possible.

9. Check System Startup and Self-Test Behavior

After the system has cooled, restart only if clinically safe and allowed by facility procedure.

Action:

  • Power the system down normally.
  • Wait briefly before restarting.
  • Observe startup behavior, self-test messages, fan behavior, and whether the thermal warning returns immediately.

Expected outcome: A heat-related warning should not return immediately after proper cool-down.

If the thermal warning appears immediately at startup while the system is cool, suspect a temperature sensor, cooling control, fan, generator, power, or internal electronics issue.

10. Perform Basic Functional Verification

Once the warning clears, perform a limited operational check according to site policy.

Action:

  • Confirm the system powers normally.
  • Verify monitor display and image chain operation.
  • Confirm C-arm movement is normal.
  • Check that no cooling, temperature, generator, or system error returns.
  • Perform a safe test exposure only if permitted and appropriate.

Expected outcome: The system operates normally without recurring thermal warning.

If the warning returns quickly under normal use, remove the device from service.

If the Problem Persists

If the cooling or thermal warning remains active, returns immediately after cool-down, or occurs during normal workload, common external causes have been ruled out.

The Cios Alpha should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair, bench evaluation, or vendor service

Possible internal causes may include failed cooling fans, restricted internal airflow, temperature sensor failure, generator cooling issues, power supply problems, or internal control faults.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not continue using a C-arm with unresolved thermal warnings during patient procedures.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a thermal warning during an active case unless the clinical team confirms it is safe. If imaging is still required, move to another available C-arm or imaging system first. A thermal warning may be protective, but it can still interrupt imaging at a critical time.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported a cooling/thermal warning on the Siemens Cios Alpha during fluoroscopy use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found the warning occurred after extended imaging use with restricted airflow near the system vents."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleared airflow obstruction, allowed system to cool, verified normal startup and imaging function, and returned the C-arm to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested
  • Power behavior
  • Room temperature or unusual heat conditions
  • Approximate fluoro time before warning appeared
  • Whether warning occurred during continuous or repeated imaging
  • Whether vents were blocked
  • Fan noise normal or abnormal
  • Any unusual smell, heat, or noise
  • Whether the warning cleared after cool-down
  • Whether warning returned immediately at startup
  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Cooling and thermal warnings should be handled with patient safety first and logic second. Start with workload, airflow, room conditions, and fan behavior before assuming an internal failure. If the warning does not clear or returns under normal use, remove the C-arm from service and document the findings clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
