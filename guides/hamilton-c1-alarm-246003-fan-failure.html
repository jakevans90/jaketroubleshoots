Hamilton C1

Alarm: 246003 Fan failure

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm: 246003 Fan failure on a Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm may occur when cooling airflow is blocked, the cooling fan is not operating correctly, dust or debris is restricting ventilation, or the ventilator is at risk of overheating.

A fan failure condition should be treated seriously because inadequate cooling can lead to internal overheating, repeated technical alarms, unexpected shutdown behavior, or unreliable ventilator operation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot a cooling fan failure while the ventilator is supporting a patient unless clinical staff confirm it is safe.
  • Remove the Hamilton C1 from patient use if the alarm is active and cannot be cleared, the ventilator feels unusually hot, the fan is not running, ventilation performance is affected, or the alarm repeats after restart.
  • Transfer the patient to another ventilator or approved backup ventilation device before continuing extended troubleshooting.
  • Confirm patient ventilation is maintained using a safe and reliable device.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the display shows Alarm: 246003 Fan failure.
  • Observe whether the alarm occurs immediately at startup, after running for several minutes, during high workload ventilation, intermittently, or after cleaning, transport, or storage.
  • Check for associated symptoms such as fan not spinning, weak or no airflow from vents, unusual fan noise, excessive heat, dust buildup, or other technical alarms.
  • Confirm whether the alarm is reproducible or clearly observed before assuming a hardware failure.

Remove the Ventilator From Bedside Obstructions

  • Inspect the ventilator location and make sure cooling vents are not blocked by bedding, drapes, papers, equipment covers, walls, carts, accessory bags, cleaning wipes, or packaging.
  • Move the ventilator to an open area with proper ventilation clearance.
  • Confirm that cooling intake and exhaust areas are open and unrestricted.
  • If this resolves the alarm, run the ventilator long enough to confirm normal fan operation, complete required checks, document the obstruction, and return the device to service only if normal operation is confirmed.

Inspect Vents for Dust or Contamination

  • Inspect the cooling intake and exhaust areas for dust buildup, lint, debris, fluid residue, tape, labels covering openings, or cleaning chemical buildup.
  • Clean external vent areas according to hospital-approved cleaning practice.
  • Do not force tools deep into the device or perform internal cleaning beyond approved procedure.
  • If external cleaning resolves the alarm, verify operation, document the cleaning performed, and confirm the alarm does not return before releasing the ventilator.

Listen and Feel for Fan Operation

  • Power the ventilator in a safe bench area or non-patient setting.
  • Check for normal fan sound and air movement at the vent.
  • Listen for abnormal grinding, clicking, rubbing noise, fan cycling briefly and stopping, or no fan operation at all.
  • If the fan is not running or sounds abnormal, do not return the ventilator to clinical use.

Check for External Signs of Overheating

  • Inspect for a warm or hot housing, heat smell, temperature-related alarms, shutdown or restart behavior, or fan alarms that appear after the unit warms up.
  • Confirm the ventilator does not become excessively hot during normal operation.
  • If overheating is suspected, power down the device, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair evaluation.

Check Recent Service, Cleaning, or Transport History

  • Ask staff or review service history for recent cleaning, filter or accessory work, drops, impacts, dusty storage, transport, prior intermittent fan alarms, or previous overheating complaints.
  • Identify whether anything changed before the alarm appeared.
  • Use the history to determine whether debris, blockage, transport damage, or an intermittent fan problem may be involved.

Inspect Accessible Fan Wiring Only if Appropriate

  • Perform only non-invasive or approved inspection allowed by your facility procedure.
  • If safe and authorized, check for obvious fan connection issues such as a loose visible connector, pinched wiring, damaged harness, fluid intrusion, or obvious fan obstruction.
  • Do not perform board-level repair or deep internal disassembly unless trained and authorized.
  • If an accessible issue is corrected, verify fan operation and confirm the alarm does not return before releasing the ventilator.

Isolate the Likely Failure

  • Classify the issue based on findings: blocked vent, dust buildup, fan obstruction, fan failure, wiring issue, or internal device fault.
  • If the alarm clears after removing an obstruction or cleaning vents, document the external cause and verify continued normal operation.
  • If the fan does not run, runs abnormally, or the alarm persists despite clear vents and normal external conditions, suspect internal fan failure, wiring failure, fan control failure, or cooling system fault.
  • Do not ignore repeated fan failure alarms because they protect the ventilator from overheating and internal failure.

Stop When Resolved

  • Run the ventilator in a safe test setup after any corrective action.
  • Confirm Alarm: 246003 Fan failure does not return.
  • Confirm the fan runs normally, no overheating occurs, no related technical alarms appear, and required pre-use or operational checks pass.
  • Return the ventilator to service only after normal operation is confirmed.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm: 246003 Fan failure remains after checking vents, airflow, dust buildup, fan obstruction, overheating signs, recent handling, and accessible connections, the likely cause is internal fan failure, wiring failure, fan control failure, or a temperature/cooling system fault.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Do not return it to patient use.
  • Send it for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Repair or replace the fan assembly if confirmed defective.
  • Perform required operational checks before returning the ventilator to service.

Persistent fan failure alarms strongly indicate the ventilator cannot be trusted for clinical use until repaired.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient when cooling, alarm reliability, or ventilator operation may be affected. Move the patient to a backup ventilator first. Do not rely on a ventilator with an unresolved cooling fan alarm because overheating can lead to unexpected device failure during patient care.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator reported Alarm: 246003 Fan failure. Staff stated the cooling fan alarm appeared during operation, and the unit was removed from patient use for evaluation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External vents were inspected for blockage and dust buildup. Fan operation was checked. Alarm persisted after external airflow checks, indicating likely internal cooling fan, fan wiring, or fan control failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service and labeled Out of Service. Sent for repair/bench evaluation due to persistent fan failure alarm and potential overheating risk. Device not returned to clinical use pending repair and operational verification."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A fan failure alarm on a Hamilton C1 ventilator should be treated as a patient safety issue, not a minor nuisance alarm. Start with simple external airflow checks, confirm fan behavior, and rule out blocked vents or contamination first. If the alarm persists, remove the ventilator from service and escalate for repair. Clear documentation protects the patient, the technician, and the clinical team.

That is successful troubleshooting.
