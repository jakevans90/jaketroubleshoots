Mindray SynoVent E5

Flow Sensor Calibration Fault

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

SynoVent E5

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting flow sensor calibration faults on a Mindray SynoVent E5 ventilator. Flow sensor errors can prevent accurate measurement of patient ventilation parameters and are often caused by external issues such as sensor misalignment, circuit setup errors, or contamination, before assuming internal sensor failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Do not troubleshoot the ventilator while it is connected to a patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or support device first.

Power Cycle the Ventilator

  • Turn the ventilator off and back on after a brief pause.
  • This resets software flags and can clear transient calibration faults.

Check Circuit Connections

  • Ensure that the inspiratory and expiratory limbs are properly connected.
  • Misconnected or partially inserted tubing can cause erroneous flow readings.

Inspect Flow Sensor for Obstructions or Contamination

  • Remove the flow sensor according to standard protocol.
  • Check for condensation, secretions, or debris. Clean using approved methods if contamination is present.

Verify Correct Sensor Orientation

  • Confirm the sensor is installed in the proper orientation as indicated by manufacturer markings.
  • Flow sensors installed backward or rotated can trigger calibration errors.

Perform a Manual Flow Calibration

  • Follow the ventilator’s flow calibration procedure using the built-in menu.
  • Monitor for completion without error messages.

Check Environmental Conditions

  • Ensure the ventilator is not in a location with excessive humidity, dust, or temperature extremes.
  • Environmental factors can affect flow sensor accuracy.

Swap with a Known Good Flow Sensor (if available)

  • Replacing the sensor with a verified working unit can confirm if the fault is sensor-specific.

If the Problem Persists

External causes have likely been ruled out.

The ventilator should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for manufacturer repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop prevents further risk to patient safety and ensures proper escalation.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not use the ventilator on a patient while the flow sensor is reporting calibration errors.

Move the patient to another ventilator or support device first.

Only troubleshoot when the device is disconnected from a patient and safe to manipulate.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Ventilator reports "Flow Sensor Calibration Fault" during pre-use check.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Flow sensor contaminated with moisture; or improperly oriented in circuit.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Checklist details: verified connections, inspected for condensation, replaced sensor if needed, calibration completed.

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Verified circuit connections
  • Inspected flow sensor for condensation or debris
  • Replaced flow sensor if contamination or fault suspected
  • Flow calibration procedure completed successfully
  • Ventilator removed from service if error persisted

Final Thought

Flow sensor faults are often caused by setup or environmental issues rather than internal electronics. Following a structured troubleshooting sequence ensures patient safety, preserves device integrity, and prevents unnecessary service calls. Accurate CCR documentation supports both compliance and future reference.

That is successful troubleshooting.
