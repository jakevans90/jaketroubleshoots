Philips SureSigns VS4

SpO₂ Probe Error

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

SureSigns VS4

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Philips SureSigns VS4 monitor reports an SpO₂ probe error. Symptoms may include missing or erratic SpO₂ readings, absence of waveform, alarms indicating probe error, or failure to recognize the probe. Most issues originate from external causes such as probe disconnection, sensor damage, or cable issues, rather than internal board failures.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Probe Connection

  • Ensure the SpO₂ probe is securely connected to the monitor port.
  • Loose connections can prevent the monitor from reading the sensor.

Inspect the Probe for Damage

  • Check the sensor for cracks, exposed wiring, or bent connectors.
  • Damaged probes often trigger error messages and require replacement.

Swap Probes or Cables

  • If a spare probe is available, connect it to the monitor.
  • Swapping helps isolate whether the issue is with the probe or the monitor itself.

Check for Obstructions or Patient Interference

  • Ensure the sensor site is clean, dry, and properly attached.
  • Remove any nail polish, excessive motion, or external light sources affecting readings.

Restart the Monitor

  • Power cycle the monitor to clear temporary errors.
  • Errors persisting after a restart suggest hardware or cable issues.

Verify Software and Firmware

  • Confirm the monitor is running the latest Philips-approved firmware.
  • Outdated software can occasionally misinterpret probe signals.

If the Problem Persists

  • External checks have been exhausted.
  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for bench evaluation or repair.
  • Continuing to troubleshoot without proper isolation may risk patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt troubleshooting on an actively monitored patient.
  • Move the patient to another functioning monitor first.
  • Only troubleshoot the device when patient safety is ensured.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor shows SpO₂ probe error; no readings displayed.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Probe cable connector was loose; no physical damage to monitor port.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Reconnected probe securely; verified proper waveform and readings. Replaced probe when error persisted with spare probe test.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested (if device is not battery-powered).
  • Indicator lights on probe port.
  • Alarms displayed and frequency.
  • Any unusual sounds or heat near the port.
  • Final device status: functioning with a verified probe or removed from service.

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting balances patient safety, logical stepwise checks, and knowing when to escalate. Proper documentation ensures accountability and prevents repeated errors. Always start with external causes before assuming internal failure.

That is successful troubleshooting.
