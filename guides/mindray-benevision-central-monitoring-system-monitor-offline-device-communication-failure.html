Mindray BeneVision Central Monitoring System

Monitor Offline / Device Communication Failure

Asset Type

Central Monitoring Station

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

BeneVision Central Monitoring System

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting offline bedside monitors, missing network communication, or loss of patient data at the Mindray BeneVision central station.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

A central station communication failure can prevent staff from seeing alarms, waveforms, or vital signs at the central monitoring location.

Action:

  • Confirm the patient is still being monitored at the bedside monitor.
  • Verify bedside alarms are active and audible.
  • Notify clinical staff that the central station may not be receiving data from the affected monitor.
  • If central monitoring is required for the patient, have staff increase direct observation or move the patient to another monitored bed as appropriate.

Expected outcome: Patient monitoring remains active at the bedside while central communication is investigated.

Why it matters: The central station is a secondary display and alarm management point. Loss of central visibility should not leave the patient unmonitored.

Confirm the Reported Offline Condition

Verify whether the issue affects one monitor, one bed, one unit, or the entire central station.

Action:

  • Check the BeneVision Central Monitoring System display for the affected bed.
  • Look for messages such as offline, disconnected, no data, communication failure, or blank patient sector.
  • Ask staff when the issue started and whether the bedside monitor was recently moved, rebooted, admitted, discharged, or reassigned.
  • Compare the affected bed to other beds on the same central station.

Expected outcome: The failure is narrowed to a single monitor, a specific network drop, a bed assignment issue, or a larger system/network issue.

If all beds are offline, skip ahead to checking the central station network, switch, and server communication.

Check the Bedside Monitor First

Go to the affected bedside monitor and confirm it is powered on and functioning normally.

Action:

  • Verify the bedside monitor is powered on.
  • Confirm it is displaying patient parameters locally.
  • Check for local alarms, error messages, or network indicators.
  • Confirm the monitor is not frozen, rebooting, in standby, or disconnected from the patient.

Expected outcome: The bedside monitor should be operating normally and displaying valid patient data.

If the bedside monitor itself is not working, troubleshoot the bedside monitor before focusing on the central station.

Verify Network Cable Connection

For hardwired monitors, check the physical network connection.

Action:

  • Inspect the Ethernet cable at the bedside monitor or docking station.
  • Confirm the cable is fully seated and latched.
  • Check the wall data jack connection.
  • Look for damaged cable ends, broken tabs, sharp bends, or loose connections.
  • If safe and available, swap with a known-good Ethernet cable.

Expected outcome: A loose or damaged network cable is corrected, and the monitor reconnects to the central station.

If the monitor reconnects after reseating or replacing the cable, document the cable or connection issue and stop.

Check the Network Jack or Bed Location

Determine whether the issue follows the monitor or stays with the room/data jack.

Action:

  • Ask whether the monitor was recently moved from another room.
  • Confirm the monitor is connected to the correct clinical network jack, not a general-use or inactive port.
  • If allowed by site policy, test the monitor on a known-good network jack.
  • Alternatively, connect a known-good monitor to the suspect jack.

Expected outcome: The issue is isolated to either the bedside monitor or the network port/location.

If the problem stays with the jack, escalate to IT/network support for port, VLAN, switch, or jack troubleshooting.

Confirm Wireless Connection If Applicable

If the BeneVision monitor is using wireless communication, check for wireless connection problems.

Action:

  • Confirm the monitor is connected to the correct hospital wireless network.
  • Check the wireless signal indicator.
  • Ask whether the issue occurs only in one room, hallway, or unit area.
  • Move the monitor only if clinically safe and approved.
  • Look for recent access point, wireless, or network changes reported by IT.

Expected outcome: Weak wireless signal, incorrect wireless connection, or coverage issues are identified.

If wireless coverage appears poor or the monitor cannot join the correct network, escalate to IT/network support.

Verify Bed Assignment and Patient Association

A monitor may appear offline or missing if the bed, device, or patient assignment is incorrect.

Action:

  • Check the central station bed sector assignment.
  • Confirm the bedside monitor is assigned to the correct bed/location.
  • Verify the patient was not discharged, transferred, or admitted under a different bed.
  • Confirm the device name, bed label, or monitor ID matches the expected central station location.

Expected outcome: The bedside monitor and central station are looking for the same bed/device assignment.

If correcting the bed assignment restores communication, document the assignment mismatch and stop.

Check Central Station Display and Sector Status

Inspect the central station for software or display issues.

Action:

  • Confirm the central station is not frozen.
  • Verify mouse, keyboard, touchscreen, or user interface response.
  • Check whether other patient sectors update normally.
  • Look for system alerts, network icons, server messages, or abnormal timestamps.

Expected outcome: The central station is responsive and only the affected monitor is offline.

If the central station is frozen or multiple beds are not updating, the issue may be central station software, server, or network related.

Confirm Central Station Network Connection

Check the central station workstation network connection.

Action:

  • Inspect the Ethernet cable at the central station computer.
  • Confirm the cable is seated securely.
  • Check for link lights at the network port if visible.
  • Verify the workstation has not been moved, unplugged, or connected to the wrong network jack.
  • Ask staff whether any equipment, carts, cleaning, or cable movement occurred near the station.

Expected outcome: The central station has a stable physical network connection.

If the central station network connection is loose and restoring it brings beds back online, document the finding and stop.

Check for Unit-Wide or System-Wide Failure

Determine whether the problem is isolated or widespread.

Action:

  • Check additional central stations, if available.
  • Ask the unit whether multiple monitors dropped offline at the same time.
  • Contact IT, network operations, or the monitoring system administrator if multiple devices are affected.
  • Check whether there was recent maintenance, switch replacement, server work, power outage, or network change.

Expected outcome: A larger network or server issue is identified if multiple devices are affected.

If multiple monitors or multiple central stations are affected, do not treat it as a single-device failure.

Reboot Only When Safe and Appropriate

Rebooting should not be the first step and should only be done when patient monitoring is protected.

Action:

  • Confirm clinical staff are aware before rebooting any bedside monitor or central station workstation.
  • Do not reboot a monitor actively supporting critical monitoring unless alternate monitoring is in place.
  • If allowed by site policy, reboot the affected bedside monitor after patient safety is addressed.
  • If the central station workstation is frozen or not updating, follow facility-approved restart procedures.

Expected outcome: Temporary software or communication lockup clears and the device reconnects.

If communication returns after reboot, monitor for recurrence and document the reboot and final status.

Check for Recurring or Intermittent Communication Drops

Intermittent offline events may indicate a cable, jack, network, or device communication problem.

Action:

  • Ask staff how often the monitor drops offline.
  • Check whether the failure occurs with patient movement, bed movement, cable contact, or monitor docking.
  • Inspect for loose docking connections, damaged network cables, or unstable wall jacks.
  • Record the time, bed, device ID, and symptoms.

Expected outcome: A pattern is identified that helps isolate the issue.

If the issue is intermittent and cannot be corrected with external checks, remove the monitor from service for bench evaluation or escalate to IT if the network path is suspected.

If the Problem Persists

If power, bedside monitor function, network cable, wall jack, bed assignment, wireless connection, and central station status have all been checked, the issue may involve internal device communication hardware, central station software, server configuration, or hospital network infrastructure.

The affected device should be removed from service if the bedside monitor or central station cannot reliably communicate.

The affected device should be labeled Out of Service if it is suspected to be equipment-related.

The affected device should be sent for repair, bench evaluation, vendor support, or IT/network escalation as appropriate.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not repeatedly reboot, swap settings, or leave an unreliable monitoring path in clinical use without escalation.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a central monitoring communication failure as if it is only a computer issue. First confirm the patient is safely monitored at the bedside. If central visibility is required, staff should use another monitored bed, alternate monitoring, or increased direct observation until communication is restored. Do not troubleshoot on an active patient without moving the patient to a backup device or verified monitoring path first. Therapy and monitoring continuity must be maintained before troubleshooting continues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Nursing reported that Bed 12 was showing offline at the Mindray BeneVision central station with no waveform or vital sign data displayed."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Bedside monitor was powered on and monitoring locally, but the Ethernet cable at the wall jack was loose and not maintaining network communication."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated the Ethernet cable, verified the monitor reconnected to the BeneVision central station, confirmed live parameters were updating, and returned the system to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior at bedside and central station
  • Accessories swapped, including Ethernet cable, docking connection, or known-good monitor if applicable
  • Power behavior, including bedside monitor power, central station power, and whether the unit was rebooting or frozen
  • Environmental factors, including recent room move, admit, discharge, transfer, cleaning, cable movement, or IT/network changes
  • Indicator lights, network link lights, wireless signal status, or system messages observed
  • Final device status, including whether the monitor reconnected, remained offline, was labeled Out of Service, or was escalated to IT, vendor support, or bench evaluation

Final Thought

A monitor offline condition should be handled with patient safety first, then logical isolation of the communication path. Start with the bedside monitor, cable, wall jack, network connection, and bed assignment before assuming central station or internal equipment failure. Clear CCR documentation helps separate equipment issues from network, workflow, or assignment problems.

That is successful troubleshooting.
