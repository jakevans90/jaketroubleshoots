Connex 6000 Series

Battery Not Charging

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

Welch Allyn

Model

Connex 6000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Connex 6000 Series monitor battery does not charge. Symptoms may include the battery icon not increasing while connected to power, battery alarms, the device shutting down when unplugged, or the battery status indicating a charging failure. Many charging issues are caused by external factors such as power supply problems, docking station issues, or battery degradation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the monitor is not actively being used on a patient before beginning troubleshooting.
  • If the device cannot maintain battery power, move the patient to another monitor before removing the device from service.

Verify the device is connected to AC power

  • Confirm the monitor is plugged into a known-good hospital outlet or charging dock.
  • If the device is not receiving AC power, the battery will not charge.
  • Look for:
  • Power indicator lights
  • Charging icon on the display
  • Docking station indicators (if used)
  • If the device powers off when unplugged, the battery may be depleted or failed.

Check the AC power adapter and power cord

  • Inspect the power supply and cord for:
  • Loose connections
  • Frayed or damaged cable
  • Bent pins at the connector
  • If available, test with a known-good power adapter compatible with the Connex 6000 Series.
  • A faulty power adapter is a common cause of charging failure.

Inspect the docking station (if used)

  • Many Connex 6000 monitors charge through a wall mount or rolling stand dock.
  • Check for:
  • Loose dock connection
  • Bent or dirty charging contacts
  • Debris on docking contacts
  • Remove the monitor from the dock and reconnect it firmly to ensure proper contact.
  • If possible, test charging using the direct AC adapter instead of the dock.

Check battery charge status in the system menu

  • Navigate to the device settings or battery status screen to confirm:
  • Battery presence detected
  • Charging status
  • Battery health information if available
  • If the monitor does not detect the battery, the battery may be improperly seated or failed.

Power cycle the monitor

  • A system restart may reset battery management functions.
  • Steps:
  • Turn the monitor off
  • Disconnect AC power
  • Wait 30 seconds
  • Reconnect power and restart the device
  • Observe whether the charging indicator appears after reboot.

Inspect battery compartment (external check only)

  • If accessible without disassembly:
  • Verify the battery is properly installed
  • Ensure the battery latch is secure
  • Check for corrosion or debris on contacts
  • If the battery appears swollen, damaged, or hot, remove the device from service immediately.

Test with a known-good battery if available

  • If a spare battery is available:
  • Install a known-good battery
  • Connect the monitor to AC power
  • Observe whether charging begins
  • If the replacement battery charges normally, the original battery is likely at end-of-life.

If the Problem Persists

If the monitor still does not charge after verifying power, adapters, docking connections, and battery installation, the issue is likely internal. Possible causes include:

  • Failed internal charging circuit
  • Battery interface failure
  • Power board issue

At this point the device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or further bench evaluation

Recognizing when external troubleshooting has been exhausted is proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot charging issues while the monitor is actively being used on a patient. If the device cannot maintain battery power, move the patient to another monitor before removing the device from service.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nursing reported the Connex 6000 monitor battery was not charging and the device shut off when unplugged.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Battery remained at 0% despite being connected to AC power. Tested with known-good power adapter and dock with same result. Battery likely failed.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Removed device from service and replaced battery. Verified charging indicator and confirmed battery level increased while connected to AC power.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and confirmed working
  • Power adapter inspected and/or swapped
  • Docking station tested
  • Charging icon status observed
  • Battery condition checked
  • Device power behavior when unplugged
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Battery charging problems are often caused by simple external issues such as power adapters, docking connections, or worn batteries. Following a logical troubleshooting process helps Clinical Engineering quickly identify these problems while ensuring patient safety. When external causes are ruled out, escalating the device for repair is the correct course of action.

That is successful troubleshooting.
