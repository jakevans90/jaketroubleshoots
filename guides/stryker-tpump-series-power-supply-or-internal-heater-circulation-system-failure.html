Stryker T/Pump Series

Power Supply or Internal Heater / Circulation System Failure

Asset Type

Patient Warming System

Manufacturer

Stryker

Model

T/Pump Series

What This Guide Helps With

Unit not powering on, shutting off, or not producing heat due to power issues, setup problems, or internal heater/circulation failures.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from active patient use and transition to an alternate warming method.
  • Expected: No patient connected during troubleshooting.

Verify Power Source

  • Plug the unit into a known-good hospital-grade outlet. Test the outlet with another device if needed.
  • Expected: Reliable power available.
  • If outlet is faulty → move to a working outlet and retest.

Inspect Power Cord and Plug

  • Check for frayed cable, bent prongs, or loose connections. Ensure cord is fully seated in the unit.
  • Why it matters: Damaged cords can mimic internal power failure.
  • If damaged → replace cord and retest.

Check Power Switch and Indicators

  • Turn the unit on and observe for display, lights, or pump/fan activity.
  • Expected: Normal startup indicators present.
  • If no response → continue troubleshooting power path.

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn the unit off, wait 10–15 seconds, then power back on.
  • Why it matters: Resets internal control and safety circuits.
  • If unit powers normally → issue resolved.

Check for Alarm or Safety Lockout Conditions

  • Look for over-temperature or system fault alarms.
  • Why it matters: Safety circuits may disable heating or operation after a fault.
  • If alarm clears after reset → monitor for recurrence.

Inspect Hose and Pad / Blanket Connections

  • Ensure hoses are securely connected with no kinks or leaks. Verify pad/blanket is properly attached.
  • Why it matters: Restricted or improper circulation can prevent heating.
  • If corrected and system resumes heating → issue resolved.

Verify Fluid Level (if applicable)

  • Check reservoir level per facility protocol (if unit design allows).
  • Why it matters: Low fluid can prevent proper heat transfer and circulation.
  • If low → correct level and retest.

Verify Temperature Setting and Mode

  • Confirm setpoint is appropriate (~37–41°C) and correct mode is selected.
  • Why it matters: Incorrect settings can appear as heating failure.
  • If corrected → verify warming output.

Assess Circulation and Heat Output

  • Run the unit and feel for warmth at the pad/blanket and check for signs of circulation (subtle vibration or flow).
  • Expected: Gradual, consistent warming.
  • If unit powers on but no heat or circulation is present → likely internal heater or pump issue.

Check for Unusual Signs

  • Look for abnormal noise (no pump sound or grinding), burning smell, or excessive heat at the unit housing.
  • Why it matters: Indicates internal component failure.
  • If present → stop use immediately.

If the Problem Persists

If the unit does not power on or powers on without producing heat after external checks, internal failure of the power supply, heater, or circulation pump is likely.

Remove the device from service, label Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting and protects patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a patient warming system while connected to a patient. Always transition to an alternate warming method first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Unit would not power on / no heat output during patient use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Internal heater or circulation system failure suspected after verifying outlet, cord, and setup."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed from service, labeled out of service, and sent to Biomed/vendor for repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Power cord condition
  • Indicator lights or display status
  • Alarm presence or absence
  • Circulation felt or not
  • Heat output observed or not
  • Any unusual noise, smell, or heat
  • Final device status (removed from service)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple external checks before assuming internal failure. Understanding that this system relies on heated fluid circulation—not a heater plate—helps guide accurate diagnosis and safe escalation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
