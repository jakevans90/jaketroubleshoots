Hamilton C1

Alarm: 244011 Battery communication error

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm 244011 Battery communication error on the Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates the ventilator cannot communicate correctly with the battery system, which may make battery status, charge level, runtime, or backup power availability unreliable.

Because battery communication affects backup power confidence, this alarm should be treated as a patient safety concern, especially if the ventilator is in use, running on battery power, or expected to be available for transport or backup operation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the ventilator from patient use if it is actively alarming, running on battery, or if battery status cannot be trusted.
  • If the ventilator is in use on a patient, connect the ventilator to reliable external AC power immediately.
  • Transfer the patient to another ventilator if battery reliability, ventilation stability, or alarm status cannot be quickly confirmed.
  • Do not rely on battery operation until the alarm is resolved and battery status displays normally.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the screen displays Alarm: 244011 Battery communication error.
  • Check battery icon behavior, charge indication, charging status, and external power status.
  • Look for additional battery or power alarms that may help identify a larger power system issue.
  • Note whether the alarm appears at startup, when unplugged from AC power, when the battery is installed, intermittently during use, or after battery replacement.

Connect to External Power

  • Plug the ventilator into a known-good AC outlet.
  • Confirm the AC power indicator is present.
  • Verify the power cord is fully seated at the wall outlet and ventilator.
  • Check whether the battery begins charging or displays normal battery information.
  • If the alarm clears and battery status returns to normal, continue monitoring and document the finding.

Inspect the Battery Installation

  • Power the ventilator down only if safe and not connected to a patient.
  • Remove the battery and inspect whether it was fully inserted and latched correctly.
  • Reinstall the battery firmly.
  • Restart the ventilator and check whether the alarm returns.
  • If reseating clears the alarm, confirm normal charging and battery status before returning the ventilator to service.

Inspect Battery Contacts

  • Remove the battery again if safe.
  • Inspect the battery contacts and ventilator-side contacts for bent pins, contamination, corrosion, fluid residue, damage, debris, loose contacts, or recessed contacts.
  • Clean only according to approved facility or manufacturer practices.
  • If damaged contacts are found, remove the affected battery or ventilator from service as appropriate.

Try a Known-Good Battery

  • Install a known-good compatible Hamilton C1 battery.
  • Power on the ventilator.
  • Verify battery recognition, charge status, and alarm status.
  • If the known-good battery works, the original battery is likely defective.
  • If the alarm remains with a known-good battery, suspect a ventilator-side battery manager, connector, charging, or communication issue.

Check Battery Age and Condition

  • Check the battery date, service history, and maintenance records.
  • Inspect for swelling, case damage, leakage, overheating, or unusual smell.
  • Review whether the battery has had repeated charge, runtime, recognition, or communication complaints.
  • Replace the battery if it is aged, damaged, repeatedly faulting, or no longer reporting correctly.

Check for Related Power or Charging Issues

  • Verify the battery charges while the ventilator is connected to AC power.
  • Confirm the battery charge percentage changes normally.
  • Check for repeated low battery alarms with a charged battery.
  • Confirm no external power loss alarms are active.
  • Verify the power supply connection is stable.
  • Watch for unexpected shutdowns or reboots.

Isolate the Likely Cause

  • If reseating clears the alarm, suspect poor battery seating.
  • If cleaning or correcting contacts clears the alarm, suspect a contact-related issue.
  • If the fault follows one battery, suspect a defective battery.
  • If multiple known-good batteries produce the same alarm, suspect a ventilator-side battery manager or internal communication hardware issue.
  • If the alarm is intermittent, suspect loose contacts, a battery latch issue, or an internal battery communication path problem.

Stop When Resolved

  • Confirm the ventilator recognizes the battery.
  • Confirm charging status appears normal.
  • Confirm no battery communication or power alarms return after restart.
  • Confirm external power and battery status are stable.
  • Perform any required operational or pre-use checks before returning the ventilator to service.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm 244011 remains after reseating the battery, inspecting contacts, confirming external power, and testing with a known-good battery, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent battery communication errors may indicate a device-side issue such as a battery manager fault, internal communication hardware problem, damaged battery connector, charging system fault, internal wiring issue, or control board communication problem.

The ventilator should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation. Do not return the ventilator to patient use if battery status or backup power reliability cannot be confirmed.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot battery communication faults on an active patient if battery status or backup power reliability is uncertain. Move the patient to a backup ventilator first, or confirm the ventilator is on reliable external power and clinical staff have determined the patient is safe before any troubleshooting continues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator reported Alarm 244011 Battery communication error. Staff stated the ventilator displayed a battery communication fault and battery status could not be confirmed."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery communication between the ventilator and battery system was abnormal. Battery was reseated, contacts were inspected, and known-good battery testing was performed to determine whether the issue followed the battery or remained with the ventilator."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated battery, inspected battery contacts, tested unit on external AC power, and verified battery status. Installed known-good battery for comparison. Ventilator passed power and battery checks and was returned to service, or was removed from service for battery manager/internal communication repair evaluation if the alarm persisted."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Alarm 244011 should be handled as a backup power reliability concern, not just a nuisance message. Start with patient safety, confirm the exact alarm, check external power, inspect battery seating and contacts, and use a known-good battery to separate battery failure from ventilator-side communication faults. If the alarm remains after external checks, remove the ventilator from service and escalate for repair with clear documentation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
