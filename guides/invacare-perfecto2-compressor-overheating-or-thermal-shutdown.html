Invacare Perfecto2

Compressor Overheating Or Thermal Shutdown

Asset Type

Oxygen Concentrator

Manufacturer

Invacare

Model

Perfecto2

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting Perfecto2 compressor overheating, hot operation, intermittent shutdown, airflow restriction, and cooling-related external causes.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

If the Perfecto2 is in active patient use and shuts down, overheats, alarms, or produces unreliable oxygen flow, remove it from patient use immediately.

Transfer the patient to a backup oxygen source according to facility policy.

Expected outcome: The patient is not dependent on an oxygen concentrator with unstable compressor operation.

Confirm the Reported Symptom

Power the unit in a controlled Biomed area and verify the complaint.

Check whether the issue is:

  • Unit shuts down after running for a period of time
  • Compressor becomes unusually hot
  • Thermal protection trips
  • Reduced airflow or low oxygen alarm occurs before shutdown
  • Cabinet is excessively warm
  • Fan is not running or airflow is weak

Expected outcome: The overheating complaint is confirmed before parts or repair decisions are made.

Check for Proper Placement and Ventilation

Verify the concentrator is not pushed against a wall, bed, curtain, cabinet, or other obstruction.

Confirm the air intake and exhaust areas have adequate clearance.

Expected outcome: Air can move freely around the cabinet.

If poor placement was causing heat buildup, reposition the unit, run it under observation, and stop if normal operation returns.

Inspect the External Cabinet and Air Openings

Look for blocked vents, lint accumulation, dust, pet hair, plastic bags, labels, or bedding material restricting airflow.

Clean external debris using approved facility cleaning methods.

Expected outcome: Cooling air paths are visibly clear.

Restricted airflow is a common external cause of compressor overheating.

Check the Intake Filter Condition

Inspect the cabinet intake filter or external filter area for dust loading, clogging, moisture, or improper installation.

Clean or replace the filter according to facility practice and manufacturer-compatible parts policy.

Expected outcome: The concentrator can pull in adequate room air.

If a clogged filter caused the issue, run the unit and verify stable operation.

Verify the Cooling Fan Operation

Power the unit on and observe whether the cooling fan runs normally.

Listen for abnormal fan noise, scraping, intermittent operation, or no fan operation.

Expected outcome: The fan runs consistently and moves air through the unit.

If the fan does not run, runs intermittently, or has weak airflow, remove the unit from service for repair evaluation.

Check Room Temperature and Environment

Confirm the unit is not operating in a hot room, near a heater, in direct sunlight, inside a closet, or in a poorly ventilated area.

Expected outcome: The unit is operating in a reasonable clinical environment with normal room ventilation.

Excess ambient heat can cause compressor temperature to rise even when the concentrator itself is not internally failed.

Inspect the Power Cord and Outlet

Check the power cord for damage, loose plug fit, heat discoloration, or signs of arcing.

Test the outlet with an approved electrical safety or outlet tester if appropriate.

Expected outcome: The unit is receiving stable AC power without evidence of cord or outlet overheating.

If the outlet or cord is suspect, stop testing and correct the power source issue before further evaluation.

Check for Abnormal Compressor Sound or Vibration

While running the unit, listen for loud knocking, buzzing, grinding, excessive vibration, or compressor strain.

Expected outcome: Compressor sound is steady and consistent.

Abnormal sound combined with overheating may indicate compressor wear, restriction, or internal mechanical load.

Verify Output Flow Setting

Confirm the flowmeter is set within the rated operating range for the Perfecto2.

Make sure the ball is stable and not bouncing unusually.

Expected outcome: Flow is set appropriately and remains stable.

Running outside normal flow expectations can contribute to performance complaints or alarm conditions.

Check Patient Tubing and External Accessories

Inspect tubing, humidifier bottle, cannula, and connectors for kinks, blockage, water accumulation, crushed tubing, or incorrect attachment.

Temporarily test with known-good tubing and no humidifier bottle if facility procedure allows.

Expected outcome: External accessories are not restricting output flow.

If the concentrator runs normally after removing or replacing a restricted accessory, document the accessory issue and return the unit only after performance is verified.

Check for Humidifier Bottle Restriction

If a humidifier bottle is used, inspect for overfilling, clogged diffuser, incorrect bottle type, cracked cap, or cross-threaded connection.

Expected outcome: The humidifier does not create excessive back pressure or flow restriction.

A blocked humidifier can make the concentrator work harder and may contribute to heat or alarm complaints.

Run the Unit Under Observation

After correcting external issues, run the concentrator at a normal clinical flow setting in a ventilated Biomed area.

Monitor for:

  • Stable operation
  • Normal fan airflow
  • No thermal shutdown
  • No unusual heat
  • No burning smell
  • No abnormal compressor noise
  • Stable flow

Expected outcome: The concentrator operates without overheating or shutting down.

If the issue does not recur and all checks pass, document findings and return according to facility policy.

Check Oxygen Output Performance if Available

Use an approved oxygen analyzer after the unit has warmed up and stabilized.

Expected outcome: Oxygen concentration is within acceptable facility/manufacturer expectations.

If oxygen concentration is low, unstable, or alarm behavior continues, remove the unit from service for further evaluation.

Do Not Continue Repeated Thermal Testing

If the unit repeatedly overheats, shuts down, smells hot, or trips thermal protection, stop testing.

Expected outcome: The device is not stressed further and is not returned to patient use.

Repeated overheating may damage internal components or create a reliability risk.

If the Problem Persists

If ventilation, filters, external tubing, humidifier setup, outlet power, and environmental causes have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal.

The Perfecto2 should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Possible internal causes may include fan failure, compressor wear, restricted internal airflow, failing thermal protection, sieve bed restriction, valve issues, or internal wiring concerns.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A concentrator with recurring compressor overheating should not be returned to patient use without repair and verification.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot an overheating oxygen concentrator on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup oxygen source first, then evaluate the device in a controlled Biomed area. Oxygen delivery reliability and fire safety both matter when heat-related complaints are reported.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported the Invacare Perfecto2 shut down after extended operation and the cabinet felt unusually hot."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Biomed found the intake filter heavily dust-loaded and the unit positioned with restricted rear cabinet ventilation."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleaned external vents, replaced the intake filter, repositioned the unit for proper airflow, ran the concentrator under observation, and verified stable operation without thermal shutdown."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Whether the unit was in active patient use
  • Flow setting at time of shutdown
  • Runtime before overheating occurred
  • Outlet tested
  • Power cord condition
  • Intake filter condition
  • Cabinet vent condition
  • Fan operation confirmed
  • Tubing or humidifier bottle swapped
  • Alarm behavior observed
  • Oxygen concentration reading if tested
  • Unusual compressor noise
  • Unusual heat or burning smell
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Compressor overheating should be approached logically and safely. Start with airflow, placement, filters, power, and accessories before assuming an internal compressor fault. If the Perfecto2 continues to overheat after external causes are ruled out, removing it from service is the correct Clinical Engineering decision. Clear CCR documentation supports patient safety, repair history, and future troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
