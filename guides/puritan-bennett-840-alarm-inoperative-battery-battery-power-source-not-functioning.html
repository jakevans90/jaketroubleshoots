Puritan Bennett 840

Alarm: INOPERATIVE BATTERY Battery power source not functioning

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Puritan Bennett

Model

840

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps with the INOPERATIVE BATTERY alarm on the Puritan Bennett 840 ventilator when the battery power source is not functioning. This condition means the ventilator may have no usable battery backup, creating a risk of ventilation loss during AC power failure or transport.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the ventilator is in use, immediately confirm the patient is being ventilated safely.
  • Connect the ventilator to a confirmed AC power source.
  • Move the patient to a backup ventilator or alternate ventilation method if backup power is in question.
  • Do not rely on transport or continued clinical use with an active battery failure alarm.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Power on the ventilator and confirm the INOPERATIVE BATTERY alarm is present.
  • Observe whether the alarm appears on startup or during operation.
  • Check whether battery runtime is unavailable when AC power is removed.
  • Look for battery indicators showing failure, unavailable status, or no backup capability.

Check the AC Power Source

  • Verify the ventilator is plugged into a known-good AC outlet.
  • Test the outlet with another device if needed.
  • Make sure the power cord is fully seated at the wall and ventilator.
  • Confirm the unit operates normally on AC power.

Inspect the Power Cord and AC Distribution Path

  • Inspect the full power cord for visible damage, looseness, or wear.
  • Reseat all AC input connections.
  • Avoid questionable extension cords or worn power accessories.
  • Confirm stable AC input so battery charging conditions are not being interrupted externally.

Perform a Power Cycle

  • Turn the ventilator off.
  • Wait 30 to 60 seconds.
  • Power the unit back on.
  • Check whether the alarm clears after restart.

Run the Extended Self Test (EST)

  • Access the EST according to the device procedure.
  • Allow the test to complete fully.
  • Review whether the test identifies a battery or internal power-system fault.
  • Use the EST result to support service disposition.

Check Battery Function Only When Safe

  • Only perform this step when the ventilator is not connected to a patient.
  • Disconnect AC power briefly and observe whether the ventilator remains powered.
  • Watch for immediate shutdown or return of the battery-related alarm.
  • Immediate loss of power supports an inoperative battery condition.

If the Problem Persists

If the INOPERATIVE BATTERY alarm remains active after verifying the AC source, power cord, connections, restart behavior, and EST results, likely external causes have been ruled out. Remove the ventilator from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation. Battery replacement or internal power-system repair may be required.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first before checking battery function or confirming loss of backup power.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator displayed INOPERATIVE BATTERY alarm and would not maintain power when unplugged from AC."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified stable AC power input. EST indicated battery failure, and the ventilator shut down immediately when AC power was briefly removed off patient."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for battery replacement and bench evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery failure on a ventilator is a critical safety issue, not a minor warning. Confirm the alarm, rule out simple external power issues first, escalate appropriately, and document clearly so the device does not return to use without reliable backup power.

That is successful troubleshooting.
