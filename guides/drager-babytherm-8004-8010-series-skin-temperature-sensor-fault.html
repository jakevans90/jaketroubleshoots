Drager Babytherm 8004 / 8010 Series

Skin Temperature Sensor Fault

Asset Type

Infant Warmer

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Babytherm 8004 / 8010 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting a skin temperature sensor fault, disconnected probe, incorrect reading, alarm condition, or failure to operate safely in skin control mode.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

If the warmer is in use with an infant, notify clinical staff and move the patient to another approved warming device or safe thermal support method before troubleshooting.

Do not rely on automatic skin temperature control while a sensor fault is active. A detached or failed sensor can produce unsafe temperature control conditions.

Expected outcome: The infant is safely supported without dependence on the affected warmer before testing begins.

Why it matters: In skin control mode, an incorrectly attached or disconnected sensor may measure air temperature rather than infant skin temperature, creating an overheating risk.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm Condition

Power on the Babytherm only after it is removed from patient use. Confirm the reported condition, including any of the following:

  • Red alarm LED blinking.
  • Audible alarm sounding.
  • Three middle segments or dashes blinking in the skin temperature display.
  • Display message: “Skin temp. sensor fault” or “Plug in skin temp. sensor.”

Expected outcome: The exact displayed message and alarm behavior are identified and documented.

Why it matters: The operating instructions identify these messages as consistent with a faulty or disconnected skin temperature sensor.

3. Confirm the Correct Sensor Connection

Inspect the skin temperature sensor connection at the warmer.

  • Confirm the central skin temperature sensor is fully seated in the yellow skin temperature sensor socket.
  • Confirm the connector is not loose, partially inserted, contaminated, bent, cracked, or visibly damaged.
  • Verify that the sensor cable is not accidentally connected to the peripheral temperature sensor location, when equipped.

Reseat the sensor connector and observe whether the alarm clears.

Expected outcome: A properly connected sensor produces a displayed skin temperature value without a sensor fault alarm.

If the issue resolves: Complete an operational check, document the corrected loose or disconnected sensor, and stop troubleshooting.

4. Inspect the Sensor and Cable Externally

Examine the entire visible sensor assembly from probe tip to connector.

Look for:

  • Cracked, cut, pinched, stretched, or frayed cable insulation.
  • Fluid intrusion or contamination.
  • Damaged probe surface.
  • Bent, loose, or broken connector components.
  • Evidence that the sensor was pulled or strained during use.

Do not attempt to repair a damaged patient temperature sensor cable.

Expected outcome: The sensor is either visibly intact or identified as damaged and unsuitable for further use.

If damage is found: Remove the damaged sensor from use and replace it with an approved compatible sensor.

5. Replace the Sensor With an Approved Compatible Sensor

Disconnect the suspected faulty sensor and install a known-good, approved skin temperature sensor intended for use with the Babytherm 8004/8010.

Use the correct sensor connection and confirm the replacement sensor is fully inserted.

Expected outcome: The “Skin temp. sensor fault” or “Plug in skin temp. sensor” message clears and a plausible skin temperature reading appears.

Why it matters: Drager documentation directs replacement of the skin temperature sensor for a sensor fault and specifies using approved sensors with the Babytherm 8004/8010.

6. Verify Basic Sensor Response Off Patient

With the replacement sensor connected and the warmer off patient, allow the sensor reading time to stabilize. Handle the sensor gently and observe whether the displayed skin temperature value responds gradually and remains stable without returning to a sensor fault alarm.

Do not use this check to validate clinical temperature accuracy or return the warmer to patient care without required facility testing.

Expected outcome: The displayed value is present, stable, and free of sensor fault alarms.

If the alarm returns with a known-good sensor: Suspect a device-side connector, input circuitry, or internal measurement failure.

7. Verify Skin Control Mode Can Be Selected Without Fault

After the sensor alarm has cleared, select skin temperature control mode as appropriate for an off-patient operational check. Confirm:

  • The skin control indicator activates.
  • A skin temperature value remains displayed.
  • No sensor fault alarm reappears.
  • No abnormal alarm behavior occurs during the check.

The Babytherm operating instructions indicate that, after replacing the sensor, the unit may be switched back from manual mode to skin control mode.

Expected outcome: Skin temperature control mode is available without recurring sensor fault alarms.

If the issue resolves: Complete required operational verification according to department policy before returning the warmer to service.

8. Check for Use-Related Conditions Before Returning the Unit

Before release, remind the requesting department that the sensor must be correctly positioned and secured during use.

Verify that staff have an approved sensor and appropriate attachment supplies available. The sensor must not be placed underneath the infant, and secure attachment is necessary because a detached sensor can measure air temperature and increase overheating risk.

Expected outcome: The equipment and approved accessories are ready for safe clinical setup.

If the Problem Persists

If a known-good approved skin temperature sensor remains unrecognized, the sensor fault returns, or the unit cannot operate in skin temperature control mode without alarms, common external causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely internal to the sensor input connection or temperature measurement circuitry.

The warmer should be:

  • Removed from service.
  • Labeled Out of Service.
  • Sent for bench evaluation, authorized repair, or manufacturer service support.

Do not proceed with internal disassembly or board-level troubleshooting in this guide. Knowing when to stop after ruling out the sensor and accessible connection is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a skin temperature sensor fault while an infant depends on the warmer for thermal support. Move the patient first, then evaluate the sensor, connection, alarms, and operational status only when safe.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"NICU reported that the Drager Babytherm 8010 displayed “Skin temp. sensor fault” with an audible alarm and would not maintain skin control monitoring."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Confirmed the alarm with the original skin temperature sensor connected; fault cleared after installing a known-good approved replacement sensor, identifying the original probe as defective."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the defective skin temperature sensor from use, installed a compatible replacement sensor, verified stable skin temperature display and normal skin control mode operation off patient, and returned the warmer to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Exact displayed message: “Skin temp. sensor fault” or “Plug in skin temp. sensor”
  • Red alarm LED and audible alarm behavior
  • Whether the original sensor was connected to the yellow socket
  • Visible sensor or cable damage
  • Whether the sensor connector was reseated
  • Replacement sensor used and whether it cleared the fault
  • Skin control mode operational check result
  • Final device status: returned to service or removed from service

Final Thought

A skin temperature sensor fault on an infant warmer must be handled with patient safety first. Logical troubleshooting starts with the sensor connection and approved replacement probe before suspecting internal failure. Clear escalation and accurate CCR documentation support safe equipment decisions and reliable follow-up.

That is successful troubleshooting.
