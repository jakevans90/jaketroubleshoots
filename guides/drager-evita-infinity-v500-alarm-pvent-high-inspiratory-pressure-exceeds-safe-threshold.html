Drager Evita V500

Alarm: PVENT high Inspiratory pressure exceeds safe threshold

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses the PVENT high alarm, indicating excessive inspiratory pressure that may lead to lung injury, ineffective ventilation, or circuit-related issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s respiratory status
  • Provide alternate ventilation if needed (bag-valve mask or backup ventilator)
  • Confirm adequate oxygenation and ventilation

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm “PVENT high” is displayed on the ventilator
  • Observe when the alarm occurs (startup, inspiration, intermittent)
  • Check pressure waveform and readings
  • Note any accompanying alarms

Check Breathing Circuit for Obstruction

  • Inspect tubing for kinks, twists, or compression
  • Check for water accumulation in the circuit
  • Ensure filters are clean and not clogged

Inspect Patient Interface (ET Tube / Mask)

  • Verify the airway device is not kinked or obstructed
  • Check for secretions or mucus plugs
  • Confirm proper positioning and patency

Review Ventilation Settings

  • Check pressure limits and alarm thresholds
  • Review tidal volume and inspiratory time settings
  • Ensure settings are appropriate for the patient

Check Expiratory Valve Function

  • Ensure the valve is properly installed and seated
  • Inspect for contamination or sticking
  • Verify proper exhalation function

Run System Self-Test (SST)

  • Perform SST if no external issue is found
  • Confirm the test completes without failure

Run Extended System Test (EST)

  • Perform EST if the alarm persists
  • Verify no faults are detected during extended diagnostics

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and the alarm continues:

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label the device as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a ventilator on an active patient. Always transfer the patient to a backup device before continuing troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator displaying PVENT high alarm with elevated inspiratory pressure during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"No circuit obstruction or airway issue identified; pressure remained elevated, suggesting possible internal sensor or valve issue."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service and sent for repair. Backup ventilator provided."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

High inspiratory pressure alarms require immediate attention. Always rule out external causes first, and escalate quickly if pressure control cannot be verified. Proper documentation and patient safety must remain the priority.

That is successful troubleshooting.
