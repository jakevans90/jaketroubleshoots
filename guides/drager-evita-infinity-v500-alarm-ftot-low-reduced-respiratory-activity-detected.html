Drager Evita V500

Alarm: fTOT low reduced respiratory activity detected

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses the alarm: fTOT low reduced respiratory activity detected, indicating a low total respiratory rate. This condition may lead to hypoventilation, inadequate gas exchange, and potential patient instability if not corrected promptly.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s breathing and ventilation status
  • Provide alternate ventilation (bag-valve mask or backup ventilator) if needed
  • Confirm oxygenation and clinical stability

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm “fTOT low” is displayed on the ventilator
  • Observe respiratory rate values on the screen
  • Note whether breaths are spontaneous, mandatory, or absent

3. Check Patient Condition (Most Critical Cause)

  • Assess for reduced respiratory drive or apnea
  • Observe chest rise and breathing effort
  • Review sedation level or clinical condition

4. Verify Ventilation Mode and Settings

  • Check mandatory respiratory rate setting
  • Confirm apnea backup ventilation is enabled
  • Review alarm limits for appropriateness

5. Check Trigger Sensitivity and Circuit Interaction

  • Verify trigger sensitivity settings
  • Ensure patient effort is sufficient to trigger breaths
  • Adjust sensitivity if clinically appropriate

6. Inspect Breathing Circuit and Sensors

  • Check for leaks or disconnections in the circuit
  • Inspect flow sensors for contamination or moisture
  • Verify expiratory valve function

7. Perform System Test if Indicated

  • Run system self-test (SST)
  • Run extended system test (EST) if available
  • Confirm system passes without errors

If the Problem Persists

If all external, patient, and configuration causes have been ruled out:

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label the device as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a ventilator while actively supporting a patient. Always transfer the patient to a backup device before attempting adjustments or diagnostics.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming fTOT low with reduced respiratory rate during patient use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Patient hypoventilation and low trigger sensitivity preventing breath detection."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Adjusted ventilation settings and trigger sensitivity; verified circuit integrity and patient condition."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Low respiratory rate alarms must always be treated as potential patient emergencies first. Proper troubleshooting separates patient condition, ventilator settings, and device performance before escalating appropriately.

That is successful troubleshooting.
