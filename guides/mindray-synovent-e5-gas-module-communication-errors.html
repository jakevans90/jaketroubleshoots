Mindray SynoVent E5

Gas Module Communication Errors

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

SynoVent E5

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting gas module communication errors on a Mindray SynoVent E5 ventilator. Communication errors between the ventilator and gas module can prevent accurate gas delivery, trigger alarms, or limit the ability to safely ventilate a patient. Most errors are caused by external or easily verifiable factors such as loose connectors, cable damage, power interruptions, or environmental conditions, before assuming an internal gas module fault.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power and Boot Sequence

  • Ensure the ventilator is powered with a stable outlet.
  • Check that the main unit and gas module power indicators are on.
  • Why: Intermittent or low power can prevent proper module initialization.

Inspect Physical Connections

  • Examine the gas module connector for bent pins, debris, or misalignment.
  • Disconnect and reconnect the gas module firmly.
  • Why: Loose or damaged connectors are the most common cause of communication errors.

Check Cable Integrity

  • Inspect the gas module cable for fraying, cuts, or kinks.
  • Swap with a known good cable if available.
  • Why: Damaged cables can prevent communication even if indicators appear normal.

Restart the Ventilator and Gas Module

  • Power down the ventilator completely.
  • Wait 30–60 seconds before powering back on.
  • Observe the boot sequence for successful gas module recognition.
  • Why: Electronics may lock up and require a full reset to reestablish communication.

Check Environmental Conditions

  • Ensure temperature and humidity are within the device specifications.
  • Avoid proximity to strong electromagnetic interference (other medical devices, power supplies).
  • Why: Extreme conditions or interference can affect gas module signaling.

Review Device Logs or Error Codes

  • Access the ventilator’s error log to identify the specific communication fault.
  • Note if the error is intermittent or persistent.
  • Why: Documentation helps determine if the issue is recurring or situational.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks pass and errors remain:

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send the ventilator and gas module for bench evaluation or manufacturer repair.

Knowing when to stop is part of proper troubleshooting and prevents risk to patients.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not use the ventilator on an active patient until communication errors are resolved.

Move patients to another functioning ventilator while troubleshooting.

Troubleshoot only in a safe environment where patient safety is not compromised.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator displays gas module communication error and will not recognize module.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Observed intermittent connection between main unit and gas module. Cable and connector inspection revealed no external damage. Restart did not resolve error.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation. Patient was safely transferred to another ventilator.

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Gas module and ventilator power indicator status.
  • Cable and connector condition.
  • Any error codes displayed.
  • Environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, EMI exposure).
  • Intermittent vs persistent error behavior.

Final Thought

Patient safety and logical troubleshooting are paramount. Always start with easily verifiable external issues, escalate appropriately when necessary, and document each step clearly. Accurate documentation supports future maintenance and risk reduction.

That is successful troubleshooting.
