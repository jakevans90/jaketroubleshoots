GE Healthcare MAC 5500 HD

Lead Noise or Artifact on Acquisition

Asset Type

Electrocardiograph (EKG) Machine

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

MAC 5500 HD

What This Guide Helps With

Identifies common external causes of lead noise or signal artifact, including electrode placement, lead integrity, patient motion, and environmental interference.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm patient is stable and not undergoing a critical procedure.
  • Expected outcome: Safe environment to troubleshoot without affecting patient care.

Inspect Electrodes

  • Check that all electrodes are properly placed, clean, and have good skin contact. Replace any dried or expired electrodes.
  • Expected outcome: Reduced baseline noise; proper signal capture.

Check Lead Wires and Connections

  • Inspect all lead wires for frays, kinks, or loose connections. Ensure leads are fully seated in both the machine and electrodes.
  • Expected outcome: Elimination of intermittent signal artifacts caused by poor connections.

Minimize Patient Motion

  • Ask the patient to remain still and relaxed during acquisition. Secure limbs to reduce movement.
  • Expected outcome: Stabilized waveform, reduced motion artifacts.

Assess Environmental Interference

  • Ensure the machine is not near high-voltage equipment, mobile devices, or strong electromagnetic fields. Move or shield the EKG machine as needed.
  • Expected outcome: Cleaner trace without 50/60 Hz interference or erratic spikes.

Perform Self-Test or Calibration

  • Run the MAC 5500 HD’s internal self-test or calibration routine to confirm lead acquisition circuits are functioning correctly.
  • Expected outcome: Confirmation that device electronics are operational.

Swap Leads if Necessary

  • If a specific lead consistently shows noise, swap with a known good lead to isolate the problem.
  • Expected outcome: Determine if noise is lead-specific or system-wide.

If the Problem Persists

If lead noise continues after verifying electrodes, leads, patient positioning, and environmental factors:

  • Remove the machine from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for internal repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop avoids causing patient risk or misdiagnosis.

Clinical Use Tip

Always attach the patient to an alternative EKG machine if monitoring is critical. Never troubleshoot on an actively monitored patient without backup.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported intermittent lead noise during EKG acquisition."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Poor electrode contact and frayed lead wire detected."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced electrodes and swapped lead wire; noise eliminated and waveform stabilized."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Electrodes tested and replaced
  • Lead wires inspected/swapped
  • Patient motion minimized
  • Environmental interference assessed
  • Self-test/calibration results
  • Final device status: Operational

Final Thought

Logical, stepwise troubleshooting ensures patient safety and efficient resolution. Documenting actions helps track recurring issues and supports cost-effective maintenance. Escalate appropriately when external causes are ruled out.

That is successful troubleshooting.
