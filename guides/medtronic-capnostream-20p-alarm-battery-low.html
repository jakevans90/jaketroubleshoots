Medtronic Capnostream 20p

Alarm: BATTERY LOW

Asset Type

Capnography Monitor

Manufacturer

Medtronic

Model

Capnostream 20p

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the BATTERY LOW alarm on a Medtronic Capnostream 20p capnography monitor. This alarm indicates the battery charge level is low and the monitor may shut down soon. During patient monitoring, this can interrupt EtCO2, respiration rate, SpO2, pulse rate, and alarm monitoring if the issue is not corrected quickly.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the Capnostream 20p is actively monitoring a patient, do not rely on a monitor that may shut down soon.
  • Connect the monitor to AC power immediately if safe and available.
  • If AC power is not immediately available, or if the alarm cannot be corrected quickly, move the patient to another working monitor.
  • Do not continue patient monitoring on battery power when the device is warning that shutdown may occur soon.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm that the displayed alarm is exactly BATTERY LOW.
  • Ask staff when the alarm appeared, such as during transport, after unplugging the power cord, after a power outage, or while connected to AC power.
  • Check whether the monitor shows low battery indication, dim display behavior, power warnings, or signs that shutdown may occur soon.
  • Confirm whether the alarm clears, repeats, or remains active after AC power is connected.

Connect the Monitor to AC Power

  • Plug the Capnostream 20p into a known working hospital-grade AC outlet.
  • Confirm the power cord is fully seated at the wall outlet and at the monitor power connection.
  • Look for normal AC power or charging indication on the monitor.
  • Allow the monitor to remain connected long enough to begin recovering battery charge.

Check the AC Outlet

  • If the alarm remains after plugging in the monitor, try a different known working outlet.
  • Check whether the original outlet is switched off, loose, damaged, tripped, or not supplying power.
  • Use an approved outlet tester or facility-approved method if that is part of local practice.
  • Do not assume the monitor is charging just because the cord is plugged into the wall.

Inspect the Power Cord

  • Inspect the power cord for cuts, bent prongs, damaged insulation, loose fit, or signs of overheating.
  • Confirm the cord is the correct type for the monitor and facility use.
  • Make sure the cord is not loose at the monitor connection.
  • If available and allowed by facility policy, test with a known good compatible power cord.

Check for Charging or AC Power Indication

  • Verify that the monitor recognizes AC power.
  • Confirm whether the battery level begins increasing after the unit remains connected to AC power.
  • If the monitor is plugged in but does not recognize AC power, suspect an external cord/outlet issue or an internal power/charging issue.
  • Do not return the monitor for transport or battery-dependent use until charging behavior is confirmed.

Allow Time for Battery Recovery

  • If the battery was deeply discharged, leave the monitor connected to AC power and observe whether the battery level improves.
  • Verify that the BATTERY LOW alarm clears after charging begins.
  • Confirm normal startup, monitoring display, and alarm operation before returning the device to service.
  • If the battery does not recover after a reasonable charging period, remove the monitor from service.

Check for Repeated or Premature Battery Low Alarms

  • Ask staff whether the alarm occurs shortly after removing the monitor from AC power.
  • Check whether the alarm appears during normal transport use even after the battery was charged.
  • Look for signs that the battery is weak, aged, or unable to hold charge.
  • If the battery drops quickly after charging, send the monitor for repair or battery evaluation according to facility procedure.

Inspect for Physical or Environmental Issues

  • Check for liquid damage, cracked housing, damaged power inlet, loose connectors, excessive heat, burning smell, or signs of electrical damage.
  • Confirm the monitor is not being stored or used in an environment that may affect battery performance or charging.
  • Remove the monitor from service immediately if there are signs of unsafe electrical or physical damage.

Stop When Resolved

  • If connecting to AC power clears the alarm and the battery charges normally, document the issue as a low battery or unplugged AC power condition.
  • Verify normal monitoring display, alarm behavior, power indication, and charging status.
  • Return the monitor to service only after safe operation is confirmed.

If the Problem Persists

If the BATTERY LOW alarm remains while connected to known good AC power, or if the monitor does not charge properly, external causes such as outlet failure, loose power connection, unplugged AC power, and damaged power cord should be considered ruled out.

A persistent battery low condition may indicate a failed battery, charging problem, damaged power inlet, internal power supply issue, or related device failure.

Remove the monitor from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient if monitoring may be interrupted. Move the patient to a backup monitor first, then troubleshoot the Capnostream 20p after patient monitoring is safely maintained.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Capnostream 20p displayed BATTERY LOW alarm. Staff reported battery charge was low and the monitor may shut down soon."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Monitor was operating on battery power with low remaining charge. AC power was not connected. Power cord and outlet were inspected and no damage was found."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Connected monitor to known working AC outlet, verified secure power cord connection, confirmed AC/charging indication, and observed BATTERY LOW alarm cleared. Device returned to service after normal operation was verified."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A BATTERY LOW alarm should be treated as a patient monitoring reliability issue first and a battery problem second. Start with patient safety, confirm the alarm, check AC power and the cord, then verify whether the battery charges normally. If the alarm persists after basic external checks, remove the monitor from service and escalate appropriately. Clear CCR documentation helps show what was checked, what corrected the issue, or why the device was sent for repair.

That is successful troubleshooting.
