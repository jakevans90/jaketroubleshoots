GE Portrait VSM Vital Signs Monitor

Power-On Self-Test Fails / Device Won’t Boot

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Portrait VSM

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting Portrait VSM monitors that fail their power-on self-test (POST) or do not boot. Common causes are external power issues, battery problems, corrupted software, or peripheral connection errors. The guide focuses on simple, verifiable checks before considering internal repair or software reload.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check AC Power Source

  • Ensure the monitor is connected to a known working outlet.
  • Confirm that the outlet is live using a voltmeter or a test device.
  • Why: A monitor won’t boot if it isn’t receiving stable AC power.

Inspect the Power Cable and Connections

  • Verify that the power cable is properly seated at both the monitor and outlet.
  • Look for frayed wires, loose connectors, or physical damage.
  • Swap with a known good cable if available.

Check Battery Status

  • Remove the battery and inspect for corrosion or swelling.
  • Reinsert or replace with a known good battery.
  • Why: A faulty or missing battery can prevent boot or POST completion.

Perform a Soft Reset (if applicable)

  • Press and hold the power button for 10–15 seconds to reset the monitor.
  • Attempt to boot again.

Disconnect Non-Essential Accessories

  • Remove NIBP cuffs, SpO₂ sensors, or other peripherals.
  • Attempt boot with only essential connections.
  • Why: Some devices fail POST due to peripheral detection errors.

Check for Visual/Indicator Cues

  • Note LED patterns, error messages, or unusual beeps.
  • Document these for CCR and future reference.

If the Problem Persists

External power, battery, and peripheral causes have been ruled out. The device may have corrupted firmware or an internal hardware failure.

  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send to the manufacturer or internal repair bench for evaluation.
  • Recognize that continuing to attempt boot without proper equipment can risk further damage.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot POST failures on a monitor attached to a patient. Ensure patient monitoring is maintained on a backup device before performing any checks.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reports the monitor displays “POST failed” and will not boot.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“AC power confirmed, battery tested, peripherals removed, device still fails to boot. Likely internal firmware or hardware fault.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent to repair/bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet verified as live
  • Power cable swapped/tested
  • Battery condition noted
  • Peripheral devices disconnected
  • POST error codes or LED patterns observed
  • Final status: Device non-operational, sent for repair

Final Thought

Patient safety and logical troubleshooting are paramount. Systematically checking power, battery, and connections prevents unnecessary internal intervention. Proper documentation ensures accurate maintenance records and supports appropriate escalation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
