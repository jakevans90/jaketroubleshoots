Roche cobas h 232

Battery or Power Failure

Asset Type

Blood Gas Analyzer

Manufacturer

Roche

Model

cobas h 232

What This Guide Helps With

Analyzer not powering on, shutting down unexpectedly, or failing to charge due to battery, charger, or external power issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot while the analyzer is needed for active patient testing. Move testing to a backup device.
  • Expected: No interruption to patient care
  • Why it matters: Loss of analyzer availability delays critical cardiac marker results

2. Verify the Reported Power Issue

  • Attempt to power on the analyzer using the normal power button.
  • Observe:
  • No power at all
  • Device powers on briefly then shuts off
  • Low battery or charging indicator issues
  • Expected: Issue is reproducible
  • Why it matters: Confirms a true power failure vs user error or intermittent issue

3. Check Battery Charge Level (Most Common Cause)

  • Low or depleted batteries are the most frequent cause.
  • Action:
  • Place the analyzer on the charging dock
  • Observe for charging indicator (LED or screen icon)
  • Allow several minutes and attempt power-on again
  • Expected: Device powers on after charging
  • Why it matters: Deeply discharged batteries may require time before responding

If resolved, return device to service.

4. Verify Charger/Docking Station Power

  • Ensure the charging system is receiving power.
  • Action:
  • Confirm outlet is working (test with another device)
  • Check power adapter connections to dock
  • Look for power indicator lights on the dock
  • Expected: Dock shows power and supports charging
  • Why it matters: No charger power = no battery recovery

5. Reseat Analyzer in Dock Properly

  • Poor contact can prevent charging.
  • Action:
  • Remove analyzer and reseat firmly in docking station
  • Ensure proper alignment with charging contacts
  • Check for debris on contacts
  • Expected: Charging indicator activates
  • Why it matters: Misalignment or contamination interrupts charging

6. Inspect Charging Contacts

  • Dirty or damaged contacts can block power transfer.
  • Action:
  • Inspect analyzer and dock contacts for dirt, corrosion, or damage
  • Clean gently with approved method if needed
  • Expected: Clean contacts restore charging function
  • Why it matters: Reliable electrical contact is required for charging

7. Test with Known-Good Charger or Dock

  • Rule out external charging equipment failure.
  • Action:
  • Place analyzer on a known-good dock/charger
  • Attempt to charge and power on
  • Expected: Device charges and powers normally
  • Why it matters: Isolates whether issue is analyzer vs charger

If resolved, remove faulty dock/charger from service.

8. Observe for Abnormal Behavior

  • Check for signs of deeper issues.
  • Look for:
  • Excessive heat during charging
  • No charging indication at all
  • Device shutting down even while docked
  • Expected: Normal charging behavior without overheating
  • Why it matters: Abnormal signs may indicate internal battery or power circuit failure

If the Problem Persists

If all external power sources, chargers, and connections have been verified, the issue is likely internal (battery failure or internal power circuitry).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and wasted troubleshooting time.

Clinical Use Tip

Always ensure a backup analyzer is available before removing a unit from service. Do not attempt to troubleshoot power issues during active patient testing.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Analyzer would not power on and showed no charging indication."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Failed internal battery—unit would not charge on multiple known-good docks."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed from service and sent for repair; verified charger functionality."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Charging dock indicator status
  • Battery/charging behavior observed
  • Contacts inspected/cleaned
  • Known-good dock tested
  • Any heat, smell, or abnormal shutdown behavior
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Power-related issues should always be approached from the outside in. Verify power sources, charging systems, and connections before suspecting internal failure. Prioritizing patient safety and clear escalation ensures reliable equipment availability and efficient troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
