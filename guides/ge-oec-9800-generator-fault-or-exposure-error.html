GE Healthcare OEC 9800

Generator Fault or Exposure Error

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

OEC 9800

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a GE Healthcare OEC 9800 C-Arm that displays generator faults or exposure errors during X-ray operation. Symptoms may include inability to generate X-ray, error messages on the display, aborted exposures, or repeated system warnings. Most issues are caused by external or easily verifiable problems before internal generator faults are considered.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Verify System Power

  • Check that the C-Arm is plugged into a verified, live outlet.
  • Confirm all main power switches are on.
  • Ensure no tripped breakers or blown fuses.

Why: Generator errors can be caused by unstable or missing input power.

2. Check Footswitch and Exposure Controls

  • Inspect the footswitch cable for damage or loose connections.
  • Test the footswitch response with the system powered on.
  • Try swapping with a known working footswitch if available.

Why: A malfunctioning footswitch can mimic a generator fault.

3. Inspect X-Ray Tube and Collimator Connections

  • Verify that the X-ray tube cable is fully seated and free of visible damage.
  • Check collimator interlocks and ensure they are not engaged incorrectly.

Why: Loose or damaged connections can prevent exposure and trigger generator errors.

4. Review System Status and Error Codes

  • Note any error codes or messages displayed on the operator console.
  • Cross-reference codes with the OEC 9800 service documentation.

Why: Identifying the specific code helps target the fault without unnecessary disassembly.

5. Check for Overheating or Duty Cycle Limits

  • Confirm that the system has not exceeded recommended X-ray exposure limits.
  • Allow the unit to cool if it has been in continuous use.

Why: Generator errors may occur if the tube or generator overheats.

6. Verify Generator Enable Interlocks

  • Check emergency stop buttons, system interlocks, and patient table positions.
  • Ensure all interlocks are released.

Why: Active interlocks prevent exposure and may be misinterpreted as generator faults.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out. The generator or internal control module may be faulty.

Action:

  • Remove the C-Arm from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for internal repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to escalate protects both patient safety and equipment.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt exposures on a patient if the generator is showing errors. Move the patient to a verified functioning C-Arm. Troubleshoot only when patient safety is ensured.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“C-Arm displays generator fault when attempting exposure; X-ray will not fire.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Footswitch tested and operational; power verified; interlocks disengaged; error persists, likely internal generator or control module issue.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for vendor repair evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet voltage and verification method
  • Footswitch continuity or substitution results
  • Error codes displayed
  • X-ray tube connections inspected
  • Interlock status
  • Device cooling period status

Final Thought

Systematic, stepwise troubleshooting ensures that external and user-accessible factors are resolved first. Escalating only after logical checks preserves both patient safety and equipment integrity. Documentation using the CCR method provides clear communication for repair and future reference.

That is successful troubleshooting.
