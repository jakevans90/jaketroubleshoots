Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: INSP O2 LOW !!!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

INSP O2 LOW !!! alarm indicating low inspiratory oxygen concentration, risking hypoxia due to supply issues, incorrect settings, leaks, or gas delivery problems.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the anesthesia machine from active patient use if oxygen delivery is unreliable
  • Transition to an alternate oxygen/ventilation source immediately if needed

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the INSP O2 LOW !!! alarm is present
  • Check measured FiO2 on the display
  • Observe when the alarm occurs (startup vs during ventilation)
  • Determine if oxygen concentration is consistently low or fluctuating

3. Check Oxygen Supply Source

  • Confirm wall oxygen pipeline is connected and active
  • Verify pressure is within normal range (~50 psi)
  • If using a cylinder, ensure it is open and adequately filled

4. Inspect Gas Hoses and Connections

  • Ensure O2 hose is securely connected
  • Inspect for kinks, disconnections, or damage
  • Confirm correct gas connection (not cross-connected)

5. Verify Flow Control Settings

  • Ensure O2 flow control valve is opened appropriately
  • Check that other gases are not set too high relative to O2
  • Adjust flows to increase FiO2

6. Check Lower O2 Alarm Limit Setting

  • Confirm the low O2 alarm limit is set appropriately
  • Compare measured FiO2 to alarm threshold

7. Inspect Breathing Circuit for Leaks or Disconnection

  • Check all circuit connections from machine to patient
  • Look for loose fittings, leaks, or disconnections
  • Verify proper assembly of circuit components

8. Evaluate Oxygen Sensor/Measurement Function

  • Observe if FiO2 responds to flow adjustments
  • Check for delayed, fixed, or inaccurate readings

9. Stop When Resolved

  • Confirm FiO2 returns to expected levels
  • Ensure alarm clears
  • Return device to service

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and the alarm continues:

  • Internal failure is likely (O2 sensor, gas mixing system, internal leak)
  • Remove the device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot oxygen delivery issues on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first before investigating.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"INSP O2 LOW !!! alarm during case, FiO2 reading below expected level."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Low O2 flow setting and loose hose connection identified causing reduced oxygen delivery."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Adjusted O2 flow, secured connection, verified normal FiO2 and alarm cleared."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Low oxygen alarms must always be taken seriously. Start with supply and settings, confirm delivery, and escalate appropriately to protect patient safety.

That is successful troubleshooting.
