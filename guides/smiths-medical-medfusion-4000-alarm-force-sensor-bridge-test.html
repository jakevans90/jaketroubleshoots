Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000

Alarm: Force Sensor Bridge Test

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Medfusion 4000

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot a Force Sensor Bridge Test alarm on the Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000 infusion pump. This alarm indicates a problem with the force sensing system used to support occlusion detection and safe syringe delivery. If the force sensor bridge test cannot be trusted, the pump should not be used for patient infusion therapy until the issue is evaluated and corrected.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the Medfusion 4000 from patient use immediately if the alarm is active or recurring.
  • Use another verified infusion pump for patient therapy.
  • Confirm that patient infusion therapy is continued on a known-good device.
  • The force sensor is part of the occlusion detection system. If it cannot be trusted, the pump should not be used for patient delivery.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Power the pump on in a controlled Biomed setting and confirm the exact alarm shown.
  • Verify that the alarm is Force Sensor Bridge Test.
  • Note whether the alarm occurs at startup, during self-test, during syringe loading, during infusion setup, or intermittently during operation.
  • Observe any related symptoms, such as occlusion alarms, failure to detect syringe or plunger force correctly, unexpected system failure, inability to start infusion, or force readings that appear stuck, drifting, or unrealistic.
  • Document the confirmed alarm and behavior before assuming a part failure.

Remove Obvious External Causes First

  • Inspect the syringe loading area, plunger drive area, and force sensing contact area for visible contamination, fluid residue, cracked plastic, or physical obstruction.
  • Clean external surfaces according to facility-approved cleaning procedures.
  • Do not force the plunger mechanism or attempt to bypass the alarm.
  • Confirm the plunger area is clean, dry, and free of obstruction.
  • External contamination or mechanical interference can affect plunger force transfer and may cause abnormal force sensor behavior.

Check for Physical Damage

  • Inspect the pump for signs of drop damage or stress near the syringe barrel clamp, plunger assembly, and case.
  • Look for cracked housing, loose plunger assembly, misaligned syringe loading components, sticky or rough plunger movement, evidence of fluid intrusion, damaged connectors, or exposed wiring.
  • If physical damage is found, remove the pump from service and send it for repair evaluation.

Access Biomed Diagnostics

  • Enter the Biomed service area and navigate to Biomed > Diagnostics > Monitor Analog Sensors.
  • Observe the force sensor reading.
  • Check whether the force sensor value appears stable at rest, responsive to controlled plunger force, reasonable compared with expected behavior, and free from excessive drift, noise, or sudden jumps.
  • The Force Sensor Bridge Test alarm points toward the force sensing circuit, not simply a syringe loading complaint.

Evaluate Force Sensor Behavior

  • With the pump removed from patient use, gently observe force sensor response during appropriate diagnostic testing.
  • Do not use excessive force.
  • Check for no response from the force sensor, a reading stuck high or low, unstable or noisy readings, readings that change without mechanical input, or readings that fail bridge or test circuit checks.
  • Force sensor output should change predictably and remain stable when no force is applied.
  • If the sensor reading is abnormal, continue troubleshooting toward sensor calibration or repair.

Recalibrate the Force Sensor if Appropriate

  • If your facility allows Clinical Engineering calibration and the pump is otherwise physically intact, perform the force sensor calibration using approved service procedures.
  • After calibration, repeat the diagnostic check and restart the pump to verify whether the alarm clears.
  • Confirm the force sensor calibrates successfully and the Force Sensor Bridge Test alarm does not return.
  • If calibration resolves the issue, perform required post-service verification before returning the pump to use.

Confirm Alarm Resolution

  • After calibration or corrective action, power cycle the pump and confirm that the alarm does not return.
  • Verify that startup completes normally, no system failure alarm appears, force sensor readings remain stable, occlusion detection-related checks pass according to your facility process, and the pump passes required operational testing.
  • If this resolves the issue, return the device to service only after all required testing is complete.

If the Problem Persists

If the Force Sensor Bridge Test alarm remains after external checks, diagnostic review, and appropriate calibration, external causes have been ruled out and the issue likely involves the force sensing circuit or related internal signal path.

Possible internal causes include plunger force sensor failure, force sensor cable issue, Plunger PCB failure, Main PCB signal path issue, force sensor bridge excitation or test circuit failure, or amplification, filtering, or zero-offset circuit problems.

At this point, remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Do not continue using the pump if the force sensing system cannot be verified.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm on an active patient. A force sensor bridge or test failure can compromise occlusion detection, so the pump should be replaced with a verified working pump before further evaluation.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Medfusion 4000 infusion pump reported Force Sensor Bridge Test alarm during startup. Pump could not be cleared for patient use due to force sensing system failure affecting occlusion detection reliability."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Force sensor diagnostic check showed abnormal force sensor behavior consistent with force sensing circuit failure. External syringe area and plunger path inspected with no user-correctable obstruction found."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed pump from service. Checked force sensor behavior in Biomed > Diagnostics > Monitor Analog Sensors. Force sensor alarm persisted after evaluation. Labeled device Out of Service and sent for repair or bench evaluation of plunger force sensor, force sensor cable, Plunger PCB, and Main PCB path."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The Force Sensor Bridge Test alarm should be treated as a patient safety concern because it affects the force sensing system used for occlusion detection. Start with basic visual checks, verify the alarm in diagnostics, recalibrate only when appropriate, and escalate when the force sensing path cannot be trusted. Clear CCR documentation helps show why the pump was removed from service and what repair path was identified.

That is successful troubleshooting.
