Nonin Onyx II 9590

Probe or Clip Damage

Asset Type

Pulse Oximeter

Manufacturer

Nonin

Model

Onyx II 9590

What This Guide Helps With

Physical damage to the integrated clip or sensor causing poor fit, inaccurate readings, or inability to obtain SpO₂ values.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from use if readings are unreliable or inconsistent.
  • Use an alternate pulse oximeter for patient monitoring.

Expected: Patient is safely monitored with a functioning device

Why it matters: Damaged probes can produce inaccurate SpO₂ readings, impacting clinical decisions

Perform Visual Inspection of Clip and Sensor Area

  • Inspect for cracks or broken plastic
  • Check for loose hinge or weak spring tension
  • Look for misaligned sensor pads

Expected: Clip is intact, aligned, and provides firm pressure

Why it matters: Proper alignment and pressure are required for accurate light transmission and detection

Check Sensor Window Condition

  • Inspect for dirt, debris, or residue
  • Look for scratches or clouding
  • Clean gently with approved wipes if contaminated

Expected: Sensor windows are clean and unobstructed

Why it matters: Any obstruction can interfere with light absorption and signal accuracy

Verify Clip Tension and Finger Fit

  • Place the device on a test finger
  • Ensure the clip closes fully
  • Confirm consistent pressure across the finger

Expected: Device grips finger securely without slipping

Why it matters: Poor contact leads to signal loss or erratic readings

Test Device Functionality

  • Power on the unit and apply to a known good test subject
  • Observe SpO₂ and pulse readings
  • Check for stability and consistency

Expected: Stable waveform and accurate readings

Why it matters: Confirms whether damage is affecting performance

Check for Housing Separation or Structural Damage

  • Inspect for separation at seams
  • Check for internal movement when handling
  • Look for signs of impact damage

Expected: Device housing is fully intact

Why it matters: Structural damage may affect internal sensor alignment

Compare with Known Good Device (If Available)

  • Test the same patient with another pulse oximeter

Expected: Comparable readings between devices

Why it matters: Helps confirm whether the issue is device-specific

If the Problem Persists

If physical damage, weak clip tension, or sensor misalignment is confirmed and cannot be corrected through cleaning or basic checks, the issue is likely external causes ruled out and internal or structural.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or replacement evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not attempt to use a damaged clip on an active patient. Always transfer monitoring to a reliable device before troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Pulse oximeter not staying on finger and giving inconsistent readings."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Clip hinge weakened and sensor alignment compromised due to physical wear."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed device from service and sent for repair evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Condition of clip (cracked, loose, broken hinge)
  • Sensor window condition (clean vs damaged)
  • Reading stability during testing
  • Comparison with known good device
  • Any visible impact damage
  • Final device status (removed from service or returned to use)

Final Thought

Physical integrity is critical for pulse oximetry accuracy. Always rule out external damage first, and do not attempt to compensate for a failing clip. Proper escalation and documentation protect both patient safety and equipment reliability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
