Mindray BeneVision N Series

Alarm: Battery temperature too high

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

BeneVision N Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Battery temperature too high alarm on a Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor. This alarm indicates that the battery temperature is above the allowable operating range. The issue may be caused by high ambient temperature, heat exposure, blocked cooling, battery failure, or a device-side charging or temperature sensing issue.

Because battery temperature problems can affect charging, battery operation, and safe transport use, this alarm should be treated as a real safety condition until the cause is verified.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the patient is being monitored safely.
  • If the monitor is in active use, notify clinical staff before troubleshooting.
  • If monitoring is unstable, transfer the patient to another verified monitor.
  • Do not continue relying on a monitor with an unresolved battery temperature alarm during patient transport.
  • Do not troubleshoot the battery or charging system while the monitor is the only active monitoring device for the patient.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact alarm displayed is Battery temperature too high.
  • Check whether the alarm appears at startup, while operating on battery, while connected to AC power, during charging, after transport, or after storage in a hot area.
  • Look for related symptoms such as the battery not charging, short battery runtime, abnormal battery icon, abnormal charging status, repeated alarm after restart, or the monitor feeling unusually warm.
  • Record whether the alarm is constant or intermittent.

Remove the Monitor From Heat Exposure

  • Check whether the monitor or battery was exposed to direct sunlight, a heater, a warming device, a hot storage area, or nearby equipment producing heat.
  • Move the monitor to a normal room-temperature clinical environment.
  • Allow the monitor and battery to cool before retesting.
  • If the alarm clears after cooling and does not return, perform a functional check before returning the monitor to service.

Check the Ambient Temperature

  • Verify the room, transport area, or storage location is not excessively warm.
  • Ask staff where the monitor was being used when the alarm occurred.
  • If the alarm occurred in a hot location, retest the monitor in a normal clinical environment.
  • Confirm the alarm does not return once the monitor is used in an appropriate environment.

Check External Cooling and Physical Condition

  • Inspect the monitor for blocked vents or restricted airflow.
  • Remove covers, bags, blankets, or accessories that may trap heat or block ventilation.
  • Inspect the monitor and battery area for damage, swelling, contamination, fluid intrusion, burning smell, or unusual heat.
  • Do not continue using or charging a battery that appears swollen, damaged, leaking, or physically abnormal.

Restart the Monitor After Cooling

  • Power the monitor down using the normal shutdown process.
  • Allow the monitor to sit in a normal room-temperature area.
  • Reconnect the monitor to known-good AC power.
  • Restart the monitor and observe whether the alarm returns.
  • If the alarm clears, verify charging status and basic monitor operation before returning the device to service.

Check Battery Charging Behavior

  • Connect the monitor to known-good AC power.
  • Verify the AC power indicator and charging status.
  • Allow the battery to charge under observation.
  • Check whether the Battery temperature too high alarm returns during charging.
  • If the alarm returns during charging in a normal environment, suspect a faulty battery or charging-related issue.

Isolate the Battery if Possible

  • If the monitor uses a removable battery, remove the suspect battery only if safe and allowed by hospital procedure.
  • Inspect the battery contacts and battery compartment for damage, contamination, or signs of overheating.
  • Install a known-good compatible battery.
  • Restart the monitor and observe alarm behavior.
  • If the alarm clears with a known-good battery, remove the suspect battery from service and replace it.

Evaluate for Device-Level Failure

  • Confirm that external causes have been ruled out.
  • Verify the monitor is in a normal room-temperature environment.
  • Confirm there is no heat exposure or blocked ventilation.
  • Confirm the battery has been replaced or tested with a known-good battery if possible.
  • If the alarm still returns, suspect an internal charging, sensing, power-management, or device-level thermal issue.

If the Problem Persists

If Battery temperature too high continues after the monitor is cooled, used in a normal environment, checked for blocked airflow, and tested with a known-good battery, remove the monitor from service.

At that point, simple external causes have been ruled out. The condition may involve a faulty battery, battery temperature sensing issue, charging circuit issue, internal power-management failure, or device-level thermal problem.

  • Remove the monitor from patient use.
  • Label the device Out of Service.
  • Do not continue charging or using a battery that appears damaged, swollen, leaking, or unusually hot.
  • Send the monitor for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm on an active patient if the monitor is being relied on for critical monitoring or transport. Move the patient to a backup monitor first, then evaluate the battery, charging behavior, environment, and device condition.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor displaying Battery temperature too high alarm during battery use/charging. Staff concerned battery may not be safe for continued monitoring or transport."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery temperature alarm confirmed. Monitor was checked for heat exposure, high ambient temperature, blocked airflow, physical battery damage, and abnormal charging behavior. Alarm cleared after cooling / followed suspect battery / persisted with known-good battery depending on findings."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Monitor removed from patient use during evaluation. Unit was allowed to cool in normal operating environment, AC power and charging status were checked, ventilation and battery condition were inspected, and battery was replaced or tested with a known-good battery as needed. If alarm persisted, monitor was labeled Out of Service and sent for bench evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery temperature alarms should not be bypassed or treated as nuisance alarms. Start with patient safety, then check the environment, heat exposure, airflow, battery condition, and charging behavior. If the alarm persists after simple external causes are ruled out, remove the monitor from service and escalate appropriately.

That is successful troubleshooting.
