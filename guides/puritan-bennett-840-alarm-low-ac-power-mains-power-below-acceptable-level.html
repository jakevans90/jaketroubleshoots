Puritan Bennett 840

Alarm: LOW AC POWER mains power below acceptable level

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Puritan Bennett

Model

840

What This Guide Helps With

LOW AC POWER alarm indicating insufficient mains voltage causing potential battery use, unstable operation, or risk of ventilator shutdown.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the ventilator is alarming or power is unstable, immediately transfer the patient to a backup ventilator if needed
  • Confirm continuous ventilation is maintained

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the LOW AC POWER alarm is active on the display
  • Observe if the power indicator is flickering or off
  • Check if the unit is switching to battery operation

3. Check AC Power Source (Most Common Cause)

  • Plug a known-good device into the same outlet
  • Ensure the outlet is hospital-grade and active
  • Try a different verified outlet

4. Inspect Power Cord and Connections

  • Ensure the power cord is fully seated at both ends
  • Inspect for cuts, fraying, or damage
  • Reseat all connections securely

5. Check Power Distribution Path

  • Remove any extension cords or power strips
  • Plug the ventilator directly into a wall outlet
  • Ensure circuits are not overloaded

6. Verify Voltage Configuration (If Applicable)

  • Confirm correct voltage setting in service mode per facility standard
  • Check for recent service changes that may have altered configuration

7. Observe for Resolution

  • Confirm the alarm clears after corrections
  • Verify AC power indicator remains stable
  • Ensure the unit stays on AC power without reverting to battery

If the Problem Persists

If LOW AC POWER alarm continues after verifying outlet, cables, and configuration:

  • External power issues have been ruled out
  • Persistent alarm indicates internal power supply or sensing failure
  • Remove ventilator from service
  • Label device Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot power-related alarms on an actively ventilated patient—always secure alternate ventilation before addressing the device.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming LOW AC POWER; unit intermittently switching to battery during use"

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified stable wall power and connections; alarm persisted indicating likely internal power supply issue"

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service and sent for repair evaluation"

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Power alarms must be taken seriously. Always rule out external causes first, but recognize persistent voltage alarms as indicators of internal failure and act quickly to protect patient safety.

That is successful troubleshooting.
