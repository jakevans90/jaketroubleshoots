Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000

Alarm: Check Syringe Flange Sensor

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Medfusion 4000

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot a Check Syringe Flange Sensor alarm on the Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000 infusion pump. This alarm may occur when the syringe is not loaded correctly, the syringe flange holder is not positioned properly, the barrel clamp is not fully engaged, or the flange sensor detection does not match the expected syringe loading state.

A syringe flange sensing issue can prevent the pump from accepting the syringe, starting an infusion, or confirming that the syringe is safely loaded for delivery.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the pump is connected to a patient and infusion delivery is interrupted or cannot be trusted, remove the pump from patient use.
  • Transfer the infusion to another verified pump if clinically required.
  • Do not continue using the pump if syringe loading, flange position, or sensor confirmation cannot be trusted.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the display shows Check Syringe Flange Sensor.
  • Note whether the alarm occurs during syringe loading, at startup, when attempting to begin infusion, intermittently during use, or after the barrel clamp or syringe was moved.
  • Identify associated symptoms such as inability to start infusion, syringe not recognized, repeated loading prompts, or alarm recurrence after reloading.

Remove and Reload the Syringe

  • Remove the syringe and reload it carefully.
  • Confirm the syringe barrel is seated correctly in the barrel clamp.
  • Confirm the syringe flange is properly positioned in the flange holder.
  • If the alarm clears after proper reloading, verify normal operation before returning the pump to service.

Check the Syringe Flange Holder

  • Inspect the flange holder for obstruction, damage, stiffness, contamination, or anything preventing full travel.
  • Confirm the flange holder pulls back toward the side of the case and returns freely.
  • Look for sticky residue, dried medication, cracked plastic, or debris around the holder path.

Confirm Barrel Clamp Engagement

  • Check that the barrel clamp fully captures the syringe barrel.
  • Make sure the barrel clamp is not lifted, partially seated, obstructed, or held away from the syringe.
  • Confirm the syringe size and position appear correct for the selected syringe type.

Check Plunger Holder Engagement

  • Confirm the plunger holders are properly engaged around the syringe plunger.
  • Make sure the plunger driver is not misaligned, forced, or partially engaged.
  • Correct any obvious syringe loading issue before retesting the alarm.

Inspect for Physical Damage or Contamination

  • Look for cracked plastic, bent holders, sticky residue, dried medication, broken clips, or debris near the barrel clamp, flange holder, and plunger holder area.
  • Clean external surfaces according to facility procedure if contamination is present.
  • Do not open the pump or perform internal disassembly during front-line troubleshooting.

Try a Known-Good Compatible Syringe

  • Load a known-good syringe of the correct type and size.
  • Confirm the selected syringe model and size match the syringe being used.
  • If the alarm clears with a known-good syringe, suspect syringe compatibility, syringe damage, or loading technique.

Check the Flange Sensor in Biomed Diagnostics

  • Access Biomed > Diagnostics > Monitor Digital Sensors if available to trained service personnel.
  • Observe the flange sensor state while moving the flange holder through its normal range.
  • The flange sensor state should change appropriately as the flange holder is moved.

Compare Sensor Behavior to Mechanical Movement

  • While watching the digital sensor state, slowly move the flange holder and confirm the sensor response matches the physical movement.
  • If the sensor does not change, changes intermittently, or shows the wrong state, suspect a flange holder, sensor, wiring, or related electronics issue.
  • If the mechanical movement is sticky or incomplete, correct the external obstruction or remove the pump from service for evaluation.

Determine Whether the Issue Is External or Device-Related

  • If the alarm clears after reloading the syringe, correcting holder position, or removing obstruction, the likely cause was syringe misloading or mechanical interference.
  • If the alarm remains with a known-good syringe and the flange holder moves normally, the issue is likely device-related.
  • Do not return the pump to clinical use if flange sensor response is inconsistent or cannot be verified.

If the Problem Persists

If the Check Syringe Flange Sensor alarm remains after verifying syringe loading, barrel clamp position, flange holder movement, plunger holder engagement, syringe compatibility, and digital sensor response, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent alarm may indicate a flange sensor, flange holder mechanism, wiring, or related internal electronics issue.

Remove the pump from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot syringe loading alarms on an active patient while infusion delivery is needed. Move the patient infusion to a backup verified pump first before continuing checks that require unloading, reloading, or manipulating the syringe.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"User reported Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000 displaying Check Syringe Flange Sensor alarm. Pump would not accept syringe loading and infusion could not be started."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Syringe flange holder was not fully returning to the side of the case due to external residue near the holder path. Flange sensor remained active during syringe loading."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed pump from service for evaluation. Cleaned external flange holder area per facility procedure, confirmed barrel clamp, flange holder, and plunger holders moved freely. Verified flange sensor changed state correctly in Biomed > Diagnostics > Monitor Digital Sensors. Reloaded known-good syringe and alarm cleared. Pump passed functional check and was returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The Check Syringe Flange Sensor alarm should be approached as a syringe loading and sensor confirmation problem first. Start with patient safety, verify the alarm, check the syringe and external holders, then use available diagnostics to confirm flange sensor response. If the alarm remains after proper loading and sensor checks, remove the pump from service and escalate for repair. Clear CCR documentation helps show whether the problem was corrected externally or requires further bench evaluation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
