Nonin Onyx II 9590

SpO2 Reading Inaccuracy

Asset Type

Pulse Oximeter

Manufacturer

Nonin

Model

Onyx II 9590

What This Guide Helps With

Inaccurate or inconsistent SpO2 readings caused by poor placement, low perfusion, motion artifact, or environmental interference.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If readings are being used for clinical decisions, verify with another pulse oximeter or monitoring device.
  • Expected: Patient oxygenation confirmed by a reliable device
  • Why it matters: Inaccurate SpO2 can lead to improper treatment decisions

Verify Proper Finger Placement

  • Ensure the patient’s finger is fully inserted into the device with the nail facing upward toward the sensor
  • Reposition finger fully into the clip
  • Ensure proper alignment of emitter and detector
  • Expected: Stable and consistent reading
  • Why it matters: Misalignment is one of the most common causes of inaccurate readings

Check for Motion Artifact

  • Observe if the patient is moving their hand or finger during measurement
  • Ask patient to remain still
  • Support the hand if needed
  • Expected: Reading stabilizes when motion stops
  • Why it matters: Movement disrupts the light signal used for SpO2 calculation

Assess Perfusion (Circulation Issues)

  • Check for signs of poor circulation such as cold fingers, low blood pressure, or vasoconstriction
  • Warm the patient’s hand
  • Try a different finger
  • Expected: Improved waveform and stable reading
  • Why it matters: Low perfusion reduces signal quality and accuracy

Inspect for External Interference

  • Check for anything blocking or altering light transmission
  • Remove nail polish or artificial nails
  • Check for dirt, dried blood, or debris in the sensor area
  • Expected: Reading improves after obstruction is removed
  • Why it matters: Optical interference affects sensor accuracy

Evaluate Ambient Light Conditions

  • Strong external light sources can interfere with readings
  • Shield the device from bright overhead lights or sunlight
  • Expected: More stable and accurate readings
  • Why it matters: Excess light can disrupt the sensor’s photodetection

Check Battery Status

  • Low battery voltage can affect device performance
  • Replace batteries with known good ones
  • Expected: Device operates normally with stable readings
  • Why it matters: Inadequate power can cause inconsistent sensor output

Compare with Known Good Device

  • Cross-check readings with another calibrated pulse oximeter
  • Expected: Readings should be within acceptable variance
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether the issue is device-specific

If the Problem Persists

If inaccurate readings continue after verifying placement, perfusion, environment, and power, external causes have been ruled out and the issue is likely internal (sensor degradation or internal circuitry fault).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not rely on a questionable SpO2 reading for patient care decisions. Always confirm with another device before troubleshooting. Move the patient to a backup device first to maintain therapy continuity.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Pulse oximeter giving inconsistent and inaccurate SpO2 readings compared to other devices."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Poor signal quality due to internal sensor degradation after external factors were ruled out."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Device removed from service and sent for further evaluation and repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Finger placement verified
  • Motion observed or ruled out
  • Perfusion status (cold extremities, etc.)
  • Nail polish or obstruction present
  • Ambient lighting conditions
  • Battery replaced/tested
  • Comparison with known good device
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Accurate SpO2 readings depend heavily on proper use and patient conditions. Always rule out simple external causes before suspecting device failure. Protect patient safety by verifying readings and documenting clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
