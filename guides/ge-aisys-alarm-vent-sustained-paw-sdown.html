GE Healthcare Aisys

Alarm: VENT SUSTAINED PAW SDOWN

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Aisys

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot VENT SUSTAINED PAW SDOWN on a GE Healthcare Aisys anesthesia machine. This alarm can occur when high airway pressure causes a ventilator failure condition and the machine enters monitoring-only operation.

This condition may be associated with Vent Fail. Monitoring Only. and airway pressure remaining above the expected safe range. Because ventilation may be affected, this alarm should be treated as a serious patient safety issue.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not rely on the anesthesia machine for automatic ventilation if ventilation is not reliable.
  • Have anesthesia staff manually ventilate the patient or move the patient to another functional anesthesia machine or ventilator as clinically appropriate.
  • Confirm adequate oxygenation, ventilation, and patient monitoring before troubleshooting the machine.
  • Do not troubleshoot the machine while it is actively supporting a patient unless clinical staff determine it is safe.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact alarm reported is VENT SUSTAINED PAW SDOWN.
  • Check whether the display also showed Vent Fail. Monitoring Only.
  • Ask staff when the alarm occurred, such as during startup, mechanical ventilation, manual ventilation, or after a circuit change.
  • Determine whether airway pressure was reported above 100 cmH2O for an extended period.

3. Check for Obvious External High-Pressure Causes

  • Inspect the breathing circuit for kinked, crushed, or occluded hoses.
  • Check the patient circuit, airway adapter, filters, and connectors for blockage or restriction.
  • Look for a blocked or saturated filter that could restrict flow.
  • Verify that the expiratory path is not closed, blocked, or restricted.
  • If the event occurred during manual ventilation, check the APL valve and manual bag setup.

4. Check the Breathing System Setup

  • Verify the inspiratory and expiratory limbs are connected correctly.
  • Check that the absorber canister is seated properly.
  • Confirm the flow sensors are seated correctly.
  • Inspect the bag arm, breathing bag, and scavenging connection.
  • Review any recently changed circuit components, filters, hoses, or patient adapters.

5. Review Ventilation Settings and Alarm Context

  • Review the ventilation mode active when the alarm occurred.
  • Check pressure settings, PEEP, inspiratory pressure, tidal volume, flow settings, and patient size configuration.
  • Look for settings that could create excessive airway pressure, especially with a restricted circuit or low-compliance condition.
  • Correct any obvious setting or setup issue before repeating checkout.

6. Power Cycle the System After Patient Is Safe

  • Only reboot the anesthesia machine after it has been removed from patient use.
  • Power cycle the system and allow it to complete startup checks.
  • Observe whether the alarm returns during startup, self-test, or checkout.
  • Do not return the machine to service based only on a reboot.

7. Perform a Functional Check

  • Run the appropriate anesthesia machine checkout according to facility procedure.
  • Use a test lung or approved test setup.
  • Confirm the ventilator cycles normally.
  • Verify airway pressure readings appear reasonable and return to baseline.
  • Confirm no repeated Vent Fail or Monitoring Only message appears.

8. Inspect Airway Pressure Reading Behavior

  • Observe the Paw value during testing.
  • Look for a pressure reading that is stuck high, unstable, jumping unexpectedly, or inconsistent with test lung behavior.
  • Check whether the pressure reading remains elevated with no real obstruction present.
  • If available and approved by facility procedure, check the airway pressure signal in Service Mode.

9. Isolate External Setup Issue vs Device Fault

  • If the alarm does not return after correcting an external issue, document the setup issue found.
  • Likely external causes include kinked hoses, blocked filters, incorrect circuit setup, scavenging restriction, patient-side obstruction, or inappropriate ventilation settings.
  • Likely device-related causes include repeated alarm with a known-good circuit, abnormal Paw reading, failed checkout, or recurring ventilator failure message.
  • Escalate the unit for bench evaluation if the failure repeats after external causes are ruled out.

10. Return to Service Only If Fully Resolved

  • Return the anesthesia machine to service only if the alarm does not return.
  • Confirm checkout passes.
  • Confirm test lung ventilation passes.
  • Confirm Paw readings behave normally.
  • Confirm no unresolved Vent Fail or Monitoring Only condition remains.

If the Problem Persists

If VENT SUSTAINED PAW SDOWN continues after reboot, known-good circuit setup, external restriction checks, and functional testing, remove the anesthesia machine from service.

At that point, external causes such as kinked hoses, blocked filters, incorrect breathing circuit setup, scavenging restriction, and improper ventilation settings have been ruled out.

Label the unit Out of Service and send it for repair or bench evaluation. A repeating high Paw shutdown alarm may indicate an internal pressure sensing, ventilator control, or pressure measurement issue that should not be cleared casually.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm on an active patient. If the machine displays Vent Fail. Monitoring Only., move the patient to a backup device or alternate safe ventilation method before beginning Clinical Engineering evaluation.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"GE Healthcare Aisys anesthesia machine reported VENT SUSTAINED PAW SDOWN with Vent Fail. Monitoring Only. during use. Staff reported high airway pressure alarm and ventilator shutdown."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found breathing circuit restriction from kinked hose causing excessive airway pressure. No internal device fault found during checkout."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed kinked circuit, installed known-good circuit, rebooted system, completed anesthesia machine checkout and test lung ventilation. Paw readings normal. Alarm did not return. Returned unit to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

VENT SUSTAINED PAW SDOWN should be treated as a serious ventilator failure condition, not just a nuisance alarm. Start with patient safety, confirm the exact alarm, rule out external pressure causes, reboot only after the patient is safe, and escalate if the Paw signal or ventilator behavior remains abnormal.

Good troubleshooting shows whether the event was caused by setup, obstruction, or a likely internal failure.

That is successful troubleshooting.
