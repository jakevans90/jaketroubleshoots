Medtronic Valleylab FT10

Footswitch Communication Failure

Asset Type

Electrosurgical Unit (ESU)

Manufacturer

Medtronic

Model

Valleylab FT10

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Valleylab FT10 ESU when the footswitch is not recognized, intermittent, or fails to trigger energy delivery. The focus is on verifying external connections, footswitch condition, and system settings before assuming internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Footswitch Connection

  • Ensure the footswitch cable is fully seated in the ESU footswitch port.
  • Loose or partially connected cables are a common cause of communication errors.

Inspect Footswitch and Cable

  • Check the footswitch for visible damage (cracks, frayed wires, bent pins).
  • Flex the cable gently while observing if the ESU registers any input.
  • A damaged footswitch or cable often causes intermittent or failed communication.

Swap Footswitch (if available)

  • If a spare, compatible footswitch is available, connect it to the ESU.
  • If the replacement works, the original footswitch is likely faulty.

Check ESU Settings

  • Navigate to the ESU’s accessory or footswitch menu.
  • Confirm that the ESU is configured to the correct footswitch type (single pedal vs dual pedal).
  • Misconfigured settings may prevent proper recognition.

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn off the ESU, wait 10–15 seconds, then power it back on.
  • Some communication faults resolve after a reset.

Observe Footswitch Response

  • Press the pedal(s) and check the ESU display for acknowledgment.
  • Listen for any relays or clicks if applicable.
  • Document any erratic or non-responsive behavior.

If the Problem Persists

External causes have been ruled out.

The ESU should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Attempting internal repairs is not recommended and can void warranty or compromise safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not use the ESU on a patient until footswitch function is confirmed.
  • Temporarily move the patient to another fully operational ESU if available.
  • Footswitch communication failures can result in unexpected energy delivery or inability to deliver energy, which is a patient safety risk.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reports that the ESU footswitch is unresponsive and energy cannot be triggered.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Footswitch cable fully seated, inspected for damage, swapped with spare — still unresponsive; settings verified.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Unit labeled Out of Service and sent to vendor/repair bench for evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Footswitch type and serial number
  • Cable inspection results
  • Replacement footswitch tested (Y/N)
  • ESU menu configuration for footswitch
  • Unit power cycle observations

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting relies on systematically checking connections, accessories, and settings before assuming internal failure. Documenting each step ensures accountability, safety, and clear communication with repair services. Patient safety is paramount — never bypass a nonfunctional footswitch.

That is successful troubleshooting.
