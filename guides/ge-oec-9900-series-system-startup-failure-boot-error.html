GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series

System Startup Failure / Boot Error

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

OEC 9900 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series C-Arm that fails to boot or initialize. Symptoms may include:

  • No display on the control panel
  • No fan or indicator activity
  • Error messages on the monitor during startup
  • Inability to reach system menus

The focus is on external checks and safe procedures, escalating to repair only when necessary.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Do not attempt imaging or troubleshooting on a patient with this device. Move the patient to another operational C-Arm before addressing the failed unit.

Verify Power Source

  • Confirm the C-Arm is plugged into a known working outlet.
  • Check if a wall switch or outlet breaker is off.

Reason: Without proper power, the system cannot boot.

Check Power Cables and Connections

  • Inspect all power cords for damage, frays, or loose connections.
  • Ensure the main power cord is fully seated in both the C-Arm and the wall outlet.

Reason: Loose or damaged cables are common causes of startup failure.

Check Fuses and Circuit Breakers on the C-Arm

  • Locate the main fuse or breaker panel on the OEC 9900.
  • Replace blown fuses or reset tripped breakers.

Reason: Protects the system from electrical faults; blown fuses prevent boot.

Observe Indicator Lights and Sounds

  • Power on the unit and note any LED lights or audible beeps.
  • Compare to normal startup indicators (green lights, fan start).

Reason: Lights and sounds provide clues to power or system board issues.

Attempt a Soft Reboot

  • Disconnect the C-Arm from power for 1–2 minutes, then reconnect.
  • Power on and observe any change in behavior.

Reason: Resets internal electronics that may be stuck in a fault state.

Check Environmental Factors

  • Ensure the device is not overheated; verify room temperature is within operating range.
  • Check for excessive dust, moisture, or obstruction near vents.

Reason: Overheating or blocked vents can prevent proper boot.

If the Problem Persists

External checks have been exhausted; the startup error may be internal.

  • Remove the C-Arm from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Escalate to GE Healthcare service or qualified repair bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is essential to avoid further damage or risk to patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Troubleshoot only when the system is powered down and safe to handle.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“C-Arm will not power on or display any startup screens.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Power verified; cables intact; soft reboot attempted; unit remains unresponsive. Indicators show no activity.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, escalated to vendor service for internal repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and confirmed working
  • Power cables inspected and intact
  • Fuse/breaker status checked
  • Indicator lights absent or abnormal
  • Fan did not spin or system failed to initialize
  • Unit status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount. Logical, stepwise troubleshooting helps identify external issues before risking internal components. Proper documentation and timely escalation prevent downtime and protect staff and patients.

That is successful troubleshooting.
