Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: VENTILATOR FAIL !!!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

VENTILATOR FAIL alarm indicating ventilator mounting, sealing, or hose connection issue causing inability to deliver or control mechanical ventilation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately remove the machine from active patient use if ventilation is compromised.
  • Transition to a backup anesthesia machine or manual ventilation.
  • Expected: Patient ventilation is safely maintained by alternate means.
  • Why it matters: Ventilator failure can result in complete loss of mechanical ventilation.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the VENTILATOR FAIL !!! alarm is present.
  • Observe:
    • Does the alarm occur at startup or when switching ventilation modes?
    • Is the ventilator not initiating breaths or failing to maintain pressure?
    • Any additional pressure or flow-related alarms?
  • Expected: Alarm is reproducible with loss or instability of ventilator function.
  • Why it matters: Confirms a true system-detected ventilator issue.

3. Check Ventilator Mounting and Seating

  • Ensure the ventilator module is fully seated and properly mounted.
  • Check that the cover is completely closed and latched.
  • Expected: Ventilator is securely mounted with no gaps or misalignment.
  • Why it matters: Improper mounting prevents proper sealing and operation.

4. Inspect Ventilator Membrane and Seal

  • Open and inspect for misalignment, debris, or damage.
  • Reseat the membrane if applicable.
  • Close and secure the cover fully.
  • Expected: Membrane is clean, intact, and properly seated.
  • Why it matters: A poor seal can prevent pressure generation and trigger failure alarms.

5. Check PEEP/PMAX Line Connection

  • Ensure the hose is connected securely at both ends.
  • Check for kinks, disconnections, or leaks.
  • Reseat connections if needed.
  • Expected: Line is fully connected, unobstructed, and leak-free.
  • Why it matters: This line is critical for pressure sensing and control.

6. Inspect Breathing System for Leaks or Disconnections

  • Ensure all hoses are connected properly.
  • Look for loose fittings, cracks, or disconnections.
  • Confirm no major leaks are present.
  • Expected: Circuit is intact and properly assembled.
  • Why it matters: Leaks can mimic ventilator failure conditions.

7. Reset Ventilation Mode

  • Place the machine in Standby mode.
  • Then return to the previous ventilation mode.
  • Expected: Alarm clears and normal ventilation resumes.
  • Why it matters: Resets system checks and clears transient faults.

8. Stop When Resolved

  • If the alarm clears and normal ventilation is restored:
  • Return the device to service.
  • Verify stable operation under test conditions.

If the Problem Persists

If all external connections, mounting, and seals have been verified, persistent VENTILATOR FAIL !!! alarm indicates likely internal ventilator module failure.

Remove device from service, label Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilator-related alarms on an active patient. Always secure ventilation first before attempting any corrective actions.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"VENTILATOR FAIL !!! alarm present. Ventilator not initiating or maintaining mechanical ventilation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Loose ventilator mounting / disconnected PEEP line or internal ventilator module failure indicated after external checks."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated ventilator module, secured membrane and cover, reconnected PEEP/PMAX line. If unresolved, removed from service and sent for repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • When alarm occurs (startup vs during ventilation)
  • Ventilator mounting condition
  • Membrane condition and seating
  • PEEP/PMAX hose status
  • Circuit leak check performed
  • Reset attempt (Standby → ventilation mode)
  • Final operational status

Final Thought

Ventilator failure alarms should always be taken seriously. Focus on sealing, connections, and mounting first, and escalate quickly if unresolved to protect patient safety.

That is successful troubleshooting.
