Medtronic Capnostream 20p

Error: CO2 ERROR

Asset Type

Capnography Monitor

Manufacturer

Medtronic

Model

Capnostream 20p

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot CO2 ERROR on the Medtronic Capnostream 20p when the monitor cannot operate the CO2 monitoring function. This condition may prevent reliable EtCO2 readings, CO2 waveform display, respiration rate calculation, and CO2-related alarm monitoring.

The reported condition is: Failure has occurred which prohibits the operation of the CO2 function.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the monitor is connected to a patient and CO2 monitoring is needed, transfer the patient to another working monitor or use an alternate approved monitoring method.
  • Do not rely on a monitor with an active CO2 ERROR for ventilation monitoring.
  • Notify clinical staff immediately if the error occurs during active monitoring.

Expected outcome: Patient ventilation monitoring is maintained without depending on a device with a CO2 function failure.

Why it matters: A CO2 error can prevent reliable capnography monitoring and may delay recognition of respiratory compromise.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact displayed message is CO2 ERROR.
  • Note whether the error appears at startup, after connecting the sampling line, during active monitoring, or intermittently during use.
  • Check whether the CO2 waveform, EtCO2 value, respiration rate, or CO2-related alarms are missing or unreliable.

Expected outcome: The CO2 error is confirmed and the loss of CO2 function is clearly observed.

Why it matters: This separates a true system-reported CO2 failure from a patient condition, setup issue, or accessory problem.

Check the Patient and Clinical Condition First

  • If the monitor is alarming during patient use, verify the patient’s respiratory status before assuming the monitor is wrong.
  • Confirm whether the patient is breathing, ventilated, or connected properly to the breathing circuit or cannula.
  • Escalate immediately to clinical staff if there is any concern about the patient’s respiratory condition.

Expected outcome: Patient-related causes are addressed before equipment troubleshooting continues.

Why it matters: CO2 monitoring issues can occur during true clinical events, and patient safety must come first.

Inspect the CO2 Sampling Line or FilterLine

  • Verify the sampling line is compatible with the monitor and patient setup.
  • Confirm the line is fully seated in the monitor.
  • Check for kinks, crushing, blockage, wet tubing, contamination, or expired accessories.
  • Verify the line is properly connected to the patient, airway adapter, cannula, or breathing circuit.
  • Replace the sampling line with a known-good compatible line if available.

Expected outcome: CO2 operation restores if the issue was caused by a defective, blocked, or incompatible sampling line.

Why it matters: CO2 monitoring depends on correct sampling line flow and connection.

Check for Moisture, Secretions, or Contamination

  • Inspect the sampling line connection area and patient connection for moisture, secretions, or visible contamination.
  • Replace contaminated accessories according to facility policy.
  • Do not blow into the device or force fluid through the sampling path.

Expected outcome: External contamination or accessory blockage is identified and corrected.

Why it matters: Moisture or blockage can interfere with CO2 measurement and may trigger CO2-related faults.

Power Cycle the Monitor

  • Remove the device from patient use before restarting it.
  • Turn the monitor off, wait briefly, then restart it.
  • Allow the unit to complete startup and observe whether CO2 ERROR returns.

Expected outcome: Temporary software or startup-related faults clear after restart.

Why it matters: A restart may clear a transient fault, but a recurring CO2 ERROR suggests a deeper CO2 subsystem failure.

Check Power Source and Battery Status

  • Connect the monitor to a known-good AC outlet.
  • Verify the power cord, power supply, and battery indicators appear normal.
  • Confirm the monitor is not operating with a low or unstable battery condition.

Expected outcome: The monitor has stable power and the CO2 error is not related to low power or startup instability.

Why it matters: Unstable power can cause unreliable operation or startup faults.

Check for Physical Damage

  • Inspect the monitor housing for cracks or impact damage.
  • Check the CO2 sampling port for damage, contamination, or looseness.
  • Look for signs of fluid intrusion, unusual odor, heat, noise, or evidence that the unit was dropped.

Expected outcome: Obvious external damage is identified before further troubleshooting.

Why it matters: Physical damage near the CO2 sampling area can cause failure of the CO2 measurement function.

Attempt Operation with Known-Good Accessories

  • Use a known-good compatible sampling line.
  • If available, test the monitor in a controlled setup away from the patient.
  • Confirm whether the CO2 waveform or values return.

Expected outcome: The issue is isolated as accessory-related or device-related.

Why it matters: If the error remains with known-good accessories, the monitor is more likely reporting an internal CO2 function failure.

Stop if the Error Clears

  • If CO2 ERROR clears after replacing the sampling line, correcting the connection, stabilizing power, or restarting the device, perform a basic functional check.
  • Confirm the CO2 waveform displays appropriately when tested.
  • Confirm EtCO2 values appear when tested appropriately.
  • Verify related alarms behave normally and CO2 ERROR does not return during observation.

Expected outcome: The device operates normally and can be returned to service if it passes functional checks.

Why it matters: A cleared error still requires verification before the monitor is used again for patient monitoring.

If the Problem Persists

If CO2 ERROR remains after checking the sampling line, patient connection, power source, physical condition, restart behavior, and known-good accessories, remove the monitor from service.

At that point, external and common causes have been ruled out. The remaining issue is likely internal to the CO2 measurement system.

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send it for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Escalate to Medtronic / Oridion authorized service personnel as required.

Do not continue using the monitor for patient CO2 monitoring while this error is unresolved.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot an unresolved CO2 error on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup monitor or alternate approved monitoring method first, especially if the patient requires continuous ventilation monitoring, procedural sedation monitoring, or respiratory compromise observation.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Capnostream 20p displaying CO2 ERROR. CO2 function unavailable and no reliable EtCO2 waveform or CO2 value available."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified CO2 ERROR during startup/use. Checked sampling line connection, known-good compatible sampling line, power source, and visible external condition. Error persisted, indicating likely internal CO2 function failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed monitor from service and labeled Out of Service. Device requires repair or bench evaluation by authorized service personnel before return to patient use."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A CO2 ERROR on the Capnostream 20p should be treated seriously because it affects the monitor’s ability to provide capnography data. Clinical Engineering should verify the error, rule out simple external causes, avoid unnecessary internal disassembly, and escalate appropriately when the CO2 function remains unavailable. Clear documentation helps show that patient safety was protected and troubleshooting was performed logically.

That is successful troubleshooting.
