GE Healthcare CARESCAPE R850 Ventilator

Exhalation Valve Failure

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE R860

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the CARESCAPE R860 ventilator experiences exhalation valve failure. Symptoms may include persistent high airway pressure alarms, difficulty triggering breaths, abnormal exhaled volumes, or ventilator alarms indicating valve malfunction. Most issues originate from external factors like improper setup, blockage in tubing, or loose connections before assuming internal valve failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Switch the patient to a backup ventilator or manual ventilation before troubleshooting.
  • Confirm oxygen delivery and patient stability.

Inspect Ventilator Circuit Connections

  • Check all tubing, connectors, and Y-piece connections for tight fit.
  • Loose or misaligned tubing can mimic exhalation valve errors.

Check for Obstructions

  • Examine the expiratory limb, filters, and valve for condensation, secretions, or kinking.
  • Remove and clean any visible blockages; ensure filters are properly seated.

Power Cycle the Ventilator

  • Turn the ventilator off, wait 30–60 seconds, and power back on.
  • Observe if the alarm persists; this can reset sensor and valve electronics.

Review Ventilator Settings

  • Ensure settings like PEEP, tidal volume, and expiratory sensitivity are appropriate.
  • Misconfigured settings may trigger exhalation valve alarms.

Run Built-In Self-Test (BIST) or Calibration

  • Initiate the ventilator’s automated self-test if available.
  • Confirm the test completes without error; record any failures.

Swap External Components if Possible

  • Replace the expiratory valve assembly with a verified functional spare if available.
  • Retest to determine if the issue is the valve module itself.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks pass and alarms continue:

  • The exhalation valve or internal sensor assembly is likely faulty.
  • Remove ventilator from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for manufacturer repair or bench evaluation.
  • Knowing when to stop prevents patient risk and improper internal handling.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot the exhalation valve with the patient connected; high pressures can cause harm.
  • Always ensure backup ventilation is available.
  • Document observations and actions to prevent repeated alarms and ensure patient safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator alarm “Exhalation Valve Failure” with high airway pressures.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Expiratory limb was partially obstructed by condensation; tubing connections verified.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Cleared obstruction, reseated tubing, and ran self-test. Ventilator passed all tests and returned to service.”

Alternate Example if Escalated:
“Device labeled Out of Service and sent for manufacturer repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Ventilator self-test results.
  • Filter status and type.
  • Any condensation or secretions noted.
  • Alarm history and waveform patterns.
  • Final device status: functional, OOS, or sent for repair.

Final Thought

Patient safety and logical troubleshooting are paramount. Start with simple external checks before escalating. Proper CCR documentation ensures traceability and helps prevent future errors. Knowing when to remove a device from service is part of competent Clinical Engineering practice.

That is successful troubleshooting.
