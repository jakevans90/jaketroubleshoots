Midmark QuickClean QC Series

Tank, Drain, Valve, or Plumbing Leakage

Asset Type

Ultrasonic Cleaner

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

QuickClean QC Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting visible fluid leakage from the tank, drain hose, drain valve, plumbing connections, or a damaged/pitted ultrasonic bath.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Safety First

Remove the ultrasonic cleaner from clinical use as soon as leakage is reported or observed.

Turn the unit off, unplug it from AC power, and prevent staff from running additional cleaning cycles until the leak source is identified. Do not handle spilled cleaning solution without appropriate PPE, and clean any fluid from surrounding counters or floors according to facility procedure.

Expected outcome: The unit is electrically isolated, and staff are protected from fluid exposure, slip hazards, and possible electrical risk.

Why it matters: A leaking ultrasonic cleaner may allow solution to reach electrical components or create unsafe environmental contamination.

2. Confirm the Reported Leak Condition

Ask staff when the leak occurs:

  • While the tank is filled but idle
  • Only during an ultrasonic cleaning cycle
  • While draining the tank
  • After the drain valve is closed
  • Continuously from beneath or behind the unit

Dry the exterior surfaces and surrounding counter area. With the unit unplugged, visually inspect for evidence of prior leakage, including dried residue, staining, moisture tracks, or pooled solution.

Expected outcome: The leak pattern is identified before additional testing.

If no evidence of leakage is found: Document the reported condition and proceed with a controlled fill observation before returning the device to use.

3. Inspect the Drain Hose and Discharge Routing

Check the externally accessible drain hose for:

  • Cracks, splits, pinholes, stiffness, or discoloration
  • Loose connection at the drain outlet
  • Kinking, pulling, or tension on the hose
  • Improper routing that allows fluid to run back toward the unit
  • Drain hose end positioned where overflow or splashback may be mistaken for a unit leak

The QuickClean QC Series uses an external drain hose and shutoff valve arrangement, with the hose size and configuration varying by model and installation type.

Expected outcome: The drain hose is intact, securely routed, and not the source of leakage.

If the hose is visibly damaged or leaking: Keep the unit out of service and replace the affected drain component through appropriate repair channels. Stop troubleshooting.

4. Check the Drain Valve Position and External Valve Area

Confirm the drain valve is fully closed before filling the tank. Inspect the accessible valve area and surrounding fitting for moisture, residue, looseness, or active dripping.

With the unit still unplugged, add a small controlled amount of water sufficient to observe the drain outlet and valve area without exceeding normal operating limits. Watch for leakage with the valve closed.

Expected outcome: No dripping occurs from the drain valve or external drain connection while the valve is closed.

If fluid drips from the valve, outlet, or associated fitting: The valve or plumbing connection is not sealing correctly. Remove the unit from service and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

5. Check for Overfill, Spillover, or Misidentified Leakage

Inspect the tank fill level and ask whether the leak was observed after the bath was recently filled, moved, or loaded with instruments.

Confirm that:

  • The fluid level was not above the indicated maximum fill level.
  • Instruments or baskets were not displacing solution over the rim.
  • Fluid did not spill down the exterior during filling or draining.
  • The unit was not operated with visible solution on the case.

Expected outcome: Overflow or handling-related fluid is ruled out as the apparent leak source.

If the reported “leak” was caused by overfill or spillover: Clean and dry the unit, verify normal operation with a controlled fill, educate staff as appropriate, document the finding, and stop.

6. Inspect the Tank Interior for Visible Damage

After the tank is drained, cool, and dry, inspect the bath interior under good lighting. Look for:

  • Pitting or roughened areas in the bottom of the tank
  • Pinholes, cracks, or corrosion-like damage
  • Wear marks where the basket may have contacted the tank
  • Damaged or missing protective coating on the basket feet

Midmark states that contaminants contacting the vibrating bath can act as abrasives and, in extreme cases, cause the bath to develop holes and leak. Midmark also identifies severe cavitation erosion and excessive pitting in the tank base as causes of fluid leakage requiring replacement of the ultrasonic cleaner.

Expected outcome: The tank interior is smooth and intact, with no visible evidence of erosion or perforation.

If tank damage, pitting, or a suspected pinhole is present: Remove the unit from service. Do not attempt to seal or continue using the bath. Stop troubleshooting.

7. Perform a Controlled Static Fill Observation

Only when no obvious damaged hose, faulty valve, or tank defect has been identified, place the unplugged unit on a clean, dry surface with absorbent material positioned beneath and behind the cleaner.

Add water to the normal operating level. Leave the unit unplugged and observe the unit for active moisture from:

  • The tank underside or case seam
  • The drain valve area
  • The drain hose connection
  • Any accessible external plumbing location

Expected outcome: The unit remains dry while filled and stationary.

If leakage occurs while the unit is unplugged and idle: The issue is mechanical, involving the tank, drain valve, hose, or plumbing connection. Remove the unit from service and stop.

8. Determine Whether the Leak Is Cycle-Related

Do not operate the unit if any leak has already been confirmed.

If the unit remains completely dry during the unplugged static fill observation, and the original report occurred only during operation, the problem may be an intermittent leak, vibration-related plumbing issue, or fluid displacement during loading.

Run an operational test only when permitted by department procedure, with the device isolated from clinical use and monitored continuously. Stop the cycle immediately if any moisture appears.

Expected outcome: No solution escapes during operation or draining.

If fluid appears during vibration or draining: Remove the unit from service and send it for bench evaluation. Midmark’s troubleshooting information identifies plumbing joints and severe tank cavitation erosion as primary causes of fluid leakage.

9. Verify Final Condition Before Return to Service

Return the device to service only when:

  • No active leak is found during fill, cycle, and drain observation.
  • The tank is visually intact.
  • The drain valve closes and drains normally.
  • The external drain hose is secure and undamaged.
  • The unit and surrounding area remain dry.
  • The reported cause has been identified and corrected, such as overfill or a loose external drain-hose setup.

Expected outcome: The cleaner operates without leakage and can be safely returned to clinical processing use.

If the leak source cannot be confidently identified: Do not return the unit to service.

If the Problem Persists

If leakage continues after external hose routing, valve closure, fill level, spillover, and visible tank condition have been evaluated, common external causes have been ruled out. The remaining concern is likely a failed drain valve, plumbing connection, fitting, or leaking tank assembly.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Do not continue operating an ultrasonic cleaner with unresolved fluid leakage. Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting, especially when fluid may reach electrical components or contaminated solution may escape into the work area.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a leaking QuickClean while it is actively being used for instrument processing. Have staff use another approved cleaning process or functioning ultrasonic cleaner first. Troubleshoot only after the tank is safe to handle, the unit is unplugged, and spilled solution has been appropriately contained and cleaned.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported that the Midmark QuickClean QC Series ultrasonic cleaner was leaking cleaning solution onto the counter during tank draining."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Inspection found active dripping from the drain valve area with the valve closed, while the tank and external drain hose showed no visible damage."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the ultrasonic cleaner from service, labeled it Out of Service, drained and cleaned the unit, and sent it for bench evaluation of the drain valve and associated plumbing components."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Whether leakage occurred while filled, operating, or draining
  • Drain valve position when leakage was observed
  • Drain hose condition and routing
  • Evidence of overfill or spillover
  • Tank interior condition, including pitting or visible damage
  • Basket foot condition
  • Location of pooled fluid or residue
  • Whether leakage occurred during static fill observation
  • Whether leakage occurred only during a cleaning cycle
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A leaking ultrasonic cleaner should be approached logically: protect staff first, isolate electrical power, identify whether fluid is escaping from the hose, valve, plumbing, or tank, and escalate when the source is not safely correctable externally. Clear CCR documentation supports patient safety, infection prevention, and efficient repair follow-up.

That is successful troubleshooting.
