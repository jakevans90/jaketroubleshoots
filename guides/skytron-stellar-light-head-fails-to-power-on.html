Skytron Stellar Surgical Light

Light Head Fails to Power On (Electronics/Controller Faults)

Asset Type

Surgical Light

Manufacturer

Skytron

Model

Stellar

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Skytron Stellar surgical light head fails to power on due to potential electronics or controller faults. These issues may manifest as no illumination, unresponsive controls, or error indicators. The focus is on external, easily verifiable checks before assuming internal electronics failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Source

  • Ensure the light is properly connected to the electrical supply.
  • Check the circuit breaker or fuse supplying the surgical light.
  • Confirm outlet voltage with a multimeter.

Why it matters: No power or low voltage can mimic electronic failure.

Inspect External Cables and Connectors

  • Check power cables for visible damage or loose connections.
  • Inspect any modular connectors between the control module and light head.
  • Re-seat connectors to ensure proper contact.

Why it matters: Loose or damaged connectors are a common, easily fixed cause of power loss.

Check Wall or Remote Control Panel Functionality

  • Confirm the wall/remote control panel powers on and responds correctly.
  • Observe for error codes or unresponsive buttons.

Why it matters: Controller faults can prevent the light head from receiving activation commands.

Cycle Power

  • Power down the light system completely for 1–2 minutes.
  • Power it back on and observe any status indicators.

Why it matters: Electronics may require a reset to clear temporary faults.

Inspect Indicator LEDs

  • Look for any fault or warning LEDs on the control module.
  • Note blinking sequences or colors and compare with manufacturer documentation if available.

Why it matters: Indicator LEDs can help identify whether the fault is in the controller or the light head.

Swap or Test Controller Module (if available)

  • If the facility has a spare controller module compatible with Stellar, swap to determine if the controller is defective.

Why it matters: Confirms whether the issue is in the light head or the controller electronics.

If the Problem Persists

After completing external and easily verifiable checks, if the light head still fails to power on, the issue is likely an internal electronics fault.

  • Remove the light from service.
  • Label as “Out of Service.”
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation by qualified personnel.

Knowing when to stop is a critical part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt repairs while the device is in use on a patient.
  • Move surgical procedures to another functioning light if possible before troubleshooting.
  • Ensure patient safety by confirming adequate illumination from alternate sources.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Light head fails to power on; control panel unresponsive.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Power verified; cables and connectors secure; wall/remote panel functional; controller module may be faulty.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Light removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for bench evaluation.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested: Yes / No
  • Cable inspection: No damage, fully seated
  • Control panel indicators: Any error codes noted
  • Reset attempted: Yes / No
  • Spare controller tested: Yes / No
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Systematic, logic-based troubleshooting ensures patient safety and minimizes unnecessary repairs. Proper documentation and knowing when to escalate prevent unsafe conditions and preserve equipment integrity.

That is successful troubleshooting.
