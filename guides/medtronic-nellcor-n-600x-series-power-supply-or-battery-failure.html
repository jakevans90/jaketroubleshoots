Medtronic Nellcor N-600x Series

Power Supply or Battery Failure

Asset Type

Pulse Oximeter

Manufacturer

Medtronic

Model

Nellcor N-600x Series

What This Guide Helps With

Device not powering on, intermittent shutdowns, or failure to run on battery due to power supply, cord, or battery-related issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from active patient use if it cannot maintain power reliably
  • Transfer monitoring to a known working unit

Expected: Patient is safely monitored on alternate equipment

Why it matters: Loss of power results in complete loss of monitoring

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to power on the device using both AC power and battery
  • Observe behavior:
    • No power at all
    • Powers on only when plugged in
    • Shuts down when unplugged
    • Flickering or intermittent power

Expected: Clear identification of how the failure presents

Why it matters: Helps isolate AC vs battery-related issue

Check AC Power Source

  • Plug the device into a known working outlet
  • Test outlet with another device
  • Try a different outlet if needed

Expected: Device receives consistent AC power

Why it matters: Rules out facility power issues before device troubleshooting

Inspect Power Cord and Adapter

  • Ensure cord is fully seated in device and outlet
  • Inspect for fraying, bent prongs, or damage
  • Swap with a known good cord if available

Expected: Device powers on consistently with a good cord

Why it matters: Damaged cords are a common and easily overlooked cause

Check Power Indicator Lights

  • Confirm AC power light illuminates when plugged in
  • Look for charging indicator when battery is installed

Expected: Indicators reflect proper power input and charging status

Why it matters: Helps determine if internal power supply is functioning

Test Battery Operation

  • Disconnect AC power and attempt to run on battery
  • Ensure battery is properly installed
  • Observe runtime and stability

Expected: Device operates normally on battery

Why it matters: Confirms battery health and connection

Inspect Battery Condition

  • Look for swelling, leakage, or corrosion
  • Check battery age if known
  • Replace with a known good battery if available

Expected: Device functions properly with a good battery

Why it matters: Batteries degrade over time and are a common failure point

Check Battery Contacts and Compartment

  • Ensure contacts are clean and not bent
  • Remove any debris or corrosion

Expected: Solid connection between battery and device

Why it matters: Poor contact can prevent charging or operation

Perform a Power Reset

  • Remove AC power
  • Remove battery
  • Wait 30–60 seconds
  • Reconnect AC power and restart

Expected: Device powers on normally if issue was transient

Why it matters: Clears minor internal power control faults

If the Problem Persists

If the device still does not power on or fails to maintain power after these checks, common external causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely internal (power supply board, charging circuit, or internal wiring).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and wasted time.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot power issues while the device is actively monitoring a patient. Always transfer the patient to a functioning monitor before testing power reliability.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Device will not power on and shuts off when unplugged from AC."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Failed internal battery not holding charge and degraded power cord connection."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced battery and power cord; verified proper operation on AC and battery power."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and confirmed working
  • Power cord swapped
  • Battery replaced or tested
  • Indicator lights status
  • Any intermittent shutdown behavior
  • Physical condition of battery compartment
  • Final device status (operational or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Power issues should always be approached from the outside in—starting with outlets, cords, and batteries before assuming internal failure. This protects patient safety, saves time, and ensures accurate troubleshooting. Proper escalation and clear documentation are just as important as fixing the problem.

That is successful troubleshooting.
