Medivators DSD-201 / DSD Edge Series

Door Not Sealing or Latch Failure

Asset Type

Endoscope Reprocessor (AER)

Manufacturer

Medivators

Model

DSD-201 / DSD Edge Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting door seal or latch failures preventing cycle start, causing leaks, alarms, or inability to maintain proper pressure during reprocessing.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not attempt troubleshooting during an active reprocessing cycle with contaminated scopes inside
  • Remove scopes if safe to do so
  • Follow infection control protocol

Expected: No exposure risk to staff or equipment

Why it matters: Prevents contamination and ensures safe handling of high-level disinfectants

Verify the Reported Issue and Behavior

  • Attempt to close and latch the door while observing system response
  • Determine if the door fails to latch
  • Check if the system alarms or refuses to start
  • Look for visible gaps or resistance when closing

Expected: Clear identification of latch failure vs sealing issue

Why it matters: Helps distinguish mechanical vs alignment or sensor-related problems

Inspect Door for Obstructions (Most Common Cause)

  • Remove any debris, tubing, or improperly routed scope lines
  • Ensure trays and scopes are properly seated and not interfering with the door

Expected: Door closes fully without resistance

Why it matters: Even small obstructions can prevent latch engagement or seal compression

Check Door Gasket / Seal Condition

  • Look for cracks, flattening, or buildup of residue
  • Wipe gasket clean with approved materials

Expected: Gasket is intact, clean, and evenly seated

Why it matters: A damaged or dirty seal prevents proper compression and leak-free operation

Verify Proper Door Alignment

  • Close the door slowly and observe alignment
  • Look for uneven gaps or misalignment
  • Gently apply pressure to see if latch engages

Expected: Door aligns evenly and latches without force

Why it matters: Misalignment can prevent latch engagement or proper sealing

Check Latch Mechanism Operation

  • Listen for latch engagement (click or motorized action if applicable)
  • Check for loose or damaged latch components
  • Ensure handle (if present) moves freely

Expected: Smooth latch engagement with no binding

Why it matters: Mechanical wear or failure can prevent proper locking

Inspect Door Sensor / Interlock Feedback

  • Check for door closed indicator on the display
  • Open and close the door multiple times
  • Look for intermittent detection

Expected: System consistently recognizes door closed state

Why it matters: A failed interlock sensor can prevent cycle initiation even if the door is physically closed

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Power off the unit
  • Wait 30 seconds
  • Power back on and retry door closure

Expected: Normal latch detection restored

Why it matters: Clears minor control or sensor faults

If the Problem Persists

If the door still does not seal or latch properly after these checks, common external causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely internal (latch mechanism failure, hinge misalignment, or interlock system fault).

Remove the unit from service
Label as Out of Service
Send for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop prevents further damage and ensures safe, compliant operation.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not attempt to force the door closed or bypass latch mechanisms. Always move scopes to another AER before troubleshooting to maintain proper reprocessing workflow and infection control.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"AER door would not latch; cycle would not start and door open alarm present."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Door gasket contaminated with residue and slight obstruction from improperly routed scope tubing preventing full closure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleared obstruction, cleaned gasket, verified proper door closure and successful cycle initiation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Door fully closing or not
  • Presence of obstruction or misloaded scopes
  • Gasket condition (cracked, dirty, worn)
  • Latch engagement sound or absence
  • Door closed indicator status
  • Any alarms displayed
  • Final operational status after testing

Final Thought

Door sealing and latch issues are often simple mechanical or loading-related problems, but they directly impact disinfection effectiveness and safety. Always start with external checks, avoid forcing components, and escalate appropriately when alignment or latch integrity is compromised. Clear documentation supports infection control compliance and repeat issue tracking.

That is successful troubleshooting.
