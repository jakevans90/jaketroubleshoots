Midmark 411 / 417 Series

Backrest Not Adjusting or Stuck

Asset Type

Exam Table

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

411 / 417 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Backrest not moving, stuck in position, or only partially adjusting due to control, obstruction, or power-related issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the table from active patient use before troubleshooting.
  • Expected: Patient safely relocated.
  • Why it matters: Unexpected movement or inability to reposition can cause patient injury.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to adjust the backrest using all available controls (hand control, footswitch if equipped).
  • Expected: No movement, partial movement, or abnormal response.
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether the issue is consistent and isolated to the backrest.

Check Power to the Table

  • Confirm the table is plugged into a known working outlet. Test outlet if needed.
  • Expected: Table has stable power (other functions may operate normally).
  • Why it matters: Insufficient power can prevent actuator movement.
  • If no power → restore power and retest
  • If other functions work → proceed to next step

Inspect Hand Control / Footswitch

  • Check for stuck, unresponsive, or damaged buttons—especially the backrest control.
  • Expected: Buttons depress normally and return without sticking.
  • Why it matters: A failed or stuck control can prevent signal to the actuator.
  • If control is faulty → replace and retest

Check for Mechanical Obstruction

  • Visually inspect the backrest hinge and surrounding areas for:
  • Paper roll interference
  • Pinched upholstery
  • Foreign objects or debris
  • Expected: Backrest moves freely with no visible obstruction.
  • Why it matters: Physical blockage is a common and easily missed cause.
  • If obstruction found → remove and retest

Inspect Cable Connections (External/Accessible Only)

  • Check visible cables between the control and table base (if accessible without disassembly).
  • Expected: Connections are secure with no visible damage.
  • Why it matters: Loose or damaged cables can interrupt control signals.
  • If loose → reseat and retest
  • If damaged → remove from service

Listen for Actuator Response

  • Activate the backrest control and listen for motor noise.
  • Expected: Audible motor/actuator sound when engaged.
  • Why it matters: Helps determine if the actuator is receiving power.
  • No sound → likely control, wiring, or power issue
  • Sound but no movement → possible mechanical bind or actuator failure

Check for Load or Positioning Issues

  • Ensure no excessive weight or uneven pressure is applied to the backrest.
  • Expected: Backrest moves under normal load conditions.
  • Why it matters: Overloading can prevent actuator movement.
  • If overloaded → remove load and retest

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal (actuator failure, internal wiring, or control board fault).

Remove the table from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and is part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot backrest movement with a patient on the table. Always transfer the patient to a safe surface first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Backrest not adjusting on Midmark 411 exam table."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Backrest actuator unresponsive after confirming power and control function."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed table from service and sent for actuator evaluation/replacement."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Control functionality checked
  • Presence or absence of actuator noise
  • Any visible obstruction
  • Cable condition
  • Final device status (operational or removed from service)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple, visible causes and progresses logically. Prioritize patient safety, avoid unnecessary disassembly, and escalate appropriately when internal failure is suspected. Proper documentation ensures clarity and continuity of care.

That is successful troubleshooting.
