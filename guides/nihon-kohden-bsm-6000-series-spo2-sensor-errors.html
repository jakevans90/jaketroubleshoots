Nihon Kohden BSM-6000 Series

SpO₂ Sensor Errors

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

BSM-6000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting SpO₂ (oxygen saturation) sensor errors on Nihon Kohden BSM-6000 series monitors. Common symptoms include missing readings, intermittent signal loss, low signal quality alarms, or error messages. Most issues are external and related to sensor placement, integrity, or patient factors before considering internal monitor faults.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check Sensor Connection

  • Ensure the SpO₂ sensor is firmly connected to the monitor port.
  • Loose or partially connected sensors can prevent readings.

Inspect the Sensor and Cable

  • Look for visible damage, kinks, frays, or exposed wires.
  • Confirm that the sensor type is compatible with the BSM-6000 series.

Verify Sensor Placement

  • Confirm proper placement on the patient (finger, toe, or earlobe depending on the sensor).
  • Ensure the sensor site is clean, dry, and free from nail polish, excessive movement, or ambient light interference.

Swap with a Known Good Sensor

  • If possible, replace the current sensor with a known working sensor.
  • This helps identify whether the issue is the sensor or the monitor input.

Check Monitor Settings

  • Confirm the SpO₂ module is enabled in the monitor settings.
  • Verify alarm thresholds and averaging times are appropriate for the patient.

Assess Patient Factors

  • Poor perfusion, tremors, or cold extremities can prevent accurate readings.
  • If safe, try an alternative measurement site.

Power Cycle the Monitor

  • If external checks fail, power off the monitor, wait 30 seconds, and restart.
  • Some transient errors can be cleared with a simple reboot.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks are completed and the problem continues:

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send to the biomedical repair bench or vendor for evaluation.

Knowing when to escalate is part of proper troubleshooting and prevents patient risk.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot the SpO₂ sensor while a patient is actively dependent on it.

Move the patient to another monitor if oxygen saturation monitoring is critical.

Only perform troubleshooting when it is safe for the patient.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor shows SpO₂ error, no readings available.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Sensor cable appeared intact, but swapping with a known working sensor restored readings. Original sensor likely defective.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced SpO₂ sensor. Verified accurate readings on patient. Monitor returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested (if applicable for powered sensors)
  • Sensor swapped and tested
  • Alarm behavior observed
  • Indicator lights (sensor status LED)
  • Any unusual sounds or heat from the sensor module
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Always prioritize patient safety and logical, stepwise troubleshooting. External checks are often sufficient to resolve sensor errors, and escalation should be done promptly when needed. Proper documentation ensures continuity of care and accountability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
