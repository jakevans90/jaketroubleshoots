3M Bair Hugger 775 Series

Error: FC-005 Hose end sensor 2 shorted

Asset Type

Patient Warming System

Manufacturer

3M

Model

Bair Hugger 775 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Error FC-005 on the 3M Bair Hugger 775 Series Patient Warming System. FC-005 indicates hose end sensor 2 is shorted, which can cause a sensor-related warming fault, interrupt patient warming, or prevent the unit from safely monitoring and controlling delivered air temperature.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the Bair Hugger 775 from patient use if the fault occurs during active warming.
  • Stop using the warming unit on the patient.
  • Provide an alternate warming method if patient warming is still clinically needed.
  • Do not continue using the unit if FC-005 remains active or returns after reset.
  • Allow clinical staff to determine whether patient warming must continue with another device.

Expected outcome: Patient warming is maintained safely without relying on a unit reporting a sensor fault.

Why it matters: A hose end sensor fault can affect temperature monitoring at the hose end and may prevent safe temperature control.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Check the display for Error FC-005.
  • Ask staff when the fault appeared, such as startup, warm-up, active patient warming, after hose movement, or intermittently.
  • Power the unit off, disconnect it from power, and allow it to sit briefly before reconnecting.
  • Restart the unit and observe whether FC-005 returns.

Expected outcome: Error FC-005 is reproducible or clearly observed.

Why it matters: This confirms a true system-reported hose end sensor short condition rather than a one-time user report, power interruption, or accessory handling issue.

Remove the Unit from Use and Inspect the Hose Assembly

  • Disconnect the unit from power.
  • Inspect the hose for cracks, kinks, crushing, loose hose connection, contamination, liquid exposure, or damage near the hose end.
  • Check that the hose is fully seated and properly attached.
  • Inspect the hose end area for signs of wear or sensor damage.

Expected outcome: Hose is intact, dry, properly connected, and free of obvious damage.

Why it matters: A shorted hose end sensor can be caused by hose damage, moisture, or failure of the sensor wiring path.

Check for Moisture or Contamination

  • Inspect the hose end and elbow sensor area for moisture, residue, cleaning solution, or fluid contamination.
  • Do not return the unit to service if liquid contamination is suspected.
  • Allow only appropriate drying or cleaning per facility process and manufacturer guidance.
  • If contamination is significant, remove the unit from service for bench evaluation.

Expected outcome: Hose and elbow sensor area are clean and dry.

Why it matters: Moisture can create abnormal sensor readings or electrical shorts, especially near temperature sensor connections.

Test with a Known-Good Hose if Available

  • Disconnect power before changing the hose.
  • Install a known-good compatible hose assembly.
  • Reconnect power and power on the unit.
  • Run the unit long enough to confirm whether FC-005 returns.
  • If FC-005 clears with a known-good hose, the original hose assembly is likely faulty.
  • If FC-005 remains, the issue may involve the elbow sensor, internal sensor circuit, or control electronics.

Expected outcome: Testing helps determine whether the fault follows the hose or remains with the warming unit.

Why it matters: Swapping the hose is one of the cleanest ways to separate an accessory-related failure from a device-side fault.

Inspect the Elbow Sensor Area

  • Disconnect power.
  • Inspect the elbow sensor area for damage, loose connection, bent or damaged contacts, moisture, overheating, or contamination.
  • Reseat accessible external connections only if appropriate.
  • Do not perform deep internal disassembly during basic troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: Elbow sensor area appears clean, dry, undamaged, and properly connected.

Why it matters: FC-005 may be caused by hose end sensor failure, hose issue, or elbow sensor fault.

Replace the Faulty Hose or Elbow Sensor as Indicated

  • Replace the hose if the fault follows the hose or clears with a known-good hose.
  • Replace or escalate for elbow sensor repair if the hose is not the cause.
  • After replacement, reconnect the unit and verify the fault no longer appears.

Expected outcome: FC-005 clears after the failed hose or elbow sensor issue is corrected.

Why it matters: A shorted sensor condition should not be bypassed or ignored. The temperature sensing path must be reliable before patient use.

Perform Calibration and Return-to-Service Checks

  • Perform calibration per service procedure.
  • Verify the unit powers on normally.
  • Confirm no FC-005 fault returns.
  • Verify normal airflow and temperature operation.
  • Inspect the hose connection and physical condition one final time.
  • Document final testing before returning the device to service.

Expected outcome: Unit passes calibration, operates normally, and does not reproduce FC-005.

Why it matters: Sensor-related repairs can affect temperature control. Calibration confirms the device is safe and ready for clinical use.

Stop When Resolved

  • If the hose or elbow sensor repair resolves FC-005 and the unit passes calibration and functional testing, return the device to service.
  • Remove any Out of Service tag.
  • Clean the unit according to facility process.
  • Return the unit to its clinical area.
  • Document the repair clearly in the work order.

Expected outcome: Device is safely returned to service with the FC-005 fault corrected.

If the Problem Persists

If FC-005 remains after checking power reset, hose condition, moisture, hose replacement, and elbow sensor condition, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent FC-005 fault may indicate a device-side sensor circuit, elbow sensor, wiring, or control-related failure.

  • Remove the unit from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send it for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Do not return the unit to patient use until the fault is corrected, calibration is completed, and functional testing passes.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first if active warming is needed. Do not troubleshoot a Bair Hugger with an unresolved FC-005 fault while it is being used on a patient. A hose end sensor short can affect the system’s ability to safely monitor and control delivered air temperature.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Bair Hugger 775 reported Error FC-005 during use/startup. Display indicated hose end sensor 2 shorted. Unit removed from patient use for evaluation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found fault related to hose end temperature sensing path. Hose end sensor 2 short condition suspected due to hose assembly issue, elbow sensor fault, moisture, or sensor circuit failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Inspected hose, hose end, and elbow sensor area for damage, moisture, and contamination. Tested with known-good hose as applicable. Replaced faulty hose or elbow sensor as indicated. Performed calibration and functional testing. Unit passed testing with no FC-005 fault returning and was returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

FC-005 should be treated as a real temperature sensor fault until proven otherwise. Start with patient safety, confirm the exact error, check the hose and sensor area first, and isolate the issue before assuming deeper device failure. If the fault persists after external checks and accessory replacement, escalation is the correct repair path. Clear documentation protects the patient, the technician, and the next person who touches the device.

That is successful troubleshooting.
