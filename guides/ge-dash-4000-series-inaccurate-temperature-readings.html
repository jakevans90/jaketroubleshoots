GE DASH 4000 Series Patient Monitor

Inaccurate Temperature Readings

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

DASH 4000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting DASH 4000 monitors when temperature measurements are inconsistent, delayed, or clearly incorrect. Common symptoms include readings that do not match the patient’s expected temperature, sudden spikes or drops, or alarms indicating sensor malfunction. This guide focuses on verifying external connections and sensor integrity before considering internal device issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Patient Safety and Sensor Placement

  • Ensure the patient is on a stable surface and that temperature probes are placed according to manufacturer guidelines (oral, rectal, esophageal, or skin).
  • Incorrect placement can give false readings.

Check Sensor Type and Compatibility

  • Confirm the probe is compatible with DASH 4000.
  • Using the wrong probe type (adult vs pediatric, rectal vs skin) can cause inaccurate readings.

Inspect Sensor and Cable for Damage

  • Look for kinks, cuts, or exposed wires.
  • Check connectors for corrosion, bent pins, or dirt.
  • Replace the probe if any damage is found.

Reconnect the Sensor

  • Disconnect and reconnect the temperature probe to the monitor.
  • Ensure the connection is secure; loose connections can cause intermittent or inaccurate readings.

Check for Environmental Interference

  • Ensure the patient is not near high airflow vents or heat sources, which can skew readings.
  • Avoid wrapping the sensor in insulating material unless required by the probe type.

Confirm Monitor Settings

  • Verify the monitor is set to the correct probe type and measurement units (°C or °F).
  • Check for any configuration errors in the measurement mode.

Swap with a Known-Good Sensor

  • If available, connect a working, verified temperature probe.
  • If readings normalize, the issue is likely the original sensor.

If the Problem Persists

If external checks and sensor swaps do not correct the inaccurate readings:

  • The monitor may have an internal temperature measurement module failure.
  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service.”
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation.

Stopping troubleshooting when external issues are ruled out prevents unnecessary patient risk.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot temperature readings on an active patient without a backup measurement method.

Use a secondary thermometer to confirm patient temperature while troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nurse reports patient temperature reads 35°C while clinical signs indicate fever.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Temperature probe cable had intermittent connection; no monitor internal faults observed yet.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Reconnected probe; readings stabilized. Replaced probe as precaution.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Sensor serial number and type
  • Cable condition
  • Monitor temperature configuration (unit, mode)
  • Any recent exposure to heat sources or cooling fans
  • Final device status after troubleshooting

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount—accurate temperature readings are critical. Always start with the simplest external checks before escalating. Proper documentation ensures continuity of care and accountability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
