Getinge ED-Flow

Drying Cycle Failure or Incomplete Drying

Asset Type

Endoscope Reprocessor (AER)

Manufacturer

Getinge

Model

ED-Flow

What This Guide Helps With

Failure of the drying cycle, wet scopes after processing, or drying phase aborts due to airflow, connection, or configuration issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not release or use any endoscope that has not completed a full drying cycle.
  • Use an alternate, properly processed scope if needed.

Expected: Patient care continues safely using a fully processed scope
Why it matters: Moisture promotes microbial growth and compromises disinfection

Verify the Reported Issue and Cycle Behavior

  • Confirm whether the drying cycle failed, shortened, or completed with wet scopes
  • Note any alarms or messages related to airflow or drying

Expected: Clear identification of when drying fails
Why it matters: Helps determine if this is a consistent failure or intermittent condition

Check Compressed Air Supply (Most Common Cause)

  • Verify facility compressed air is connected (if applicable)
  • Check pressure is within acceptable range
  • Listen for airflow during drying phase

Expected: Stable air supply with audible airflow
Why it matters: Insufficient air pressure prevents proper channel and exterior drying

Inspect Air Filters and Dryer Components

  • Check air intake filters for blockage or contamination
  • Inspect for overdue filter replacement
  • Ensure no visible obstruction in air pathways

Expected: Clean filters allowing unrestricted airflow
Why it matters: Reduced airflow leads to incomplete moisture removal

Verify Proper Scope Channel Connections

  • Confirm all channel connectors are correctly attached
  • Ensure correct hookup for scope type
  • Check for loose or disconnected tubing

Expected: Secure, correct channel connections
Why it matters: Drying requires airflow through all internal channels

Check for Kinked or Blocked Tubing

  • Inspect all drying hoses and tubing for kinks or damage
  • Straighten or replace as needed

Expected: Unobstructed tubing path
Why it matters: Blocked airflow prevents proper drying

Verify Drying Cycle Settings and Duration

  • Check programmed drying time
  • Confirm cycle parameters match facility protocol
  • Ensure no custom cycle is reducing drying duration

Expected: Adequate drying time configured
Why it matters: Insufficient time may leave residual moisture even with proper airflow

Check Door Seal Integrity During Drying Phase

  • Inspect door gasket for damage or debris
  • Ensure door fully seals during operation

Expected: Tight seal with no air leakage
Why it matters: Air leaks reduce drying efficiency

Run a Test Cycle Without a Scope

  • Run a full cycle empty
  • Observe airflow and drying phase completion

Expected: Drying cycle completes without errors
Why it matters: Determines if issue is scope-related or system-related

If the Problem Persists

If airflow, connections, filters, and settings have been verified, the issue is likely internal (blower system, valves, sensors, or control failure).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Do not attempt internal disassembly. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never assume a scope is safe if it appears only slightly damp. Even minimal retained moisture can compromise high-level disinfection. Always use a fully validated drying cycle.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported scopes coming out wet after ED-Flow cycle completion."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Clogged air intake filter restricting airflow during drying phase."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced air filter and verified proper airflow and complete drying cycle operation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Air supply pressure verified
  • Filters inspected/replaced
  • Tubing checked for kinks
  • Channel connections confirmed
  • Alarm messages observed
  • Drying cycle duration verified
  • Door seal condition
  • Test cycle results
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Drying failures are most often airflow or connection related and can usually be resolved without invasive repair. Always prioritize patient safety by ensuring scopes are fully processed. When external causes are ruled out, escalation is the correct decision. Proper documentation supports both compliance and future troubleshooting efficiency.

That is successful troubleshooting.
