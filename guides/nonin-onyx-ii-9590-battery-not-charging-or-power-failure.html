Nonin Onyx II 9590

Battery Not Charging or Power Failure

Asset Type

Pulse Oximeter

Manufacturer

Nonin

Model

Onyx II 9590

What This Guide Helps With

Device will not power on, shuts off unexpectedly, or shows no response due to battery depletion or charging-related issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from patient use if it cannot power on or maintain operation. Use an alternate pulse oximeter.
  • Expected: Patient monitoring continues safely
  • Why it matters: Intermittent or no readings can lead to missed hypoxia

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to power on the device and observe behavior.
  • Check for:
    • No display or screen activity
    • Device powers on briefly then shuts off
    • No response when button is pressed
  • Expected: Clear confirmation of power-related failure
  • Why it matters: Confirms true power issue vs user operation error

Check Battery Installation and Type

  • Open the battery compartment and verify batteries are installed correctly.
  • Action:
    • Confirm polarity (+/- orientation)
    • Ensure correct battery type (typically AAA alkaline)
    • Check for loose or improperly seated batteries
  • Expected: Properly installed batteries allow device to power on
  • Why it matters: Incorrect installation is a common cause of failure

Replace Batteries with Known Good Set

  • Remove existing batteries and install a new set of high-quality alkaline batteries.
  • Action:
    • Do not reuse partially depleted batteries
    • Avoid mixing old and new batteries
  • Expected: Device powers on normally
  • Why it matters: Low or depleted batteries are the most common cause of power issues

Inspect Battery Contacts

  • Examine the battery compartment for contact issues.
  • Check for:
    • Corrosion or residue on contacts
    • Bent or recessed terminals
    • Dirt or debris preventing contact
  • Action:
    • Clean lightly with approved method if needed
    • Ensure contacts are intact and making firm connection
  • Expected: Clean, intact contacts restore power path
  • Why it matters: Poor contact prevents proper power delivery

Check for Physical Damage or Moisture Exposure

  • Inspect the device housing and battery compartment.
  • Look for:
    • Cracks or damage near battery area
    • Signs of fluid ingress
    • Warping or discoloration
  • Expected: No visible damage affecting power system
  • Why it matters: Internal damage can interrupt power flow

Test Power Button Function

  • Press and hold the power button firmly.
  • Observe:
    • Any flicker or intermittent response
    • Lack of tactile feedback
  • Expected: Consistent response when pressing button
  • Why it matters: Worn or failed button can mimic power failure

Attempt Multiple Power Cycles

  • With fresh batteries installed, attempt to power cycle the unit several times.
  • Expected: Device consistently powers on
  • Why it matters: Rules out intermittent electrical contact issues

If the Problem Persists

If the device still does not power on after verifying batteries, contacts, and external condition, the issue is likely internal (power circuit or board failure).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary time spent on non-serviceable internal failures.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a pulse oximeter on a patient. Always move the patient to a working device before beginning troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Pulse oximeter would not power on during patient use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Batteries depleted and battery contacts slightly corroded."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced batteries and cleaned contacts; device powered on and passed functional check."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Batteries replaced with new set
  • Battery orientation verified
  • Contacts inspected/cleaned
  • Power button response checked
  • Device powered on successfully or not
  • Any signs of corrosion or damage
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Power issues on handheld devices are most often simple battery or contact problems. Start with these basic checks before assuming failure. Patient safety comes first, and proper escalation ensures efficient use of time and resources.

That is successful troubleshooting.
