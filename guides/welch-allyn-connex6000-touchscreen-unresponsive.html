Welch Allyn Connex 6000 Series

Touchscreen Unresponsive

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

Welch Allyn

Model

Connex 6000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Connex 6000 Series touchscreen does not respond to user input. Symptoms may include taps not registering, delayed response, partial touchscreen functionality, or the screen appearing frozen. Many touchscreen issues are related to software lockups, debris on the screen, accessories interfering with the display, or power-related problems rather than immediate hardware failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Confirm the device is powered on and not in sleep mode

  • Ensure the monitor is powered on and the display is active. Press the power button once to wake the screen if necessary. A dark or sleeping display can appear unresponsive.

Verify the device is not actively monitoring a patient before troubleshooting

  • Ensure the device is not in use on a patient. Move the patient to another monitor if necessary before proceeding.

Inspect the touchscreen surface

  • Check the screen for fluids, gel, dirt, or debris. Dried cleaning solution, ultrasound gel, or bodily fluids can interfere with capacitive touch detection. Clean the screen with an approved hospital disinfectant wipe and dry it with a lint-free cloth.

Remove gloves and test touch response

  • Some thick or specialty gloves can reduce touchscreen sensitivity. Test the screen briefly with a bare finger to determine if the issue is glove-related.

Check for protective covers or screen protectors

  • Verify that no aftermarket screen protectors, plastic covers, or tape are interfering with the touchscreen. Remove anything covering the display and test again.

Inspect for physical damage

  • Check the display for cracks, pressure marks, or liquid intrusion. Physical damage can cause partial or complete touchscreen failure.

Reboot the monitor

  • Perform a controlled reboot by pressing and holding the power button until the device powers down, then power it back on. Many touchscreen issues are caused by temporary software lockups.

Verify system responsiveness during reboot

  • Observe whether the screen responds during the boot process. If the display shows normal startup behavior but becomes unresponsive afterward, the issue may be software related.

Disconnect external accessories

  • Remove USB devices, barcode scanners, or other accessories that may be connected. Occasionally peripheral communication issues can cause interface delays or freezing.

Test multiple screen areas

  • Tap different areas of the screen to determine whether the issue affects the entire touchscreen or only a specific region. Partial failures often indicate digitizer damage.

Check AC power and battery condition

  • Ensure the monitor is properly connected to AC power and the battery is not critically low. Low voltage conditions can sometimes cause unstable behavior.

If the Problem Persists

If the touchscreen remains unresponsive after cleaning, rebooting, and verifying external conditions, the likely causes include touchscreen digitizer failure, internal display controller issues, or software corruption.

At this point the device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Avoid opening the device in the clinical area. Internal display components require controlled service procedures.

Knowing when to stop troubleshooting and escalate is proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt to troubleshoot touchscreen problems while the device is actively monitoring a patient. Move the patient to a functioning monitor first to ensure continuous vital sign monitoring and patient safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Nursing reported the Connex 6000 touchscreen was not responding to touch inputs and they were unable to enter patient information.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Touchscreen was completely unresponsive despite normal display operation. Cleaning, accessory removal, and device reboot did not restore functionality.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
Device removed from service and labeled Out of Service. Unit sent to Clinical Engineering shop for display/touchscreen evaluation and potential replacement.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested
  • Battery charging status observed
  • Touch response in different screen areas
  • Screen cleaned and inspected
  • Reboot performed
  • Accessories disconnected
  • Any visible screen damage noted
  • Final device status (removed from service or returned to use)

Final Thought

Touchscreen problems often appear serious but are frequently caused by simple issues such as debris, software lockups, or external accessories. A logical troubleshooting process that starts with basic checks helps prevent unnecessary repairs while ensuring patient safety. When external causes are ruled out, proper escalation protects both the patient and the equipment.

That is successful troubleshooting.
