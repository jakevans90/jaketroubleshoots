Philips SureSigns VS4

Temperature Probe Not Recognized

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

SureSigns VS4

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Philips SureSigns VS4 monitor fails to recognize the temperature probe. Symptoms may include an error message when attempting a temperature reading, missing or erratic temperature values, or failure to detect the probe at all. Most issues are related to external factors such as connection issues, damaged cables, or improper probe insertion rather than internal electronics.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Inspect the Probe and Cable

  • Check the temperature probe for visible damage (cracks, bent pins, broken connectors).
  • Inspect the cable for fraying, cuts, or kinks.
  • Why: Damaged probes or cables often prevent recognition.

Verify Proper Connection

  • Ensure the probe is fully and securely inserted into the correct temperature port.
  • Some probes have orientation keys; confirm alignment.
  • Why: Loose or misaligned connections can prevent the monitor from detecting the probe.

Swap with a Known-Good Probe

  • Replace the probe with another functioning, compatible Philips VS4 temperature probe.
  • Why: Confirms whether the issue is with the probe or the monitor port.

Check Monitor Port

  • Inspect the temperature input port for debris, bent pins, or visible damage.
  • Gently clean with a soft, dry cloth if necessary.
  • Why: Port issues can block probe recognition.

Power Cycle the Monitor

  • Turn the monitor off, wait 30 seconds, and turn it back on.
  • Attempt to use the temperature probe again.
  • Why: Some software glitches resolve after a power cycle.

Confirm Software Settings

  • Check that the monitor is set to read from the correct temperature probe type (if configurable).
  • Why: Incorrect settings may prevent detection.

If the Problem Persists

All common external causes have been ruled out. The monitor may have an internal port or circuit failure.

Next Steps:

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label the monitor “Out of Service.”
  • Send to Biomedical Engineering bench or manufacturer service for repair.
  • Do not attempt internal repair beyond this point.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt troubleshooting on a patient currently connected to the monitor.
  • Move the patient to another functioning monitor before continuing.
  • Always ensure probes are clean and compatible to prevent false readings or alarms.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Temperature probe not recognized; monitor shows error and no temperature reading.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Probe was tested with known-good unit; monitor failed to recognize any compatible probe. External connections verified; cable and probe in good condition.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent to Biomedical Engineering for bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Confirmed power source and outlet functionality.
  • Checked for firmware updates if applicable.
  • Checked probe type compatibility.
  • Noted monitor software version.
  • Verified no other active alarms interfering.

Final Thought

Patient safety comes first: never leave a patient on a non-functioning temperature monitor. Troubleshooting should proceed logically from simplest to most complex, knowing when to escalate. Proper CCR documentation ensures clarity for follow-up and repair tracking.

That is successful troubleshooting.
