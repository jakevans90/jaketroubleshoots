Mindray WATO EX Series

Touchscreen / UI Issues

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

WATO EX Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the WATO EX Series anesthesia machine:

  • Does not respond to touchscreen input
  • Shows intermittent or erratic UI behavior
  • Freezes or displays unregistered commands
  • Exhibits delayed or lagging response to user input

Focus is on external, easily verifiable causes first—such as power, connections, and environmental factors—before considering internal electronics.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Source

  • Ensure the machine is properly plugged into a grounded outlet.
  • Check the main power switch and any secondary power buttons.

Why: Fluctuating or insufficient power can cause touchscreen malfunctions.

Inspect Cables and Connections

  • Check all external connections, including USB or auxiliary interfaces, for loose or damaged cables.
  • Disconnect unnecessary peripherals to isolate the issue.

Why: Faulty connections can interfere with the touchscreen interface.

Check for Contamination or Obstructions

  • Inspect the touchscreen for dirt, liquids, or residue that may prevent proper touch response.
  • Clean gently with manufacturer-approved wipes.

Why: Surface contaminants can block capacitive or resistive touch sensors.

Restart the Machine

  • Perform a controlled shutdown and restart.
  • Observe whether the touchscreen responds after booting.

Why: Software glitches can often be resolved with a simple reboot.

Verify Environmental Conditions

  • Ensure room temperature and humidity are within operational specifications.
  • Avoid direct sunlight or strong electromagnetic interference near the touchscreen.

Why: Extreme conditions can degrade touchscreen sensitivity.

Check Software Version and Updates

  • Verify that the device is running the latest software/firmware version.
  • Apply updates according to Mindray procedures if accessible.

Why: Older firmware can lead to UI instability or unresponsiveness.

If the Problem Persists

External causes have been ruled out. The touchscreen hardware or internal control board may be faulty.

  • Remove the machine from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service.”
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is a key part of proper troubleshooting and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot the touchscreen while a patient is actively on the machine.
  • Move the patient to a backup anesthesia device before performing any troubleshooting.
  • Always confirm alarms and patient monitoring are functional on an alternative device first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Touchscreen intermittently unresponsive and lagging during use.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Observed touchscreen unresponsive after verifying power, connections, and environmental conditions. No visible damage or contamination.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
Restarted the machine; issue persisted. Device removed from service and sent to repair.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Power outlet tested and verified
  • All cables and peripherals disconnected and reconnected
  • Machine rebooted multiple times
  • Environmental conditions checked
  • Observed touchscreen behavior (freeze, lag, erratic response)
  • Final device status: Out of service

Final Thought

Clinical Engineering troubleshooting relies on logical, stepwise evaluation, focusing first on simple, verifiable external factors. Escalation to repair is not a failure—it protects patient safety and preserves device integrity. Proper CCR documentation ensures traceability and supports future troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
