Enthermics DC Series Blanket Warmer

Erratic or False “High Temp” / Overheat Alarms

Asset Type

Blanket Warmer

Manufacturer

Enthermics

Model

DC Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where an Enthermics DC Series blanket warmer displays intermittent or persistent “High Temp” or overheat alarms, but the unit does not appear excessively hot.

The focus is on ruling out common external causes—such as airflow restriction, sensor obstruction, loading practices, and power quality—before assuming internal thermostat, control board, or sensor failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure the Unit Is Not in Active Patient Use

  • Confirm blankets have been removed from patient care.
  • Move the patient to an alternate warmer if needed.
  • Silence alarms only after patient impact has been addressed.
  • Patient safety always comes first.

2. Verify Set Temperature vs. Displayed Temperature

  • Compare the programmed setpoint with the actual displayed chamber temperature.
  • Determine whether:
    • The chamber temperature is truly elevated, or
    • The alarm is occurring without a matching temperature rise.
  • This helps determine whether you are dealing with actual overheating or possible sensor misreading.

3. Check for Overloading of Blankets

  • Open the door and inspect load quantity.
  • Remove excess blankets if tightly packed.
  • Ensure blankets are not pressed directly against rear or top airflow vents.
  • Overpacking can:
    • Block airflow
    • Cause localized heat buildup
    • Trigger high-limit alarms even if overall chamber temperature seems normal

4. Inspect Internal Airflow Path

  • With the unit powered off:
  • Inspect rear plenum area (if visible without disassembly).
  • Check for:
    • Obstructed air channels
    • Collapsed blankets blocking return air
    • Lint or debris accumulation near vents
  • Restricted airflow is one of the most common causes of false high-temp conditions.

5. Verify Door Seal and Closure

  • Inspect gasket for damage or deformation.
  • Confirm door closes completely and latches securely.
  • Look for warped hinges or misalignment.
  • A poor seal can:
    • Disrupt airflow patterns
    • Cause temperature instability
    • Lead to erratic alarm behavior

6. Check Ambient Environment

  • Confirm unit is not placed directly next to:
    • HVAC supply vents
    • Heat sources
    • Sunlight exposure
  • Ensure required rear and side clearance is maintained.
  • Environmental heat or poor ventilation can influence internal temperature readings.

7. Inspect Power Source

  • Verify the outlet is stable and properly grounded.
  • Confirm no extension cords or power strips are in use.
  • If possible, move the unit to a known-good outlet temporarily.
  • Voltage fluctuations can cause control instability or erratic alarm behavior.

8. Observe Alarm Behavior Pattern

  • Document:
    • Does alarm occur during heat-up only?
    • Does it occur randomly?
    • Does it clear after power cycle?
    • Is there a strong odor or abnormal heat detected?
  • If chamber feels excessively hot or smells overheated, discontinue use immediately.

9. Perform Power Reset

  • Turn unit off.
  • Unplug from wall for 60 seconds.
  • Reconnect and power on.
  • Allow full heat cycle while monitoring.
  • If alarm recurs under controlled conditions with proper loading, external causes are likely ruled out.

If the Problem Persists

If:

  • Airflow is clear
  • Loading is appropriate
  • Environment is compliant
  • Power source is stable
  • Alarm continues under controlled testing

The issue is likely internal, such as:

  • Temperature sensor drift
  • High-limit thermostat fault
  • Control board malfunction
  • Heater regulation failure
  • Remove the device from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Send for controlled bench evaluation or authorized repair

Do not bypass safety thermostats or override temperature protections.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a blanket warmer while it is actively supporting patient care.

  • Immediately move blankets to an alternate warmer.
  • Verify patient comfort and skin condition.
  • Troubleshoot only after patient safety has been ensured.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Unit alarming ‘High Temp’ intermittently during use. Staff reports blankets do not feel excessively hot.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Airflow restriction noted due to overpacked blankets blocking rear vent channels. Chamber temperature spiking during heat cycle.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Removed excess blankets, cleared airflow path, verified normal heating cycle. Unit tested for 30 minutes with no further alarms. Returned to service.”

“Removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested
  • Power cord condition verified
  • Door seal inspected
  • Load quantity adjusted
  • Ambient room conditions noted
  • Odor or abnormal heat observed
  • Final operational status

Final Thought

Blanket warmers are simple devices, but airflow and loading practices greatly influence temperature stability. Most erratic high-temperature alarms are caused by external factors, not internal failure.

Start with airflow, loading, and environment. Escalate only after logical external causes are ruled out. Document clearly and protect patient safety at every step.

That is successful troubleshooting.
