Mindray TE Series Ultrasound System

Probe Not Detected

Asset Type

Ultrasound System

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

TE Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a Mindray TE Series ultrasound system does not recognize a connected probe. Common symptoms include:

  • “Probe Not Detected” or “No Probe Connected” messages
  • The probe appearing unavailable in the probe selection menu
  • Intermittent probe recognition
  • The system preventing imaging

This guide focuses on external and easily verifiable causes before assuming internal probe port or system hardware failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify the Probe is Fully Connected

  • Disconnect the probe and reconnect it firmly to the probe port.
  • Ensure the connector is fully seated.
  • Ensure the connector locking mechanism is engaged if present.
  • Ensure there is no looseness at the port.
  • Why this matters: Partial probe connections are a common cause of probe detection errors.

Inspect the Probe Connector

  • Examine the probe connector carefully for bent or damaged pins.
  • Check for contamination such as gel or debris.
  • Inspect for cracked connector housing.
  • If contamination is present, clean the connector using approved methods (lint-free cloth or approved wipes).
  • Why this matters: Dirty or damaged connector pins can prevent the system from recognizing the probe.

Inspect the System Probe Port

  • Look inside the probe port on the ultrasound system for bent or recessed pins.
  • Check for debris or dried ultrasound gel.
  • Inspect for physical damage.
  • Do not insert tools into the connector.
  • Why this matters: Probe port damage can prevent proper electrical contact with the probe.

Try a Different Probe

  • Connect another compatible probe to the same probe port.
  • Observe whether the system detects the second probe.
  • If the second probe works normally, the original probe may be faulty.
  • If neither probe is detected, the issue may be related to the probe port or system hardware.

Move the Probe to Another Available Port

  • If the system has multiple probe ports, connect the probe to a different port.
  • Observe whether the system recognizes it.
  • Why this matters: This helps determine whether the issue is isolated to one probe port.

Reboot the Ultrasound System

  • Perform a controlled system restart.
  • After reboot, reconnect the probe.
  • Check if the system now recognizes it.
  • Why this matters: Software or communication faults can sometimes prevent probe detection until the system is restarted.

Check for Cable Damage

  • Inspect the probe cable along its full length for cuts.
  • Look for kinks.
  • Check for crushed sections.
  • Inspect for strain at the connector.
  • Why this matters: Internal cable damage can interrupt communication between the probe and the ultrasound system.

If the Problem Persists

If the probe is still not detected after these checks, common external causes have been ruled out.

The issue is likely due to:

  • Probe internal failure
  • Probe port hardware failure
  • Internal system communication faults

At this point, the device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or further bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop external troubleshooting and escalate for repair is proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot probe detection issues while the device is actively being used on a patient.

If the probe is not recognized during a study:

  • Move the patient to another ultrasound system
  • Ensure patient care is not delayed
  • Perform troubleshooting only after the device is no longer needed for clinical use

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reported the ultrasound system displayed ‘Probe Not Detected’ and imaging could not begin.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Primary probe not recognized by the system. Second probe functioned normally in the same port, indicating probe failure.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Removed defective probe from service and sent for repair. Verified system operation using a known good probe.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Probe connector inspected
  • Probe port condition
  • Alternate probe tested
  • Alternate port tested (if available)
  • System reboot performed
  • Cable condition observed
  • Error message displayed
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting begins with simple physical checks such as connections, probe condition, and system ports before assuming internal hardware failure. A structured approach helps identify whether the issue lies with the probe or the ultrasound system itself. Prioritizing patient safety and documenting findings clearly ensures proper repair decisions and reliable equipment availability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
