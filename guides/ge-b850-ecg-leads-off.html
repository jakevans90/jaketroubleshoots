GE Healthcare CARESCAPE B850 Patient Monitor

ECG Leads Off Detection Failure

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE B850

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the CARESCAPE B850 fails to detect when ECG leads are off. Symptoms may include continuous ECG alarms, inaccurate heart rate readings, missing ECG waveform segments, or failure of the monitor to recognize disconnected leads. Many issues are caused by external factors such as lead placement, cable integrity, or connector contamination.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check ECG Lead Connections

  • Ensure all lead wires are properly connected to the patient and the monitor.
  • Loose or partially connected leads can prevent proper detection.

Inspect ECG Leads for Damage

  • Examine cables for visible wear, cuts, kinks, or broken connectors.
  • Damaged leads may intermittently fail to register as connected.

Swap or Test Leads

  • Replace the ECG leads with a known working set to determine if the issue persists.
  • This isolates whether the problem is with the monitor or the leads themselves.

Check Electrode Placement and Quality

  • Verify electrodes are correctly placed according to standard ECG positioning.
  • Ensure the skin is clean, dry, and properly prepped to reduce impedance.

Check for Connector Contamination

  • Inspect lead connectors for dried gel, moisture, or debris.
  • Clean connectors using manufacturer-approved wipes or alcohol swabs.

Verify Monitor Settings

  • Confirm that the ECG lead detection settings are correctly configured for the lead type in use (e.g., 3-lead, 5-lead, 12-lead).
  • Incorrect settings can prevent proper detection of leads off.

Perform a System Reset (if safe)

  • If no external issues are found, reboot the monitor following safe procedures.
  • Re-check ECG lead detection after reboot.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks have been completed and the issue remains:

  • Remove the monitor from patient service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send the monitor for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to escalate prevents unnecessary patient risk and wasted troubleshooting time.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot leads on an active patient if alarms are unreliable; first move the patient to another monitor.

Troubleshoot ECG lead issues only when it is safe and does not compromise patient monitoring.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor fails to detect disconnected ECG leads; alarms continuously sound.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Leads tested; connectors inspected; electrodes placement confirmed; issue persisted on known working leads.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Monitor removed from service and sent for bench evaluation; labeled Out of Service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Lead type (3-lead, 5-lead, 12-lead)
  • Cable condition
  • Connector cleanliness
  • Electrode brand/type
  • Any error codes displayed
  • Patient impact (e.g., false alarms)

Final Thought

Patient safety is the priority. Step logically through external checks before assuming internal failure. Proper documentation ensures continuity of care and supports future maintenance decisions. Escalation to repair is appropriate when all external troubleshooting fails.

That is successful troubleshooting.
