3M Versaflo TR-300 / TR-600 Series

Hose or Filter Connection Leaks

Asset Type

Powered Air-Purifying Respirator (PAPR)

Manufacturer

3M

Model

Versaflo TR-300 / TR-600 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a 3M Versaflo TR-300 or TR-600 PAPR exhibits leaks at the hose or filter connections. Leaks can reduce airflow efficiency, trigger low-flow alarms, and compromise user protection. The guide focuses on external, easily verifiable issues before assuming internal component failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Inspect hose and filter connection points

  • Ensure the hose is fully seated on both the blower unit and the helmet/hood.
  • Check the filter cartridge is properly installed in its housing.
  • Why: Loose connections are the most common cause of leaks and can trigger airflow alarms.

Check for cracks or damage

  • Examine hoses, seals, and filter housing for visible cracks, tears, or deformations.
  • Why: Damaged hoses or fittings will allow air to escape, reducing performance.

Confirm correct filter type and orientation

  • Verify that the filter is compatible with the TR-300 / TR-600 series and installed in the correct orientation.
  • Why: Incorrect or reversed filters may not seal properly, causing leaks.

Tighten connection clamps or locking rings

  • If the hose or filter has a threaded or clamped connection, ensure it is securely tightened but not overtightened.
  • Why: Proper torque ensures a good seal without damaging components.

Perform a positive pressure check

  • With the PAPR running, cover the intake or filter end to observe if the blower maintains pressure and no air escapes from hose connections.
  • Why: This helps identify subtle leaks that may not be visible but affect airflow.

Clean and inspect sealing surfaces

  • Remove the hose and filter, then clean all mating surfaces with a soft cloth. Check for debris, dust, or residues that could prevent a proper seal.
  • Why: Even small particles can create air gaps leading to leaks.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks are complete and leaks remain, the internal hose, seals, or blower components may be compromised.

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service.”
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation by qualified personnel.

Reminder: Knowing when to escalate is proper Clinical Engineering troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not use the PAPR on a patient or staff member while troubleshooting.
  • Ensure the user has an alternative respiratory device if needed.
  • Perform leak checks in a controlled environment, away from patients.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“PAPR hose and filter connections are leaking; user reports low airflow.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Loose hose connections and/or misaligned filter cartridge observed during inspection.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Connections reseated, filter orientation corrected, sealing surfaces cleaned; leak test passed and device returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Hose and filter inspection completed
  • Filter type verified
  • Positive pressure test performed
  • Device cleaned and reassembled
  • Final status: Passed leak check

Final Thought

Patient safety and staff protection rely on fully functional PAPRs. External checks are quick, safe, and often resolve the issue. Proper escalation and documentation ensure reliable operation and traceability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
