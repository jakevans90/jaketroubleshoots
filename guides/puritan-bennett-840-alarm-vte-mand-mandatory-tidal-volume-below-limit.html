Puritan Bennett 840

Alarm: ↓VTE MAND Mandatory tidal volume below limit

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Puritan Bennett

Model

840

What This Guide Helps With

↓VTE MAND alarm indicating delivered mandatory tidal volume is too low, resulting in inadequate ventilation to the patient.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s ventilation status
  • Provide manual ventilation or switch to a backup ventilator if needed
  • Confirm patient is receiving adequate breaths and oxygenation

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ↓VTE MAND alarm is active
  • Check delivered tidal volume vs set tidal volume
  • Observe if alarm occurs during mandatory breaths
  • Review waveforms for reduced volume delivery

3. Check Patient Condition (Most Critical First Step)

  • Check airway patency (ET tube or trach)
  • Look for secretions, kinking, or biting
  • Assess lung compliance (ARDS, bronchospasm, stiff lungs)

4. Inspect Patient Circuit for Leaks or Disconnections

  • Check all connections from ventilator to patient
  • Inspect for loose fittings or partial disconnections
  • Look for leaks in tubing or humidifier connections

5. Check Ventilator Settings

  • Confirm set tidal volume and alarm limits
  • Review pressure limits that may restrict delivery
  • Verify ventilation mode matches patient needs

6. Run Short Self Test (SST)

  • Initiate SST per device procedure
  • Observe for failures or abnormal results

7. Stop When Resolved

  • Return ventilator to service once tidal volume normalizes
  • Continue monitoring for stability

If the Problem Persists

If the ↓VTE MAND alarm continues after external causes are ruled out, this indicates a ventilator performance issue such as flow delivery, sensors, or internal control fault.

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot low tidal volume alarms on an active patient without backup ventilation available. Always transition the patient first before continuing troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming ↓VTE MAND with low delivered tidal volume during mandatory breaths."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Unable to restore proper tidal volume after verifying patient airway, circuit integrity, and ventilator settings; SST performed—suspected internal ventilator issue."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent to Biomed shop/vendor for further evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Low tidal volume alarms require immediate attention. Always start with patient safety and external checks, but recognize when persistent issues indicate deeper device faults requiring escalation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
