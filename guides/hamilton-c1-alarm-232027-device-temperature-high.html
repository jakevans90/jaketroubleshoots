Hamilton C1

Alarm: 232027 Device temperature high

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm: 232027 Device temperature high on a Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates the internal ventilator temperature is elevated and safe operation may be affected.

A high internal temperature condition may be caused by blocked vents, dust buildup, restricted airflow, environmental heat exposure, abnormal cooling fan operation, or an internal temperature-control fault. Because overheating can affect ventilator reliability, patient safety must be handled first.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not continue using a ventilator that may be overheating if patient ventilation could be affected.
  • Remove the Hamilton C1 from patient use if the alarm is active, recurring, or associated with unusual heat, smell, fan noise, shutdown behavior, or performance issues.
  • Transfer the patient to another ventilator or approved backup ventilation method before troubleshooting.
  • Do not troubleshoot temperature, airflow, or fan-related problems while the ventilator is actively supporting a patient unless clinical staff confirm it is safe.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the display shows Alarm: 232027 Device temperature high.
  • Ask when the alarm occurred: at startup, during extended use, after transport, in a warm room, after connection to external power, or after the ventilator had been running for a period of time.
  • Check whether the housing feels unusually warm.
  • Listen for abnormal cooling fan behavior such as no fan sound, grinding, rattling, or continuous high-speed operation.
  • Check for other technical alarms, unexpected shutdowns, restarts, unusual smells, or reduced airflow from vent openings.

Remove the Ventilator From Heat Stress

  • Move the ventilator away from direct sunlight, radiant warmers, wall-mounted heat sources, and other equipment exhausting warm air.
  • Make sure the ventilator is not covered by blankets, drapes, plastic bags, transport covers, or other materials.
  • Confirm the room temperature is reasonable for ventilator use.
  • Place the ventilator in an open-air location with adequate space around the housing.

Check for Blocked Vents or Restricted Airflow

  • Inspect all external vents and air intake areas.
  • Look for blocked openings, dust buildup, lint, tape, labels, bed linens, drapes, or equipment mounted too close to the vent openings.
  • Clear external obstructions only.
  • Do not open the ventilator housing during basic troubleshooting.
  • Do not return the device to service based only on a cleared alarm; verify stable operation after the unit cools.

Inspect for Dust Buildup Around Air Intake Areas

  • Check visible intake and exhaust areas for heavy dust or lint.
  • If facility procedure allows, clean only the external vent surfaces using approved methods.
  • Do not force debris deeper into the ventilator.
  • Do not use liquid near vent openings.
  • If dust or lint was found, document the condition and the cleaning performed.

Check Cooling Fan Operation

  • Listen for fan operation during startup and while the ventilator is running.
  • Check for no fan sound, intermittent fan operation, grinding, rattling, or fan speed that seems abnormal.
  • Verify that warm air appears to be exhausting normally from expected vent areas.
  • If the fan is not running, sounds abnormal, or the alarm returns after the unit warms up, remove the ventilator from service.

Allow the Ventilator to Cool

  • If the alarm followed heat exposure or extended operation, power the ventilator down if clinically safe and allowed by procedure.
  • Place the device in a well-ventilated, room-temperature area.
  • Allow the housing and internal components to cool.
  • Restart the ventilator after cooling and observe whether Alarm 232027 returns.
  • If the alarm returns after cooling, do not return the ventilator to patient use.

Check Power and External Setup

  • Confirm the ventilator is connected to an appropriate power source.
  • Inspect the power cord and external power connection for damage, looseness, or heat.
  • Verify the ventilator is not placed tightly against a wall, cabinet, bed frame, transport mount, or equipment cart that restricts airflow.
  • Check that accessories, mounting hardware, cable routing, and labels are not covering vent openings.

Run a Basic Operational Check After Cooling

  • Power on the ventilator after correcting any external cause.
  • Confirm Alarm 232027 does not immediately return.
  • Allow the ventilator to run long enough to verify stable operation.
  • Check for normal display behavior, normal fan operation, and no unusual heat, smell, or sound.
  • Perform the required facility pre-use check or operational verification before returning the ventilator to clinical use.

Isolate the Likely Cause

  • If the alarm cleared after removing blankets, clearing vents, cleaning external dust, or moving the unit away from heat, treat it as an external airflow or environmental issue.
  • If the fan is noisy, not running, or the alarm returns after cooling, suspect a cooling fan or internal temperature management problem.
  • If the alarm appears immediately at startup in a normal environment, suspect an internal temperature sensor, fan control, power, or system board-related issue.
  • Do not perform internal disassembly as part of basic clinical troubleshooting.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm: 232027 Device temperature high continues after checking room temperature, blocked vents, dust buildup, fan operation, air intake path, nearby heat sources, physical clearance, power setup, and cooling time, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent high internal temperature alarms strongly suggest an internal cooling, fan, sensor, or control failure.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Do not return it to patient use.
  • Send it for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Document the alarm behavior, environmental checks, vent condition, fan behavior, cooling attempt, and final device status.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot an overheating ventilator while it is actively supporting a patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or approved backup ventilation method first. A ventilator with unresolved temperature alarms should not be trusted for continued clinical use, even if it appears to run normally for a short time.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator reported Alarm 232027 Device temperature high. Staff stated the ventilator became warm during use and the alarm returned after running."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Checked ambient temperature, nearby heat sources, vent openings, dust buildup, air intake path, power connection, and external obstructions. External airflow was acceptable. Cooling fan operation was abnormal and the alarm returned after cooldown, indicating likely internal cooling system failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation/repair. Device was not returned to patient use due to recurring high internal temperature alarm."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Device temperature alarms should be taken seriously because they can point to blocked airflow, poor environmental conditions, fan failure, or internal thermal control problems. Start with patient safety, confirm the alarm, check simple external causes, allow the device to cool, and escalate when the condition returns or cannot be clearly corrected. Clear documentation protects the patient, the technician, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
