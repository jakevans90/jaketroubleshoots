Philips NM3

Power, Battery, Display, or Startup Failure

Asset Type

Capnography Monitor

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

NM3

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting NM3 power, battery, display, or startup issues caused by external power, accessories, settings, or device failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

If the Philips NM3 is being used for active patient monitoring, do not continue using it if the screen is blank, the unit will not power on, or the device cannot complete startup.

Move the patient to another working monitor or approved monitoring method before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: Patient CO2 monitoring continues safely on another device.

Why it matters: A capnography monitor that cannot power on, display values, or complete startup cannot be trusted for ventilation monitoring.

Verify the Reported Failure

Confirm the exact symptom before replacing parts or sending the unit out.

Check whether the issue is:

  • No power at all
  • Powers on briefly, then shuts down
  • Display is blank but power indicators are active
  • Display is dim, distorted, or frozen
  • Startup screen appears but does not complete
  • Battery does not hold charge
  • Device only works when plugged in
  • Device only fails on battery power

Expected outcome: The failure mode is clearly identified.

Why it matters: A dead unit, weak battery, display issue, and startup lockup are different problems with different likely causes.

Check the AC Power Source

Plug the NM3 into a known-good hospital-grade outlet.

If possible, test the outlet with another device or outlet tester per facility practice.

Avoid using a questionable power strip, loose wall outlet, or non-approved extension cord.

Expected outcome: The device receives stable AC power.

If this resolves the issue: Document the faulty outlet or power source and return the NM3 to service only after functional checks pass.

Inspect the Power Cord and Power Supply

Check the power cord and external power supply, if applicable, for:

  • Loose connection
  • Bent or damaged plug
  • Cut or crushed cable
  • Damaged strain relief
  • Burn marks
  • Excessive heat
  • Missing hospital-grade plug integrity

Reseat all power connections firmly.

Expected outcome: The NM3 powers on normally with a secure power connection.

If this resolves the issue: Replace the damaged or loose power accessory and document the correction.

Try a Known-Good Compatible Power Supply or Cord

If your facility has another compatible NM3 power supply or approved power cord, swap it for testing.

Do not use an incorrect voltage, incorrect polarity, or non-approved adapter.

Expected outcome: The unit powers on normally with a known-good power source.

Why it matters: External power accessories are easier and safer to verify before suspecting an internal power fault.

If this resolves the issue: Remove the failed power accessory from service and replace it.

Check for Battery-Only Failure

Disconnect AC power and observe whether the NM3 remains powered on.

Then reconnect AC power and confirm whether charging indicators or battery status messages appear.

Look for signs such as:

  • Battery low message
  • Battery not charging
  • Unit shuts off immediately when unplugged
  • Battery icon missing or abnormal
  • Unit only works on AC power

Expected outcome: Battery operation and charging behavior are understood.

Why it matters: A device that works on AC but fails on battery likely has a battery, charging, or battery connection issue.

Allow the Battery to Charge

If the battery is fully discharged, connect the NM3 to AC power and allow charging per facility practice before retesting battery operation.

Do not assume battery failure immediately after a long storage period.

Expected outcome: The monitor powers up and operates after charging.

If this resolves the issue: Document that the battery was depleted and the device passed power-up and battery verification after charging.

Inspect the Battery Area Externally

If the battery is accessible without deep disassembly, inspect for:

  • Loose battery door
  • Battery not seated correctly
  • Swollen battery
  • Corrosion
  • Cracked housing
  • Liquid intrusion
  • Heat or odor

Do not continue troubleshooting if the battery appears swollen, leaking, overheated, or chemically damaged.

Expected outcome: Battery condition is safe enough for continued evaluation.

Why it matters: Battery damage can create safety risks and should move the device directly to repair evaluation.

Check the Display Brightness and Screen Visibility

If the unit appears powered but the display is blank or hard to see, check whether the display is dimmed, in standby, or affected by viewing angle or room lighting.

Look for:

  • Power LED
  • Backlight glow
  • Audible startup tones
  • Button response
  • Alarm sounds
  • CO2 pump or internal fan noise, if present

Expected outcome: You determine whether this is a true no-power issue or a display/backlight issue.

If this resolves the issue: Correct the display setting and verify normal operation.

Check for Frozen Startup or Software Lockup

If the Philips NM3 powers on but freezes during startup, disconnect patient accessories, sampling line, and external connections.

Power the device off, wait briefly, then restart it on known-good AC power.

Expected outcome: The monitor completes startup without external accessories connected.

Why it matters: A connected accessory, communication cable, or transient fault may interfere with startup.

If this resolves the issue: Reconnect accessories one at a time to identify the item causing the startup problem.

Check CO2 Sampling Line and Accessories

Remove the sampling line or airway adapter from the monitor and inspect for blockage, liquid contamination, broken connectors, or incorrect accessory type.

Then restart the monitor without the accessory connected.

Expected outcome: The NM3 starts normally without a defective or contaminated accessory attached.

Why it matters: Some monitor faults may appear during startup or self-test when an accessory problem is present.

Check for Physical Damage or Fluid Intrusion

Inspect the NM3 housing, display, buttons, ports, and connector areas.

Look for:

  • Cracked case
  • Damaged display lens
  • Sticky buttons
  • Broken power jack
  • Bent pins
  • Evidence of cleaning fluid intrusion
  • Dried residue near seams or connectors
  • Burn smell or unusual heat

Expected outcome: Any visible damage or contamination is identified.

Why it matters: Damage or fluid intrusion can cause unsafe power, display, and startup behavior.

Test with Minimum Configuration

Power the NM3 using known-good AC power with only the minimum required accessories connected.

Do not connect the device to a patient for this test.

Confirm:

  • Unit powers on
  • Display is readable
  • Startup completes
  • No critical startup error remains
  • Battery or charging status appears normal
  • Controls respond normally

Expected outcome: The device completes a clean startup in a controlled bench condition.

If this resolves the issue: Add accessories back one at a time and document the failed accessory or condition found.

Perform a Basic Functional Check

After the unit powers up, perform a basic operational check appropriate for your facility before returning it to service.

Verify:

  • Display remains stable
  • Buttons respond
  • Alarm audio works
  • CO2 function initializes
  • Battery status is acceptable
  • No startup or system errors remain
  • Device remains powered during brief movement of the power cord and connector

Expected outcome: The NM3 operates normally and is safe for clinical use.

If this resolves the issue: Return the device to service after documentation.

Do Not Ignore Intermittent Power Failures

If the device powers off when moved, loses display intermittently, resets randomly, or only works when the cord is held in a certain position, remove it from service.

Expected outcome: Intermittent power problems are treated as repair issues, not cleared as “could not duplicate” without adequate evaluation.

Why it matters: Intermittent power loss during patient monitoring can create a serious patient safety risk.

If the Problem Persists

If the Philips NM3 still will not power on, will not charge, has a blank or failed display, freezes during startup, resets intermittently, or shows signs of internal power/display failure after external checks are complete, remove it from service.

The device should be:

  • Removed from patient use
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation
  • Evaluated for battery, power input, charging circuit, display, keypad, internal connection, or main board failure as appropriate

Common external causes have been ruled out at this point. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not perform deep internal disassembly in a clinical area or return the monitor to service with unresolved power, display, or startup behavior.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a failed capnography monitor on an active patient. Move the patient to another working monitor first, especially if EtCO2, respiration rate, or apnea monitoring is clinically required. A power or display failure can mean the monitor is no longer providing reliable respiratory monitoring.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported the Philips NM3 would not power on and the display remained blank when connected for patient monitoring."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified the wall outlet was functional, but the NM3 power supply cable had an intermittent connection at the monitor input."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced the faulty power supply, confirmed normal startup, verified display operation, checked charging status, and returned the NM3 to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior or startup messages observed
  • Accessories swapped or removed during testing
  • Power behavior on AC power and battery power
  • Environmental factors such as outlet condition, recent power loss, cleaning fluid exposure, heat, odor, or fluid intrusion
  • Indicator lights, battery icon, charge status, display condition, startup behavior, and button response
  • Final device status: returned to service, accessory replaced, labeled Out of Service, or sent for repair

Final Thought

Power, battery, display, and startup failures should be approached from the outside in. Confirm patient safety first, then verify the outlet, cord, power supply, battery behavior, display visibility, and connected accessories before suspecting internal failure. If the monitor cannot start reliably or stay powered, remove it from service and document the troubleshooting clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
