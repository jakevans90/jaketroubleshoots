GE Healthcare Panda Warmer

Heater Lamp or Radiant Panel Failures

Asset Type

Infant Warmer

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Panda Warmer

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting heater lamp or radiant panel failures on the GE Panda Warmer. Symptoms may include the heater lamp not turning on, uneven warming, or alarms indicating heater faults. The focus is on external and easily verifiable checks before considering internal component failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Connection

  • Ensure the warmer is plugged into a live, grounded outlet.
  • Check for tripped circuit breakers or GFCI outlets.
  • Why: Without proper power, the heater lamp cannot function.

Inspect the Power Switch and Control Panel

  • Confirm the warmer powers on and the display is active.
  • Look for any error messages or indicator lights related to heater failure.
  • Why: Some faults are reported immediately on the user interface, allowing quick identification.

Check the Heater Lamp

  • Ensure the lamp is seated correctly in its socket and not visibly damaged.
  • Look for discoloration, burn marks, or broken filaments.
  • Why: Loose or damaged lamps are common causes of heating failure.

Observe Radiant Panel Function

  • Turn the heater to a low setting while monitoring the lamp.
  • Confirm that the panel emits heat and light uniformly.
  • Why: Uneven warming indicates possible lamp or reflector misalignment or partial failure.

Verify Safety Interlocks

  • Inspect for tripped tilt, canopy, or overheat sensors that may prevent heater operation.
  • Why: Safety sensors can disable heating to protect the patient.

Check for Alarm History

  • Review recent heater fault alarms in the warmer’s log.
  • Note if failures are intermittent or continuous.
  • Why: Intermittent faults may indicate loose connections rather than complete component failure.

If the Problem Persists

After performing the above checks, if the heater lamp or radiant panel still fails:

  • Remove the warmer from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for manufacturer repair or bench evaluation.
  • Recognize that further internal troubleshooting requires specialized service tools. Knowing when to stop prevents unsafe patient conditions.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not attempt to operate the warmer on an active patient if heating is unreliable.

Move the infant to another functioning warmer first.

Troubleshooting should always prioritize patient safety over completing the repair.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Heater lamp does not turn on; warming is inconsistent.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Lamp not seated properly; no visible damage to radiant panel; all safety interlocks functional."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated lamp and verified alignment; powered on warmer; heater still non-functional. Device removed from service and sent for repair."

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Lamp seating checked
  • Radiant panel visual inspection
  • Safety interlocks and tilt sensors tested
  • Any audible or visible alarms noted
  • Final device status: removed from service

Final Thought

Clinical Engineering troubleshooting should follow a logical sequence from external checks to escalation. Always prioritize patient safety, properly document findings using the CCR method, and escalate when the device cannot safely perform its function.

That is successful troubleshooting.
