Precision Medical Flowmeter Series

Flow Does Not Match Selected LPM Setting

Asset Type

Oxygen Flowmeter

Manufacturer

Precision Medical

Model

Flowmeter Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting oxygen flowmeter output that appears higher, lower, unstable, or inconsistent with the selected LPM setting.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Oxygen delivery issues can directly affect patient care.

Action:

  • Confirm whether the flowmeter is currently being used on a patient.
  • If the flow does not appear accurate or stable, have clinical staff move the patient to another verified oxygen source before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: The patient receives oxygen from a reliable source before the suspect flowmeter is evaluated.

Why it matters: A flowmeter that does not deliver the selected flow may under-deliver or over-deliver oxygen.

Verify the Reported Flow Setting Issue

Action:

  • Ask staff what flow was selected and what they observed.
  • Confirm whether the issue is flow lower than selected, flow higher than selected, float ball not aligning with the setting, flow changing after adjustment, or flow present even when setting appears low or off.

Expected outcome: The reported symptom is clearly identified before testing.

Why it matters: A flow accuracy issue may be caused by reading technique, wall pressure, outlet condition, humidifier restriction, tubing, or internal flowmeter damage.

Check That the Flowmeter Is Installed on the Correct Gas Outlet

Action:

  • Verify the flowmeter is connected to an oxygen outlet and not the wrong gas service.

Expected outcome: The flowmeter is confirmed to be connected to the proper oxygen source.

Why it matters: Incorrect gas connection is a serious safety issue and must be ruled out first.

Inspect the Wall Outlet Connection

Action:

  • Remove and reseat the flowmeter into the oxygen wall outlet.
  • Confirm it locks in securely and does not wiggle, leak, or partially disengage.

Expected outcome: The flowmeter sits firmly in the outlet and receives a stable gas supply.

If this resolves the issue, stop troubleshooting and document the loose or poorly seated connection.

Check for Audible Leaks

Action:

  • With oxygen flowing, listen near the wall connection, flowmeter body, flow control knob, humidifier adapter, and tubing connection.

Expected outcome: No hissing or leaking is heard.

Why it matters: A leak can make the delivered flow different from the selected LPM setting.

Verify the Flow Is Being Read Correctly

Action:

  • View the float ball straight on at eye level.
  • Confirm the LPM reading is taken at the correct reference point on the float, typically the center of the ball unless the device labeling specifies otherwise.

Expected outcome: The displayed flow is read consistently and correctly.

Why it matters: Parallax error or reading the wrong part of the float can make the flow appear inaccurate.

Check for Sticking or Dragging of the Float Ball

Action:

  • Slowly increase and decrease the flow through the range normally used in the department.
  • Watch whether the float ball rises and falls smoothly.

Expected outcome: The float moves smoothly and settles at the selected flow setting.

If the ball sticks, jumps, hesitates, or does not return smoothly, remove the flowmeter from service.

Inspect the Flow Tube for Contamination or Damage

Action:

  • Visually inspect the flow tube for cracks, cloudiness, residue, fluid contamination, damaged markings, or a tilted/stuck float.

Expected outcome: The tube is clean, readable, intact, and free of visible contamination.

Why it matters: Contamination or physical damage can affect float movement and make the LPM indication unreliable.

Remove Accessories and Retest

Action:

  • Remove the humidifier bottle, adapter, extension tubing, cannula tubing, or mask tubing.
  • Test the flowmeter outlet directly using a known-good test setup if available.

Expected outcome: Flow becomes stable and matches the selected setting when accessories are removed.

If this resolves the issue, replace the restricted or leaking accessory and stop troubleshooting.

Check the Humidifier Bottle or Adapter

Action:

  • Inspect the humidifier bottle, diffuser, gasket, threads, and adapter for cross-threading, blockage, cracking, water intrusion, or poor fit.

Expected outcome: The humidifier assembly is secure, unrestricted, and leak-free.

Why it matters: A restricted or leaking humidifier can cause poor flow delivery even when the flowmeter appears set correctly.

Compare Against a Known-Good Flowmeter

Action:

  • Install a known-good oxygen flowmeter on the same wall outlet and compare operation.

Expected outcome: The known-good flowmeter performs normally at the same outlet.

Why it matters: This helps separate a flowmeter issue from an outlet, pipeline pressure, or room-specific gas supply problem.

Test the Suspect Flowmeter on a Known-Good Outlet

Action:

  • Move the suspect flowmeter to a verified oxygen outlet and retest flow adjustment and float movement.

Expected outcome: The flowmeter should operate normally if the original outlet was the issue.

If the issue follows the flowmeter, remove it from service.

Check for Flow Control Knob Problems

Action:

  • Turn the flow control knob slowly through its normal adjustment range.
  • Check for stiffness, looseness, rough movement, excessive free play, or inability to make fine adjustments.

Expected outcome: The knob adjusts smoothly and the float responds predictably.

Why it matters: A worn or damaged control valve can cause flow to drift or not match the selected LPM setting.

Confirm Flow Accuracy With Approved Test Equipment if Available

Action:

  • Use an approved oxygen flow analyzer or calibrated test device per department procedure.
  • Compare measured flow to selected flow at several common settings.

Expected outcome: Measured flow is within acceptable department or manufacturer tolerance.

If measured flow is outside acceptable limits, remove the flowmeter from service.

Check for Physical Abuse or Drop Damage

Action:

  • Inspect the flowmeter body, outlet adapter, tube, knob, fittings, and labels for cracks, bent fittings, loose parts, or impact marks.

Expected outcome: No damage is found.

Why it matters: Dropped flowmeters may appear usable but still have inaccurate flow control or internal valve damage.

If the Problem Persists

If the flow still does not match the selected LPM setting after outlet, connection, accessory, reading, and basic function checks have been completed, the issue is likely internal to the flowmeter.

External causes such as the wall outlet, gas connection, accessory restriction, humidifier condition, tubing, reading technique, and basic flow control behavior have been ruled out.

The device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair, replacement, or bench evaluation according to department policy.

Do not continue clinical use of a flowmeter with questionable accuracy. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot an oxygen flowmeter while it is actively supplying a patient unless clinical staff confirm it is safe. Move the patient to another verified oxygen source first, especially when flow accuracy is in question, so therapy continuity is maintained.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the Precision Medical oxygen flowmeter did not deliver flow matching the selected LPM setting."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Flowmeter was tested against a known-good outlet and accessory setup; flow remained inconsistent and the float response was not reliable."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the oxygen flowmeter from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for bench evaluation or replacement."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior or reported oxygen delivery concern
  • Oxygen outlet tested
  • Flowmeter reseated at wall outlet
  • Known-good flowmeter comparison performed
  • Suspect flowmeter tested on another outlet
  • Accessories swapped, including humidifier bottle, adapter, tubing, cannula, or mask tubing
  • Power behavior or gas supply behavior at the wall outlet
  • Environmental factors, including room-specific outlet concerns or repeated issues in the same area
  • Indicator lights, markings, float ball movement, or visible scale readability issues
  • Humidifier bottle removed or replaced
  • Tubing or cannula swapped
  • Float ball movement observed
  • Flow control knob condition
  • Audible leaks noted or ruled out
  • Flow analyzer reading, if used
  • Unusual sounds, damage, contamination, or loose fittings
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Oxygen flowmeter troubleshooting should stay focused on patient safety and simple external causes first. Confirm the outlet, connection, accessories, float movement, and reading technique before assuming internal failure. If flow accuracy remains questionable, remove the device from service and document the complaint, cause, and resolution clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
