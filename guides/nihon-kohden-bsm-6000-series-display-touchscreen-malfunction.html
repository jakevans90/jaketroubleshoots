Nihon Kohden BSM-6000 Series

Display or Touchscreen Malfunction

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

BSM-6000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting display or touchscreen malfunctions on Nihon Kohden BSM-6000 series monitors. Symptoms may include unresponsive touch input, flickering display, blank screen, distorted graphics, or delayed response. Most issues can be traced to power, connection, or environmental factors before considering internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check the power source

  • Verify that the monitor is plugged into a live outlet.
  • Confirm the AC adapter or power cable is securely connected.
  • Test the outlet with another device to rule out outlet failure.
  • Why: Power instability is a common cause of display issues.

Inspect the battery (if applicable)

  • Ensure battery is properly seated and charged.
  • Replace with a known-good battery if available.
  • Why: Low or failing battery can cause flickering or blank screens.

Check for external damage

  • Inspect the touchscreen for cracks, scratches, or liquid exposure.
  • Look for loose or damaged connectors at the display interface.
  • Why: Physical damage can prevent proper touchscreen response.

Perform a soft reset

  • Power off the monitor completely, wait 30 seconds, and power back on.
  • Observe if the display initializes correctly.
  • Why: Software or temporary glitches often resolve with a reset.

Verify settings and brightness

  • Adjust screen brightness via the monitor menu if visible.
  • Ensure no “sleep” or “screen saver” mode is active.
  • Why: Misconfigured display settings can mimic hardware failure.

Check environmental factors

  • Ensure the monitor is not exposed to extreme temperatures or electromagnetic interference.
  • Why: Environmental stress can affect touchscreen responsiveness.

Try alternate inputs

  • If touchscreen is unresponsive, check if the monitor responds to button controls.
  • Why: Confirms whether issue is limited to touchscreen interface.

If the Problem Persists

External causes have likely been ruled out.

Device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled “Out of Service”
  • Sent to the Biomedical Engineering bench or authorized repair center for internal evaluation.

Why: Internal LCD/touch controller failures require specialized repair; continuing use may compromise patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot on an actively monitored patient.
  • Move the patient to a backup monitor before attempting further testing.
  • Always ensure alternative monitoring is in place before removing the monitor from service.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Touchscreen is unresponsive, display flickers intermittently, and some buttons do not respond.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Confirmed power outlet functional, AC adapter and battery seated correctly, soft reset performed, environmental factors normal. Touchscreen remains unresponsive.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Monitor removed from service, labeled ‘Out of Service’, sent to Biomedical Engineering bench for internal evaluation/repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested: Yes/No
  • AC adapter or battery swapped: Yes/No
  • Display flicker, blank, or distorted: Observed behavior
  • Soft reset results: Pass/Fail
  • External damage or liquid exposure: Observed/None
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety must always come first. A methodical approach ensures that external, easily corrected issues are addressed before assuming internal failure. Proper CCR documentation provides traceability and supports maintenance decisions. Knowing when to escalate prevents unnecessary patient risk and reduces downtime.

That is successful troubleshooting.
