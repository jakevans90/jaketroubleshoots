STERIS AMSCO 4085

Hydraulic Fluid Leaks

Asset Type

Surgical Table

Manufacturer

STERIS

Model

AMSCO 4085

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a STERIS AMSCO 4085 surgical table exhibiting hydraulic fluid leaks. Symptoms may include:

  • Visible fluid around the base, column, or lift mechanisms
  • Slow or uneven table movement
  • Unusual noises or jerky motion during adjustments

Focus is on identifying external issues, containment, and patient safety before assuming internal hydraulic failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Move patient to a backup table if actively in use.
  • Do not operate table while leaks are present; risk of slip or sudden movement exists.

Inspect the Environment

  • Check for any fluid on the floor around the table.
  • Determine if leak is coming from the table or external sources (spills from IV pumps, cleaning solution, etc.).

Check Hydraulic Reservoir Level (External Access Only)

  • If accessible, inspect the hydraulic reservoir for obvious cracks or loose fittings.
  • Do not open or disassemble internal components beyond what the manufacturer allows.

Examine Visible Hoses, Fittings, and Cylinders

  • Look for signs of wetness or weeping around hydraulic lines.
  • Confirm that all fittings appear tight and not corroded or damaged.

Operate Table Slowly (If Safe and No Patient Present)

  • Observe movement for areas where fluid appears.
  • Listen for hissing or unusual sounds, which may indicate a ruptured seal.

Contain and Clean Minor Leaks

  • Use absorbent pads or a tray under leak points to prevent slips or contamination.
  • Mark area clearly to avoid accidental contact.

If the Problem Persists

After external inspection, if leak source cannot be resolved, internal hydraulic components (cylinders, seals, pumps) are likely compromised.

  • Remove table from service.
  • Label “Out of Service – Hydraulic Leak.”
  • Send to STERIS-authorized service or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting—internal hydraulic repair requires specialized service and should not be attempted in the OR.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt adjustments with a patient on a table exhibiting hydraulic leaks.
  • Even small leaks can cause sudden shifts or loss of table stability.
  • Ensure backup table availability before testing or moving the affected table.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“OR staff reported hydraulic fluid pooling under the surgical table and slow movement.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“External inspection revealed fluid around the base and lift cylinders; hoses and fittings appear intact, but internal seals may be compromised.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Table removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for STERIS service evaluation. Area cleaned and absorbent pads placed to contain fluid.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Location and severity of visible leaks
  • Table movement behavior (slow, jerky, uneven)
  • Any abnormal sounds during operation
  • Final table status (Out of Service, tagged)

Final Thought

Patient safety must always take priority. External checks can identify obvious leak sources, but internal hydraulic failure requires professional service. Proper escalation, containment, and CCR documentation protect staff and patients while ensuring compliance with hospital policy.

That is successful troubleshooting.
