Philips HeartStart MRx

Battery Not Holding Charge / Weak Battery

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

HeartStart MRx

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the HeartStart MRx battery:

  • Fails to hold a charge fully
  • Shows “Battery Low” or “Replace Battery” alarms even after charging
  • Experiences reduced runtime or rapid voltage drop

Focus is on external checks and proper escalation rather than internal battery repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Visual Inspection of Battery

  • Check for physical damage, corrosion on contacts, or swelling.
  • Why: Damaged batteries can fail to charge or deliver current safely.

Verify Battery Seating

  • Remove and reinstall the battery to ensure it’s fully seated.
  • Check for firm clicks and alignment indicators.
  • Why: Poor contact can prevent proper charging and trigger alarms.

Check Charger Functionality

  • Inspect the charging cradle/adapter for damage or loose connections.
  • Verify charger indicator lights (green for charging, amber for fault).
  • Why: A malfunctioning charger can mimic battery failure.

Test with a Known Good Battery

  • Swap the current battery with a fully charged, verified battery.
  • Observe if the device charges and powers normally.
  • Why: Confirms whether the issue is with the battery or the device’s charging circuit.

Charge and Monitor Battery Behavior

  • Fully charge the battery and monitor voltage and runtime under normal use.
  • Look for rapid drops or failure to reach full charge.
  • Why: Weak batteries often show incomplete charge or short runtime.

If the Problem Persists

If a known good battery charges correctly, the original battery is faulty.

If the device still fails to charge a verified battery, escalate to bench evaluation; the charging circuitry may be compromised.

  • Remove faulty battery from service.
  • Label “Out of Service – Battery Fault.”
  • Send battery or defibrillator to approved Philips service center if internal charging fault is suspected.

Knowing when to stop prevents device damage and maintains patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never rely on a defibrillator with a weak or failing battery during patient care.
  • Always have a backup defibrillator available when one unit is being charged or evaluated.
  • Avoid attempting internal battery repairs—these are high-voltage and risk safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Battery not holding charge” and device alarms after charging.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Battery fails to reach full charge and rapidly loses voltage; charger confirmed functional with test battery.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Battery removed from service, labeled, and replaced with verified battery. Device function confirmed.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Charger type and indicator behavior
  • Battery age and cycle count if available
  • Runtime observed during test
  • Any unusual heat or swelling on the battery
  • Final device status: operational with verified battery

Final Thought

Battery reliability is critical for patient safety. Always troubleshoot logically: check battery seating, charger function, and confirm with a known good battery. Escalate promptly when external checks indicate an internal issue. Proper CCR documentation ensures a clear history for future reference.

That is successful troubleshooting.
