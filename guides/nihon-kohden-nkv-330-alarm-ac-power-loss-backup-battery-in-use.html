Nihon Kohden NKV-330

Alarm: AC Power Loss/Backup Battery In Use

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

NKV-330

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Nihon Kohden NKV-330 alarm AC Power Loss/Backup Battery In Use. This alarm indicates that AC power and main battery power are lost, and the ventilator is operating on backup battery power.

This condition should be treated as urgent because normal power redundancy is compromised and remaining backup battery runtime may be limited.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately confirm the patient is being ventilated safely.
  • Check the patient first.
  • Confirm chest rise, oxygenation, airway status, and ventilator-delivered breaths.
  • Notify clinical staff that the ventilator is running on backup battery power.
  • Prepare an alternate ventilator or manual ventilation source if AC power cannot be restored quickly.
  • Do not leave the ventilator unattended while it is operating on backup battery power.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact alarm displayed is AC Power Loss/Backup Battery In Use.
  • Check whether the ventilator is unplugged, plugged into a dead outlet, or connected to emergency power.
  • Confirm whether the main battery is missing, depleted, failed, or not recognized.
  • Observe the power indicators and battery status on the ventilator.
  • Ask staff whether the unit was recently moved, unplugged, transported, or had its battery changed.

Connect the Ventilator to AC Power Immediately

  • Plug the ventilator power cord into a known working hospital-grade AC outlet.
  • Use emergency power if available and appropriate.
  • Confirm the power cord is fully seated at the ventilator and wall outlet.
  • Check for a loose plug, damaged cord, tripped power strip, or unplugged connection.
  • Avoid using extension cords or non-approved power strips.

Verify the Outlet Has Power

  • Test the outlet with an approved outlet tester or another known working device if hospital policy allows.
  • Check whether other equipment on the same outlet or circuit lost power.
  • Try another known working outlet, preferably emergency power.
  • Report suspected outlet or circuit issues to Facilities.

Inspect the Power Cord and AC Connection

  • Inspect the power cord for cuts, crushed insulation, bent blades, damaged strain relief, or loose connectors.
  • Confirm the cord is the correct type and is firmly connected.
  • Check for signs of heat, arcing, burning smell, or discoloration.
  • Replace the power cord if damage is found.

Check the Main Battery Status

  • Confirm the main battery is installed correctly.
  • Check whether the main battery is depleted, not seated, expired, or not recognized.
  • If a fully charged spare main battery is available, replace the main battery as soon as possible.
  • Confirm the ventilator recognizes the replacement battery.
  • Allow the installed battery to charge after AC power is restored, if appropriate.

Check for Recent Transport or Power Change

  • Ask whether the ventilator was recently moved between rooms.
  • Confirm whether it was unplugged for transport and not reconnected.
  • Check whether the main battery was removed or swapped.
  • Verify whether staff expected the ventilator to be running on battery power.

Confirm Alarm Clears and Power Status Is Stable

  • Confirm AC power is restored.
  • Confirm the main battery is present and charging or fully charged.
  • Confirm the backup battery alarm has cleared.
  • Watch the ventilator for several minutes to ensure the alarm does not recur.
  • Verify no additional battery, power supply, or charging alarms are present.

Stop When Resolved

  • If reconnecting AC power and restoring a valid main battery clears the alarm, confirm stable ventilator operation.
  • Confirm no active power-related alarms remain.
  • Document the outlet, cord, battery, and final status.
  • Return the ventilator to service if it passes normal operational checks.

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm continues after confirming a known good AC outlet, secure power cord connection, and known good main battery, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent AC Power Loss/Backup Battery In Use conditions may indicate an internal power input, battery charging, battery detection, or power management failure.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Provide alternate ventilation support.
  • Send the ventilator for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Do not rely on the backup battery as a long-term power source.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm casually while the ventilator is supporting an active patient. Backup battery operation means power redundancy is compromised. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or restore stable power immediately if the alarm cannot be cleared quickly.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator displayed Alarm: AC Power Loss/Backup Battery In Use. Staff reported the ventilator was operating on backup battery power after loss of AC and main battery power."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Ventilator was connected to a non-powered outlet after transport, and the main battery was depleted. Backup battery alarm activated as expected."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Connected ventilator to known working AC emergency-power outlet. Installed fully charged spare main battery. Confirmed AC power restored, main battery recognized, charging status present, and alarm cleared. Monitored operation with no recurrence. Returned device to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The AC Power Loss/Backup Battery In Use alarm should be treated as urgent because the ventilator has lost normal AC power and main battery support. Start with patient safety, restore AC power, verify the main battery, and escalate if stable power cannot be confirmed. Clear documentation protects the patient, the clinical team, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
