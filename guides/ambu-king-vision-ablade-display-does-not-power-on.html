Ambu King Vision aBlade

Display Does Not Power On

Asset Type

Video Laryngoscope

Manufacturer

Ambu

Model

King Vision aBlade

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting a King Vision aBlade display that will not power on due to battery, contact, accessory, or external damage issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Confirm the device is not needed for an active airway procedure before troubleshooting.

If the King Vision display fails during setup or patient care, provide clinical staff with another ready video laryngoscope or direct laryngoscope per facility procedure.

Expected outcome: Patient care is not delayed by a nonfunctional display.

Verify the Reported Power Failure

Press the power button and observe the display.

Check whether the unit is completely blank, briefly flashes, shows a low battery indication, or powers off immediately.

Expected outcome: The failure mode is confirmed and documented.

Check Battery Installation

Open the battery compartment and verify the batteries are installed in the correct polarity.

Remove and reinstall the batteries, ensuring they seat firmly against the contacts.

Expected outcome: Display powers on normally if the issue was caused by loose or incorrectly installed batteries. If resolved, stop troubleshooting.

Replace with Known-Good Batteries

Install fresh, facility-approved batteries.

Do not rely on batteries removed from another unknown device.

Expected outcome: Display powers on if the original batteries were depleted, weak, or expired. If resolved, stop troubleshooting.

Inspect Battery Contacts

Check the battery contacts for corrosion, bent terminals, fluid residue, or visible contamination.

Do not scrape aggressively or use excessive force. Clean only according to facility-approved procedure.

Expected outcome: Contacts appear clean, intact, and able to maintain battery pressure.

Inspect the Battery Door and Housing

Verify the battery door closes securely and is not cracked, loose, or missing.

Inspect the display housing for impact damage, liquid intrusion, damaged seams, or evidence of being dropped.

Expected outcome: Display housing and battery compartment are physically intact.

Remove the Blade and Retest the Display

Disconnect the aBlade and attempt to power on the display by itself.

A damaged or improperly seated blade should not be allowed to confuse the initial power check.

Expected outcome: Display powers on without the blade if the attached blade was contributing to the issue. If resolved, replace the blade and retest.

Test with a Known-Good aBlade

Attach a known-good compatible aBlade and power the unit on again.

Confirm the blade locks in properly and does not place stress on the display connection.

Expected outcome: Display powers on and produces an image if the issue was related to the blade or blade connection. If resolved, remove the defective blade from use.

Check for Intermittent Power

With fresh batteries installed, gently move the display in normal handling positions.

Do not twist, flex, or stress the housing.

Watch for flickering, rebooting, or power loss.

Expected outcome: Display remains powered without interruption. Intermittent behavior suggests battery-contact, power-button, or internal connection failure.

Confirm Cleaning or Fluid Exposure History

Ask whether the device was recently cleaned, soaked, sprayed heavily, dropped, or stored with batteries installed.

Excess liquid or residue near the battery compartment can prevent reliable power-on.

Expected outcome: Any likely external exposure is identified and documented.

Do Not Attempt Internal Repair During Floor Troubleshooting

If the display remains blank after fresh batteries, battery contact inspection, blade removal, and known-good accessory testing, stop troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: The device is removed from clinical use rather than being repeatedly tested in patient-care areas.

If the Problem Persists

If the King Vision aBlade display still does not power on after batteries, contacts, battery door, housing condition, and blade-related causes have been checked, common external causes have been ruled out.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair, replacement evaluation, or bench inspection

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A video laryngoscope display that will not power on should not remain available for airway use.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a failed video laryngoscope during an active airway attempt. Provide another working airway device first, then evaluate the failed unit only when patient care is protected.

Maintain therapy continuity by moving patient care to a backup device before troubleshooting the failed unit.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported the Ambu King Vision aBlade display would not power on during airway setup."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Fresh batteries were installed and the battery contacts, battery door, housing, and blade connection were checked, but the display remained blank."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Device was removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation due to suspected internal display or power-control failure."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Fresh batteries installed
  • Battery polarity verified
  • Battery contacts inspected
  • Battery door condition checked
  • Display blank, flickering, or briefly powering on
  • aBlade removed and retested
  • Known-good aBlade tested
  • Any drop, cleaning, fluid exposure, or corrosion noted
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A King Vision aBlade display that does not power on must be handled with patient safety first. Start with batteries, contacts, housing condition, and blade-related checks before suspecting an internal fault. Clear CCR documentation helps show that external causes were ruled out and that escalation was appropriate.

That is successful troubleshooting.
