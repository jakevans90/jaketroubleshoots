Siemens Healthineers Cios Alpha

Flat Detector Image Artifact or No Image

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

Siemens Healthineers

Model

Cios Alpha

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting flat detector image artifacts, poor image display, or no image conditions caused by setup, cabling, detector, or system communication issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

Do not troubleshoot imaging failure during an active procedure unless the clinical team confirms it is safe.

Action:

  • Confirm whether the Cios Alpha is actively being used on a patient.
  • If image quality is poor, artifacts are present, or no image is displayed during a procedure, notify the clinical team immediately.
  • If imaging is required for patient care, have staff move to another verified imaging system if available.
  • Do not repeatedly expose while troubleshooting unless clinical need and radiation safety practices are followed.

Expected outcome: Patient care continues safely without relying on unreliable imaging.

If this resolves the immediate clinical risk, stop and document the issue for follow-up.

2. Verify the Reported Symptom

Confirm whether the issue is truly with the flat detector image path.

Action:

  • Ask staff what they observed:
  • No live image
  • No stored image
  • Image artifacts
  • Lines or bands
  • Dark or washed-out image
  • Intermittent image dropout
  • Image visible on one monitor but not another
  • Confirm whether the system powers up normally and whether the monitor cart displays menus or system messages.

Expected outcome: The failure is narrowed to image acquisition, image display, detector communication, or monitor/display output.

3. Check for Obvious Physical Obstruction or Positioning Issues

Artifacts may be caused by something in the beam path rather than a detector failure.

Action:

  • Inspect the C-arm position and detector area.
  • Check for metal objects in the field.
  • Check for table hardware.
  • Check for patient positioning aids.
  • Check for cables crossing the imaging field.
  • Check for lead shielding.
  • Check for tools or surgical instruments.
  • Check for improper collimator positioning.

Expected outcome: The image field is clear of unintended objects.

If removing an object clears the artifact, return the unit to use after verifying image quality. Stop.

4. Confirm Proper System Startup

A flat detector or imaging chain issue may occur if the system did not initialize correctly.

Action:

  • Ask whether the issue appeared after startup, transport, reboot, or repositioning.
  • If clinically safe, power down the system using the normal shutdown process.
  • Allow the system to fully shut down.
  • Restart the system and allow it to complete initialization without moving the C-arm or detector.
  • Watch for startup errors, detector communication messages, or imaging warnings.

Expected outcome: The system completes startup without detector or imaging errors.

If rebooting restores normal imaging and no error returns, document the reset and return to service after functional verification. Stop.

5. Check Detector and C-Arm Physical Condition

The flat detector is a critical and expensive imaging component. Physical damage should be ruled out early.

Action:

  • Inspect the flat detector housing for cracks.
  • Inspect the flat detector housing for impact marks.
  • Inspect the flat detector housing for loose covers.
  • Inspect the flat detector housing for fluid intrusion.
  • Inspect the flat detector housing for damaged edges.
  • Inspect the flat detector housing for signs of collision.
  • Inspect the flat detector housing for unusual heat.
  • Inspect the flat detector housing for burning smell.
  • Check whether the detector was recently struck, bumped, or forced into position.

Expected outcome: No visible damage or unsafe condition is found.

If damage, fluid intrusion, unusual heat, or burning smell is present, remove the system from service immediately. Stop.

6. Verify Cable Connections Between the C-Arm and Monitor Cart

No image or intermittent image may be caused by loose, damaged, or poorly seated system cables.

Action:

  • Inspect external cables between the C-arm stand and monitor cart.
  • Confirm connectors are fully seated and locked if applicable.
  • Look for bent pins.
  • Look for loose strain relief.
  • Look for damaged cable jacket.
  • Look for crushed cable sections.
  • Look for tension on connectors.
  • Look for cables caught under wheels.
  • Do not force connectors.

Expected outcome: All external image/system cables are secure and undamaged.

If reseating a loose external cable restores the image, perform a test exposure or image check according to site practice. Stop.

7. Check Monitor Cart Display Function

A “no image” complaint may be a display issue rather than a detector issue.

Action:

  • Confirm the monitor cart is powered on.
  • Check whether system menus, patient/exam information, stored images, or status screens are visible.
  • Determine whether both monitors are blank.
  • Determine whether only one monitor is blank.
  • Determine whether menus appear but live image does not.
  • Determine whether live image appears but stored images do not.
  • Adjust brightness only if appropriate and verify the display is not simply dimmed.

Expected outcome: Display failure is separated from detector or acquisition failure.

If one display is failed but the system is otherwise imaging normally, remove from service or limit use according to site policy and repair the display issue.

8. Check Exposure Readiness and Imaging Mode

The system may appear to have no image if it is not ready to expose or is in an unexpected imaging mode.

Action:

  • Confirm the system is not in standby, service mode, review-only mode, or another non-imaging state.
  • Verify the selected imaging mode is appropriate for the clinical use.
  • Check whether the footswitch or hand switch is being recognized.
  • Confirm the system gives normal exposure-ready indications.

Expected outcome: The system is ready for imaging and responds normally to exposure controls.

If the issue is caused by an incorrect mode or standby condition, correct the setting and verify imaging. Stop.

9. Inspect Footswitch or Hand Switch Operation

If no image is produced, the system may not be receiving an exposure command.

Action:

  • Check whether the footswitch or hand switch is connected properly.
  • Inspect the cable and connector for damage.
  • Try an alternate approved exposure switch if available and allowed by site practice.
  • Confirm whether the system responds when the switch is activated.

Expected outcome: Exposure controls are confirmed functional or ruled out as the cause.

If a faulty switch is found, remove the switch from service and replace it with a known-good approved accessory. Stop.

10. Check for System Error Messages

Detector communication or image processing errors may appear on the monitor cart.

Action:

  • Review any active system messages, warning icons, or error codes.
  • Look for messages related to flat detector.
  • Look for messages related to image acquisition.
  • Look for messages related to image processing.
  • Look for messages related to generator readiness.
  • Look for messages related to monitor cart communication.
  • Look for messages related to system cable connection.
  • Look for messages related to temperature.
  • Look for messages related to calibration or correction failure.
  • Record the exact wording of any error message.

Expected outcome: Any system-reported fault is captured accurately for repair documentation.

If a specific detector, generator, or image processing error remains active after basic checks, remove the system from service.

11. Compare Live Image Versus Stored Image Behavior

This helps narrow the issue between acquisition, processing, and display.

Action:

  • If safe and appropriate, review previously stored images.
  • Compare whether stored images display normally, but live image is missing.
  • Compare whether live image appears, but saved images are corrupted.
  • Compare whether both live and stored images show artifacts.
  • Compare whether only new images show artifacts.

Expected outcome: The issue is narrowed to display, acquisition, storage, or image processing.

If only new images are affected, suspect the current acquisition chain, detector, calibration/correction, or processing path.

12. Check for Environmental or Handling Causes

Some image artifacts may follow transport, collision, temperature change, or detector stress.

Action:

  • Ask whether the unit was recently moved between rooms, bumped, cleaned, exposed to fluids, or stored in an unusual environment.
  • Check that the system has been allowed to stabilize if it was moved from a cold or hot area.
  • Confirm the detector area is clean and dry.
  • Do not apply cleaners directly to seams, detector surfaces, connectors, or vents.

Expected outcome: Obvious environmental or handling causes are identified or ruled out.

13. Perform a Basic Functional Image Check

Only perform this when it is safe and allowed by facility policy.

Action:

  • Use an approved test object or site-accepted imaging check process.
  • Acquire a test image.
  • Look for uniform image field.
  • Look for no fixed lines or bands.
  • Look for no blank areas.
  • Look for no repeated artifacts.
  • Look for no image freezing.
  • Look for no delayed image update.
  • Look for normal storage or review function.

Expected outcome: The system produces a stable, usable image without artifacts.

If the test image is normal and no errors remain, document findings and return the unit to service. Stop.

14. Do Not Attempt Internal Detector or Imaging Chain Repair at Point of Use

The flat detector, generator, image processor, and internal communication assemblies require proper service procedures.

Action:

  • Do not open detector housings.
  • Do not bypass interlocks.
  • Do not attempt internal board-level repair in the clinical area.
  • Do not continue using the system if artifacts could affect clinical interpretation.

Expected outcome: The system is handled safely and escalated appropriately.

If the Problem Persists

If external causes have been ruled out and the Cios Alpha still has flat detector artifacts, intermittent image loss, or no image, the issue is likely internal to the detector, image processing chain, generator interface, system cabling, or display communication path.

The device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench/vendor evaluation.

Do not return the C-arm to clinical use until image quality and system operation are verified. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting, especially with imaging equipment where artifacts can directly affect clinical decisions.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot image artifacts on an active patient unless the clinical team confirms it is safe. If imaging is needed for the case, move to another verified C-arm or imaging system first. Poor image quality is not just an inconvenience; it can affect procedure accuracy and patient safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported that the Siemens Cios Alpha had a flat detector image artifact and intermittently produced no live image during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External inspection found the monitor cart communication cable was not fully seated after transport, causing intermittent image dropout."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated and secured the external system cable, restarted the Cios Alpha, verified normal startup, acquired a test image, and confirmed no artifacts or image loss."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior, exact error message, or warning displayed
  • Accessories swapped, including footswitch, hand switch, monitor cable, or approved external cable
  • Power behavior, including whether the system powered on normally or failed startup
  • Environmental factors, including whether the issue followed transport, cleaning, collision, cold storage, heat exposure, or fluid exposure
  • Indicator lights, monitor cart display behavior, startup warnings, detector warnings, or communication status messages
  • Final device status: returned to service or removed from service

Final Thought

Flat detector image issues should be approached carefully and logically. Start with patient safety, verify the symptom, check simple external causes, and avoid assuming detector failure too early. At the same time, do not keep a C-arm in service if image quality is unreliable. Clear CCR documentation helps repair teams understand exactly what was checked and why the unit was escalated.

That is successful troubleshooting.
