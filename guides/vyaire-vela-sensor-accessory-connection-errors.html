Vyaire VELA Ventilator

Sensor or Accessory Connection Errors

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Vyaire

Model

VELA

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting sensor or accessory connection errors on a Vyaire VELA ventilator. Symptoms may include alarms such as “sensor not detected,” “accessory disconnected,” unusual readings from flow or pressure sensors, or failure of the ventilator to recognize attached modules like temperature probes, flow sensors, or external monitoring accessories. The focus is on external checks and connection verification before assuming internal device faults.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify device power

  • Ensure the ventilator is powered on and battery is sufficiently charged.
  • Reason: Sensors may fail to initialize if the device is underpowered.

Inspect all accessory and sensor connections

  • Check that connectors for flow sensors, temperature probes, and any auxiliary modules are fully seated.
  • Look for bent pins, dirt, or debris.
  • Reason: Poor connections are the most common cause of sensor detection errors.

Check accessory compatibility

  • Confirm that connected accessories are approved for use with VELA.
  • Reason: Non-compatible sensors may trigger connection errors or fail to communicate properly.

Examine cables and tubing for damage

  • Look for cracks, fraying, or loose ends.
  • Reason: Damaged wiring or tubing can prevent accurate sensor readings.

Swap sensors or accessories

  • If duplicates are available, temporarily swap the problematic accessory with a known good one.
  • Reason: Helps isolate whether the error is with the device or the sensor/accessory itself.

Check software/configuration

  • Ensure ventilator settings correctly correspond to connected sensors.
  • Reset the device if needed to force re-initialization of modules.

If the Problem Persists

After confirming that power, connections, cables, and configuration are correct, the error is likely due to internal device issues.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service.”
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation.

Note: Recognizing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt troubleshooting while the device is attached to an active patient.
  • Move the patient to another ventilator if needed before performing checks.
  • Ensure all checks are performed safely, minimizing patient risk.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Sensor not detected” alarm appears when connecting flow sensor.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Flow sensor connector not fully seated; pins slightly misaligned.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Re-seated the flow sensor, verified proper detection, and cleared alarm.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Accessory cables inspected/swapped
  • Alarm behavior documented
  • Indicator lights noted
  • Any unusual sounds or heating
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting requires logic, patient safety awareness, and knowing when to escalate. Documenting findings clearly using CCR ensures continuity of care and proper device management. Always start with external checks before assuming internal failure.

That is successful troubleshooting.
