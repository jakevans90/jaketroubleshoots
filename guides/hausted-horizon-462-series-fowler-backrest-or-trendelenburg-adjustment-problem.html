Hausted Horizon 462 Series

Fowler/backrest or Trendelenburg adjustment problem

Asset Type

Stretcher

Manufacturer

Hausted

Model

Horizon 462 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting backrest, Fowler, Trendelenburg, or positioning problems caused by locks, handles, linkages, damage, obstruction, or mechanical failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Remove the stretcher from patient use before troubleshooting any positioning problem. If the stretcher is occupied, transfer the patient to another safe surface before testing adjustments.

Expected outcome: The stretcher can be inspected without risk of sudden movement, collapse, or patient injury.

If this resolves the concern: Stop and document that the device was not tested during active patient use.

Verify the Reported Positioning Problem

Confirm whether the issue involves the Fowler/backrest, Trendelenburg, reverse Trendelenburg, or more than one positioning function. Operate the control or release handle normally and observe what does or does not move.

Expected outcome: The exact failed function is identified.

Why it matters: A single-position issue usually points toward a localized handle, linkage, pivot, gas spring, or mechanical support problem.

Inspect for Obvious Physical Damage

Look for bent frame sections, cracked brackets, loose hardware, damaged handles, missing fasteners, broken covers, or signs of impact around the backrest and Trendelenburg mechanism.

Expected outcome: No visible damage should prevent movement or safe locking.

If damage is found: Remove the stretcher from service and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Check for Obstructions Around the Moving Sections

Inspect under and around the litter top, backrest hinge area, Trendelenburg pivot area, side rails, mattress, straps, linens, accessory mounts, and stored items. Remove anything interfering with movement.

Expected outcome: The backrest and litter section move freely without binding.

If this resolves the issue: Stop and document the obstruction removed.

Confirm the Release Handle or Control Moves Normally

Operate the Fowler/backrest or Trendelenburg release handle while observing whether the handle feels loose, jammed, disconnected, or unusually stiff. Do not force the handle.

Expected outcome: The handle should move smoothly and produce a clear mechanical response.

If the handle is damaged, loose, or nonresponsive: Remove the stretcher from service for repair.

Check Whether the Mechanism Locks and Releases Properly

Raise and lower the affected section through its normal range only if safe to do so. Confirm that the position holds securely after the handle is released.

Expected outcome: The section should move smoothly and lock without drifting, dropping, or slipping.

If it will not hold position: Remove from service immediately. A stretcher that cannot hold position is a patient safety risk.

Inspect the Backrest Support Area

For Fowler/backrest issues, inspect the hinge points, backrest frame, support arms, release cable or rod area, and visible mounting points. Look for bent hardware, missing pins, loose fasteners, or asymmetric movement.

Expected outcome: Both sides of the backrest should move evenly and remain mechanically secure.

If one side binds or shifts differently: Remove from service for repair.

Inspect Trendelenburg Movement and Stability

For Trendelenburg or reverse Trendelenburg complaints, verify that the litter top tilts evenly and that the release mechanism fully engages after positioning. Check for frame interference, uneven motion, or failure to lock.

Expected outcome: The stretcher should tilt smoothly and remain stable after adjustment.

If the litter top will not lock, tilts unevenly, or drops: Remove from service.

Check for Loose or Missing External Hardware

Inspect accessible bolts, knobs, pins, and brackets related to the affected positioning mechanism. Do not disassemble internal lift or locking components beyond normal external inspection.

Expected outcome: No loose or missing hardware should be present.

If hardware is missing or structural parts are loose: Tag the stretcher Out of Service.

Listen and Feel for Binding, Grinding, or Sudden Release

Slowly operate the adjustment while listening for grinding, popping, scraping, or sudden movement. Stop immediately if the stretcher drops, catches, or releases unexpectedly.

Expected outcome: Movement should be controlled and predictable.

If abnormal movement or noise is present: Remove from service for repair.

Perform a Safe Final Function Check

After any simple correction, such as removing an obstruction or repositioning accessories, cycle the affected function several times without a patient. Confirm smooth movement, full locking, and stable support.

Expected outcome: The backrest or Trendelenburg function operates normally and holds position securely.

If the issue is resolved: Return to service only if the stretcher is stable, safe, and fully functional.

If the Problem Persists

If the Fowler/backrest or Trendelenburg adjustment still does not move, does not lock, drifts, drops, binds, or feels mechanically unsafe, common external causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely internal to the release mechanism, linkage, gas spring/support assembly, locking mechanism, pivot hardware, or frame structure.

The stretcher should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not keep forcing a mechanical positioning system that may suddenly release or fail under patient weight.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot stretcher positioning problems with a patient on the stretcher unless there is an immediate safety need and adequate clinical support is present. A backrest or Trendelenburg mechanism that will not hold position can create a fall, pinch, entrapment, or sudden movement hazard. Move the patient to a backup stretcher or safe surface first so patient care and positioning support continue without relying on the failed device.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the Hausted Horizon 462 Series stretcher backrest would not raise and hold position during setup."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Inspection found the Fowler release handle moved, but the backrest support would not lock securely after adjustment."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Stretcher was removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation of the backrest locking mechanism."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Affected function: Fowler/backrest, Trendelenburg, reverse Trendelenburg, or multiple functions
  • Whether the section moves at all
  • Whether the section locks and holds position
  • Any drifting, dropping, binding, or uneven movement
  • Release handle condition
  • Visible bent brackets, loose hardware, or missing fasteners
  • Obstructions removed, if any
  • Unusual noise, sudden release, or rough movement
  • Alarm behavior, if any powered accessories or indicators are involved
  • Accessories swapped or repositioned, including mattress, straps, linens, or accessory mounts
  • Power behavior, if any powered positioning feature or accessory is involved
  • Environmental factors, including floor condition, room setup, or storage damage
  • Indicator lights, if any powered accessory or control indicator is present
  • Final device status: returned to service or removed from service

Final Thought

Stretcher positioning problems should be handled carefully because the failure may only appear when weight is applied or when the mechanism is moved through its range. Start with patient safety, verify the exact failed function, check for external obstruction or damage, and escalate when the stretcher cannot lock or support position reliably. Clear CCR documentation helps show what was reported, what was found, and why the device was removed from service or returned safely.

That is successful troubleshooting.
