ZOLL X Series Defibrillator

ECG Display Errors or Artifacts

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

ZOLL

Model

X Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting ECG display errors or artifacts on a ZOLL X Series defibrillator. Symptoms may include erratic waveforms, intermittent signal loss, baseline wandering, or distorted ECG tracings. The focus is on external checks and proper setup before assuming internal device issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Move the patient to an alternative monitored device if available.
  • Do not troubleshoot on an actively deteriorating patient.

Check ECG Electrode Placement and Skin Contact

  • Verify electrodes are correctly placed and oriented according to standard 3- or 5-lead configuration.
  • Ensure skin is clean, dry, and free of lotions or oils.
  • Replace electrodes if they are old, dried out, or visibly defective.

Inspect ECG Lead Wires and Connectors

  • Look for frayed wires, broken insulation, bent or corroded connectors.
  • Confirm connectors are fully seated in the defibrillator ports.
  • Swap with a known-good lead set to determine if the issue persists.

Check Device and Patient Interface

  • Ensure the ECG leads are not touching metal surfaces or IV lines, which can introduce interference.
  • Avoid running long lead wires alongside other electrical equipment that could cause noise.

Verify Device Settings and Filters

  • Confirm ECG gain, filter settings, and lead selection match intended monitoring setup.
  • Reset to default settings if configuration is unclear or corrupted.

Power Cycle the Defibrillator

  • Turn off the device, wait 10–20 seconds, then power it back on.
  • Observe if the ECG signal stabilizes after reboot.

If the Problem Persists

External causes such as electrode contact, lead issues, or interference have been ruled out.

Device may have internal ECG module failure or display electronics malfunction.

  • Remove the defibrillator from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service.”
  • Send for manufacturer repair or bench evaluation.
  • Avoid further troubleshooting beyond external checks to prevent accidental patient risk or device damage.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt invasive troubleshooting while the patient is attached.
  • Always maintain continuous ECG monitoring via an alternative device while troubleshooting.
  • Keep spare electrodes and lead sets readily available to minimize downtime.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“ECG waveform is erratic and shows intermittent artifacts.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Electrode contact poor and leads suspected of damage; artifact persists despite proper placement.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Replaced electrodes and ECG lead set; device rebooted. If issue continues, removed device from service for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Lead set swapped? Yes/No
  • Electrode age and type
  • Any observed interference (IV pumps, other electrical devices)
  • ECG waveform snapshot if available
  • Device final status (e.g., returned to service or labeled out of service)

Final Thought

Patient safety and logical troubleshooting take priority over guessing internal faults. By systematically checking electrodes, leads, and settings first, Clinical Engineering ensures accurate ECG monitoring and appropriate escalation when needed. Clear documentation preserves device history and supports safety protocols.

That is successful troubleshooting.
