Mindray WATO EX Series

Gas or Agent Delivery Errors

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

WATO EX Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Mindray WATO EX Series anesthesia machine:

  • Shows alarms related to gas or anesthetic agent delivery (e.g., low flow, agent not detected, unexpected concentrations)
  • Fails to deliver set gas mixtures or volatile agents accurately
  • Displays inconsistent readings on the agent monitor

The focus is on external, easily verifiable causes first, before assuming internal component failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power and Startup Sequence

  • Ensure the machine is plugged into a properly grounded outlet and powered on.
  • Observe the startup self-test; note any errors related to gas delivery.
  • Why: Some delivery errors occur if the machine did not complete self-tests correctly.

Check Gas Supply Lines

  • Inspect O₂, N₂O, and Air pipelines for pressure and flow.
  • Confirm cylinders are open, regulators are set correctly, and pipeline pressures are within normal range.
  • Why: Low or no supply pressure can trigger gas delivery alarms.

Inspect Flowmeters and Vaporizers

  • Ensure vaporizers are seated properly and filled with the correct agent.
  • Check for leaks at vaporizer mounts or interlocks.
  • Verify flowmeter readings respond correctly when adjusting flows.
  • Why: Misaligned vaporizers or empty reservoirs can prevent accurate agent delivery.

Examine Breathing Circuit and Connectors

  • Look for disconnections, leaks, or kinks in the circuit tubing.
  • Confirm that the CO₂ absorber is installed correctly and not bypassed.
  • Why: Circuit leaks can mimic gas delivery errors.

Review Alarm and Monitoring Settings

  • Confirm agent and gas concentration alarms are set within normal limits.
  • Reset alarms and observe if error persists.
  • Why: Incorrect alarm limits can give false error readings.

Test With a Simulated Lung (if available)

  • Run a test ventilation with a test lung using O₂ and air only.
  • Observe delivered volumes and agent readings without a patient.
  • Why: Helps determine if error is patient-specific or machine-related.

If the Problem Persists

All external checks (power, supply, circuit, vaporizers) have been verified.

Device may have internal issues such as flow sensor failure, electronic controller malfunction, or vaporizer interface fault.

  • Remove the machine from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send to the Biomedical Engineering bench or authorized repair center.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt gas delivery troubleshooting on a patient—always switch to a backup machine first.

Ensure oxygen supply is available and the patient is safely ventilated before any troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Machine is displaying low agent delivery alarm and O₂/N₂O concentrations are inconsistent.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Observed vaporizer not fully seated; gas supply pressures within normal range; flowmeters respond properly when tested.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Re-seated vaporizer and verified agent fill. Ran test lung simulation. Error resolved. Machine returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Cylinder or pipeline pressures
  • Vaporizer type and fill level
  • Flowmeter readings
  • Alarm codes displayed
  • Circuit check results
  • Test lung simulation results

Final Thought

Systematic, logic-based troubleshooting protects patient safety, minimizes unnecessary downtime, and ensures proper escalation when internal issues exist. Detailed documentation allows future reference and accountability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
