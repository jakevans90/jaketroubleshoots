GE Healthcare Panda Warmer

Alarm: Confirm Probe Jack Connection

Asset Type

Infant Warmer

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Panda Warmer

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting the Confirm Probe Jack Connection alarm when patient temperature monitoring may be unavailable or unreliable due to a patient probe connection problem, a cold probe condition, or a defective probe.

This alarm may prevent dependable patient temperature feedback and may affect safe use of patient-temperature-controlled warming modes.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the warmer is being used with an infant and the patient temperature probe connection cannot be confirmed, notify clinical staff immediately.
  • Do not rely on servo-controlled warming or displayed patient temperature while the alarm remains unresolved.
  • Move the infant to an approved backup warming device or alternate temperature monitoring method according to clinical protocol before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: The infant is no longer dependent on unreliable temperature feedback or warming control during evaluation.

Why it matters: An improperly connected or defective patient probe can prevent accurate patient temperature sensing and may affect safe warmer operation.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • With the warmer removed from patient use as appropriate, confirm that the display shows Confirm Probe Jack Connection.
  • Observe whether the alarm occurs at startup, immediately after the probe is connected, when selecting a patient-temperature-controlled mode, or intermittently when the cable is moved.
  • Confirm whether patient temperature is displayed normally, missing, unstable, or inconsistent with expected conditions.

Expected outcome: The reported alarm and associated temperature-monitoring behavior are clearly identified.

Why it matters: Confirming the condition helps distinguish a connection alarm from an incorrect setup or an isolated user report.

3. Inspect and Reseat the Patient Probe Connection

  • Inspect the patient probe connector, cable, and probe jack for incomplete insertion, visible damage, looseness, contamination, moisture, debris, pinching, stretching, or cuts.
  • Disconnect and firmly reseat the compatible patient probe using normal connection practice.
  • Retest the warmer and observe whether the alarm clears and the patient temperature indication becomes stable.

Expected outcome: The probe seats securely, the alarm clears, and temperature indication is stable.

Why it matters: A partially seated or damaged probe connection is a common external cause of this alarm.

If resolved: Verify normal operation and accurate temperature indication using an approved test method before returning the warmer to service.

4. Consider a Cold Patient Probe Condition

  • Determine whether the patient probe may have been stored in a cold location or exposed to a low-temperature environment.
  • If the probe may be below 24°C, allow it to warm to room temperature.
  • Reconnect the warmed probe securely and retest the warmer.

Expected outcome: Once the probe is above the low-temperature threshold and properly connected, the alarm no longer appears.

Why it matters: The warmer may prompt for probe connection confirmation when a connected probe is sufficiently cold.

If resolved: Document the cold probe condition, confirm normal operation, and return the warmer to service according to facility procedure.

5. Substitute a Known-Good Patient Probe

  • If the connection is secure and the probe is at an appropriate temperature, connect a compatible known-good patient probe.
  • Repeat the operating check and observe whether the alarm clears.
  • Confirm that patient temperature information is stable with the replacement probe.

Expected outcome: The alarm clears and temperature indication remains stable when using the known-good probe.

Why it matters: A defective patient probe can produce the same alarm as a loose or incomplete connection.

If resolved: Replace the defective patient probe, complete functional verification, and return the warmer to service.

6. Inspect Accessible External Connection Conditions

  • Confirm that the probe jack area is clean, dry, undamaged, and free of strain from routing, bedding, accessories, or movement.
  • Confirm that the replacement probe remains fully seated during normal cable positioning.
  • Retest for intermittent alarm behavior by observing the connection during normal external handling only.

Expected outcome: No external condition is found that interrupts the probe connection or causes recurrence of the alarm.

Why it matters: Cable tension, contamination, or jack damage may cause an alarm that appears only during use or movement.

If the Problem Persists

If the Confirm Probe Jack Connection alarm remains after confirming proper probe seating, allowing a cold probe to warm, testing with a known-good patient probe, and ruling out accessible external connection problems, common external causes have been ruled out.

A persistent alarm may indicate an internal patient probe signal-path, harness, interface board, or control board condition that requires qualified bench evaluation.

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label the device Out of Service.
  • Send the warmer for repair or qualified bench evaluation according to facility procedure.
  • Do not return the warmer to patient use with unresolved patient probe alarm behavior.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm while an infant depends on the affected probe for temperature-controlled warming. Move the patient to a backup device or approved alternate monitoring and warming method first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"GE Healthcare Panda Warmer displayed Confirm Probe Jack Connection alarm during setup for infant warming. Staff reported patient temperature monitoring could not be confirmed."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Patient temperature probe was not fully seated in the probe jack. Alarm cleared after reseating the probe and confirming stable temperature indication."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed warmer from patient use during evaluation, reseated the patient probe, confirmed alarm cleared, completed functional verification, and returned the warmer to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The Confirm Probe Jack Connection alarm should be treated as a potential loss of reliable patient temperature feedback. Starting with patient safety, checking probe seating and temperature, substituting a known-good probe, and escalating persistent failures supports safe and logical troubleshooting. Clear CCR documentation records both the clinical concern and the final service decision.

That is successful troubleshooting.
