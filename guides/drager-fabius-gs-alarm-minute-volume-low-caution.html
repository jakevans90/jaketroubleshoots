Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: MINUTE VOLUME LOW !!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

MINUTE VOLUME LOW !! alarm indicating delivered ventilation volume is below the configured alarm limit, which may result in inadequate patient ventilation due to leaks, obstruction, incorrect settings, or flow sensor issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the anesthesia machine from patient use if ventilation is inadequate
  • Provide manual ventilation or switch to an alternate machine

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm MINUTE VOLUME LOW !! alarm is displayed
  • Observe when the alarm occurs (startup vs during ventilation)
  • Check tidal volume and minute volume readings
  • Look for signs of poor ventilation such as low bellows movement

3. Check Breathing Circuit for Leaks or Disconnection

  • Inspect all connections from machine to patient
  • Ensure fittings are tight and secure
  • Check reservoir bag and bellows for leaks

4. Check for Obstructions or Kinked Hoses

  • Inspect hoses for kinks or compression
  • Ensure filters and valves are not blocked

5. Verify Ventilator Settings

  • Confirm tidal volume and respiratory rate settings
  • Check PMAX (pressure limit) setting
  • Ensure alarm limits are appropriate

6. Assess Patient and Lung Compliance Factors

  • Consider reduced lung compliance
  • Compare airway pressure to delivered volume

7. Check Fresh Gas Flow and Supply

  • Confirm gas supply is active and adequate
  • Verify flow control valve settings

8. Check Flow Sensor Function and Calibration

  • Ensure sensor cable is properly connected
  • Perform flow sensor calibration
  • Replace sensor if calibration fails or readings are inaccurate

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes, settings, and sensor checks have been completed and the alarm continues:

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilation issues on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device before beginning troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Unit alarming MINUTE VOLUME LOW !! with reduced ventilation during case."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Leak identified in breathing circuit connection causing low delivered volume."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Re-secured circuit connections and verified proper ventilation. Alarm cleared and unit returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Low minute volume alarms directly impact patient ventilation and must be addressed immediately. Start with simple external causes, verify settings, and escalate when necessary.

That is successful troubleshooting.
