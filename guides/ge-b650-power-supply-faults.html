GE Healthcare CARESCAPE B650 Patient Monitor

Power Supply Faults

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE B650

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting power supply faults on the CARESCAPE B650. Symptoms may include the monitor failing to power on, unexpected shutdowns, power error messages, or intermittent operation. Most power issues are caused by external factors such as outlet problems, power cords, or battery status rather than internal power supply failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check the AC Power Source

  • Verify that the outlet is live by testing with another device.
  • Ensure no circuit breaker trips or GFCI interruptions.
  • Why: A dead outlet or tripped breaker is the most common cause of “power fault” alarms.

Inspect the Power Cord and Plug

  • Confirm the power cord is securely connected to both the monitor and wall outlet.
  • Look for frayed wires, bent pins, or visible damage.
  • Why: Damaged cords can cause intermittent or no power.

Test with an Alternate AC Outlet

  • Plug the monitor into a different, verified outlet to rule out outlet-specific issues.
  • Why: Confirms whether the issue is localized to one power source.

Check the Internal Backup Battery

  • Confirm battery status in the monitor menu or via alarm log.
  • Replace battery if it is depleted, swollen, or failing to hold a charge.
  • Why: A failing battery can trigger power fault warnings even when AC power is present.

Inspect Power Supply Connections

  • Ensure internal connectors (if accessible without disassembly) are fully seated and undamaged.
  • Why: Loose internal connections can cause intermittent faults.

Observe Indicator Lights and Alarms During Startup

  • Note any LED patterns or error codes displayed at power-on.
  • Document behaviors for repair or vendor support.
  • Why: Provides diagnostic clues if escalation is needed.

If the Problem Persists

  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send the unit for internal repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Engineering Principle: Knowing when to escalate prevents patient risk and avoids unnecessary troubleshooting attempts that could worsen the issue.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt to troubleshoot on a patient-connected monitor.
  • Move any patient monitoring to a functioning unit first.
  • Only perform troubleshooting on a safely powered, unconnected device.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor intermittently shuts down; power fault alarm displayed.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“AC outlet tested and verified; power cord undamaged; internal battery at 40% charge.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Monitor removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • AC outlet tested and voltage confirmed
  • Alternate power cords tried
  • Battery status and age
  • LED error codes during startup
  • Any unusual smells, heat, or noises during power-on

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount. Logical, stepwise troubleshooting ensures that external and easily verifiable causes are ruled out first. Proper escalation and documentation protect staff, patients, and equipment integrity.

That is successful troubleshooting.
