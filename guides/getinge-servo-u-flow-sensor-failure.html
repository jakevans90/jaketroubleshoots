Getinge Servo-u Ventilator

Expiratory Cassette Flow Sensor Failure or Calibration Errors

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Getinge

Model

Servo-u

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Getinge Servo-u ventilator displays:

  • Expiratory flow sensor failure messages
  • Cassette calibration errors
  • Failed pre-use checks related to expiratory measurements
  • Inaccurate tidal volume or flow readings

The focus is on external, logical checks first — cassette condition, moisture contamination, proper seating, and environmental factors — before assuming internal sensor or module failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Remove the Patient from the Ventilator (If Applicable)

  • Transition the patient to another ventilator immediately.
  • Do not troubleshoot while connected to a patient.
  • This protects patient safety and prevents ventilation interruption during troubleshooting.

2. Verify the Exact Alarm or Error Message

  • Document the specific message displayed:
    • “Expiratory cassette calibration failed”
    • “Flow sensor error”
    • “Expiratory measurement fault”
    • Failed system/pre-use check
  • Precise wording matters and should be included in the work order.

3. Inspect the Expiratory Cassette

  • Remove the cassette and visually inspect:
    • Cracks in housing
    • Warped plastic
    • Damaged seals or gaskets
    • Obvious contamination
  • Why this matters: The Servo-u uses an integrated expiratory cassette design. Improper sealing or damage directly affects flow measurement accuracy.
  • If physically damaged — replace the cassette.

4. Check for Moisture or Condensation

  • Inspect for:
    • Water pooling
    • Condensation inside the cassette
    • Secretions in the expiratory limb
  • Moisture is one of the most common causes of flow calibration errors.
  • If moisture is present:
    • Replace with a dry cassette
    • Replace or dry the expiratory limb
    • Confirm heated circuits (if used) are functioning properly
    • Do not attempt to open or internally dry the cassette.

5. Confirm Proper Cassette Seating

  • Reinsert the cassette carefully:
    • Ensure it clicks fully into place
    • Confirm latch engagement
    • Check for debris in the cassette slot
  • Even slight misalignment can cause calibration failures.

6. Run a New Pre-Use Check

  • After reseating or replacing the cassette:
    • Perform a full ventilator system check
    • Observe whether calibration completes successfully
  • If calibration now passes, the issue was likely moisture, seating, or cassette condition.

7. Try a Known-Good Cassette

  • If errors persist:
    • Install a known-good expiratory cassette
    • Run calibration again
  • If the new cassette resolves the issue:
    • The original cassette is faulty and should be removed from service.
  • If the new cassette also fails:
    • Suspect internal measurement module or interface issue.

8. Inspect Cassette Interface Area

  • Without disassembly:
    • Look inside the cassette compartment
    • Check for debris
    • Inspect electrical contact points (no cleaning with liquids)
    • Ensure no bent pins or obvious physical damage
  • If internal contact damage is suspected — escalate.

If the Problem Persists

If:

  • A known-good cassette fails calibration
  • Multiple cassettes produce the same error
  • Calibration repeatedly fails after proper seating

Then common external causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely internal to the ventilator’s measurement system or cassette interface.

At this point:

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Send for bench evaluation or authorized repair

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Avoid internal sensor or board-level disassembly.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot flow measurement issues while the ventilator is in use on a patient.
  • Flow inaccuracies can result in:
    • Incorrect tidal volume delivery
    • Improper alarm triggering
    • Unsafe ventilation
  • Always transition the patient to a safe alternative before removing the cassette or running calibration tests.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator failed pre-use check with “Expiratory cassette calibration failed” error. Unit unavailable for patient use.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Moisture observed inside expiratory cassette. Calibration failed repeatedly until cassette replaced.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced expiratory cassette with known-good unit. Pre-use check passed. Ventilator returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Exact alarm wording
  • Pre-use check results
  • Cassette condition
  • Moisture presence
  • Number of cassettes tested
  • Final operational status
  • Any unusual smells or heat
  • Whether issue occurred during patient use

Final Thought

Flow measurement errors on ventilators must be approached carefully and logically. Most issues involve cassette condition, moisture, or improper seating — not internal failure. Always rule out external causes first, protect the patient by removing the device from clinical use during troubleshooting, and escalate appropriately when internal faults are suspected. Clear documentation ensures continuity and regulatory compliance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
