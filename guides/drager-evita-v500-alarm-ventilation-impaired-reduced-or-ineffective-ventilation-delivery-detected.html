Drager Evita V500

Alarm: Ventilation impaired reduced or ineffective ventilation delivery detected

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses the “Ventilation impaired” alarm, indicating reduced or ineffective ventilation delivery. This condition may result in inadequate tidal volume or pressure support, directly impacting patient ventilation and safety.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess patient ventilation status
  • If ventilation is inadequate, remove from ventilator
  • Provide alternate ventilation (bag-valve mask or backup ventilator)

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm “Ventilation impaired” is displayed
  • Observe when the alarm occurs (startup, during use, intermittent)
  • Check for reduced tidal volume or pressure delivery
  • Note any accompanying alarms (flow, pressure, sensor-related)

3. Check Patient Condition

  • Observe chest rise and breathing effort
  • Assess for coughing, agitation, or airway obstruction
  • Verify airway tube placement and patency

4. Check Breathing Circuit for Leaks or Disconnections

  • Ensure all circuit connections are secure
  • Inspect for loose fittings or disconnections
  • Check for leaks around mask or ET tube

5. Inspect Circuit for Obstruction or Flow Restriction

  • Check for kinks or compressed tubing
  • Look for condensation or water buildup
  • Inspect filters and expiratory valve for blockage

6. Verify Sensor and Flow Measurement Components

  • Ensure flow sensors are properly connected
  • Check for moisture or contamination
  • Reseat removable sensor components if applicable

7. Review Ventilator Settings and System State

  • Confirm appropriate ventilation mode
  • Review tidal volume, pressure limits, and flow settings
  • Check for recent changes or special maneuvers

8. Perform System Test (SST) if Issue Persists

  • Run system self-test if available
  • Verify device passes test without errors

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and the alarm continues, this indicates a likely internal failure such as flow system, sensor, or control issue.

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label the device Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilation issues on an active patient. Always transfer the patient to a backup ventilator before performing equipment checks.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator displaying 'Ventilation impaired' alarm with reduced tidal volume during patient use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Leak identified in breathing circuit connection causing inadequate ventilation delivery."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Secured circuit connections and cleared obstruction. Alarm resolved and device returned to service. If unresolved, unit removed from service and sent for repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

“Ventilation impaired” is a high-impact alarm directly tied to the ventilator’s ability to support breathing. Always prioritize patient safety, verify external factors first, and escalate appropriately when system performance cannot be restored.

That is successful troubleshooting.
