Hausted Horizon 462 Series

Casters, Wheels, Bearings, Litter Top, or Accessory Mounts Damaged

Asset Type

Stretcher

Manufacturer

Hausted

Model

Horizon 462 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Damaged stretcher casters, wheels, bearings, litter top surfaces, side accessories, or mounting points affecting safe transport or patient support.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Remove the stretcher from patient use before inspection. If a patient is on the stretcher, transfer the patient to another safe stretcher or bed before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: The stretcher is empty, stable, and safe to inspect.

If this resolves the immediate safety concern, stop and document the action.

Confirm the Reported Damage

Ask staff what was noticed: difficult steering, wheel noise, loose accessory, damaged litter top, wobble, cracked component, or visible breakage. Inspect the area reported.

Expected outcome: The complaint is confirmed or narrowed to a specific component.

Why it matters: Stretcher complaints are often reported generally, but the issue may be isolated to one caster, mount, rail, mattress deck, or accessory.

Check for Obvious Unsafe Conditions

Inspect for cracked caster housings, missing hardware, bent brackets, sharp edges, broken accessory mounts, torn litter top surfaces, exposed padding, or loose parts.

Expected outcome: No sharp, loose, or structurally unsafe conditions are present.

If any unsafe condition is found, remove the stretcher from service and stop troubleshooting.

Inspect All Casters and Wheels

Roll the stretcher slowly and observe each wheel. Check for flat spots, embedded debris, cracked tires, missing tread, wobble, or dragging.

Expected outcome: All wheels rotate smoothly without binding, wobble, or abnormal noise.

If debris is the issue and can be safely removed, retest movement afterward. If normal operation returns, stop.

Check Caster Swivel Action

Turn the stretcher in multiple directions and observe whether each caster swivels freely. Compare all four corners.

Expected outcome: Casters swivel smoothly without sticking, grinding, or delayed movement.

Why it matters: A caster may roll forward but still fail during turning or tight transport movement.

Listen and Feel for Bearing Problems

Move the stretcher across a clean, flat surface. Listen for grinding, clicking, squealing, or rumbling. Feel for vibration through the frame.

Expected outcome: Movement is smooth and quiet with no excessive vibration.

If bearing noise or wheel wobble is present, the caster or wheel assembly likely requires replacement or repair.

Verify Brake and Steer Interaction

Confirm that the brake and steer functions are not partially engaged while checking wheel movement. Cycle the brake and steer pedals fully, then retest rolling.

Expected outcome: Poor rolling is not caused by a partially engaged brake or steer mechanism.

If movement improves after properly cycling the pedals, document and return to service only if all functions test normally.

Inspect the Litter Top Surface

Check the litter top, deck panels, mattress support area, and patient-contact surfaces for cracks, looseness, deformation, missing fasteners, fluid intrusion, or sharp edges.

Expected outcome: The litter top is secure, intact, cleanable, and safe for patient support.

If the litter top is cracked, unstable, or cannot be properly cleaned, remove the stretcher from service.

Check Accessory Mounts and Attachment Points

Inspect IV pole sockets, oxygen tank holders, side rail mounts, restraint points, accessory brackets, and any attached accessories for looseness, bending, stripped hardware, or damage.

Expected outcome: Accessories mount securely without wobble, slippage, or damaged attachment points.

If an accessory mount is damaged, do not allow use of that accessory until repaired or replaced.

Check for Missing or Loose External Hardware

Look for missing screws, loose knobs, damaged pins, broken caps, or unsecured covers around the caster assemblies, litter top, side rails, and accessory mounting areas.

Expected outcome: External hardware is present and secure.

If hardware is loose and can be safely tightened according to facility practice, retest the affected function. If damage remains, escalate.

Perform a Basic Transport Function Check

On a flat surface, roll the stretcher forward, backward, and through turns. Test braking, steering, and accessory stability after inspection.

Expected outcome: The stretcher rolls, turns, brakes, steers, and supports accessories safely.

If the stretcher passes all checks and no damage remains, document findings and return to service.

Do Not Ignore Repeat Damage

If the same caster, mount, or litter top area repeatedly fails, inspect for environmental causes such as rough thresholds, elevator gaps, overloading, wall impacts, or improper accessory use.

Expected outcome: Repeat damage is investigated instead of repeatedly replacing parts without addressing the cause.

If the Problem Persists

If damaged casters, wheels, bearings, litter top surfaces, or accessory mounts remain after external checks, common user-correctable causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely mechanical, structural, or component-level.

The stretcher must be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair, parts replacement, or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A stretcher with questionable mobility, patient-support surfaces, or accessory mounting integrity should not remain available for patient transport.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot stretcher damage while a patient is on the stretcher unless the issue creates an immediate transport safety concern. Move the patient to another safe surface first, then inspect the stretcher. Damaged wheels, unstable litter tops, or loose accessory mounts can become patient fall, transport, or equipment-drop hazards. Therapy and transport continuity should be maintained with a safe backup stretcher or bed before troubleshooting continues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported the Hausted Horizon 462 stretcher was difficult to steer and one front caster appeared loose during patient transport."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Inspection found the front caster wheel had excessive wobble and bearing noise, with no debris or brake engagement causing the issue."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the stretcher from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for caster assembly replacement and full transport function check."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior or staff-reported warning behavior, if any
  • Accessories swapped, removed, or inspected
  • Power behavior, if any powered accessories or powered functions are involved
  • Environmental factors such as rough thresholds, elevator gaps, wall impacts, overloading, or repeated transport route issues
  • Indicator lights or visible status indicators, if any powered accessories are involved
  • Which caster or wheel location was affected
  • Whether the wheel rolled, dragged, wobbled, or made noise
  • Brake and steer pedal behavior
  • Any visible caster, fork, bracket, or wheel damage
  • Litter top condition
  • Accessory mount or bracket involved
  • Missing or loose hardware found
  • Whether debris was removed
  • Whether the stretcher passed a final roll, steer, and brake check
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Stretcher troubleshooting should always start with patient safety and visible external damage. Casters, wheels, litter tops, and accessory mounts directly affect safe transport and patient support. If the issue cannot be corrected with basic external checks, remove the stretcher from service and document the findings clearly using the CCR method.

That is successful troubleshooting.
