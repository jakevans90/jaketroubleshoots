Hamilton T1 Ventilator

Battery Not Charging or Shortened Transport Runtime

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

T1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Hamilton T1 ventilator that is not charging properly or has reduced battery runtime during transport. These issues are often caused by external power problems, battery condition, or charging configuration before assuming internal battery or charging circuit failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot while the ventilator is in use on a patient
  • Move the patient to a stable, alternate ventilator before proceeding
  • Ensure therapy continuity at all times

Verify AC Power Source

  • Confirm the ventilator is plugged into a known working outlet
  • Test the outlet with another device
  • Check for switched outlets or tripped breakers
  • This ensures the device is receiving proper external power

Check Power Supply and Cord

  • Inspect the AC power cord and power supply brick
  • Look for fraying, bent prongs, or loose connections
  • Ensure the cord is fully seated in both the outlet and ventilator
  • Damaged or loose power components can prevent charging

Confirm Charging Indicator Status

  • Power on the ventilator and check for battery/charging indicators
  • Verify the device recognizes AC power
  • Look for charging icons or status messages
  • Absence of indicators may point to power supply or internal charging issues

Inspect Battery Installation

  • If removable batteries are used
  • Ensure batteries are properly seated and latched
  • Remove and reseat batteries to confirm good connection
  • Poor battery contact can prevent charging

Check for Multiple Battery Configuration Issues

  • If the unit has multiple batteries installed
  • Remove one battery at a time and test charging
  • Identify if one battery is faulty and affecting the system
  • A single bad battery can impact overall charging performance

Evaluate Battery Age and Condition

  • Review battery service history
  • Older batteries (typically >2–3 years) may have reduced capacity
  • Swollen or overheated batteries should be removed immediately
  • Battery degradation is a common cause of shortened runtime

Perform a Functional Runtime Check

  • Fully charge the ventilator, then run it on battery power
  • Observe how long it operates compared to expected runtime
  • Note any rapid drop in battery percentage
  • This helps confirm whether the issue is capacity-related

Check for Alarms or Error Messages

  • Review the screen for battery or power-related alarms
  • Low battery warnings
  • Charging faults
  • These can help identify whether the issue is external or internal

Test with Known Good Components (If Available)

  • Swap with a known good battery
  • Use a known good power supply
  • This helps isolate whether the issue is with the battery or charger

If the Problem Persists

If all external power sources, cables, and battery checks have been completed and the issue remains, the problem is likely internal (battery management system or charging circuitry).

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or further bench evaluation

Recognizing when the issue is beyond external troubleshooting is proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot battery or charging issues while the ventilator is in active use on a patient. Always transition the patient to a stable, alternate ventilator before testing battery performance or removing batteries.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator reported not charging and losing power quickly during patient transport.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Battery found to have reduced capacity and failed runtime test; charging confirmed functional with alternate battery.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced faulty battery and verified proper charging and expected runtime on AC and battery power.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified functional
  • Power cord and adapter condition
  • Charging indicator behavior
  • Battery age and condition
  • Runtime duration observed
  • Any alarms or error messages
  • Whether known good battery/power supply was tested
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Battery-related issues are often straightforward when approached logically—starting with power sources and working toward battery condition. Proper escalation prevents unnecessary downtime while ensuring patient safety. Clear documentation supports future troubleshooting and equipment lifecycle decisions.

That is successful troubleshooting.
