3M Ranger 245

Heater Unit Fails to Reach or Maintain Temperature

Asset Type

Fluid & Blood Warmer

Manufacturer

3M

Model

Ranger 245

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a 3M Ranger 245 fluid and blood warmer fails to reach the selected temperature or cannot maintain temperature during operation. The guide focuses on common external causes such as power supply issues, disposable set problems, airflow restrictions, or setup errors before assuming an internal heater failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify the device is powered on and fully initialized

  • Confirm the unit powers on normally and completes its startup sequence without error messages. If the device does not power on properly, the heater will not function.

Check the temperature setting on the control panel

  • Ensure the temperature is set correctly according to clinical requirements. An incorrect or lower temperature setting may appear as if the device is not heating adequately.

Confirm the correct disposable warming set is installed

  • Verify that the proper Ranger disposable warming set is installed and fully seated. Improperly installed or incompatible sets can affect heat transfer and fluid flow.

Inspect the disposable set routing through the heater channel

  • Ensure the tubing is routed correctly through the warming channel with no twists, pinches, or kinks. Improper routing can reduce heat transfer and cause temperature instability.

Check fluid flow rate

  • Extremely high flow rates may prevent the device from maintaining temperature. Verify the flow rate is within the expected operating range for the warming set.

Inspect the warming channel and air path

  • Look for debris, obstructions, or improperly seated components in the heater channel. Blocked airflow can reduce heating efficiency.

Verify the unit is not placed in a restricted ventilation area

  • The Ranger heater relies on internal airflow. Ensure vents are not blocked and the unit is not positioned against walls or equipment that restrict cooling airflow.

Check for alarms or warning indicators

  • Review any system messages related to temperature, airflow, or heater performance. Alarm conditions may indicate airflow restrictions or sensor issues.

Perform a power cycle

  • Turn the device off, wait approximately 30 seconds, and power it back on. This can reset minor sensor or control faults.

Test the device with a new disposable set if available

  • If possible, replace the warming set to rule out issues with tubing integrity or heat transfer surfaces.

If the Problem Persists

If the heater unit still cannot reach or maintain temperature after these checks, common external causes have been ruled out and the issue is likely internal.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for Clinical Engineering bench evaluation or authorized repair

Potential internal causes may include heater element failure, temperature sensor malfunction, or airflow system faults. These conditions should not be addressed in the clinical environment.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a fluid or blood warmer while it is actively connected to a patient. Always move the patient to another functioning warming system before investigating device issues to maintain patient temperature management and safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Staff reported the Ranger 245 warmer was running but fluids were not reaching the expected temperature during transfusion.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Inspection revealed the disposable warming set was improperly routed in the heating channel, reducing heat transfer efficiency.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Reinstalled the warming set correctly, verified proper routing through the heater channel, and confirmed normal temperature performance during operational testing.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested
  • Device powers on normally
  • Disposable set inspected or replaced
  • Tubing routing verified
  • Ventilation openings checked
  • Flow rate evaluated
  • Alarms or warnings observed
  • Final device temperature performance verified
  • Final device status (returned to service or removed)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting begins with simple, visible causes such as setup issues, disposables, and airflow conditions. By checking these factors first, Clinical Engineering can quickly resolve many heating complaints while ensuring patient safety. Recognizing when a failure likely involves internal components and removing the device from service is also an essential part of proper troubleshooting and equipment management.

That is successful troubleshooting.
