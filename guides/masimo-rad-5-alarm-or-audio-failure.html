Masimo Rad-5

Alarm or Audio Failure

Asset Type

Pulse Oximeter

Manufacturer

Masimo

Model

Rad-5

What This Guide Helps With

Loss of audible alarms, no sound during alerts, or low/inaudible tones affecting patient monitoring awareness and safety.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If alarms are not audible, remove the device from active patient monitoring
  • Transfer the patient to a functioning monitor

Expected: Patient is safely monitored on alternate equipment
Why it matters: Audible alarms are critical for detecting patient deterioration

Verify Alarm and Audio Behavior

  • Power on the device and observe behavior
  • Check for no sound during alarms
  • Check for no keypress tones
  • Check for low or distorted audio
  • Check for intermittent sound

Expected: Confirm complete or partial loss of audio
Why it matters: Helps determine if it’s a full audio failure or configuration issue

Check Volume Settings (Most Common Cause)

  • Access the device settings menu
  • Ensure alarm volume is turned up
  • Confirm device is not muted
  • Cycle through volume levels

Expected: Audio returns if settings were incorrect
Why it matters: Volume is frequently lowered or muted during use

Verify Alarm Limits Are Active

  • Ensure SpO2 and pulse rate limits are enabled
  • Trigger a test alarm if safe

Expected: Alarm should sound when limits are exceeded
Why it matters: No alarm condition results in no sound even if audio works

Perform a Power Cycle

  • Power off the device
  • Remove and reseat batteries if applicable
  • Restart the device

Expected: Temporary software glitches may clear
Why it matters: Resets audio driver and internal processes

Inspect Speaker Area

  • Inspect speaker grille for dirt or debris
  • Ensure nothing is covering the speaker
  • Listen closely for faint output

Expected: Clear speaker path allows proper sound output
Why it matters: Blocked speakers reduce or eliminate audible alarms

Check for Physical Damage or Liquid Exposure

  • Inspect the device housing
  • Look for cracks, impact damage, or fluid ingress
  • Note any unusual smells or corrosion

Expected: No visible damage
Why it matters: Internal speaker or audio circuitry may be compromised

Test with Known Alarm Condition

  • If safe, simulate a low SpO2 or disconnect sensor briefly

Expected: Alarm should trigger with audible tone
Why it matters: Confirms whether the device can generate sound under alarm conditions

If the Problem Persists

If volume settings, alarm configuration, and external factors have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal (speaker failure or audio circuit fault).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and wasted troubleshooting time.

Clinical Use Tip

Never rely on a device with failed audio alarms for patient monitoring. Always transfer the patient before troubleshooting to maintain continuous monitoring.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported no audible alarms on Rad-5 during patient monitoring."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Internal speaker failure confirmed after volume and alarm settings verified functional."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed device from service and sent to vendor for repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior observed
  • Volume settings verified
  • Alarm limits checked
  • Power cycle performed
  • Speaker inspected for blockage
  • Any visible damage or fluid exposure
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Audio alarms are a critical safety feature, not a convenience. Always start with simple configuration checks, but escalate quickly when alarms are not reliable. Proper documentation and timely removal from service protect both patients and staff.

That is successful troubleshooting.
