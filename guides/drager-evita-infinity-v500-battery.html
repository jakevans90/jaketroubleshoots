Drager Evita Infinity V500

Battery Module Failure or Charging Circuit Faults

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita Infinity V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Drager Evita Infinity V500 ventilator:

  • Displays battery module failure messages
  • Shows “battery not charging” alerts
  • Has significantly reduced battery runtime
  • Fails to operate on battery during transport

The focus is on logical, external troubleshooting steps before assuming internal battery or charger failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Confirm AC Power Availability

  • Ensure the ventilator is plugged into a known working outlet.
  • Verify outlet voltage with a portable meter if available.
  • Why: Battery will not charge if AC power is absent or unstable.

Inspect Battery Module

  • Remove the battery and visually inspect for damage, corrosion, or debris on contacts.
  • Re-seat the battery firmly, ensuring proper alignment.
  • Why: Poor connection can prevent charging or cause false failure messages.

Check Battery Module Indicators

  • Observe any LEDs or status indicators on the battery module.
  • Compare with normal behavior documented in the device’s quick reference.
  • Why: Indicators often reveal if the module is recognized or in error state.

Swap with Known-Good Battery

  • If available, replace with another V500 battery module.
  • Observe if charging and runtime normalize.
  • Why: Confirms whether issue is battery-specific or internal to the ventilator.

Check Ventilator Charging Circuit Status

  • Use the ventilator’s self-test or system information menu to confirm battery charging function.
  • Why: The device may report charging faults separately from the battery.

Inspect Cables and Connectors

  • Check the battery connector and ventilator port for dirt, bent pins, or wear.
  • Clean lightly with approved materials if needed.
  • Why: Electrical contact issues are a common cause of charging failure.

Power Cycle the Ventilator

  • Turn off the ventilator, wait 30 seconds, then power on.
  • Recheck battery charging behavior.
  • Why: Can clear temporary faults or error states.

If the Problem Persists

External causes have been ruled out; battery module or internal charging circuit is likely defective.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send to authorized service for bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never rely on a failing battery for patient transport. Move the patient to a ventilator with a verified functioning battery first. Troubleshoot only when patient safety is not compromised.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Battery not charging and low battery alarms displayed during transport.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Observed battery contacts clean and seated properly; ventilator self-test indicates charging fault; swapping battery module confirms persistent failure.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent to Biomedical Engineering for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • AC outlet tested with meter
  • Battery module swapped with known-good module
  • Battery indicator LEDs observed
  • Error messages recorded
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety depends on reliable battery operation during transport and power interruptions. Logical, stepwise troubleshooting ensures external causes are ruled out before escalating. Accurate documentation supports repair efficiency and accountability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
