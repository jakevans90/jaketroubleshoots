Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: EXP PORT LEAKAGE !!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

EXP PORT LEAKAGE !! alarm indicating unintended expiratory flow during inspiration, causing ventilation leak, inaccurate delivery, or inability to maintain airway pressure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the anesthesia machine from active patient use if ventilation is unstable or inaccurate
  • Transition the patient to alternate ventilation
  • Confirm stable ventilation on backup equipment

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the alarm EXP PORT LEAKAGE !! is present
  • Observe when the alarm occurs (startup vs during ventilation)
  • Check ventilation mode (Volume, Pressure, Pressure Support)
  • Look for signs of leak such as low tidal volume or inability to maintain pressure

Check Breathing Circuit for Leaks or Disconnections

  • Inspect the full breathing circuit from machine to patient
  • Ensure all connections are tight and secure
  • Check for disconnected hoses
  • Look for cracks, tears, or worn components

Check Expiratory Valve Assembly

  • Inspect the expiratory valve and valve disk
  • Remove and check for moisture, debris, or residue
  • Ensure valve disk is properly seated
  • Confirm smooth movement without sticking

Check Expiratory Control Line (Hose Line)

  • Inspect hose line for kinks, disconnections, or damage
  • Ensure proper routing and secure connections

Check Flow Sensor and Connections

  • Ensure flow sensor cable is securely connected
  • Inspect for visible damage or contamination
  • Verify proper placement in the breathing system

Perform Flow Sensor Calibration

  • Perform manufacturer-recommended calibration procedure
  • Ensure calibration completes successfully

Re-test the System

  • Power cycle the unit
  • Run system check or leak test if available
  • Observe for alarm recurrence during ventilation

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm continues after all external checks:

  • External and common causes have been ruled out
  • The issue likely indicates internal valve control failure or sensor/system fault
  • Remove device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot leak-related alarms on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device before performing any troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Unit displaying EXP PORT LEAKAGE !! alarm with inability to maintain airway pressure during ventilation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Leak traced to contaminated expiratory valve or no external issue found indicating possible internal failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleaned and reseated expiratory valve and verified connections, or removed from service and sent for repair when issue persisted."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Leak-related alarms should always be approached from external to internal causes. Proper inspection of circuits and valves combined with timely escalation ensures safe and effective troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
