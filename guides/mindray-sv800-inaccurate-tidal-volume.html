Mindray SV800 Ventilator

Inaccurate Tidal Volume Delivery

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

SV800

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Mindray SV800 ventilator:

  • Delivers tidal volumes different from the set values
  • Shows alarms related to volume discrepancies
  • Exhibits inconsistent or fluctuating inspiratory flow

The focus is on logical, external troubleshooting steps first before assuming internal hardware failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Patient Circuit Setup

  • Check that all tubing, Y-piece, and patient connectors are correctly assembled.
  • Ensure there are no kinks, obstructions, or leaks.

Why: Leaks or misassembled circuits can cause measured tidal volume to differ from set values.

Inspect the Breathing Circuit for Leaks

  • Pressurize the circuit in standby or test mode if available.
  • Listen for hissing sounds or use leak detection tools.
  • Replace any damaged or worn circuit components.

Check Humidifier and Filters

  • Remove or bypass the humidifier and heat/moisture exchanger (HME) temporarily to see if readings normalize.
  • Inspect filters for clogging or excessive moisture.

Why: Obstructions or moisture accumulation can reduce delivered tidal volume.

Verify Ventilator Settings

  • Ensure the correct mode (volume control, pressure control, etc.) is selected.
  • Confirm set tidal volume, inspiratory time, and PEEP match the intended prescription.

Examine Flow Sensor and Volume Measurement

  • Inspect the expiratory and inspiratory flow sensors for contamination, condensation, or misalignment.
  • Clean sensors according to hospital-approved protocols without disassembling internal boards.

Why: Sensor contamination can falsely report delivered tidal volume.

Check for Active Leaks in Endotracheal Tube or Tracheostomy

  • Verify cuff inflation is adequate.
  • Listen for audible leaks during manual ventilation or pressure checks.

Power Cycle and Self-Test

  • Safely power down the ventilator and restart.
  • Run built-in self-tests if available to verify sensor and circuit functionality.

Why: Software recalibration may resolve measurement discrepancies.

If the Problem Persists

After external checks and routine maintenance, persistent tidal volume inaccuracies likely indicate internal sensor failure, flow module malfunction, or software calibration issues.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send for bench evaluation or manufacturer repair.

Clinical Engineering best practice: Do not attempt board-level repair or sensor replacement without proper authorization.

Clinical Use Tip

Patient Safety First: Always ensure the patient is on a functioning backup ventilator or alternative support before troubleshooting.

Avoid adjusting tidal volumes experimentally on the patient to compensate for inaccurate readings.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Patient tidal volumes do not match set values; alarms indicate volume discrepancy.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Flow sensor contamination and minor circuit leak detected; all external tubing connections intact.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Circuit and sensors cleaned; ventilator restarted; if unresolved, device removed from service for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet and power source verified
  • Tubing connections and patient interface checked
  • Flow sensor inspection and cleaning
  • Alarm codes observed
  • Patient status during troubleshooting
  • Final device status (e.g., back in service or removed)

Final Thought

Logical, stepwise troubleshooting ensures patient safety and avoids unnecessary repairs. Starting with external, verifiable factors allows Clinical Engineering to efficiently identify true equipment failures while maintaining accurate documentation. Recognize when escalation to bench repair is appropriate.

That is successful troubleshooting.
