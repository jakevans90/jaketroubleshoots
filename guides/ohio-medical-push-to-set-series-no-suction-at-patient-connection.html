Ohio Medical Push-To-Set Series

No Suction At Patient Connection

Asset Type

Suction Regulator

Manufacturer

Ohio Medical

Model

Push-To-Set Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting no suction at the patient connection caused by wall vacuum, setup, tubing, collection canister, mode, or regulator issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Suction is a patient care support function. Do not troubleshoot on an active patient if suction is needed immediately.

Action:

  • Move clinical use to another working suction setup if suction is needed for patient care.
  • Use a known-good suction regulator, portable suction device, or alternate wall vacuum location as appropriate.

Expected outcome: Patient care continues with reliable suction available.

Why it matters: A failed suction setup can delay airway clearance, drainage, or procedural support.

If alternate suction resolves the immediate clinical need, continue troubleshooting the reported regulator off the patient connection.

Verify the Complaint

Action:

  • Connect suction tubing to the patient connection port and confirm the reported issue.
  • Check whether there is:
  • No suction at all
  • Weak suction
  • Intermittent suction
  • Suction at the wall but not at the patient tubing
  • Gauge movement with no suction output

Expected outcome: The exact failure behavior is confirmed.

Why it matters: “No suction” may be caused by the wall vacuum source, regulator setting, collection setup, tubing, or internal regulator failure.

If the device is actually producing suction normally, document findings and return it to service if safe.

Confirm Wall Vacuum Is Available

Action:

  • Verify the regulator is connected to the correct vacuum outlet.
  • Confirm the wall vacuum outlet is active by testing with a known-good suction regulator or vacuum test setup.
  • Check that the wall adapter is fully seated and locked into the outlet.

Expected outcome: The wall vacuum source is confirmed present and usable.

Why it matters: A suction regulator cannot create suction by itself. It controls available hospital vacuum.

If the wall outlet has no vacuum, stop troubleshooting the regulator and report the wall vacuum issue to Facilities or the appropriate department.

Check Regulator Mode and On/Off Position

Action:

  • Confirm the Push-To-Set regulator is turned on and set to the appropriate suction mode.
  • Verify the control knob or selector is not in the off position.
  • If equipped with intermittent or continuous settings, confirm the correct mode is selected for the test.

Expected outcome: The regulator is in a mode that should deliver suction at the patient connection.

Why it matters: A regulator in OFF, standby, or an incorrect mode may appear failed even when the device is working normally.

If suction returns after correcting the mode or on/off position, stop and document the user setup issue.

Verify Suction Level Setting

Action:

  • Increase the suction setting while observing the gauge.
  • Confirm the gauge responds as the setting is adjusted.
  • Occlude the patient connection briefly during testing to confirm regulated vacuum is being generated.

Expected outcome: The gauge should show vacuum when the patient connection is occluded and the regulator is active.

Why it matters: Some regulators may show little or no vacuum unless the patient side is partially or fully occluded during testing.

If the regulator works normally when adjusted and occluded, document that the unit passed functional check.

Inspect Tubing and Patient Connection

Action:

  • Check the suction tubing connected to the patient port.
  • Look for:
  • Kinked tubing
  • Collapsed tubing
  • Loose tubing connection
  • Cracked tubing
  • Wrong tubing connected to the wrong port
  • Blockage in the tubing
  • Replace the tubing with known-good suction tubing.

Expected outcome: Suction reaches the end of the patient tubing.

Why it matters: Tubing problems are one of the most common causes of no suction at the patient connection.

If replacing the tubing restores suction, stop and document the defective or misconnected tubing.

Check Collection Canister Setup

Action:

  • Inspect the suction canister and lid setup.
  • Confirm:
  • Lid is fully seated
  • Patient and vacuum ports are connected correctly
  • Canister is not overfilled
  • Float shutoff is not activated
  • Filter is not blocked
  • Disposable liner is installed correctly, if used
  • Replace the canister, liner, lid, or filter with known-good components if needed.

Expected outcome: Suction passes through the canister setup to the patient connection.

Why it matters: An activated float shutoff, blocked filter, loose lid, or reversed tubing can completely block suction.

If suction returns after correcting the canister setup, stop and document the accessory/setup issue.

Test Regulator Without the Patient-Side Accessories

Action:

  • Disconnect the canister and patient tubing from the regulator.
  • Test vacuum directly at the regulator outlet using appropriate test tubing or a vacuum gauge setup.

Expected outcome: The regulator should provide vacuum directly at the outlet when connected to active wall vacuum and turned on.

Why it matters: This separates a regulator problem from a downstream canister, tubing, liner, or filter problem.

If the regulator works directly, the problem is downstream of the regulator.

Inspect Regulator Body and Connections

Action:

  • Visually inspect the regulator for:
  • Cracked housing
  • Loose fittings
  • Damaged patient connection
  • Damaged wall adapter
  • Missing or loose knobs
  • Fluid contamination
  • Physical impact damage
  • Unusual noise or air leak

Expected outcome: No visible damage, contamination, or loose connection is found.

Why it matters: Damage or fluid intrusion can affect regulator performance and may make the device unsafe for use.

If physical damage or contamination is found, remove the regulator from service.

Compare With a Known-Good Regulator

Action:

  • Install a known-good suction regulator on the same wall vacuum outlet.
  • Use the same suction setup if appropriate, or known-good tubing and canister parts.
  • Then install the suspected Ohio Medical Push-To-Set regulator on a known-good wall outlet.

Expected outcome: The failure follows either the regulator, the wall outlet, or the accessory setup.

Why it matters: Comparison testing helps avoid incorrectly replacing a regulator when the problem is actually the wall outlet or suction accessories.

If the failure follows the regulator, remove it from service for bench evaluation or repair.

If the Problem Persists

If wall vacuum, tubing, canister setup, filters, mode selection, and external connections have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal to the suction regulator.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Do not disassemble the regulator in a patient care area. Internal regulator faults, contaminated components, damaged seals, gauge problems, or valve issues should be handled through proper shop repair procedures or vendor service.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a suction failure while a patient depends on that exact suction setup. Have staff move to a working suction regulator, alternate wall vacuum source, or portable suction unit first. Once patient care is covered, troubleshoot the regulator and accessories safely.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported no suction from an Ohio Medical Push-To-Set suction regulator at the patient connection."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Wall vacuum was present, but the suction canister filter was occluded and prevented suction from reaching the patient tubing."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced the blocked filter and verified suction output at the patient connection with the regulator connected to active wall vacuum."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Wall vacuum outlet tested
  • Known-good regulator tested on same outlet
  • Suspected regulator tested on known-good outlet
  • Tubing swapped
  • Canister, lid, liner, and filter inspected
  • Float shutoff status checked
  • Gauge response noted
  • Mode or setting verified
  • Any visible damage, contamination, or loose fittings
  • Final device status: returned to service, removed from service, or sent for repair

Final Thought

No suction at the patient connection should be approached logically from the outside in. Confirm patient safety first, then check the vacuum source, regulator settings, tubing, canister, filter, and connections before suspecting an internal regulator problem. Clear CCR documentation helps show what was checked, what was found, and why the final action was appropriate.

That is successful troubleshooting.
