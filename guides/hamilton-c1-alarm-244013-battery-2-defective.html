Hamilton C1

Alarm: 244013 Battery 2: Defective

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm: 244013 Battery 2: Defective on a Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates Battery 2 has failed, is not usable for backup power, or cannot be trusted for safe operation during power loss, transport, or accidental disconnection from AC power.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot an unresolved ventilator battery fault while the device is supporting a patient unless patient safety is already secured.
  • If the ventilator is in active use and backup power reliability is uncertain, transfer the patient to another approved ventilator if clinically appropriate.
  • Keep the Hamilton C1 connected to external AC power while evaluating the alarm.
  • Do not rely on Battery 2 for transport or backup runtime until the fault is corrected and battery operation is verified.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ventilator displays Alarm: 244013 Battery 2: Defective.
  • Check whether the alarm appears at startup, during battery check, while charging, after unplugging AC power, or intermittently during use.
  • Review the battery status screen or battery indicator for Battery 2 status.
  • Look for signs that Battery 2 is not detected, not charging, showing abnormal charge, or not contributing runtime.

Confirm External Power Is Stable

  • Connect the ventilator to a known-good AC outlet.
  • Confirm the ventilator recognizes external power.
  • Inspect the AC power cord, power supply connection, adapter, and wall outlet for looseness or damage.
  • Do not intentionally operate the ventilator on battery power while Battery 2 is defective unless testing is controlled and allowed by facility procedure.

Check Battery 2 Seating

  • Power down the ventilator if clinically safe and allowed by facility process.
  • Remove and reseat Battery 2.
  • Make sure the battery is fully latched and properly installed.
  • Restart the ventilator and check whether Alarm: 244013 Battery 2: Defective clears.
  • If the alarm clears and Battery 2 charges normally, complete the required operational check before return to service.

Inspect Battery 2 and Battery Contacts

  • Remove Battery 2 and inspect the battery housing for cracks, swelling, leaking, burn marks, corrosion, contamination, or damaged contacts.
  • Inspect the ventilator battery bay contacts for bent pins, debris, corrosion, or visible damage.
  • Do not reinstall a swollen, leaking, burned, or physically damaged battery.
  • If physical damage is found, remove the battery from service and replace it with an approved compatible battery.

Replace Battery 2 With an Approved Compatible Battery

  • Install an approved Hamilton-compatible Battery 2 replacement.
  • Confirm the battery is fully seated and latched.
  • Power the ventilator on while connected to AC power.
  • Check whether the Battery 2: Defective alarm clears.
  • Alarm 244013 commonly requires battery replacement once seating and contact issues are ruled out.

Confirm Battery 2 Is Recognized and Charging

  • Check the battery status screen or battery indicator.
  • Confirm Battery 2 is detected, charging, and showing a reasonable charge level.
  • Verify no Battery 2 defective, battery communication, battery low, or charging-related alarms remain active.
  • Allow charging time if the replacement battery is low.
  • Complete the required operational check or pre-use check per facility procedure before returning the ventilator to service.

Compare Battery Slot Behavior if Needed

  • If Alarm: 244013 Battery 2: Defective remains after installing a known-good Battery 2, compare battery behavior only if facility policy allows.
  • Determine whether the fault follows the battery or stays with the Battery 2 position.
  • If the problem follows the battery, the battery is defective.
  • If the problem stays with the Battery 2 slot, suspect battery bay contacts, charging circuit, battery manager, or internal power management hardware.
  • Do not repeatedly swap batteries if the fault clearly remains with the ventilator.

Check for Related Power or Battery Alarms

  • Look for additional alarms such as Battery communication error, Battery low, Loss of external power, Battery 1 defective, charging failure, or power supply fault.
  • Multiple battery or power alarms may indicate a larger power management issue instead of a single failed battery.
  • If additional power faults are present, remove the ventilator from service and escalate for bench evaluation.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm: 244013 Battery 2: Defective remains after confirming stable AC power, reseating Battery 2, inspecting contacts, and installing an approved known-good replacement battery, the external and common battery causes have been ruled out.

The remaining issue may involve the ventilator-side Battery 2 bay, battery contacts, charging circuit, battery manager, or internal power management hardware.

Remove the device from service.

Label it Out of Service.

Send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Do not rely on the ventilator for patient use until the Battery 2 fault is corrected and backup power operation is verified.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first. A defective backup battery can become critical during transport, outlet failure, or accidental power disconnection.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator displayed Alarm: 244013 Battery 2: Defective. Staff reported Battery 2 warning was present during startup and battery status showed Battery 2 was not usable."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery 2 had a permanent failure. Battery contacts were inspected and no external damage was found. Alarm cleared after Battery 2 was replaced with an approved compatible battery."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced Battery 2, confirmed replacement battery was recognized and charging normally, verified no active battery alarms remained, and completed operational check. Ventilator returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery alarms on ventilators should always be treated as patient safety concerns. Start with safe power, confirm the exact alarm, inspect the battery and contacts, replace the failed battery, and verify the replacement is recognized and charging. If the alarm remains with a known-good battery, escalate the ventilator for repair instead of continuing bedside troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
