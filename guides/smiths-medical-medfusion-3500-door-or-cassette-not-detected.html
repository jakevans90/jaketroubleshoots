Smiths Medical Medfusion 3500

Door or Cassette Not Detected

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Medfusion 3500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Medfusion 3500 syringe infusion pump that reports a door open condition or fails to detect the installed syringe/cassette.

Common symptoms may include:

  • “Close Door” or similar door-related alarm
  • Pump will not start infusion
  • Pump does not recognize the installed syringe
  • Door appears physically closed but the alarm persists

Many of these issues are caused by setup problems, incorrect syringe loading, or debris interfering with the door detection mechanism rather than an internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Before troubleshooting, confirm the pump is not actively infusing on a patient.
  • If it is in use, move the patient to another pump before continuing troubleshooting.

Open and Fully Re-Close the Syringe Door

  • Open the pump door completely and close it again firmly.
  • Check for:
  • Door fully seated
  • Audible or tactile latch engagement
  • Door alignment with the pump body
  • Why this matters: The Medfusion 3500 uses a door sensor that must detect a fully closed latch. If the door is slightly misaligned, the sensor may not register it as closed.

Verify Correct Syringe Installation

  • Remove the syringe and reinstall it carefully.
  • Confirm:
  • Syringe barrel is seated correctly in the holder
  • Plunger is aligned with the pump driver mechanism
  • Syringe size matches the programmed syringe type
  • Why this matters: Improperly seated syringes can prevent the door from closing completely or interfere with cassette detection.

Inspect the Door Latch Area

  • Look closely at the latch and surrounding area.
  • Check for:
  • Cracked or loose latch components
  • Bent plastic around the door hinge
  • Foreign objects preventing full closure
  • Why this matters: Even minor mechanical damage can prevent the door switch from activating.

Check the Door Sensor Area

  • Inspect the area where the door meets the pump frame.
  • Look for:
  • Dirt or dried medication residue
  • Tape or labels interfering with closure
  • Obstructions around the door switch area
  • If contamination is present, clean the area using appropriate approved cleaning methods.

Try a Different Syringe

  • Install a different compatible syringe.
  • Check whether the pump:
  • Detects the syringe normally
  • Allows the door alarm to clear
  • Why this matters: Some syringe brands or worn plungers can interfere with proper seating and detection.

Power Cycle the Pump

  • Turn the pump off and back on.
  • After reboot:
  • Install the syringe again
  • Close the door firmly
  • Observe whether the alarm clears
  • Why this matters: Occasionally the pump software may need to reinitialize the door detection system.

If the Problem Persists

If the pump still reports a door open or cassette detection error after these checks, common external causes have likely been ruled out.

The problem may involve:

  • Door switch failure
  • Internal sensor failure
  • Latch mechanism wear
  • Internal mechanical misalignment

At this point the device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for Clinical Engineering bench evaluation or manufacturer repair

Recognizing when internal repair is required is an important part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot infusion pump mechanical issues while the device is connected to a patient.

If a door or cassette alarm occurs during therapy, move the patient to another verified pump first before evaluating the device.

This prevents therapy interruption and ensures patient safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nursing reported the Medfusion 3500 pump would not start infusion and displayed a door or cassette detection alarm.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Syringe was not fully seated in the holder, preventing the door from fully engaging the sensor.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Reinstalled syringe correctly and verified door closure. Alarm cleared and pump passed operational check.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Syringe type installed
  • Door latch condition
  • Any debris or contamination found
  • Alarm message displayed
  • Whether issue resolved after reseating syringe
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Many infusion pump door or cassette detection issues are caused by simple setup problems such as syringe misalignment or incomplete door closure. Clinical Engineering troubleshooting should begin with these external checks before assuming internal failure. When basic causes are ruled out, removing the device from service and documenting findings properly protects both patient safety and equipment reliability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
