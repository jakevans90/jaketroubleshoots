Drager Evita V500

Alarm: VTE low (spontaneous) low spontaneous tidal volume / patient hypoventilation

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses the alarm condition "VTE low (spontaneous)" indicating low exhaled tidal volume during spontaneous breathing, which can result in inadequate ventilation or patient hypoventilation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient’s respiratory status
  • Provide alternate ventilation if needed (bag-valve mask or backup ventilator)
  • Notify clinical staff if patient condition is unstable

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the VTE low (spontaneous) alarm is active
  • Observe if it occurs during spontaneous breathing modes
  • Check displayed tidal volume values
  • Look for signs of patient distress or increased respiratory rate

Check Patient Condition

  • Assess respiratory effort and chest rise
  • Evaluate sedation level or fatigue
  • Check for airway obstruction or clinical deterioration

Check Breathing Circuit for Leaks

  • Inspect all inspiratory and expiratory connections
  • Verify ET tube or mask seal
  • Look for loose fittings or disconnections

Inspect Flow Sensor and Expiratory Valve

  • Check flow sensor for moisture or contamination
  • Ensure expiratory valve is clean and properly seated
  • Confirm no obstruction affecting measurement

Review Ventilation Settings

  • Verify pressure support levels
  • Check trigger sensitivity
  • Review alarm limits for VTE

Run System/Self-Test (SST)

  • Remove device from patient use
  • Perform system self-test per manufacturer guidelines
  • Confirm system passes without errors

If the Problem Persists

If low VTE alarms continue after patient assessment, circuit checks, sensor inspection, and system testing, external causes have been ruled out.

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label the device as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilation alarms on an active patient. Always move the patient to a backup device before evaluating equipment.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming 'VTE low (spontaneous)' with low tidal volumes during spontaneous breathing mode."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Leak identified at patient circuit connection causing reduced measured exhaled tidal volume."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Secured circuit connections, verified proper seal, confirmed normal VTE readings and alarm resolution; returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Low VTE alarms should always be approached with patient condition first. Most issues are external or patient-related, but persistent alarms after all checks indicate the need for escalation and repair. Proper troubleshooting ensures patient safety and reliable ventilation support.
