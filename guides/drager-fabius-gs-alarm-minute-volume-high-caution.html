Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: MINUTE VOLUME HIGH !!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

The MINUTE VOLUME HIGH !! alarm indicates that the measured minute ventilation exceeds the configured upper alarm limit. This may be caused by incorrect ventilator settings, improper alarm limits, or inaccurate readings due to flow sensor miscalibration or failure. This condition can lead to over-ventilation or incorrect clinical interpretation of patient status.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from patient use if readings are unreliable or excessive ventilation is suspected
  • Transition the patient to a backup ventilation source
  • Confirm adequate ventilation using independent monitoring if available

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm MINUTE VOLUME HIGH !! is displayed
  • Observe when the alarm occurs (startup vs during ventilation)
  • Compare displayed minute volume to expected values
  • Check ventilation mode and consistency of readings

3. Check Alarm Limit Settings

  • Review the upper minute volume alarm limit
  • Ensure the limit is appropriate for the patient and mode
  • Adjust if set too low

4. Check Ventilator Settings

  • Review tidal volume (VT) and respiratory rate
  • Confirm settings match intended therapy
  • Identify any unintentionally high settings

5. Inspect Breathing Circuit and Accessories

  • Check for leaks, disconnections, or loose fittings
  • Verify proper assembly of the circuit
  • Inspect for condensation or obstruction

6. Check Flow Sensor Connection and Condition

  • Ensure the flow sensor cable is fully connected
  • Inspect for contamination or physical damage
  • Reseat connections as needed

7. Perform Flow Sensor Calibration

  • Follow the manufacturer calibration procedure
  • Ensure no airflow is present during calibration
  • Confirm calibration completes successfully

8. Substitute with a Known Good Flow Sensor (If Available)

  • Replace the sensor with a known good unit
  • Perform calibration after replacement
  • Verify whether the alarm clears

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm continues after verifying settings, checking the circuit, and calibrating or replacing the flow sensor, external causes have been ruled out. Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Always transition the patient to a backup device before investigating ventilation or measurement issues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Unit alarming MINUTE VOLUME HIGH !! during ventilation with elevated displayed minute volume."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Flow sensor miscalibrated causing inaccurate volume measurement."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Verified alarm, checked settings and circuit, performed flow sensor calibration and confirmed normal readings."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Alarms like MINUTE VOLUME HIGH !! often reflect measurement inaccuracies rather than true ventilation problems. Always verify settings and sensor performance first, and escalate appropriately if accuracy cannot be restored.
