Fresenius Medical Care 5008

Internal Fluid Leak Detection Alarms

Asset Type

Hemodialysis (HD) Machine

Manufacturer

Fresenius Medical Care

Model

5008

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where a Fresenius 5008 HD machine triggers internal fluid leak detection alarms. These alarms typically indicate that the machine has sensed liquid in areas where it should not be, which could result from external spills, loose connections, or internal leakage. The focus is on external checks first, ensuring patient safety, before escalating to repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety

  • Confirm the patient is not actively on dialysis at the machine.
  • Move the patient to a backup machine if necessary.
  • Power down the unit before inspecting for leaks.
    Why: Prevents risk of electrical hazard or patient exposure to fluid contamination.

Check Exterior for Visible Spills or Moisture

  • Inspect around the bloodlines, dialyzer, and dialysate connections.
  • Wipe up any accidental spills and ensure tubing is routed correctly.
    Why: External fluid can trigger internal sensors if it seeps into the machine casing.

Verify All Tubing and Connections

  • Confirm all dialysate, blood, and effluent lines are properly seated and not cracked or leaking.
  • Check clamps and connectors for tightness.
    Why: Loose or cracked lines can drip into the machine and trigger alarms.

Inspect Drain and Waste Systems

  • Ensure the drain hose is correctly positioned and free of kinks.
  • Confirm the drain pan or bottle is not overfilled.
    Why: Backflow or overflow can simulate an internal leak.

Check Internal Sensor Area for Contamination

  • Open the machine panel only if the manufacturer guidance allows, carefully inspect the fluid detection sensor area for residue or moisture.
  • Use a dry cloth to remove any detected moisture.
    Why: Residual fluid can falsely trigger alarms.

Power Cycle the Machine

  • Turn off the machine, wait 30–60 seconds, and power it back on.
  • Observe whether the alarm reoccurs without connecting a patient.
    Why: Confirms whether the alarm is persistent (possible internal leak) or transient (external contamination).

If the Problem Persists

If no external sources of fluid are found and the alarm continues:

  • Remove the machine from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send to manufacturer service or Clinical Engineering bench for internal inspection and repair.

Knowing when to stop is critical to avoid causing further damage or risking patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt dialysis with a machine showing an internal fluid leak alarm.
  • Always ensure an alternate HD machine is ready for patient use.
  • Document the alarm and your inspection clearly for continuity of care.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Internal fluid leak alarm triggered during machine startup.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“No external leaks observed; internal sensor area inspected and cleaned; alarm persisted after power cycle.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Machine removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation/repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Machine serial number and last service date.
  • Any unusual odors or moisture inside the machine.
  • Alarm codes displayed on the screen.
  • Observations during power cycle.

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting begins with logical, external checks and prioritizes patient safety. Escalating to repair when internal issues are suspected protects both staff and patients while maintaining equipment integrity. Accurate documentation ensures traceability and supports efficient service.

That is successful troubleshooting.
