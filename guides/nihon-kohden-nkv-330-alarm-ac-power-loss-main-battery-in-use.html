Nihon Kohden NKV-330

Alarm: AC Power Loss/Main Battery In Use

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

NKV-330

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the AC Power Loss/Main Battery In Use alarm on the Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator. This alarm means AC power is lost and the ventilator is operating on main battery power.

The ventilator may still be supporting ventilation, but battery runtime is limited. The priority is to confirm patient safety, restore AC power, check external power sources, and remove the ventilator from service if it cannot recognize verified AC power.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Check the patient first.
  • Confirm the patient is being ventilated appropriately.
  • Verify the ventilator is still powered and operating on main battery power.
  • Check the remaining battery level.
  • Notify clinical staff that the ventilator is no longer on AC power.
  • Prepare an alternate ventilator or manual ventilation method if AC power cannot be restored quickly.
  • Do not leave the ventilator unattended if AC power cannot be restored.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the displayed alarm reads AC Power Loss/Main Battery In Use.
  • Confirm when the alarm appeared, such as during normal use, after transport, after moving the power cord, or after plugging into a different outlet.
  • Check whether any other battery, charger, or power-related alarms are also active.
  • Confirm the ventilator is continuing to operate from the main battery.

3. Check the Wall Outlet

  • Verify the power cord is plugged into a live hospital-grade outlet.
  • Check whether the outlet is controlled by a switch or has lost facility power.
  • Look for nearby equipment plugged into the same power source that may also be off.
  • Test the outlet with an approved outlet tester or known working device if appropriate for your facility process.
  • If the outlet is not powered, move the ventilator power cord to a verified emergency-power outlet if clinically safe and appropriate.

4. Check the Power Cord Connection

  • Confirm the power cord is fully seated at the wall outlet.
  • Confirm the power cord is fully seated at the ventilator.
  • Inspect the cord for a damaged plug, bent prongs, cuts, crushed insulation, loose strain relief, or other visible damage.
  • Make sure the cord is not being pulled tight by bed movement, transport, or cable routing.
  • If the cord is damaged, remove it from use and replace it with the correct approved power cord.

5. Restore AC Power and Confirm the Alarm Clears

  • Plug the ventilator into a verified AC outlet.
  • Confirm the ventilator indicates AC power is present.
  • Confirm the AC Power Loss/Main Battery In Use alarm clears.
  • Monitor briefly to make sure the alarm does not return when the cord is gently repositioned or the bed is moved.
  • If the alarm clears and no damage or additional fault is present, document the cause and return the device to service according to facility policy.

6. Check Remaining Main Battery Power

  • Check the remaining main battery charge.
  • Inform clinical staff of the battery status.
  • Prepare a fully charged spare main battery if available.
  • Plan for patient transfer to another ventilator or reliable power source if battery runtime is limited.
  • Do not allow the ventilator to continue running on battery until the battery becomes depleted.

7. Check for a Facility Power Problem

  • Check whether nearby outlets or equipment are affected.
  • Ask staff if there was a recent power interruption.
  • Verify whether the ventilator is plugged into a normal outlet or emergency-power outlet.
  • Escalate to Facilities or Engineering support if the outlet or circuit appears faulty.

8. Isolate Device Versus External Power Issue

  • Plug the ventilator into a different verified AC outlet.
  • Use a known-good compatible power cord if available.
  • Check for visible damage at the ventilator AC inlet.
  • Look for signs of heat, odor, loose connection behavior, or abnormal power response.
  • If verified AC power is reaching the ventilator but the alarm remains active, suspect an internal AC input, AC detection, charging, or power supply issue.
  • Do not open the ventilator or perform internal power supply repair during clinical troubleshooting.

If the Problem Persists

If the AC Power Loss/Main Battery In Use alarm remains active after confirming the wall outlet, power cord, and external AC source are good, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent alarm under verified AC power conditions may indicate an internal power input, AC detection, charging, or power supply problem.

  • Remove the ventilator from patient use once clinically safe.
  • Replace it with another ventilator if needed.
  • Label the unit Out of Service.
  • Send the ventilator for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Document the verified outlet, power cord, battery status, and whether the alarm cleared or remained active.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot power-source alarms on an active patient without clinical support. Move the patient to a backup ventilator first if AC power cannot be restored quickly or if the ventilator power condition is unreliable.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator displayed AC Power Loss/Main Battery In Use alarm and staff reported the ventilator was operating on main battery power."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"AC power was not being detected by the ventilator. Outlet and power cord were checked, and the issue was related to the external power source or cord connection."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Verified patient safety, checked remaining battery level, inspected power cord and AC connections, tested outlet, restored AC power, and confirmed the alarm cleared. Ventilator was returned to service after normal operation was verified."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The AC Power Loss/Main Battery In Use alarm should be treated as a patient safety and power reliability issue first. Confirm ventilation, preserve battery runtime, check the outlet and power cord, and restore AC power as soon as possible. If the ventilator does not recognize verified AC power, remove it from service and escalate for repair. Clear documentation helps separate facility power issues from true ventilator power-system failures.

That is successful troubleshooting.
