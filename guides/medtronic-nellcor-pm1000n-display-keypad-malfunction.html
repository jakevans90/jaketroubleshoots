Medtronic Nellcor PM1000N

Display or Control Knob/Keypad Malfunction

Asset Type

Pulse Oximeter

Manufacturer

Medtronic

Model

Nellcor PM1000N

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Nellcor PM1000N display or control knob/keypad is unresponsive, intermittently fails, or shows incorrect inputs. Such issues are often caused by external factors like power, connector issues, contamination, or minor wear and can often be resolved without internal disassembly.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Source

  • Ensure the device is plugged into a working AC outlet or properly seated in a charging station.
  • Check battery charge level. A low battery may cause keypad or display malfunctions.
  • Why: Unstable or insufficient power can mimic hardware failure.

Inspect Display and Knobs/Keypad

  • Look for visible damage, cracks, or stuck buttons.
  • Ensure no debris or liquid has contaminated the keypad or display area.
  • Why: Physical obstruction or contamination can prevent proper contact.

Reset Device

  • Turn the unit off and back on.
  • If battery-operated, remove the battery, wait 30 seconds, and reinsert.
  • Why: A simple reset can resolve temporary software or input glitches.

Check Connections

  • Ensure any detachable cables (power, sensor, data) are fully seated.
  • Loose or corroded connectors can interfere with keypad or display signals.

Test User Input

  • Rotate the control knob or press each button sequentially.
  • Observe whether the display responds consistently.
  • Why: Confirms whether the failure is intermittent or constant.

Environmental Check

  • Ensure device is not exposed to excessive humidity, dust, or electromagnetic interference.
  • Why: Environmental factors can cause transient display or keypad issues.

If the Problem Persists

External checks have been completed, and the issue remains.

  • Device should be:
    • Removed from service
    • Labeled Out of Service
    • Sent for bench evaluation or repair by qualified personnel

Note: Clinical Engineering should avoid internal board-level repairs on-site.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt to troubleshoot the device while it is monitoring a patient.
  • Move the patient to another functioning monitor first.
  • Only perform troubleshooting in a safe environment to prevent patient risk.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reports display flickers and keypad inputs are not registering.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Observed that power connections were secure but some keypad buttons intermittently failed when pressed; device may have minor internal wear or contamination.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service and labeled Out of Service; sent for manufacturer or qualified bench repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Battery level and charging status noted
  • Buttons tested for responsiveness
  • Display behavior observed (flickering, blank, partial segments)
  • Device labeled Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety comes first. Proper logic-based troubleshooting involves eliminating external causes first and knowing when to escalate. Documenting the CCR thoroughly helps track patterns and informs future preventive maintenance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
