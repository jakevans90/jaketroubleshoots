Philips Avalon FM30 Fetal Monitor

Fetal / Maternal Heart Rate Signal Interference or Dropout

Asset Type

Fetal Monitor

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

Avalon FM30

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting fetal and maternal heart rate signal interference, cross-talk, or intermittent dropout on the Avalon FM30. These issues commonly stem from external factors such as transducer placement, cable integrity, or electromagnetic interference rather than internal device failure.

Fetal monitors using Doppler ultrasound can sometimes misidentify maternal signals as fetal signals or lose signal due to positioning or interference, making logical troubleshooting essential.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the patient is being monitored appropriately on another device if needed.
  • Do not troubleshoot while actively relied upon for critical monitoring.

Check Transducer Placement and Contact

  • Ensure ultrasound (US) transducer is properly positioned over the fetal heart.
  • Verify adequate gel is used.
  • Reposition slightly to improve signal acquisition.
  • Poor placement is the most common cause of signal dropout.

Differentiate Fetal vs Maternal Signal

  • Compare displayed heart rate with maternal pulse.
  • If values are similar, the monitor may be detecting maternal heart rate.
  • This is a known limitation—maternal vessels can be picked up by the transducer.

Check All Cables and Connections

  • Inspect transducer cables for damage, kinks, or loose connections.
  • Reseat connectors firmly into the monitor.
  • Swap with a known-good transducer if available.

Inspect Belts and Securing Method

  • Ensure belts are snug enough to maintain consistent contact.
  • Loose belts can cause intermittent dropout during patient movement.

Evaluate for Electromagnetic / RF Interference

  • Look for nearby sources:
  • Wireless devices (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, cordless phones)
  • Other medical equipment
  • Move the monitor or transducers away from interference sources.
  • These devices are sensitive to RF interference, which can degrade signal quality.

Check Cableless / Telemetry (If Used)

  • Verify base station connection and antenna integrity.
  • Ensure patient is within range.
  • Interference or range issues can cause intermittent signal loss.

Review Alarm Messages and Indicators

  • Look for:
  • INOP messages
  • Signal quality indicators
  • Dropout alarms
  • Document any recurring alarm patterns.

Test with Known-Good Accessories

  • Replace:
  • Ultrasound transducer
  • TOCO sensor (if applicable)
  • Helps isolate accessory vs monitor issue.

Power Cycle the Monitor

  • Turn off, wait 10–15 seconds, and restart.
  • Clears temporary processing or communication issues.

If the Problem Persists

If signal interference or dropout continues after verifying placement, accessories, and environment:

  • External causes have been ruled out
  • The issue is likely internal (receiver module, signal processing, or RF circuitry)
  • Remove the device from service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for bench evaluation or manufacturer repair

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and wasted troubleshooting time.

Clinical Use Tip

Always ensure the patient is safely monitored before troubleshooting.

If signal reliability is in question, move the patient to another monitor immediately.

Never rely on a questionable fetal heart rate signal for clinical decisions.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nursing reported intermittent fetal heart rate dropout and occasional matching maternal heart rate values.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Loose ultrasound transducer placement and interference from nearby wireless equipment causing inconsistent signal acquisition.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Repositioned transducer, replaced worn belt, and relocated monitor away from interference source. Verified stable FHR tracing.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Transducer placement verified
  • Gel applied / reapplied
  • Cables inspected or swapped
  • Wireless interference identified or ruled out
  • Alarm messages observed (INOP, signal loss)
  • Maternal vs fetal HR comparison performed
  • Accessories replaced (if applicable)
  • Final device status (returned to service or removed)

Final Thought

Fetal monitoring reliability depends heavily on proper signal acquisition and environment. Most interference or dropout issues are external and correctable without invasive repair. A structured, step-by-step approach ensures patient safety, reduces unnecessary equipment downtime, and reinforces confidence in the monitoring system. Proper documentation completes the process and supports future troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
