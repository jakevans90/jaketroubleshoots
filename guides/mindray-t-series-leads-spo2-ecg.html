Mindray BeneView T Series

Leads / SpO₂ / ECG Sensor Errors

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

BeneView T Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where a Mindray BeneView T Series patient monitor displays sensor errors, such as SpO₂ probe faults, ECG lead disconnections, or intermittent signal loss. The focus is on external, easily verifiable causes—like cables, connectors, and patient placement—before assuming internal hardware failure. The goal is to restore accurate monitoring safely and efficiently.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check Power and Device Status

  • Ensure the monitor is plugged in and powered on.
  • Confirm battery charge is sufficient if operating on battery.
  • Why: Some sensor errors can be triggered by low voltage or unstable power.

Inspect Cables and Connectors

  • Visually inspect ECG leads and SpO₂ cables for breaks, frays, or kinks.
  • Check that connectors are fully seated in the monitor ports.
  • Why: Loose or damaged connections are the most common cause of sensor errors.

Confirm Proper Lead/Probe Placement

  • Ensure ECG electrodes are attached to the correct anatomical sites with intact adhesive.
  • Verify SpO₂ probe is on a finger/toe with adequate perfusion, not cold or wet.
  • Why: Poor contact or placement can cause intermittent or false error messages.

Swap Cables/Leads if Available

  • Replace the suspect ECG leads or SpO₂ probe with a known-good set.
  • Observe if the error clears.
  • Why: Eliminates the cable as the source before suspecting monitor hardware.

Check for Interference

  • Move patient away from high electrical interference (diathermy units, MRI, or other monitors).
  • Ensure leads are not crossing high-power cords.
  • Why: Electrical noise can trigger erroneous readings.

Reboot the Monitor

  • Power cycle the monitor after checking connections.
  • Observe if sensor errors persist.
  • Why: Some software glitches resolve after a controlled reboot.

If the Problem Persists

All external causes have been ruled out. The monitor may have internal hardware or board issues affecting lead/sensor detection.

  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service.”
  • Send it for bench evaluation or repair by authorized service personnel.
  • Why: Continuing patient use with persistent sensor errors risks inaccurate monitoring and patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot sensors while the patient is actively dependent on the monitor. Move the patient to a backup monitor before replacing leads or cables. Always verify accurate signal readings after reconnecting leads or probes.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor displays SpO₂ and ECG lead errors intermittently.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“ECG leads were frayed and SpO₂ probe loose; connections not fully seated.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Replaced ECG leads and repositioned SpO₂ probe; monitored signals stable. Device returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested: ✅
  • Cable continuity checked: ✅
  • Alarm behavior: intermittent SpO₂ drop, ECG flatline alerts
  • Indicator lights: sensor status LEDs
  • Unusual sounds or heat: none observed
  • Final device status: stable after cable replacement

Final Thought

Accurate patient monitoring depends on secure sensor connections and proper placement. Logical, stepwise troubleshooting avoids unnecessary repair costs and ensures patient safety. Documenting all actions in CCR format supports accountability and regulatory compliance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
