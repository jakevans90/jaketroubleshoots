GE Healthcare Aisys

Alarm: BATTERY CHARGE FAIL

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Aisys

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot a BATTERY CHARGE FAIL alarm on a GE Healthcare Aisys anesthesia machine. This alarm may appear with a No battery backup message and indicates the machine may not have reliable battery backup because of abnormal battery charging behavior, excessive charge current, battery failure, battery connection issues, or a battery charge circuit fault.

Because the battery provides backup power during AC power interruption, this alarm should be treated as a patient safety issue until backup power status is confirmed.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the anesthesia machine from patient use if the alarm is active, repeated, or cannot be cleared.
  • If the unit is in use, notify anesthesia staff immediately and follow facility procedure for moving to another anesthesia machine or backup ventilation method.
  • Do not rely on the machine for patient care until battery backup status is verified.
  • Do not assume AC power alone makes the unit safe if the machine is reporting no battery backup.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact alarm displayed is BATTERY CHARGE FAIL.
  • Confirm whether the display also reports No battery backup.
  • Note when the alarm appears, such as during startup, while plugged into AC power, while in standby, intermittently after charging, or after battery replacement or service.
  • Check whether the machine otherwise powers on normally.

3. Confirm AC Power Is Present and Stable

  • Verify the anesthesia machine is connected to a known-good hospital-grade AC outlet.
  • Check that the power cord is fully seated at the wall outlet and at the machine connection.
  • Inspect the power cord for damage, looseness, or signs of overheating.
  • Confirm the outlet is not controlled by a wall switch or inactive emergency power circuit.

4. Check External Power Indicators

  • Observe the machine’s AC power and battery indicators.
  • Confirm whether the machine recognizes AC power.
  • Look for abnormal behavior such as the battery icon not charging, battery status unavailable, the alarm returning immediately after restart, or the machine reporting no battery backup while plugged in.
  • Separate incoming power problems from battery or charge circuit problems before moving deeper into troubleshooting.

5. Inspect the Battery Area Externally

  • With the machine removed from patient use and following facility safety procedures, inspect the battery compartment area externally.
  • Look for a loose battery cover, signs of recent battery replacement, physical damage, fluid intrusion, corrosion, burning smell, or heat.
  • Do not perform deep internal disassembly during basic troubleshooting.
  • If the battery area is hot, damaged, leaking, swollen, corroded, or smells abnormal, stop troubleshooting and escalate the unit for repair evaluation.

6. Power Cycle the Machine Only if Safe

  • If the machine is not connected to a patient and clinical use has ended, perform a controlled restart.
  • Allow the machine to complete startup checks.
  • Observe whether BATTERY CHARGE FAIL returns during startup, standby, or powered-on operation.
  • A persistent alarm after restart suggests the system is detecting an actual battery charge fault rather than a temporary state.

7. Allow Charging Time if Recently Depleted or Replaced

  • If the battery was recently depleted, replaced, or the machine was stored unplugged, leave the system connected to AC power for an appropriate charging period.
  • Recheck the alarm after charging time has passed.
  • A normal depleted battery condition should improve after charging.
  • Do not ignore a persistent BATTERY CHARGE FAIL alarm, especially if the machine continues reporting no battery backup.

8. Check Battery Charge Information in Service Software

  • If qualified and authorized, access Service Software and review the battery charge circuit information.
  • Check for abnormal battery charge current or charge circuit status.
  • Compare the battery status shown in Service Software with the reported alarm condition.
  • This helps confirm whether the system is detecting excessive battery charge current instead of a simple low battery condition.

9. Isolate Battery-Related Failure

  • If charge circuit values are abnormal, suspect a battery failure, battery connection issue, or battery charge circuit fault.
  • Do not continue repeated charging attempts if there are signs of heat, odor, corrosion, leaking, swelling, or visible battery area damage.
  • Keep the machine out of patient use until battery backup can be verified as safe and reliable.

10. Replace Battery if Indicated

  • If Service Software findings and inspection support battery failure, replace the battery according to approved service procedures.
  • After replacement, reconnect AC power and allow the system to charge.
  • Restart the machine and confirm BATTERY CHARGE FAIL does not return.
  • Verify the machine no longer reports No battery backup.

11. Return to Service Only After Verification

  • Confirm BATTERY CHARGE FAIL is no longer present.
  • Confirm the No battery backup message is cleared.
  • Verify AC power is recognized and battery status appears normal.
  • Run the required system checkout and confirm it passes.
  • Confirm no related battery or power alarms return before releasing the machine for clinical use.

If the Problem Persists

If BATTERY CHARGE FAIL continues after confirming AC power, checking external power connections, observing power indicators, allowing charge time when appropriate, reviewing Service Software battery charge data, and replacing the battery if indicated, the external causes have been ruled out.

Remove the anesthesia machine from service. Label it Out of Service and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

A persistent BATTERY CHARGE FAIL alarm may indicate an internal battery charge circuit, power supply, wiring, or control-related failure. Do not keep cycling power or repeatedly attempting to charge the battery if the alarm continues.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot battery backup alarms on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup anesthesia machine or approved alternate ventilation method first. An anesthesia machine with unresolved battery backup failure should not be relied on during a power interruption.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"GE Healthcare Aisys anesthesia machine displayed BATTERY CHARGE FAIL with No battery backup message while connected to AC power."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified AC power and power cord connection. Alarm returned after restart. Battery charge information required review in Service Software and findings indicated suspected failed battery or battery charge circuit fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed unit from service. Checked battery charge status in Service Software. Battery replaced if indicated and system retested. If alarm persisted, unit remained Out of Service for further bench evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery charge alarms on an anesthesia machine should be treated seriously because they affect backup power reliability. Start with patient safety, verify AC power and visible external conditions, confirm the alarm behavior, use Service Software when appropriate, and escalate when the battery or charge circuit cannot be verified as safe. Clear documentation supports safe repair decisions and helps prevent repeated troubleshooting of the same unresolved fault.

That is successful troubleshooting.
