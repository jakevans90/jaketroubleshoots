Philips PageWriter TC70

Error: Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) communication is incorrectly configured

Asset Type

Electrocardiograph (EKG) Machine

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

PageWriter TC70

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Philips PageWriter TC70 error message: Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) communication is incorrectly configured. This condition may affect startup, ECG acquisition, printing, data transfer, or normal system operation because it points to an internal communication or configuration problem.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the PageWriter TC70 from patient use if the error appears during startup, ECG acquisition, printing, transmission, or normal operation.
  • Use another verified ECG machine for patient testing.
  • Expected: Patient ECG testing continues on a known-good device.
  • Why it matters: An internal communication configuration error can affect core system operation and should not be ignored during patient care.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Power the cardiograph on in a controlled Clinical Engineering area.
  • Confirm the exact message: Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) communication is incorrectly configured. Please contact the Philips Response Center for further assistance.
  • Observe whether the error occurs immediately at startup, during Power On test, when entering ECG acquisition, during printing, during data transfer, or intermittently after warm-up.
  • Expected: The error is either reproducible or clearly documented.
  • Why it matters: This confirms a true system-reported internal error rather than a workflow, accessory, or user issue.

Check AC Power and Battery Condition

  • Connect the PageWriter TC70 directly to a known-good hospital-grade outlet.
  • Avoid extension cords, loose outlets, power strips, or switched receptacles.
  • Confirm the AC indicator is present and that the unit powers on normally.
  • If the battery is low, missing, or questionable, allow the unit to charge or test with a known-good battery if available.
  • Expected: The unit powers on normally without low-power behavior.
  • Why it matters: Poor power can cause unusual startup behavior, but it should not be assumed to be the root cause of an FPGA configuration error.

Perform a Controlled Restart

  • Power the cardiograph off using the normal shutdown method if possible.
  • Disconnect AC power.
  • Allow the unit to sit briefly.
  • Reconnect AC power and restart the device.
  • Expected: The error clears only if it was caused by a temporary startup or communication state.
  • Stop if resolved: If the error does not return, complete functional checks before returning the unit to service.

Disconnect External Accessories

  • Remove nonessential accessories and external connections, including network cable, USB devices, barcode scanner, external keyboard or mouse, cart accessories, and external data connections.
  • Restart the cardiograph after accessories are removed.
  • Expected: The unit starts without accessory-related interference.
  • Why it matters: External devices should be ruled out before declaring an internal failure.

Inspect the Patient Interface Module and ECG Cable

  • Disconnect and inspect the Patient Interface Module, patient data cable, and lead wires.
  • Look for bent pins, damaged connectors, fluid contamination, cracked housings, strain damage, or loose connections.
  • Reconnect securely or test with known-good ECG accessories if available.
  • Expected: ECG accessories are not damaged and do not change the FPGA error behavior.
  • Why it matters: While this error is described as internal, ECG front-end communication issues should still be separated from accessory damage.

Check for Physical Damage or Contamination

  • Inspect the cardiograph exterior, display area, keyboard or touchscreen area, printer area, connectors, and battery compartment.
  • Look for drop damage, cracked case, liquid intrusion, corrosion, burn smell, heat discoloration, or loose connector ports.
  • Expected: No obvious external cause is found.
  • Why it matters: Physical or liquid damage can support escalation decisions and repair documentation.

Run Available User or Biomed-Level Self-Tests

  • If the device allows access past the error, run available diagnostics or operational checks appropriate for Clinical Engineering.
  • Check startup behavior, display response, touchscreen response, ECG acquisition screen, printer function, network or data transfer behavior, and any logged system error history if accessible.
  • Expected: Either the unit passes functional checks or the FPGA error prevents normal operation.
  • Why it matters: This helps determine whether the error is isolated, intermittent, or blocking core ECG functions.

Do Not Attempt Deep Internal Repair at the Point of Use

  • This error specifically indicates that internal FPGA communication is incorrectly configured.
  • Do not continue troubleshooting at the bedside.
  • Do not rely on the cardiograph for patient ECGs if the error remains.
  • Expected: The device is treated as an internal system fault after external causes are ruled out.
  • Why it matters: FPGA communication involves internal electronics or configuration that requires qualified service support.

Stop When Resolved

  • If the error clears after power, accessory, or restart checks, perform a complete operational verification before returning the unit to service.
  • Confirm successful startup, no recurring error, ECG acquisition, printing, acceptable battery and AC operation, Patient Interface Module operation, and any required facility checks.
  • Return to service only if the error does not recur and the device passes functional testing.

If the Problem Persists

If the FPGA communication configuration error remains after power, restart, accessory, cable, and basic external checks, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent FPGA communication configuration error strongly indicates an internal device configuration, firmware, main electronics, or system communication failure.

The PageWriter TC70 should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for repair or bench evaluation, and escalated to the Philips Response Center or authorized service support.

Do not continue repeated resets or return the unit to clinical use with this error active. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this error on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first. Transfer ECG testing to another verified cardiograph before evaluating the PageWriter TC70. A device with an unresolved internal communication error should not be trusted for diagnostic ECG acquisition.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Philips PageWriter TC70 reported Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) communication is incorrectly configured error and staff reported unit could not be trusted for normal ECG use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External power, battery, accessory, patient cable, and visible damage checks were performed. Error persisted, indicating likely internal FPGA communication, configuration, firmware, or system electronics fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed PageWriter TC70 from service, labeled Out of Service, and escalated for Philips or authorized repair evaluation. Device was not returned to clinical use due to unresolved internal error."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Patient safety comes first when an ECG machine reports an internal FPGA communication configuration error. Clinical Engineering should confirm the error, rule out simple external causes, avoid unnecessary internal disassembly, and escalate appropriately when the fault persists. Clear CCR documentation protects the patient, the technician, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
