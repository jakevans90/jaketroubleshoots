Drager Evita V500

Alarm: Apnea No breath detected within apnea interval

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Apnea alarm indicating no detected breaths within the set apnea interval, which can result in loss of ventilation support or require immediate backup ventilation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient
  • Initiate manual ventilation or switch to a backup ventilator if needed
  • Notify clinical staff immediately

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the Apnea alarm is active on the ventilator
  • Observe for absence of spontaneous or mandatory breaths
  • Check for flat or minimal waveform activity
  • Note when the alarm occurs (continuous or intermittent)

3. Check Patient Condition

  • Visually assess chest rise and breathing effort
  • Check patient responsiveness
  • Confirm findings with clinical staff

4. Check Airway and Circuit for Obstruction

  • Inspect ET tube or mask for kinks or secretions
  • Check for dislodgement or blockage
  • Inspect circuit for occlusions or water buildup

5. Verify Circuit Connections

  • Ensure all inspiratory and expiratory connections are secure
  • Confirm proper connection at ventilator and patient interface

6. Review Ventilator Settings

  • Check apnea interval settings
  • Verify ventilation mode supports backup ventilation
  • Confirm trigger sensitivity settings

7. Check Flow Sensors and Accessories

  • Inspect flow sensor for moisture or contamination
  • Replace with a known-good sensor if available

If the Problem Persists

If external causes have been ruled out and the alarm continues:

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label the device as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot an apnea alarm on an active patient without ensuring ventilation support first. Always move the patient to a backup device before evaluating the ventilator.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarming Apnea – no breath detected, no ventilation observed."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Obstruction found in ET tube preventing airflow detection."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleared obstruction, verified ventilation restored, alarm cleared, device returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Apnea alarms indicate either a critical patient condition or failure in breath detection. Always prioritize patient safety, verify external causes first, and escalate when device reliability is uncertain.

That is successful troubleshooting.
