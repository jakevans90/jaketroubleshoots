Enthermics DC Series

Unit Not Reaching or Maintaining Set Temperature

Asset Type

Blanket Warmer

Manufacturer

Enthermics

Model

DC Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where an Enthermics DC Series blanket warmer does not reach the selected temperature or struggles to maintain it.

The focus is on external and easily verifiable causes first—such as power supply, door seal integrity, airflow obstruction, loading practices, and temperature settings—before assuming internal heater, sensor, or control board failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Never troubleshoot a blanket warmer that is actively being used for patient care.
  • If warming is required for an active case, transfer linens to another approved warmer.
  • Ensure patient care is uninterrupted before removing the unit from service.

2. Verify Power Source

  • Confirm the unit is plugged directly into a known-good wall outlet.
  • Avoid power strips or extension cords.
  • Test the outlet with a receptacle tester if needed.

Why this matters: Low or unstable voltage can prevent heating elements from operating at full capacity.

3. Confirm Set Temperature and Mode

  • Verify the temperature setpoint matches department policy (commonly 120–160°F depending on application).
  • Confirm the unit is not in a standby or low-temp mode.
  • Check for error codes or alarm indicators on the display.

Why this matters: Incorrect settings are a common cause of perceived heating issues.

4. Check Door Seal and Latch

  • Inspect the door gasket for cracks, flattening, or gaps.
  • Ensure the door closes fully and latches securely.
  • Check for blankets caught in the door seal.

Why this matters: Heat loss from a poor seal prevents temperature stability.

5. Evaluate Loading Practices

  • Confirm the warmer is not overloaded.
  • Blankets should be loosely stacked to allow airflow.
  • Remove excessive blankets and allow time to recover temperature.

Why this matters: Restricted airflow prevents even heat distribution.

6. Check Internal Airflow

  • Listen for the internal circulation fan.
  • Ensure interior vents are not blocked by linens.
  • Inspect for debris inside the chamber.

Why this matters: Proper convection is required to maintain uniform temperature.

7. Allow Adequate Warm-Up Time

  • After being loaded or powered on, allow sufficient time (often 1–2 hours depending on load size).
  • Use an independent calibrated thermometer to verify actual chamber temperature.

Why this matters: Digital displays may not reflect stabilized temperature immediately after loading.

8. Inspect for Error Codes or Over-Temp Events

  • Document any error messages.
  • Reset the unit per facility protocol if safe to do so.
  • Observe if temperature climbs but then drops unexpectedly.

Why this matters: Repeated cycling may indicate a failing temperature sensor or control relay.

If the Problem Persists

If:

  • Power source is verified
  • Door seal is intact
  • Loading is appropriate
  • Airflow is unobstructed
  • Temperature verification confirms underheating

Then common external causes have been ruled out.

The issue is likely internal (heating element degradation, thermostat/sensor failure, control board malfunction, or fan motor issue).

  • Remove the unit from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Transfer blankets to an alternate warmer
  • Schedule bench evaluation or authorized repair

Knowing when to stop external troubleshooting is proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a blanket warmer that is actively being used for patient care.

If warming is required for an active case:

  • Transfer linens to another approved warmer
  • Ensure patient care is uninterrupted
  • Perform troubleshooting only when the unit is safely out of service

Patient temperature management and infection control always take priority.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Unit not reaching set temperature of 150°F. Staff reports blankets feel lukewarm after several hours.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Door gasket intact. Unit powered properly. Chamber overloaded with tightly packed blankets restricting airflow. Internal temp verified at 118°F.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Removed excess blankets to allow airflow. Verified fan operation. Allowed unit to stabilize for 90 minutes. Temperature reached and maintained 150°F. Returned to service.”

“Removed from service. Tagged Out of Service. Sent for bench evaluation and internal component testing.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and voltage verified
  • Door seal condition
  • Loading condition
  • Independent temperature verification
  • Fan operation observed
  • Any displayed error codes
  • Final device status (Returned to Service or Sent for Repair)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with power, environment, and user setup before assuming component failure. Blanket warmers rely heavily on airflow and proper loading to function correctly.

Patient safety and temperature management are critical—never compromise care while troubleshooting. Proper documentation protects both the patient and the department.

That is successful troubleshooting.
