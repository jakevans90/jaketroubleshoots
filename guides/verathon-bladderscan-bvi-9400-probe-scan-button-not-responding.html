Verathon BladderScan BVI 9400

Probe scan button not responding

Asset Type

Bladder Scanner

Manufacturer

Verathon

Model

BladderScan BVI 9400

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting a Verathon BladderScan BVI 9400 when the probe scan button does not start a scan.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Do not troubleshoot the scanner while it is being used for an active patient assessment.

Action:

  • Remove the BVI 9400 from patient use if the scan button is not responding.
  • Provide the clinical team with another bladder scanner if one is available.

Expected outcome: Patient care continues without relying on a scanner that may not capture a valid scan.

Why it matters: A nonresponsive scan button can delay bladder volume assessment or lead staff to repeat scans unnecessarily.

Verify the Reported Problem

Confirm whether the issue is truly the probe scan button or a broader system problem.

Action:

  • Power on the BVI 9400.
  • Confirm the display turns on normally.
  • Attempt to start a scan using the probe scan button.
  • Observe whether:
  • The button does nothing.
  • The scanner freezes.
  • The scanner gives an error.
  • The button works intermittently.
  • The console controls respond normally.

Expected outcome: The failure is confirmed and separated from a general power, touchscreen, or startup issue.

If the probe button works during testing, document “could not duplicate,” clean and inspect the device, and return to service only if operation is reliable.

Check Battery Charge and Power Status

Low battery or unstable power can cause unreliable button response or incomplete operation.

Action:

  • Check the battery level on the scanner.
  • Place the unit on its charger or connect the proper charging setup.
  • Allow the battery to charge if low.
  • Retest the probe scan button after adequate charge is available.

Expected outcome: The scan button responds normally once stable power is restored.

If this resolves the issue, stop troubleshooting and document the battery/power condition.

Inspect the Probe and Button Area

The scan button may be physically stuck, damaged, contaminated, or worn.

Action:

  • Inspect the probe housing.
  • Check the scan button for:
  • Cracks
  • Fluid residue
  • Cleaning chemical buildup
  • Sticky movement
  • Loose or sunken button feel
  • Physical impact damage
  • Press the button several times and confirm whether it moves normally.

Expected outcome: The button should move freely and return to its normal position.

If the button is physically damaged, sticky, or does not return normally, remove the unit from service for repair evaluation.

Clean the Probe Exterior

Dried gel, disinfectant residue, or contamination around the button can interfere with normal movement.

Action:

  • Clean the probe exterior according to facility-approved cleaning procedures.
  • Pay close attention to the area around the scan button.
  • Do not allow fluid to enter openings or seams.
  • Allow the probe to dry fully.
  • Retest the scan button.

Expected outcome: The button responds if the issue was caused by external contamination or residue.

If cleaning resolves the issue, stop and document the cleaning performed.

Check Probe Cable and Strain Relief

A damaged probe cable or loose internal conductor can make the scan button appear nonresponsive.

Action:

  • Inspect the probe cable from the probe head to the console connection.
  • Look for:
  • Cuts
  • Kinks
  • Crushed sections
  • Loose strain relief
  • Exposed wiring
  • Intermittent response when the cable is moved
  • Gently reposition the cable while testing the scan button.

Expected outcome: The scan button should respond consistently without needing the cable held in a certain position.

If button response changes when the cable is moved, remove the scanner from service for probe/cable repair evaluation.

Verify Probe Connection at the Console

The probe may not communicate properly if the connector is loose, contaminated, or not fully seated.

Action:

  • Power the unit off.
  • Inspect the probe connection at the console.
  • Confirm the connector is fully seated and secure.
  • Check for bent pins, debris, corrosion, or visible connector damage if accessible externally.
  • Power the unit back on and retest.

Expected outcome: The probe button responds after the probe connection is confirmed secure.

If the connector is damaged, do not force it. Remove the unit from service.

Check for System Lockup or Software Freeze

The probe button may be functional, but the scanner may not respond because the system is frozen.

Action:

  • Observe whether the display updates normally.
  • Check whether other controls respond.
  • Power the unit off and restart it.
  • Allow the scanner to complete startup.
  • Retest the probe button.

Expected outcome: The probe button responds normally after restart if the issue was a temporary system lockup.

If the scanner repeatedly freezes or the button stops responding again, remove it from service for bench evaluation.

Confirm Proper Scan Mode and Readiness

The scanner may not respond if it is not in the correct state to begin a scan.

Action:

  • Confirm the scanner is fully powered on and ready to scan.
  • Verify the unit is not still starting up, processing a prior scan, printing, charging-only, or displaying an unresolved error.
  • Clear any completed scan workflow if appropriate.
  • Retest the probe scan button from the normal scan-ready screen.

Expected outcome: The probe button starts a scan when the system is in the correct operating state.

If the scanner is not reaching a scan-ready state, troubleshoot the startup, self-test, or system error separately.

Check for Liquid Intrusion or Physical Abuse

A failed probe button can result from dropped equipment or fluid exposure.

Action:

  • Inspect the scanner and probe for:
  • Cracked housing
  • Loose seams
  • Evidence of liquid entry
  • Unusual odor
  • Visible corrosion
  • Rattling parts
  • Impact marks
  • Ask clinical staff whether the unit was dropped, heavily cleaned, or exposed to fluids.

Expected outcome: No signs of impact or fluid intrusion are present.

If fluid intrusion or impact damage is suspected, remove the device from service and send for repair evaluation.

Test Repeated Button Operation

Intermittent button failures are still failures.

Action:

  • Perform several scan-button tests from the scan-ready screen.
  • Do not use a patient for repeated troubleshooting scans.
  • Confirm whether the button responds every time.

Expected outcome: The scan button should operate consistently.

If the button only works sometimes, remove the unit from service. Intermittent probe controls are not reliable for patient care.

If the Problem Persists

If the probe scan button still does not respond after checking power, battery status, probe condition, cable integrity, connector seating, cleanliness, and system readiness, common external causes have been ruled out.

The problem is likely related to the probe button assembly, probe cable, connector, internal communication path, or scanner electronics.

The device must be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not continue placing the device back into clinical use if the probe button response is unreliable.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot probe button response during an active patient scan. Move clinical staff to another scanner first if bladder volume assessment is needed. A scanner that cannot reliably initiate a scan should not be trusted for patient-care workflow until the fault is corrected.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the Verathon BladderScan BVI 9400 probe scan button would not start a bladder scan."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified the unit powered on, but the probe scan button remained nonresponsive after battery charging, cleaning, cable inspection, and connector reseating."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the BVI 9400 from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for probe/button repair evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior or any error messages displayed
  • Accessories swapped, including charger, probe connection, or approved charging setup
  • Power behavior, battery charge level, and whether the scanner powers on normally
  • Environmental factors such as cleaning residue, fluid exposure, impact damage, unusual smell, or heat
  • Indicator lights, display response, or whether other controls respond
  • Final device status, including whether the scanner was returned to service or removed from service

Final Thought

A nonresponsive probe scan button should be approached logically: protect the patient first, verify the complaint, rule out power and connection issues, inspect for external damage or contamination, and escalate when the fault remains. Clear CCR documentation helps the next technician understand what was tested and why the device was removed from service.

That is successful troubleshooting.
