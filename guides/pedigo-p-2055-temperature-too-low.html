Pedigo P-2055

Temperature Too Low

Asset Type

Blanket Warmer

Manufacturer

Pedigo

Model

P-2055

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting low blanket warmer temperature caused by power, settings, door seal, loading, airflow, or heating performance issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Do not use blankets from the warmer if staff report they are not reaching the expected temperature.

Action:

  • Remove the blanket warmer from active clinical use if warmed blankets are needed for patient care and the unit cannot maintain temperature.
  • Provide blankets from another approved warmer if available.
  • Confirm staff are not compensating by increasing settings beyond facility policy.

Expected outcome: Patient care continues without relying on a warmer that may not be maintaining proper temperature.

Why it matters: Under-warmed blankets may affect patient comfort and thermal support, while improper setting changes can create burn or overheating risk.

Verify the Reported Temperature Problem

Confirm whether the issue is an actual low-temperature condition or a misunderstanding of normal warm-up behavior.

Action:

  • Ask staff what temperature was expected, what temperature was observed, and how long the unit had been warming.
  • Check the displayed temperature or temperature indicator.
  • Compare the set temperature to the actual chamber temperature if the unit displays both.

Expected outcome: You confirm whether the warmer is truly below the set temperature.

If the unit is still warming up after being loaded, recently powered on, or frequently opened, allow reasonable recovery time and recheck.

If the temperature recovers normally, return the unit to service and stop.

Check Power Source and Cord Connection

Verify that the warmer has stable AC power.

Action:

  • Confirm the power cord is fully seated and not damaged.
  • Check that the outlet is live.
  • Verify the unit is not plugged into a switched outlet that may have been turned off.
  • Inspect for loose plugs, damaged prongs, overheated connectors, or signs of arcing.

Expected outcome: The unit remains powered without flickering, resets, or intermittent shutdowns.

If power is restored and the warmer returns to the correct temperature, document the cause and stop.

Confirm the Temperature Setting

Check that the set temperature is appropriate and has not been lowered.

Action:

  • Review the temperature control setting.
  • Confirm it matches the department’s approved blanket warming range.
  • Do not exceed facility policy or manufacturer temperature limits.

Expected outcome: The warmer is set correctly for blanket warming.

If the setting was too low, correct it, allow the warmer to stabilize, verify temperature recovery, and stop.

Inspect Door Closure and Gasket Seal

A leaking door seal can prevent the chamber from reaching or maintaining temperature.

Action:

  • Check that the door closes fully.
  • Inspect the gasket for cracks, gaps, compression damage, debris, or sections pulling away.
  • Confirm blankets or packaging are not blocking the door from sealing.

Expected outcome: The door closes evenly and the gasket makes full contact.

If a blocked door or debris issue is corrected and temperature recovers, return the unit to service and stop.

Check Blanket Loading and Air Circulation

Overloading can block airflow and cause slow or uneven heating.

Action:

  • Inspect how blankets are loaded.
  • Remove excessive blankets if the chamber is packed tightly.
  • Make sure blankets are not blocking vents, air channels, sensors, or circulation paths.

Expected outcome: Air can circulate through the chamber and around stored blankets.

If reducing the load allows the warmer to recover to set temperature, educate staff on proper loading and stop.

Check for Frequent Door Opening

Frequent access can make the warmer appear weak even when it is functioning normally.

Action:

  • Ask staff whether the door has been opened repeatedly.
  • Observe whether the door is left open during stocking or blanket removal.
  • Allow the unit to recover with the door closed.

Expected outcome: Temperature rises back toward the set point once the door remains closed.

If the warmer recovers normally, document environmental/use-related cause and stop.

Inspect for External Airflow or Room Conditions

Cold drafts or poor placement can affect temperature recovery.

Action:

  • Check whether the warmer is near an air vent, doorway, exterior wall, or high-traffic area.
  • Confirm the unit has proper clearance around it.
  • Make sure vents are not blocked by carts, walls, supplies, or dust buildup.

Expected outcome: The warmer has adequate ventilation and is not exposed to excessive cooling airflow.

If correcting placement or blocked ventilation resolves the issue, return the unit to service and stop.

Listen for Abnormal Operation

A low-temperature complaint may be related to heater, fan, relay, or thermostat performance.

Action:

  • Listen for unusual clicking, buzzing, fan noise changes, or silence when heat should be active.
  • Check for unusual heat, burning smell, discoloration, or signs of overheating.
  • Do not continue troubleshooting if there is smoke, odor, excessive heat, or electrical damage.

Expected outcome: The unit operates without abnormal sounds, smells, or visible damage.

If unsafe signs are present, remove the unit from service immediately.

Verify Temperature with an Approved Method

If the warmer appears functional but staff still report low temperature, confirm with an appropriate thermometer or facility-approved temperature verification method.

Action:

  • Allow the unit to stabilize with the door closed.
  • Measure chamber temperature according to department practice.
  • Compare the reading to the set temperature and acceptable tolerance.

Expected outcome: The measured chamber temperature agrees with the set temperature within an acceptable range.

If the temperature is correct, document verification and return the unit to service.

If the temperature remains low, remove the unit from service for bench evaluation.

If the Problem Persists

If power, settings, door seal, loading, airflow, placement, and recovery time have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Possible internal causes may include a faulty heating element, temperature sensor, thermostat/control board, relay, fan, wiring issue, or calibration-related problem.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not continue clinical use of a blanket warmer that cannot maintain safe and expected temperature.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a blanket warmer while staff are actively depending on it for patient warming. Move patient care needs to another approved warmer first, then troubleshoot the unit safely.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported the Pedigo P-2055 blanket warmer was not heating blankets adequately and chamber temperature appeared too low."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Checked power, set temperature, door seal, loading, airflow, and recovery time; unit remained below expected temperature after stabilization."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the Pedigo P-2055 from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for bench evaluation of heating and temperature control components."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested
  • Power cord condition
  • Set temperature
  • Displayed or measured temperature
  • Warm-up time allowed
  • Door gasket condition
  • Blanket load condition
  • Vent or airflow obstruction
  • Door opening frequency
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Unusual sounds
  • Unusual heat or smell
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Low-temperature complaints on blanket warmers should be approached logically. Start with safe use, power, settings, loading, door seal, and airflow before suspecting internal failure. If the warmer cannot maintain temperature after external causes are ruled out, removing it from service is the correct Clinical Engineering decision. Clear CCR documentation protects patients, staff, and the equipment history.

That is successful troubleshooting.
