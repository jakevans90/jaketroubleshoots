ZOLL R Series

Error: ECG FAULT 200

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

ZOLL

Model

R Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot ECG FAULT 200 No ECG application available on a ZOLL R Series defibrillator. This error indicates the ECG application is unavailable, which may prevent ECG monitoring, lead selection, normal startup behavior, or reliable defibrillator readiness.

Because ECG monitoring supports patient assessment, synchronized cardioversion, pacing support, and emergency readiness, this fault should be handled as a patient safety issue until the unit is evaluated and verified.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the ZOLL R Series from patient use if ECG monitoring, pacing, cardioversion, or defibrillation readiness is affected.
  • Transfer the patient to another monitor/defibrillator if active monitoring or therapy is needed.
  • Do not rely on the unit for patient care while ECG FAULT 200 is active.
  • Confirm that an alternate defibrillator or monitor is available before continuing troubleshooting.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Power the unit on and confirm the exact displayed message is ECG FAULT 200.
  • Check whether the error appears at startup, when entering ECG monitoring, when selecting leads, after a self-test, or intermittently during use.
  • Look for associated symptoms such as no ECG waveform, unavailable lead selection, ECG monitoring failure, startup fault, failure to complete normal operation, or other system messages.
  • Document when the fault occurs and whether it is repeatable.

Check ECG Cable and Lead Setup First

  • Inspect the ECG trunk cable and lead wires for bent pins, cracked connectors, contamination, fluid intrusion, or damaged insulation.
  • Reconnect the ECG cable securely.
  • If available, test with a known-good ECG cable and lead set.
  • Confirm that external ECG accessories are not contributing to confusion during troubleshooting.

Check Electrodes and Simulator Input

  • Use a known-good ECG simulator or fresh electrodes if available.
  • Connect the unit to an ECG simulator and confirm whether any ECG waveform appears.
  • Change lead selection if possible.
  • Observe whether the fault clears or remains.

Restart the Device

  • Turn the unit off.
  • Disconnect external power.
  • Remove the battery if appropriate for local process.
  • Wait briefly, then reinstall the battery, reconnect AC power, and power the device back on.
  • Observe startup behavior and confirm whether ECG monitoring becomes available.
  • If the restart clears the fault, complete operational checks per local procedure before returning the device to service.

Check Battery and AC Power Stability

  • Connect the device to a known-good AC outlet.
  • Verify AC power indicator behavior.
  • Install a known-good battery if available.
  • Restart the unit again using stable power.
  • Confirm whether the unit powers up normally without ECG FAULT 200.

Check for Recent Software, Service, or Configuration Changes

  • Ask whether the unit recently had software loading, configuration changes, main board replacement, MCU-related service, a failed software update, unexpected shutdown during update, or battery depletion during service.
  • Document any recent service activity that occurred before the error appeared.
  • Treat the fault as software-related if the error remains after basic external checks.

Do Not Treat This Like a Simple ECG Lead Failure

  • Do not continue swapping electrodes, pads, or cables once basic external checks are complete and the error remains.
  • Recognize that ECG FAULT 200 indicates the ECG application is not available to the system.
  • Shift troubleshooting from accessory checks to software or qualified service escalation.

Escalate for Software Reload

  • If ECG FAULT 200 remains after power cycling, power checks, and basic ECG accessory checks, remove the unit from service.
  • Label the device Out of Service.
  • Send the unit for bench evaluation or qualified repair.
  • Follow approved service process for reloading MCU software and ECG application software.
  • Do not return the unit to patient care until repair and operational verification are complete.

If the Problem Persists

If ECG FAULT 200 continues after external checks and restart, external and common causes have been ruled out. Persistent ECG FAULT 200 strongly indicates the ECG application software is missing, unavailable, corrupted, or not loading correctly.

The device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair, bench evaluation, or approved software reload. Do not return the unit to patient care until the software issue is corrected and full operational testing is completed.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ECG FAULT 200 on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup monitor/defibrillator first. A defibrillator with unavailable ECG application software should not be trusted for monitoring, synchronized therapy support, or emergency readiness.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"ZOLL R Series defibrillator displayed ECG FAULT 200 at startup. ECG monitoring function was unavailable and device could not be confirmed ready for patient use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"External ECG cable, lead set, power source, and restart checks did not clear the fault. Error indicated no ECG application available, consistent with software or application loading issue."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed device from service and labeled Out of Service. Sent for bench evaluation and approved reload of MCU software and ECG application software. Device not returned to service pending repair and operational verification."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

ECG FAULT 200 should be treated as a software-related ECG application availability problem, not a routine lead or cable issue. Clinical Engineering should confirm the error, rule out simple external causes, avoid unnecessary internal assumptions, and escalate for proper software reload when the fault persists. Clear CCR documentation protects patient safety and supports accurate repair history.

That is successful troubleshooting.
