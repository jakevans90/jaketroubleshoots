Mindray BeneVision N Series

Alarm: XX V UNDERVOLTAGE

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

BeneVision N Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot a Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor displaying Alarm: XX V UNDERVOLTAGE. This alarm indicates that a monitored internal voltage rail is below the expected range. The issue may be caused by unstable input power, a weak or faulty battery, an accessory or module loading the system, a power supply board issue, or a main control board fault.

An undervoltage alarm should be treated seriously because unstable internal power can affect monitor reliability, parameter availability, battery charging, module communication, display operation, and alarm performance.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not rely on a monitor with an active or recurring undervoltage alarm for primary patient monitoring.
  • Remove the monitor from patient use if the alarm is active, recurring, or associated with rebooting, freezing, lost parameters, display flicker, or abnormal power behavior.
  • Transfer the patient to another verified monitor before troubleshooting.
  • Confirm that required monitoring such as ECG, SpO2, NIBP, invasive pressure, temperature, and alarm monitoring is maintained by another device if needed.

The expected outcome is that patient monitoring continues safely without depending on a monitor reporting an internal voltage problem.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact alarm shown on the monitor is Alarm: XX V UNDERVOLTAGE.
  • Observe whether the alarm appears at startup, during normal monitoring, after connecting modules or accessories, while running on battery, while connected to AC power, or intermittently during use.
  • Look for related symptoms such as unexpected rebooting, display flicker, modules dropping offline, battery not charging, loss of parameters, frozen controls, abnormal power LEDs, or recurring startup failures.
  • Document the alarm behavior exactly as reported and observed.

This confirms that the issue is a real voltage-related system alarm instead of a vague user complaint or unrelated monitoring concern.

3. Check AC Power Source

  • Verify the monitor is connected to a known-good hospital-grade outlet.
  • Check that the power cord is fully seated at the wall and at the monitor power input.
  • Inspect the power cord for cuts, bent prongs, loose fit, damaged insulation, or signs of overheating.
  • Test the outlet with another device or outlet tester if appropriate under facility procedure.
  • Move the monitor to a known-good outlet and retest.

The monitor should power normally from a verified AC source without the undervoltage alarm returning. Poor AC input, a loose cord, or a bad outlet can create power instability before the monitor is blamed.

4. Check the Power Cord and External Power Connection

  • Try a known-good compatible power cord.
  • Confirm the AC power indicator behaves normally when the monitor is plugged in.
  • Gently check for looseness at the monitor AC inlet.
  • Do not use the monitor if the inlet feels loose, damaged, burned, intermittent, or physically unstable.

If the alarm clears after replacing the cord or correcting the external connection, the issue may have been caused by a failing cord or poor connection.

5. Restart the Monitor

  • Remove the monitor from patient use before restarting.
  • Power the monitor down using the normal shutdown method.
  • Disconnect AC power.
  • Allow the monitor to sit briefly.
  • Reconnect AC power and restart the monitor.
  • Observe the boot process and confirm whether the alarm returns.

A restart may clear a temporary power management fault or software-detected voltage event. If the alarm clears, continue functional testing and observation before returning the monitor to service.

6. Test on AC Power Only

  • Connect the monitor to verified AC power.
  • Confirm the AC indicator is active.
  • Check whether the undervoltage alarm occurs while the monitor is powered by AC.
  • If the battery is removable and facility procedure allows, evaluate whether the issue changes with the battery removed or replaced.

If the alarm only occurs during battery operation, the issue may involve the battery, charging path, or internal power regulation during battery use.

7. Check Battery Status and Charging Behavior

  • Check the battery charge indicator.
  • Confirm whether the battery charges when AC power is connected.
  • Look for battery-related messages, charging failures, swelling, heat, or abnormal battery behavior.
  • Try a known-good approved battery if available.
  • Retest the monitor after correcting any obvious battery issue.

A weak or faulty battery can contribute to low-voltage conditions, especially during startup, transport, or when the monitor is operating with connected modules.

8. Remove Accessories and External Loads

  • Disconnect nonessential external accessories.
  • Remove optional parameter modules if applicable and safe to do so.
  • Disconnect network, USB, recorder, external display, or other nonessential connections.
  • Restart the monitor with the minimum required configuration.
  • Reconnect accessories one at a time while watching for the alarm to return.

If the monitor remains stable with accessories removed, or the alarm returns when a specific module or accessory is connected, that accessory may be loading the power rail or triggering the fault condition.

9. Inspect for Physical Damage or Contamination

  • Inspect the monitor housing, power inlet, battery compartment, module slots, and connector areas.
  • Look for liquid intrusion, cracked housing, burn marks, corrosion, bent pins, debris in module bays, drop damage, unusual heat, or abnormal odor.
  • Do not return the monitor to patient use if damage, overheating, contamination, or intermittent power behavior is found.

Damage or contamination can cause short circuits, unstable voltage rails, internal connection issues, or power board failure.

10. Perform a Basic Functional Check

  • Verify the monitor boots normally.
  • Confirm the display, touchscreen or knob, speaker, alarm light, battery indicator, and AC indicator function correctly.
  • Connect a simulator or approved test accessories as available.
  • Check common parameters such as ECG, SpO2, NIBP, temperature, and IBP if applicable.
  • Confirm alarms annunciate properly.
  • Continue observing the monitor to confirm the alarm does not return.

Clearing the alarm alone is not enough. The monitor must still be verified as safe and functional before return to service.

11. Determine Whether the Issue Is Device-Related

  • If the alarm returns after verified AC power, cord check, battery evaluation, accessory isolation, restart, and inspection, suspect an internal device issue.
  • Possible internal causes include a faulty power supply board, faulty main control board, internal power regulation failure, intermittent internal connection, or voltage rail instability under load.
  • Do not perform internal disassembly while the device is in clinical use.

Persistent XX V UNDERVOLTAGE means a monitored internal voltage rail remains below the expected range after external causes have been ruled out.

If the Problem Persists

If the XX V UNDERVOLTAGE alarm persists after restart, verified AC power, power cord check, battery check, accessory isolation, and basic inspection, remove the monitor from service.

  • Document that external causes were ruled out.
  • Remove the device from patient use.
  • Label the monitor Out of Service.
  • Send the device for repair or bench evaluation.
  • Escalate for approved service evaluation of the power supply board, main control board, battery charging path, or internal power regulation circuit as appropriate.

Persistent undervoltage alarms can indicate internal power failure, and continued use may create patient monitoring risk.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot an active undervoltage alarm while the monitor is being used as the primary patient monitor. Move the patient to a backup verified monitor first, especially if parameters are dropping out, the monitor is rebooting, or alarms are unreliable.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor displayed Alarm: XX V UNDERVOLTAGE during use. Staff reported the monitor had a voltage-related alarm and was not trusted for active patient monitoring."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified AC outlet and power cord, restarted monitor, checked battery charging behavior, and isolated external accessories. Alarm persisted after external checks, indicating likely internal power supply board or main control board fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed monitor from patient use, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench repair/evaluation. Recommended internal power supply and main control board evaluation per approved service process."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

An XX V UNDERVOLTAGE alarm should be treated as more than a nuisance message. It points to a power rail problem that may affect monitor reliability. Start with patient safety, verify the alarm, rule out simple external causes, and escalate when the issue remains. Clear documentation protects patients, staff, and the repair process.

That is successful troubleshooting.
