Skytron Stellar Surgical Light

LED Module or Cluster Failure

Asset Type

Surgical Light

Manufacturer

Skytron

Model

Stellar

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Skytron Stellar surgical light exhibits burned-out LED modules, clusters, or uneven light patterns. These issues can reduce illumination during procedures. The focus is on external, easily verifiable checks before assuming LED module or internal electronic failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Supply

  • Confirm the light head is connected to a powered outlet.
  • Check breaker panels and ensure the circuit is active.
  • Why: LED failure may appear as module burnout when the light simply isn’t receiving power.

Check Main Control Interface

  • Operate the light using wall-mounted control panels or hand controls.
  • Ensure brightness settings are at an adequate level.
  • Why: Communication errors may mimic LED failure.

Inspect LED Indicators

  • Observe any error codes or warning lights on the light head.
  • Note patterns of LEDs that do not illuminate.
  • Why: Identifies whether failure is isolated to specific clusters.

Visual Inspection of Light Head

  • Look for physical damage, discoloration, or smoke residue.
  • Inspect connectors for secure seating.
  • Why: Loose connectors can cause cluster failures.

Swap or Test Light Head Modules

  • If the system allows, replace the suspected LED module or cluster with a known good one.
  • Why: Confirms whether the failure is module-specific or systemic.

Check Environmental Factors

  • Ensure room temperature and ventilation are adequate.
  • Why: Overheating can prematurely damage LEDs.

Review Manufacturer Logs (if available)

  • Check for LED error codes or alerts logged in the Stellar control software.
  • Why: Helps confirm the scope and location of failure.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out; the LED module or cluster likely requires repair or replacement.

  • Remove the surgical light from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send to manufacturer or authorized service for LED module replacement.

Stopping at this point is proper troubleshooting—do not attempt board-level repairs.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not attempt LED module troubleshooting during active procedures.

Ensure alternate lighting is available before removing the Stellar light from service.

Always confirm patient safety first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Light head shows dark or uneven illumination; some LED clusters not illuminating.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Visual inspection confirmed LED cluster failure; power and control inputs verified good.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent to manufacturer for module replacement.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and confirmed powered.
  • Control panel operation verified.
  • LED error indicators logged.
  • Any unusual heat or discoloration noted.
  • Final device status: Out of Service, pending LED module replacement.

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting balances patient safety, methodical checks, and knowing when to escalate. Documenting observations and actions ensures accountability and guides repair decisions. Replacing LED modules promptly restores reliable surgical illumination.

That is successful troubleshooting.
