Mindray BeneVision N Series

Alarm: TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS PULLED OUT

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

BeneVision N Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS PULLED OUT alarm on a Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor. This alarm indicates that the monitor does not detect the transport module battery, which may affect backup power, transport readiness, and safe portable operation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the patient is being monitored safely before troubleshooting.
  • Verify the monitor is connected to AC power if available.
  • If the monitor is being used for transport, do not rely on it until the battery condition is corrected.
  • Use another verified monitor if battery operation is required and the alarm cannot be cleared quickly.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact alarm displayed is TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS PULLED OUT.
  • Note whether the alarm appears at startup, while connected to AC power, after battery installation, during transport module use, or intermittently during movement.
  • Check whether the monitor recognizes any battery capacity or battery status.
  • Look for any related battery, power, or transport module alarms.

Confirm the Battery Is Physically Installed

  • Verify that the transport module battery is installed.
  • Remove and reseat the battery if accessible without forcing it.
  • Confirm the battery is fully seated and latched properly.
  • Inspect for anything preventing the battery from locking into place.
  • If the alarm clears after reseating the battery, monitor the device briefly, confirm normal battery status, and complete a basic functional check.

Check for Battery or Bay Contamination

  • Remove the battery if safe to do so.
  • Check the battery contacts for dirt, residue, corrosion, bent contacts, or physical damage.
  • Check the battery bay for debris, broken plastic, loose parts, or obstruction.
  • Do not scrape, bend, or force contacts.
  • Clean only according to approved facility procedures.

Restart the Monitor

  • Notify clinical staff before restarting the monitor.
  • Make sure patient monitoring is maintained by another method if needed.
  • Power cycle the monitor according to normal facility workflow.
  • Allow the monitor to fully restart.
  • Check whether the TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS PULLED OUT alarm returns.

Test With a Known-Good Battery

  • Install a known-good compatible transport module battery if available.
  • Confirm the known-good battery seats properly.
  • Restart the monitor if needed.
  • Observe whether the alarm clears.
  • If the known-good battery works, remove the original battery from service according to facility battery handling procedure.

Check Whether the Issue Follows the Battery

  • If safe and allowed by facility workflow, test the suspect battery in another compatible BeneVision N Series setup.
  • Observe whether the same battery detection alarm appears.
  • If the alarm follows the battery, the battery is likely defective.
  • If the alarm stays with the original monitor, the monitor, transport module, battery bay, or battery detection circuit may require repair.

Inspect for Physical Damage

  • Check the monitor, transport module, battery latch, and battery area for impact damage.
  • Look for cracked plastic, broken latch points, loose battery fit, liquid contamination, damaged contacts, heat, odor, or swelling.
  • Gently verify that the battery does not move excessively when installed.
  • Do not continue using the monitor for transport if the battery cannot remain secure.

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm continues after confirming the battery is installed, reseating the battery, checking contacts, restarting the monitor, and testing with a known-good battery, the likely cause is a failed battery, battery contact issue, transport module issue, or internal battery detection problem.

At this point, external causes have been ruled out. Remove the device from service if battery operation is required or if the alarm cannot be cleared. Label the device Out of Service and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Do not continue troubleshooting beyond basic external checks if the alarm remains active.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm on an active patient when battery operation or transport use is required. Move the patient to a backup monitor first, then troubleshoot the BeneVision N Series monitor after patient monitoring is safely maintained.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor displaying TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS PULLED OUT alarm. Staff reported transport module battery not detected."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Transport module battery was removed, not fully seated, faulty, or not detected by the monitor. Battery seating, battery bay, and external contact checks were performed."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Verified alarm condition, reseated transport module battery, inspected battery contacts and bay, restarted monitor, and tested with known-good battery. Monitor was returned to service after alarm cleared and functional check passed. If alarm persisted, device was removed from service and sent for bench evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery alarms should be treated as patient safety issues because they affect backup power and transport readiness. Start with the simple checks first: confirm the alarm, verify the battery is installed, reseat it, inspect contacts, restart the monitor, and compare with a known-good battery. If the alarm persists after those steps, remove the device from service and escalate for repair. Clear documentation helps show whether the issue was a battery, connection, or likely internal detection failure.

That is successful troubleshooting.
