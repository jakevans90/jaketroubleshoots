Nihon Kohden NKV-330

Alarm: Battery Empty

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

NKV-330

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Battery Empty alarm on a Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator. This alarm indicates a critically low battery reserve condition while the ventilator is connected to AC power, such as the main battery being below 10% or the backup battery being below 30%.

This condition matters because the ventilator may not have adequate battery backup if AC power is interrupted. The goal is to keep the patient safely ventilated, confirm AC power, charge or replace the main battery as appropriate, and remove the ventilator from service if the battery condition does not recover.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot battery-related alarms in a way that risks loss of ventilation.
  • Confirm the ventilator is connected to AC power.
  • Verify the patient is being ventilated appropriately.
  • Have clinical staff assess the patient and prepare an alternate ventilator if the alarm cannot be corrected quickly.
  • Do not disconnect AC power while the Battery Empty alarm is active.

Expected outcome: The patient remains safely ventilated while the battery condition is evaluated.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the displayed alarm is exactly: Alarm: Battery Empty.
  • Observe whether the ventilator is connected to AC power.
  • Check whether any related battery or power alarms are also active.
  • Confirm whether the alarm appeared at startup, during use, or after the ventilator was unplugged and reconnected.

Expected outcome: The Battery Empty alarm is confirmed and associated with a low main battery or backup battery condition.

3. Confirm AC Power Is Actually Present

  • Check that the AC power cord is fully seated at the wall outlet and at the ventilator.
  • Verify the outlet has power.
  • Confirm the ventilator shows AC power operation, not battery-only operation.
  • Try a known-good outlet if needed.

Expected outcome: The ventilator remains powered by AC and is able to charge the batteries.

4. Identify Which Battery Condition Is Likely Causing the Alarm

  • Check the battery status indicators or battery information screen if available.
  • Look for a main battery charge below 10%.
  • Look for a backup battery charge below 30%.
  • Check for battery not charging, battery not detected, or abnormal battery status messages.

Expected outcome: Battery status confirms whether the main battery, backup battery, or both are critically low.

5. Allow the Batteries to Charge on AC Power

  • Keep the ventilator connected to AC power.
  • Allow the main and backup batteries to charge.
  • Monitor whether the Battery Empty alarm clears as charge increases.
  • Do not remove the ventilator from AC power during charging.

Expected outcome: Battery charge increases and the alarm clears once the battery level is no longer critically low.

6. Check the Main Battery Seating and Condition

  • With the ventilator connected to AC power, inspect the main battery installation.
  • Confirm the battery is fully seated and latched.
  • Check for visible battery damage, swelling, contamination, or damaged contacts.
  • If a known-good charged main battery is available, replace the main battery while keeping the ventilator connected to AC power.

Expected outcome: A properly seated, charged main battery is recognized by the ventilator.

7. Do Not Remove the Main Battery Without AC Power

  • Before replacing the main battery, verify AC power is connected and stable.
  • Keep the ventilator plugged into AC throughout battery replacement.
  • Do not remove the main battery if the ventilator is not on reliable AC power.

Expected outcome: The ventilator remains powered during main battery replacement.

8. Check for Charging Failure

  • After the ventilator has remained on AC power, confirm the battery percentage increases.
  • Check the AC power connection.
  • Check main battery seating.
  • Review battery age or condition if known.
  • Check battery status messages and charging indicator behavior.

Expected outcome: Battery charge should gradually increase while the ventilator is connected to AC power.

9. Determine Whether the Issue Is Battery-Related or Device-Related

  • If available, test with a known-good charged main battery.
  • Observe whether the alarm clears.
  • If the main battery charges normally, document the condition and continue monitoring per facility policy.
  • If the main battery does not charge or the backup battery remains low, remove the ventilator from service.

Expected outcome: The issue is narrowed to a depleted battery, faulty main battery, backup battery issue, or ventilator charging problem.

10. Return to Service Only When Battery Status Is Acceptable

  • Confirm the Battery Empty alarm has cleared.
  • Verify AC power operation.
  • Confirm battery status is acceptable.
  • Perform any required operational checks per facility policy.

Expected outcome: The ventilator operates normally without active battery alarms.

If the Problem Persists

If the Battery Empty alarm remains after the ventilator has been connected to known-good AC power and the main battery has been charged, reseated, or replaced, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent Battery Empty alarms may indicate a failed main battery, backup battery failure, charging circuit issue, battery communication issue, or internal power system fault.

Remove the ventilator from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient when ventilation could be interrupted. Move the patient to a backup ventilator first if reliable battery backup cannot be confirmed or if the alarm cannot be corrected quickly.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator reported Alarm: Battery Empty while connected to AC power. Battery status indicated a critically low reserve condition."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Main battery charge was below acceptable level and required charging while ventilator remained connected to AC power."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Confirmed AC power was present, kept ventilator plugged in, charged batteries, verified Battery Empty alarm cleared, and confirmed battery status acceptable before returning device to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery Empty on the Nihon Kohden NKV-330 should be treated as a patient safety concern because the ventilator may not have adequate battery reserve if AC power is lost. Start with AC power and battery status, keep the ventilator plugged in during battery replacement, and escalate when charging or battery recognition does not recover.

That is successful troubleshooting.
