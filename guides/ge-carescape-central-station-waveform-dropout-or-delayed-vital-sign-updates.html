GE Healthcare CARESCAPE Central Station

Waveform Dropout or Delayed Vital Sign Updates

Asset Type

Central Monitoring Station

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE Central Station

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting intermittent waveform loss, delayed parameter updates, or slow bedside monitor data refresh at the central station.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Waveform dropout or delayed vital sign updates can affect alarm awareness and patient visibility at the central station.

Action:

  • Confirm the patient is being actively monitored at the bedside monitor.
  • Notify clinical staff that central monitoring data may be delayed or intermittent.
  • Have staff rely on the bedside monitor until central station display accuracy is confirmed.
  • Do not assume the central station is showing real-time patient data until verified.

Expected outcome: Patient monitoring continues safely while the central display issue is investigated.

Why it matters: The bedside monitor is the immediate source of patient data. A central station display delay may create a false sense of visibility.

Verify the Reported Behavior

Confirm whether the issue is waveform dropout, delayed numeric updates, missing parameters, or a complete bed communication loss.

Action:

  • Ask staff which bed, waveform, or parameter is affected.
  • Check whether ECG, SpO2, NIBP, invasive pressure, respiration, or all parameters are delayed.
  • Compare the central station display to the bedside monitor in real time.

Expected outcome: The issue is narrowed to one parameter, one bed, multiple beds, or the entire central station.

If the central station updates correctly after comparison and no dropout occurs, document the finding and stop.

Check the Bedside Monitor First

Verify that the bedside monitor itself has stable waveforms and current vital signs.

Action:

  • Go to the affected bedside monitor.
  • Confirm the waveform is present and stable locally.
  • Check for loose ECG leads, disconnected SpO2 probes, inactive modules, or paused measurements.
  • Verify that the bedside monitor is not in standby, discharge, transport, demo, or disconnected mode.

Expected outcome: The bedside monitor should display clean, current patient data.

If the bedside monitor is not producing stable data, correct the bedside issue first. The central station cannot display data that the source monitor is not providing.

Confirm the Issue Is Not Patient Lead or Accessory Related

A waveform dropout at the central station may be caused by poor signal quality at the patient connection.

Action:

  • Inspect ECG lead wires, trunk cable, SpO2 sensor, pressure cables, and other connected accessories.
  • Look for intermittent movement-related dropout.
  • Ask clinical staff whether the issue happens during patient movement, repositioning, bathing, transport, or lead changes.
  • Swap a suspected external accessory if available and safe to do so.

Expected outcome: The waveform should remain stable at the bedside and central station after poor accessories or connections are corrected.

If replacing or reseating the external accessory resolves the dropout, document the accessory issue and stop.

Check Bed Assignment and Patient Sector

Confirm the bedside monitor is assigned to the correct bed and is displayed in the correct central station sector.

Action:

  • Verify the bed label shown at the bedside monitor.
  • Compare it to the bed label at the CARESCAPE Central Station.
  • Confirm the patient sector is not showing an old bed, wrong unit, discharged patient, or duplicate bed association.

Expected outcome: The bedside monitor and central station should show the same bed association.

If the wrong bed is assigned, correct the bed assignment through the approved clinical workflow or escalate to the appropriate system administrator.

Check for a Single-Bed Versus Multi-Bed Problem

Determine whether the issue affects only one monitored bed or multiple beds.

Action:

  • Review other patient sectors on the CARESCAPE Central Station.
  • Ask staff if other beds have delayed updates or waveform dropout.
  • Compare one known-good bedside monitor to the central station.

Expected outcome:

  • If only one bed is affected, focus on that bedside monitor, cable path, network jack, or bed assignment.
  • If multiple beds are affected, suspect a central station, network, switch, gateway, or system communication issue.

Inspect Network and Physical Connections

External network problems can cause delayed updates, intermittent waveform loss, or communication dropouts.

Action:

  • Check the bedside monitor network cable if wired.
  • Verify the cable is fully seated at the monitor, wall jack, and any approved local network connection.
  • Inspect for damaged cables, loose connectors, bent clips, or cables under tension.
  • Check the central station network connection if accessible and safe.
  • Do not move network cables between unknown ports unless following site-approved network labeling and configuration practices.

Expected outcome: Network cabling should be secure, undamaged, and connected to the correct labeled ports.

If a loose or damaged cable is found and corrected, verify stable real-time waveform updates before returning the setup to normal use.

Check for Local Display or Workstation Lag

Sometimes the patient data is being received, but the central station display is slow, frozen, or not refreshing properly.

Action:

  • Look for delayed mouse response, frozen patient sectors, slow screen redraw, or repeated display lag.
  • Check whether only one display is affected or all CARESCAPE Central Station screens are affected.
  • Confirm whether alarms and numerics are updating while waveforms lag, or whether all data is delayed.

Expected outcome: The central station should respond normally and refresh patient data without visible lag.

If the central station appears frozen or sluggish, follow your facility-approved restart or escalation process. Do not reboot a central monitoring station without notifying clinical staff and ensuring patient monitoring coverage.

Review Alarm and Communication Indicators

Communication indicators can help distinguish a signal issue from a network or central station issue.

Action:

  • Look for monitor offline messages, communication loss indicators, delayed alarm banners, missing waveforms, or stale numeric values.
  • Check whether alarm annunciation still occurs when waveform dropout happens.
  • Confirm whether the bedside monitor shows any network or central monitoring communication alerts.

Expected outcome: Communication alarms or offline indicators help identify whether the issue is network-related rather than patient-signal-related.

If alarms are delayed or not annunciating, treat the issue as higher priority and escalate immediately.

Verify Recent Changes

Recent changes often explain central monitoring communication problems.

Action:

  • Ask whether the bedside monitor was recently moved, replaced, discharged, admitted, transferred, or reassigned.
  • Check whether the central station, network switch, CARESCAPE Gateway, or monitoring system had recent maintenance.
  • Ask if the issue began after a power outage, network event, software update, or unit move.

Expected outcome: Recent changes may identify a configuration, assignment, or network path issue.

If the issue started after a known system change, escalate to the monitoring system administrator, network team, or vendor support as appropriate.

Perform a Controlled Recheck

After correcting any external issue, verify that updates are stable.

Action:

  • Compare the bedside monitor and central station for several minutes.
  • Confirm waveforms are continuous.
  • Confirm numerics update within the expected clinical workflow.
  • Confirm alarms display and annunciate at the central station according to facility policy.

Expected outcome: The central station should show current, stable patient data matching the bedside monitor.

If the waveform and vital sign updates remain stable, document the resolution and return the system to service.

If the Problem Persists

If waveform dropout or delayed vital sign updates continue after bedside signal quality, accessories, bed assignment, cabling, and obvious workstation issues have been checked, common external causes have been ruled out.

The issue may involve internal central station software, network infrastructure, communication services, CARESCAPE Gateway configuration, switch issues, or bedside monitor communication hardware.

The affected device or monitoring path should be:

  • Removed from active clinical reliance when safe
  • Labeled Out of Service if the device itself is suspected
  • Escalated for repair, bench evaluation, network review, or vendor support

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Central monitoring communication issues can affect alarm visibility and should not be treated as minor if real-time patient data is unreliable.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot waveform dropout by leaving staff dependent on an unreliable central station. Confirm bedside monitoring is active, notify clinical staff, and ensure patients are watched locally until central monitoring reliability is restored.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported intermittent ECG waveform dropout and delayed vital sign updates for Bed 12 on the GE CARESCAPE Central Station."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Bedside monitor waveforms were stable, but the network cable at the bedside monitor was loose and caused intermittent central station communication."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated the bedside network cable, verified correct bed assignment, confirmed stable waveform and numeric updates at the central station, and returned monitoring path to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet or UPS status checked if central station power was involved
  • Affected bed number or unit
  • Whether one bed or multiple beds were affected
  • Bedside monitor waveform status
  • Central station waveform behavior
  • Vital sign update delay observed
  • Any monitor offline or communication messages
  • Network cable or wall jack checked
  • Accessories swapped or reseated
  • Alarm behavior during dropout
  • Recent bed move, discharge, admit, or system change
  • Indicator lights observed on monitor, network jack, switch, workstation, or connected equipment
  • Final device or monitoring path status

Final Thought

Waveform dropout and delayed vital sign updates should be approached logically from the patient outward. Confirm patient safety first, then verify bedside data, accessories, bed assignment, network connections, and central station behavior. If the issue remains unresolved, escalate appropriately because central monitoring reliability directly affects alarm awareness and clinical response. Clear CCR documentation helps identify repeat problems and supports future repair decisions.

That is successful troubleshooting.
