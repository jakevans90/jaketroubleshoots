Smiths Medical Level 1 Series H-1000 / H-1200

Temperature Sensor Calibration Drift

Asset Type

Fluid & Blood Warmer

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Level 1 Series H-1000 / H-1200

What This Guide Helps With

Displayed temperature does not match expected output, drifting readings, or inconsistent warming performance caused by setup issues, disposables, or sensor-related concerns.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from active patient use.
  • Transfer to an alternate warming method before troubleshooting.
  • Expected: No patient connected during evaluation.

Verify Reported Issue

  • Compare the displayed temperature to expected output or external thermometer if available.
  • Expected: Confirm whether true drift exists vs perception.

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn the unit off, wait 10–15 seconds, then power back on.
  • Why it matters: Clears temporary sensor or software anomalies.
  • If readings return to normal → issue resolved.

Check Set Temperature and Mode

  • Verify correct temperature setpoint (~37–41°C typical) and proper operating mode.
  • Why it matters: Incorrect settings can appear as drift.
  • If corrected and stable → issue resolved.

Inspect Disposable Cassette / Set

  • Ensure cassette is fully seated, properly latched, and not damaged.
  • Expected: Secure fit, no leaks or deformities.
  • Why it matters: Poor thermal transfer can mimic sensor drift.
  • If corrected → recheck temperature.

Verify Fluid Flow Conditions

  • Confirm appropriate flow rate (not excessively high or intermittent).
  • Why it matters: High flow reduces heating efficiency and may appear as inaccurate temperature.
  • If adjusted and stable → issue resolved.

Check for Air in the Line

  • Inspect for air bubbles or incomplete priming.
  • Why it matters: Air reduces heat transfer and affects sensed temperature.
  • Prime system as needed and reassess.

Inspect for Environmental Influences

  • Check if unit is exposed to cold airflow, OR vents, or drafts.
  • Why it matters: External cooling can cause temperature discrepancies.
  • If repositioned and stabilized → issue resolved.

Observe for Consistency

  • Monitor whether temperature fluctuation is constant, drifting, or intermittent.
  • Why it matters: Helps distinguish setup-related issues from true sensor instability.

Cross-Check with Another Unit (If Available)

  • Run the same setup on a known-good unit.
  • Expected: If issue follows setup → external cause. If not → device-specific issue.

If the Problem Persists

If temperature readings remain inaccurate or drifting after ruling out setup, disposables, flow, and environmental factors, the issue is likely internal (sensor calibration or control circuitry).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot temperature accuracy on an active patient. Always transition to a backup warmer before evaluating performance.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported fluid warmer not maintaining accurate temperature; readings appeared lower than expected."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Temperature discrepancy persisted after confirming proper setup, flow, and cassette—suspected sensor calibration drift."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed unit from service and sent to Biomed shop/vendor for calibration verification and repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet temperature vs setpoint comparison
  • Cassette type and condition
  • Flow rate during testing
  • Presence of air in line
  • Environmental conditions (drafts, OR airflow)
  • Alarm behavior or lack of alarms
  • Consistency of drift (constant vs intermittent)
  • Final device status (removed from service)

Final Thought

Temperature accuracy directly impacts patient safety, especially during rapid infusions. Always rule out setup and environmental factors first, then escalate appropriately. Clear documentation supports safe, repeatable troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
