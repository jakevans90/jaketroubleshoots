Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: APNOEA FLOW !!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

APNOEA FLOW alarm indicating low or absent tidal volume (<20 mL), suggesting apnea, circuit disconnection, or leak impacting ventilation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient and ventilation status
  • If compromised, switch to manual ventilation or alternate device
  • Ensure adequate chest rise and oxygenation

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm APNOEA FLOW !! alarm is active
  • Observe ventilator mode and timing of alarm
  • Check measured tidal volume (<20 mL)
  • Assess for chest rise

3. Check Patient Connection

  • Verify ET tube or mask is properly connected
  • Check for dislodgement or extubation
  • Inspect Y-piece connections

4. Inspect the Breathing Circuit for Leaks or Disconnections

  • Check inspiratory and expiratory limbs
  • Ensure all fittings are tight
  • Look for cracks or holes
  • Verify reservoir bag and CO₂ absorber are properly seated

5. Check Ventilator Mode and Settings

  • Confirm correct ventilation mode
  • Check respiratory rate settings
  • Verify apnea ventilation configuration
  • Review tidal volume or pressure settings

6. Assess Gas Flow and Supply

  • Ensure adequate fresh gas flow
  • Verify oxygen and air supply pressures
  • Check flowmeter function

7. Check Sensors and Flow Measurement

  • Inspect for moisture or condensation
  • Ensure sensors are properly connected
  • Confirm no obstructions

8. Perform a System Leak Test

  • Remove patient from circuit safely
  • Run leak test per protocol
  • Check for inability to maintain pressure

9. Stop When Resolved

  • Confirm normal tidal volume restored
  • Ensure alarm clears
  • Return device to service

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and the alarm continues, an internal flow sensor or ventilator fault is likely.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilation alarms on an active patient. Always move the patient to a backup device before continuing.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"APNOEA FLOW alarm active with low tidal volume during ventilation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Leak found at Y-piece connection causing insufficient tidal volume delivery."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Re-secured circuit connection and verified normal ventilation and alarm resolution."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Apnea-related alarms directly indicate loss of ventilation and must be addressed immediately. Always start with simple external checks, prioritize patient safety, and escalate when necessary.

That is successful troubleshooting.
