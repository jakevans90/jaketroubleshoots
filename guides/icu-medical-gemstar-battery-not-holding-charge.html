ICU Medical GemStar

Battery Not Holding Charge

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

ICU Medical

Model

GemStar

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting ICU Medical GemStar infusion pumps that fail to maintain a charge or display low battery alarms. Many battery issues are caused by easily verifiable external factors such as AC power problems, battery age, or environmental conditions. This guide emphasizes safe, step-by-step troubleshooting before assuming internal battery or electronics failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify AC Power Connection

  • Ensure the pump is connected to a known good AC outlet.
  • Check for loose plugs, tripped breakers, or power strips that may be off.
  • Why it matters: A pump may not charge if the AC source is unstable or interrupted.

Inspect the Power Cord and Connector

  • Look for frayed wires, bent pins, or visible damage.
  • Confirm the connector seats securely in the pump.
  • Why it matters: Damaged cords prevent proper battery charging even when outlet power is present.

Check Battery Age and Status

  • Confirm the battery is within the manufacturer’s recommended service life.
  • Remove and reseat the battery to ensure proper contact.
  • Why it matters: Old or worn batteries lose capacity and may not hold a charge.

Run a Battery Calibration or Charge Cycle (if available)

  • Follow the pump’s on-screen procedure for a full charge/discharge cycle.
  • Why it matters: Some pumps require calibration to accurately display battery capacity.

Observe Charge Indicators

  • Verify that LEDs or screen icons indicate charging when plugged in.
  • Note any abnormal blinking, error messages, or failure to reach full charge.
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether the pump is attempting to charge the battery.

Test with a Known Good Battery

  • If available, install a verified functional battery and observe charging behavior.
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether the issue is with the battery or pump electronics.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out.

The pump should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Engineering should not attempt internal battery or electronics repair.

Recognizing when to escalate is a key part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot the battery while the pump is on an active patient.

Move the patient to a different pump before testing or replacing the battery.

Only handle charging or battery replacement when the pump is safe and not in clinical use.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Pump displays low battery and will not maintain charge.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Verified AC outlet and power cord; battery age at 5+ years; pump fails to charge with known good cord.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Pump removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent to biomedical repair bench.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested: Yes/No
  • Power cord condition: Intact/Damaged
  • Battery age: Years/months
  • Charge indicators observed: Normal/Abnormal
  • Any unusual smells or heat: None/Noted

Final Thought

Proper battery troubleshooting ensures patient safety, prevents unexpected pump failures, and avoids unnecessary replacement costs. Documenting all steps provides a clear record and supports timely repair. Always escalate when external checks are exhausted.

That is successful troubleshooting.
