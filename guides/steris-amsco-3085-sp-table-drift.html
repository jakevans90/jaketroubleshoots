STERIS AMSCO 3085 SP

Table Drift or Inability to Hold Position

Asset Type

Surgical Table

Manufacturer

STERIS

Model

AMSCO 3085 SP

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a STERIS AMSCO 3085 SP surgical table that drifts unintentionally or cannot maintain a set position. Symptoms may include:

  • Table slowly lowering, raising, or tilting without input
  • Difficulty keeping lateral, trendelenburg, or height positions stable
  • Slight continuous movement when controls are idle

These issues often stem from hydraulic or mechanical external factors before considering internal component failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Table is on a Level Surface

  • Uneven floor can cause apparent drift.
  • Ensures accurate assessment of hydraulic stability.

Check Power Connection

  • Confirm main power cord is securely connected and outlet is functional.
  • A table without stable power can intermittently lose hydraulic pressure.

Inspect Hand Control / Pendant

  • Ensure buttons are not stuck or continuously pressed.
  • Faulty pendant signals may mimic drift.

Examine Hydraulic Fluid Level and Leaks

  • Look for visible leaks around cylinders or under the table.
  • Low hydraulic fluid can prevent the table from maintaining position.

Check for Obstructions

  • Ensure nothing is pressing on the table’s moving parts or rails.
  • Objects or linens caught under the table can contribute to drift.

Test All Movements Slowly

  • Observe if drift occurs in multiple axes (height, tilt, lateral).
  • Note if drift correlates to a specific movement or actuator.

Listen for Unusual Sounds

  • Hissing, bubbling, or knocking may indicate internal hydraulic issues.
  • Confirms whether problem is hydraulic or mechanical.

If the Problem Persists

External checks completed: power, pendant, level, and leaks ruled out.

Likely causes: internal hydraulic valve wear, cylinder seal failure, or internal linkage issues.

  • Remove table from service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for bench evaluation or repair

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot the table while a patient is on it.
  • Move the patient to a stable, functioning table first.
  • Only test movement when the area is clear and safe.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Table drifts downward when height is set, cannot hold position.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Hydraulic fluid level adequate; no external leaks detected; hand control functioning normally; drift persists in all axes.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Table removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent to vendor for hydraulic valve inspection and repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Floor surface: level?
  • Pendant behavior: any stuck buttons?
  • Observable leaks or stains
  • Drift behavior: slow/jerky, which axes affected
  • Sounds during movement
  • Final table status (OOS, in-service elsewhere)

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount. Using a logical, stepwise approach allows Clinical Engineering to isolate external factors before escalating. Proper documentation and timely escalation prevent risk, ensure repair efficiency, and support ongoing cost avoidance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
