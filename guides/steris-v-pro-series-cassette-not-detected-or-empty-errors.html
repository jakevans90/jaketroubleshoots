STERIS V-PRO Series Sterilizer

Hydrogen Peroxide Cassette Not Detected or Empty Errors

Asset Type

Sterilizer

Manufacturer

STERIS

Model

V-PRO Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting issues where the V-PRO sterilizer does not detect the hydrogen peroxide cassette or reports it as empty. These errors are often caused by improper cassette installation, expired or incompatible cassettes, or sensor/connection issues rather than internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot while sterilization is required for patient care
  • Move workload to another sterilizer before proceeding
  • Ensure no patient-critical instruments are delayed

Verify the Error Message

  • Confirm the exact message on the display (e.g., “Cassette Not Detected” vs. “Cassette Empty”)
  • This helps differentiate between detection issues and actual supply depletion

Check Cassette Installation

  • Open the cassette compartment and ensure the cassette is fully seated
  • Remove and reinstall it firmly
  • Improper seating is one of the most common causes

Inspect Cassette Condition

  • Check for:
  • Visible damage
  • Leakage or residue
  • Deformation
  • A damaged cassette may not be recognized by the system

Verify Cassette Compatibility

  • Ensure the cassette type matches the V-PRO model and cycle being used
  • Using the wrong cassette type can trigger detection errors

Check Expiration Date

  • Confirm the cassette is not expired
  • Expired hydrogen peroxide cassettes may be rejected by the system

Try a Known Good Cassette

  • Replace with a new, verified cassette
  • If the issue resolves, the original cassette was likely faulty or empty

Inspect Cassette Compartment

  • Look for:
  • Debris
  • Residue buildup
  • Obstructions preventing proper seating
  • Clean externally if needed (per facility guidelines)

Check Cassette Detection Area

  • Inspect for bent guides, misalignment, or physical damage in the compartment
  • Ensure nothing is blocking the sensor path

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn the sterilizer off, wait 30–60 seconds, and power it back on
  • This can reset minor detection or communication faults

Observe for Recurrent Errors

  • If multiple known-good cassettes fail to be detected, suspect a sensor or internal detection issue

If the Problem Persists

If the cassette is properly installed, verified good, and compatible—but errors continue:

  • The issue is likely related to:
  • Cassette detection sensor failure
  • Internal communication fault
  • Control board issue
  • Remove the device from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Send for repair or further bench evaluation

Recognizing when the issue is no longer external is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not attempt to troubleshoot cassette-related errors during an active sterilization workflow. Ensure no patient-critical instruments are pending sterilization, and move workload to another sterilizer before taking the unit out of service.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Sterilizer displaying ‘cassette not detected’ and unable to start cycle.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Cassette improperly seated and not fully engaged with detection mechanism.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Reseated cassette properly; verified detection and successful cycle initiation.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Cassette type and expiration date checked
  • Known-good cassette tested
  • Compartment inspected and cleaned
  • Error message wording
  • Indicator lights or alerts
  • Number of failed attempts
  • Final device status (operational or removed from service)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with simple, external checks like cassette placement and condition before assuming internal failure. Maintaining patient safety and sterilization workflow continuity is critical. Clear documentation and timely escalation help ensure reliable equipment performance and infection control standards.

That is successful troubleshooting.
