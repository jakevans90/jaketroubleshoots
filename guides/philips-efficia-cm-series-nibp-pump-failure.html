Philips Efficia CM Series Patient Monitor

Intermittent NIBP Pump Failure

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

Efficia CM Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Efficia CM Series monitor intermittently fails to inflate the NIBP cuff or displays NIBP-related alarms such as:

  • “NIBP Pump Failure”
  • “Measurement Failed”
  • Incomplete or aborted blood pressure cycles
  • Weak or inconsistent cuff inflation

The goal is to rule out common external and setup-related causes before assuming internal pump or pneumatic module failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure the Patient Is Not Actively Dependent on This Reading

  • Move the patient to another monitor if continuous NIBP monitoring is required.
  • Do not troubleshoot NIBP on an actively unstable patient.

This ensures patient safety and prevents delayed care.

2. Verify Proper Cuff Application

  • Confirm correct cuff size for the patient.
  • Ensure the cuff is wrapped snugly and aligned properly.
  • Inspect for twisted or kinked tubing near the cuff.

Why this matters: Improper cuff application can cause inflation resistance, leading the monitor to interpret it as a pump failure.

3. Inspect NIBP Hose and Connections

  • Disconnect and reconnect the NIBP hose at both the monitor and cuff.
  • Inspect the hose for cracks, pinholes, or soft spots.
  • Check for moisture or contamination inside the hose.
  • Swap with a known-good hose and cuff.

This is one of the most common causes of intermittent pump-related alarms.

4. Check for Air Leaks

  • Start a manual NIBP cycle while observing inflation.
  • Listen for hissing sounds.
  • Watch for rapid pressure drop on the screen during inflation.

If pressure rises briefly and immediately falls, suspect a leak rather than pump failure.

5. Test with a NIBP Simulator or Test Cuff

  • Connect a NIBP simulator or calibration cuff.
  • Run multiple manual cycles.
  • Observe consistency of inflation and deflation.

If the unit operates normally on a simulator but fails on patient use, the issue is likely accessory-related rather than internal.

6. Verify Power Stability

  • Confirm the monitor is on stable AC power.
  • Check battery status.
  • Observe for reboots or screen flickers during inflation.

Low or unstable power can cause intermittent pump motor interruption.

7. Review Alarm History (If Available)

  • Access alarm or event logs.
  • Look for repeated pump-related errors.
  • Note if failures occur only after extended use (possible overheating pattern).

This can help determine whether the pump is intermittently seizing or overheating.

8. Observe Pump Behavior During Failure

  • During a failed cycle, determine if you hear the pump motor attempt to run.
  • Assess whether there is complete silence.
  • Observe whether inflation is weak or pulsing.

Findings:

  • Motor audible but weak inflation → possible leak or valve issue
  • No motor sound → possible internal pump motor or driver failure
  • Intermittent function after cooldown → possible internal pump overheating

Do not open the monitor or access internal pneumatic components in the clinical environment.

If the Problem Persists

If:

  • Cuff and hose have been ruled out
  • Simulator testing fails
  • Power source is stable
  • Repeated pump alarms continue

Then common external causes have been eliminated.

The likely cause is internal:

  • NIBP pump motor failure
  • Internal pneumatic valve malfunction
  • Pressure sensor failure
  • Main board driver issue

At this point:

  • Remove the monitor from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Send to the shop for controlled bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Continued cycling may damage the pump further.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not repeatedly attempt NIBP measurements on a patient when pump errors are occurring.

Excessive repeated inflation attempts can cause patient discomfort and bruising. Always transfer the patient to another monitor before extended troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nursing reports intermittent ‘NIBP Pump Failure’ alarm. Cuff inflates partially and aborts measurement.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Verified cuff and hose condition. Swapped with known-good accessories. Failure persisted during simulator testing. Intermittent absence of pump motor sound observed.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Removed monitor from service. Tagged Out of Service. Sent to biomed shop for internal pump evaluation and repair.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and confirmed functional
  • Battery status at time of failure
  • Accessories swapped (yes/no)
  • Simulator test results
  • Alarm frequency
  • Pump audible during failure (yes/no)
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting begins with simple, external checks and progresses logically. Many NIBP “pump failures” are caused by accessories or leaks rather than internal components. Patient safety comes first, and escalation at the correct point prevents unnecessary risk and additional equipment damage. Thorough documentation supports repair efficiency and regulatory compliance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
