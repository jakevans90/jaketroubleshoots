Drager Fabius GS

Alarm: APNOEA PRESSURE !!!

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Fabius GS

What This Guide Helps With

APNOEA PRESSURE !!! alarm indicating airway pressure is not being generated, resulting in stopped ventilation due to disconnection, leak, or ventilator failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately assess the patient and ventilation status
  • If ventilation is compromised, disconnect from the machine
  • Begin manual ventilation using a bag-valve device
  • Transition to an alternate anesthesia machine if needed

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm APNOEA PRESSURE !!! alarm is displayed
  • Observe when it occurs (startup, during ventilation, intermittent)
  • Check for no chest rise, no airway pressure waveform, or no delivered breaths

3. Check Breathing Circuit for Disconnection

  • Inspect inspiratory and expiratory limbs
  • Check Y-piece connection
  • Verify bag/mask or ETT connection
  • Reconnect any loose or disconnected components

4. Check for Circuit Leaks

  • Close APL valve
  • Occlude patient connection
  • Pressurize system
  • Listen for leaks and observe pressure decay

5. Inspect CO₂ Absorber and Components

  • Ensure absorber is properly seated and locked
  • Check seals for alignment and integrity

6. Check Ventilator Settings and Mode

  • Confirm correct ventilation mode is selected
  • Verify respiratory rate (Freq)
  • Check apnea ventilation settings
  • Confirm pressure limits are appropriate

7. Verify Gas Supply

  • Check pipeline and cylinder pressures
  • Ensure flowmeters are set correctly

8. Perform Manual Ventilation Test

  • Switch to manual/spontaneous mode
  • Use reservoir bag to ventilate
  • Observe ability to generate pressure and chest rise

9. Isolate Ventilator vs Circuit Issue

  • If manual ventilation works → suspect ventilator issue
  • If manual ventilation fails → suspect leak or disconnection

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out and the alarm continues, this indicates internal ventilator failure or control issue.

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ventilation alarms while the patient depends on the machine. Always secure ventilation first using manual or alternate equipment.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Unit alarming APNOEA PRESSURE !!! with no ventilation and no airway pressure observed during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Leak or disconnection in breathing circuit or suspected ventilator failure after verifying connections, leak test, and gas supply."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Secured circuit connections, performed leak test, verified gas supply and settings. Alarm persisted → removed from service and sent for repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Apnea pressure alarms indicate failure to generate airway pressure—most commonly due to disconnections or leaks. Always start with simple external checks and escalate appropriately to protect patient safety.

That is successful troubleshooting.
