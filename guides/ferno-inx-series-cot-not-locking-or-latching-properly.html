Ferno iNX Series

Cot not locking or latching properly

Asset Type

Stretcher

Manufacturer

Ferno

Model

iNX Series

What This Guide Helps With

Cot not securing in position, failing to latch during height changes, or releasing unexpectedly due to mechanical, alignment, or external obstruction issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the stretcher from active patient use before troubleshooting.
  • Expected: Patient safely transferred to another device.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Operate the cot through full raise/lower cycles and attempt to engage the latch at multiple positions.
  • Expected: Clear confirmation of failure to lock or inconsistent latching.
  • Why it matters: Confirms true mechanical issue vs improper operation.

Inspect for Obstructions in Locking Mechanism

  • Check latch points, hooks, and frame contact areas for debris, linen, or foreign objects.
  • Expected: Clean, unobstructed locking surfaces.
  • Why it matters: Even small debris can prevent full latch engagement.
  • If cleared and latch works → stop troubleshooting.

Check Alignment of Frame and Latch Points

  • Visually inspect for bent components, misalignment, or uneven frame positioning.
  • Gently cycle the cot while observing how latch components meet.
  • Expected: Proper alignment with smooth engagement.
  • Why it matters: Misalignment prevents the latch from fully seating.
  • If alignment corrected and function returns → stop troubleshooting.

Verify Latch Release Handle / Cable Operation

  • Operate the release handle and observe cable or linkage movement.
  • Expected: Smooth movement with full return to resting position.
  • Why it matters: A partially engaged release mechanism can prevent locking.
  • If cable is sticking, loose, or not returning → note for escalation.

Check for Wear on Latch Components

  • Inspect latch hooks, pins, and engagement surfaces for excessive wear, rounding, or damage.
  • Expected: Sharp, intact contact surfaces.
  • Why it matters: Worn components may not securely hold under load.
  • If worn → remove from service.

Test Under Light Load (No Patient)

  • Apply light downward pressure while engaging the latch.
  • Expected: Secure lock without slipping or disengaging.
  • Why it matters: Confirms latch integrity without risking patient safety.
  • If latch slips or fails → escalate.

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes (obstruction, alignment, linkage, and wear) have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal to the locking mechanism.

Remove the stretcher from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unsafe use and ensures proper repair.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot or test locking mechanisms with a patient on the stretcher. Always verify secure latching before patient transport.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Stretcher cot not locking properly when raised; staff reports slipping during positioning."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Debris and partial obstruction found in latch engagement area preventing full lock."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleared obstruction, verified proper latch engagement, and returned stretcher to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Latch engagement behavior (partial vs full)
  • Presence of debris or obstruction
  • Condition of latch components (worn, bent, intact)
  • Cable/handle responsiveness
  • Alignment of frame during operation
  • Any slipping under load testing
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Locking and latching failures are high-risk issues that directly impact patient safety. Always start with simple mechanical checks, verify alignment and linkage, and escalate when integrity is questionable. Clear documentation supports safe decision-making and repair tracking.

That is successful troubleshooting.
