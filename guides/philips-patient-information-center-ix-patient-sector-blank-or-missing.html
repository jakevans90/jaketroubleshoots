Philips Patient Information Center iX

Patient Sector Blank or Missing

Asset Type

Central Monitoring Station

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

Patient Information Center iX

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting a blank or missing patient sector caused by assignment, network, display, bed label, or central station communication issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Do not rely on a central station sector that is blank, missing, or not displaying the correct patient.

Action:

  • Confirm the patient is still being monitored at the bedside monitor.
  • Ask clinical staff to verify alarms and waveforms directly at the bedside.
  • If central monitoring is required and unavailable, follow the department’s patient monitoring escalation process.

Expected outcome: Patient monitoring continues safely while the central station display issue is investigated.

Why it matters: A missing patient sector can create a false sense that the patient is not connected, discharged, or not being monitored.

Verify the Reported Problem

Confirm exactly what is missing.

Action:

Check whether:

  • The entire patient sector is blank.
  • The sector is present but has no waveforms or numerics.
  • The bed label is missing.
  • The wrong bed is showing.
  • Only one patient sector is affected.
  • Multiple sectors are affected.

Expected outcome: The issue is narrowed to a single bed assignment, one sector layout, or a broader central/network issue.

If the sector reappears after confirming the display view or layout, document the finding and stop.

Confirm the Bedside Monitor Is Powered On and Monitoring

Check the bedside monitor first before assuming the central station is the problem.

Action:

  • Go to the patient room or ask clinical staff to verify that the bedside monitor is powered on.
  • Confirm the bedside monitor has active ECG, SpO2, NIBP, or other expected parameters.
  • Check for local alarms or communication messages at the bedside.

Expected outcome: The bedside monitor is active and capable of sending patient data.

If the bedside monitor is off, disconnected, or not monitoring the patient, restore bedside monitoring first, then recheck the central station.

Check Network or Communication Status at the Bedside Monitor

A central station sector may go blank if the bedside monitor is not communicating on the monitoring network.

Action:

  • Look for network, central monitoring, or communication indicators on the bedside monitor.
  • Confirm the network cable is connected if the monitor uses a wired connection.
  • If wireless monitoring is used, confirm the device is connected to the correct clinical network and has adequate signal.

Expected outcome: The bedside monitor shows normal communication with the monitoring network.

If a loose cable or wireless communication issue is corrected and the sector returns, document the cause and stop.

Verify the Patient or Bed Assignment

A missing sector may be caused by an incorrect bed assignment, discharged patient, or patient assigned to a different central station view.

Action:

  • At the central station, verify the correct unit, care area, patient list, and bed label.
  • Check whether the patient was admitted, discharged, transferred, or moved to another bed.
  • Ask clinical staff whether the patient was recently moved or reassigned.

Expected outcome: The central station is looking for the correct patient and correct bed.

If the patient was assigned to the wrong bed or discharged in the system, have the appropriate clinical workflow corrected and recheck the sector.

Check Central Station View, Layout, and Sector Configuration

The patient may not be missing from the system; the display layout may simply not be showing that bed.

Action:

  • Check whether the central station is on the correct screen, unit view, or patient sector layout.
  • Look for available beds that may be hidden, minimized, off-screen, or on another display page.
  • Confirm the expected number of sectors are visible.

Expected outcome: The correct care area and sector layout are displayed.

If changing back to the correct view restores the patient sector, document the layout/view issue and stop.

Confirm the Display Monitor Is Working Properly

A sector may appear missing if part of the display is not visible or if a secondary monitor is off.

Action:

  • Check that all central station display monitors are powered on.
  • Confirm video cables are secure.
  • Check for a frozen screen, black display, wrong input source, or monitor sleep mode.
  • If multiple displays are used, verify the missing sector is not located on another screen.

Expected outcome: The central station displays all expected sectors across the correct monitors.

If the issue was caused by a display monitor being off or on the wrong input, correct it, verify sector visibility, document, and stop.

Check Whether Other Patients Are Affected

Determine if this is isolated or system-wide.

Action:

  • Ask staff if other patients are missing from the central station.
  • Compare the affected bed to nearby beds or another central monitoring station if available.
  • Check whether the issue follows one patient, one bedside monitor, one room, one network jack, or the entire central station.

Expected outcome: The failure pattern is identified.

If multiple beds are missing, suspect a central station, network, server, or monitoring infrastructure issue and escalate appropriately.

Restart Only When Clinically Safe and Approved

Do not reboot a central monitoring station without understanding the patient safety impact.

Action:

  • Confirm clinical staff are actively monitoring patients at the bedside or through another approved central station.
  • Follow site policy before restarting the Patient Information Center iX workstation or display.
  • After restart, confirm the expected patient sectors return.

Expected outcome: The central station reloads the correct patient sector layout and active monitoring data.

If a restart restores the sector, document that the workstation or application display state was recovered.

Verify Alarm Visibility After Recovery

Do not stop troubleshooting once the sector visually returns.

Action:

  • Confirm the patient sector displays the correct bed label, patient information, waveforms, numerics, and alarm status.
  • Ask clinical staff to verify the sector appears normal from their perspective.
  • Confirm no patient is duplicated, missing, or assigned to the wrong sector.

Expected outcome: The central station is displaying the correct active patient data.

If the sector is restored and verified, return the central station to service and document the result.

If the Problem Persists

If the patient sector remains blank or missing after checking bedside monitoring, network connection, patient assignment, central station view, display monitors, and safe restart options, common external causes have been ruled out.

The device or system should be:

  • Removed from service if safe central monitoring cannot be confirmed.
  • Labeled Out of Service if the workstation or display cannot be trusted.
  • Escalated for repair, network review, Philips support, or clinical monitoring system evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A central station issue can affect patient surveillance, so unresolved missing sectors should not be treated as a minor display complaint.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a missing central station sector as if it is only a computer display problem. First confirm the patient is safely monitored at the bedside or on another approved central station before restarting, reassigning, or changing views.

Do not troubleshoot on an active patient while the affected central station is the only source of patient surveillance. Move monitoring responsibility to bedside staff, another approved central station, or a backup monitoring workflow first so therapy and alarm continuity are maintained.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the Philips Patient Information Center iX was not showing the patient sector for Bed 12."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found bedside monitor communicating normally, but the central station was on the incorrect unit layout and the expected bed sector was not visible."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Restored the correct central station view, verified Bed 12 waveform and numerics were visible, and confirmed normal display with clinical staff."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior observed.
  • Accessories swapped or checked.
  • Outlet or UPS power status checked.
  • Central workstation powered on and responsive.
  • Display monitor power and video cables checked.
  • Bedside monitor powered on and actively monitoring.
  • Network cable or wireless status verified.
  • Patient admit, discharge, transfer, or bed assignment checked.
  • Correct unit, care area, and sector layout verified.
  • Number of patients affected.
  • Any communication messages or alarm behavior observed.
  • Environmental factors such as network jack, wireless signal, workstation location, or display configuration.
  • Indicator lights on workstation, display monitor, network connection, or bedside monitor.
  • Whether restart was performed.
  • Final device status.

Final Thought

A blank or missing patient sector should be handled carefully because the risk is not only equipment downtime; it is missed patient visibility. Start with bedside patient safety, then work through assignment, communication, display, and workstation causes in a logical order. If the issue cannot be quickly verified and corrected, escalate the central monitoring system for repair or higher-level support and document the failure clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
