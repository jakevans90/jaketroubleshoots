Drager Evita Infinity V500

Internal Turbine or Blower Performance Degradation

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita Infinity V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Drager Evita Infinity V500 ventilator demonstrates reduced turbine or blower performance, which may include:

  • Low airflow or inability to reach set tidal volumes
  • Prolonged breath delivery times
  • Unusual noises from the blower module
  • Turbine failure or performance degradation alarms

The focus is on external, logical troubleshooting steps first, avoiding internal disassembly.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Supply

  • Confirm ventilator is plugged into a functional wall outlet or fully charged battery.
  • Check power cords and connectors for visible damage.

Why: Inconsistent voltage can cause blower performance warnings.

Check Airway Circuit and Filters

  • Inspect inspiratory and expiratory circuits for kinks, occlusions, or condensation.
  • Remove and inspect bacterial/HEPA filters; replace if clogged.

Why: Restricted airflow can mimic turbine degradation.

Confirm Humidifier and Heated Circuit Settings

  • Ensure that any humidifier water chamber is not overfilled or misaligned.
  • Heated circuits should be intact and connected correctly.

Why: Blocked or misaligned humidifiers increase airway resistance.

Run Internal Self-Test / Pre-Use Check

  • Use the ventilator’s built-in diagnostic menu to perform a turbine self-test.
  • Observe for error codes, slow spin-up, or abnormal alarm patterns.

Why: Confirms whether the blower is internally degraded or a circuit issue.

Check Exhaust and Cooling Paths

  • Inspect vents and fan pathways for dust, lint, or obstruction.
  • Clean any accessible surfaces following infection control protocols.

Why: Poor ventilation can reduce turbine efficiency.

Evaluate Alarm and Error Logs

  • Review logged events in the ventilator menu for turbine-specific alarms.
  • Note any repeated performance warnings or airflow errors.

Why: Patterns can indicate intermittent blower degradation.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes (power, circuit occlusions, filters, cooling) have been ruled out.

The turbine or blower module may be internally degraded or failing.

  • Remove ventilator from patient care use.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send to manufacturer-authorized service for bench evaluation or turbine replacement.

Knowing when to stop and escalate is a crucial aspect of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Patient Safety First: Never troubleshoot the turbine on a ventilated patient.

Transfer the patient to a backup ventilator before performing diagnostic tests.

Ensure alarms remain active and a trained clinician monitors the patient during any device replacement.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Clinician reports ventilator unable to reach set tidal volumes; turbine alarm displayed intermittently.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“External inspection shows airway circuits and filters clear; pre-use self-test indicates turbine performance degradation.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for manufacturer-authorized repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Pre-use check results
  • Airway circuit and filter condition
  • Any unusual turbine sounds or airflow delay
  • Ventilator error codes logged
  • Final device status and out-of-service labeling

Final Thought

Logical, stepwise troubleshooting ensures patient safety, prevents unnecessary device damage, and provides clear documentation. Escalating to qualified repair when internal turbine performance is degraded is both proper and safe. Proper CCR documentation preserves critical information for clinical and engineering teams.

That is successful troubleshooting.
