Karl Storz Xenon Nova 300

Fan Failure or Overtemperature Alarm

Asset Type

Endoscopic / OR Camera System

Manufacturer

Karl Storz

Model

Xenon Nova 300

What This Guide Helps With

Addresses fan failure or overtemperature alarms caused by blocked airflow, environmental conditions, or cooling system issues affecting safe operation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the alarm occurs during a procedure, notify clinical staff and transition to a backup light source.
  • Expected: Procedure continues safely without interruption
  • Why it matters: Overheating can cause sudden light loss and equipment shutdown

Verify the Reported Alarm

  • Power on the unit and confirm the exact alarm message or indicator.
  • Observe:
  • Fan failure message or indicator
  • Overtemperature warning
  • Audible alarms
  • Reduced or shut-off light output
  • Expected: Alarm is clearly identified and reproducible
  • Why it matters: Confirms this is a true thermal or cooling issue

Check Ventilation Openings (Most Common Cause)

  • Inspect all intake and exhaust vents on the unit.
  • Remove dust, lint, or debris
  • Ensure vents are not blocked by drapes, walls, or equipment
  • Expected: Airflow paths are clear
  • Why it matters: Restricted airflow is the most common cause of overheating

Verify Proper Placement and Clearance

  • Ensure the light source has adequate space around it.
  • Maintain several inches of clearance on all sides
  • Avoid enclosed cabinets or tightly stacked equipment
  • Expected: Unit has proper ventilation space
  • Why it matters: Poor placement traps heat and triggers thermal alarms

Check Ambient Room Conditions

  • Assess the environment where the unit is operating.
  • Verify room temperature is within normal operating range
  • Ensure HVAC airflow is present and functional
  • Expected: Room is not excessively warm or poorly ventilated
  • Why it matters: High ambient temperature reduces cooling efficiency

Listen for Fan Operation

  • Power on the unit and listen for internal fan activity.
  • Listen for normal fan noise
  • Check for abnormal sounds (grinding, rattling, or no sound at all)
  • Expected: Fan runs smoothly and continuously
  • Why it matters: A failed or obstructed fan prevents heat dissipation

Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn the unit off, wait 30–60 seconds, then power it back on.
  • Expected: Alarm clears if caused by temporary thermal condition
  • Why it matters: Resets thermal protection circuits and clears transient faults

Check for External Obstructions or Foreign Objects

  • Inspect around and inside accessible vent areas for debris.
  • Look for tape, packaging material, or dust buildup
  • Remove any visible obstructions safely
  • Expected: No blockage affecting airflow
  • Why it matters: Even partial blockage can impair cooling performance

Evaluate Usage Duration and Load

  • Determine if the unit has been running continuously for extended periods.
  • Allow the unit to cool down fully before reuse if overheated
  • Expected: Alarm may clear after adequate cooling
  • Why it matters: Prolonged operation can trigger thermal protection even with normal function

If the Problem Persists

If airflow is clear, placement is appropriate, and the fan is not functioning properly or alarms persist, the issue is likely internal (fan failure, thermal sensor fault, or power/control issue).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and reflects proper troubleshooting practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot overheating equipment while in active use on a patient. Always transition to a backup system before evaluating cooling-related alarms.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Light source alarming with fan failure and shutting down during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Internal cooling fan not operating despite proper external airflow and placement."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed from service and sent for repair; provided backup unit."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Ventilation openings checked and cleared
  • Unit placement and clearance verified
  • Fan noise present or absent
  • Ambient room temperature assessed
  • Alarm behavior (intermittent vs constant)
  • Any unusual sounds or heat
  • Final device status (removed from service or returned to use)

Final Thought

Cooling-related alarms should always be taken seriously. Start with airflow and environmental checks, but do not hesitate to escalate if internal cooling failure is suspected. Patient safety and reliable visualization are the priority.

That is successful troubleshooting.
