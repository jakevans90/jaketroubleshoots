GE Portrait VSM Vital Signs Monitor

Battery Won’t Hold Charge / Monitor Won’t Power On

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Portrait VSM

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting Portrait VSM monitors when the device fails to power on or the battery will not hold a charge. Common causes include depleted or defective batteries, poor battery connections, or external power issues. This guide focuses on simple, safe checks before considering internal repair or replacement.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check External Power Source

  • Ensure the monitor is plugged into a verified working AC outlet.
  • Observe if the AC power indicator lights up when plugged in.

Why it matters: A monitor may fail to power on simply due to no AC input.

Inspect the AC Power Cable and Connections

  • Examine the cable for damage, fraying, or bent pins.
  • Confirm the connection to both the monitor and wall outlet is secure.
  • Swap the cable with a known-good unit if available.

Remove and Reseat the Battery

  • Power off the device and remove the battery pack.
  • Inspect battery contacts for corrosion, debris, or loose connectors.
  • Reseat the battery ensuring it clicks into place properly.

Attempt to Power On via AC Only

  • With the battery removed, try turning on the monitor using AC power.

Why it matters: This helps distinguish between battery failure and other internal issues.

Test or Replace the Battery

  • If the monitor powers on via AC but not on battery, the battery may be defective.
  • Swap with a known-good battery or use a battery tester if available.

Check for Monitor Response or Error Messages

  • Observe the screen for battery or power-related error messages.
  • Note any unusual beeping, LED indications, or failure codes for documentation.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out (AC power verified, cable functional, battery checked).

The issue is likely internal, such as battery charging circuitry or power board failure.

  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label it “Out of Service.”
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation by qualified personnel.

Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt to monitor a patient with a device that won’t reliably power on.
  • Move the patient to another functional monitor before troubleshooting battery issues.
  • Ensure safe handling: batteries can be hot or leak if defective.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor will not power on and battery does not hold charge.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“External AC power functional; battery contacts inspected; device powers on only with AC. Battery appears depleted or faulty.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Reseated battery; attempted AC-only power-on; battery replaced with verified good unit; monitor returned to service or sent for repair if issue persists.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • AC outlet verified (yes/no)
  • Cable condition and swap attempts
  • Battery serial number and charge status
  • Indicator lights on power attempt
  • Monitor response codes or messages
  • Final device status: functional on AC, battery replaced, or sent for repair

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount—never attempt monitoring with a device that cannot maintain reliable power. Use logical troubleshooting: confirm external power, verify battery integrity, and escalate internal failures appropriately. Proper documentation ensures traceability and supports future maintenance decisions.

That is successful troubleshooting.
