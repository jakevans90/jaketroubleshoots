Physio-Control LIFEPAK 20 / 20e

Pad / Electrode Detection Errors

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

Physio-Control

Model

LIFEPAK 20 / 20e

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting pad or electrode detection errors on a LIFEPAK 20 / 20e defibrillator. Symptoms may include alarms such as “pads not connected,” “electrode failure,” “check pads,” or inability to charge or deliver a shock. The focus is on external checks and accessories before assuming internal device failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Pad Type and Expiration

  • Confirm that the pads are approved for LIFEPAK 20 / 20e.
  • Check expiration dates; expired pads may fail detection.

Why: Incorrect or expired pads can prevent the device from recognizing electrodes.

Inspect Pads and Connectors

  • Look for tears, cracks, dried gel, or corrosion on connectors.
  • Ensure that pad connectors are clean and free of debris.

Why: Damaged or dirty connections can interrupt the signal to the device.

Reconnect Pads Firmly

  • Detach and reattach pads to the device, ensuring a full click into the connector.

Why: Loose connections are a common cause of detection errors.

Check Cable Integrity

  • Inspect electrode cables for cuts, kinks, or visible damage.
  • Swap with a known-good cable if available.

Why: Damaged cables can prevent proper detection.

Confirm Device Settings

  • Verify the device is set to recognize the correct pad type if adjustable.

Why: Some LIFEPAK devices allow configuration for adult vs. pediatric pads.

Power Cycle the Device

  • Turn off the device, wait 10 seconds, then turn back on.

Why: Sometimes the internal detection system resets after a power cycle.

Test on Known-Good Pads

  • Attach a new set of LIFEPAK-compatible pads to confirm if the device recognizes them.

Why: Confirms whether the issue is with the device or the pads.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes (pads, connectors, cables) have been ruled out.

The defibrillator should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting and protects patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not attempt to troubleshoot pads on a patient in cardiac arrest.

Always move the patient to another working defibrillator if immediate treatment is required.

Troubleshoot only when the device is not in active use for patient care.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“pads not detected” when attempting defibrillation.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Observed pads were expired; connectors had dried gel and minor corrosion.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
Replaced pads with new LIFEPAK-approved set; confirmed device recognized electrodes and passed self-test.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Pad type and expiration date
  • Cable condition and swaps
  • Indicator lights or error codes
  • Any unusual sounds or device behavior
  • Final device status after testing

Final Thought

Proper external checks prevent unnecessary repairs and ensure patient safety. Logical troubleshooting, proper documentation, and timely escalation maintain device readiness and reduce downtime.

That is successful troubleshooting.
