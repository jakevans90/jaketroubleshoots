Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000

Alarm: Motor Rate Error

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Medfusion 4000

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot a Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000 Motor Rate Error alarm caused by motor speed mismatch, mechanical resistance, sensing issues, syringe setup problems, or worn drive-train components affecting infusion delivery.

A Motor Rate Error should be treated as a delivery accuracy concern because the pump may not be advancing the syringe plunger at the commanded rate.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the Medfusion 4000 from patient use if the Motor Rate Error occurs during an active infusion.
  • Transfer the infusion to another verified pump if therapy must continue.
  • Do not continue relying on a pump that may not be delivering at the programmed rate.
  • Expected: Patient therapy is maintained using a safe backup device.
  • Why it matters: A motor rate error can affect infusion accuracy and may indicate that the pump drive system is not moving as commanded.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the display shows Motor Rate Error.
  • Note when the alarm occurs: during startup, when loading or unloading a syringe, immediately after pressing start, during an infusion, or intermittently during operation.
  • Check whether the pump stops, alarms repeatedly, fails to advance the plunger, or appears to run unevenly.
  • Expected: The alarm and associated behavior are clearly observed or reproducible.
  • Why it matters: This confirms a true motor rate condition instead of a one-time setup issue or unverified symptom.

Remove the Syringe and Check for Obvious Mechanical Resistance

  • Power the pump off as directed and remove the syringe.
  • Inspect the syringe loading area, plunger driver, syringe clamp area, and flange area.
  • Look for obstruction, sticky residue, dried medication, cracked plastic, damaged syringe tabs, or anything interfering with normal plunger driver movement.
  • Expected: The plunger drive path is clean, unobstructed, and able to move without obvious restriction.
  • Why it matters: Mechanical resistance can cause the motor to run slower than expected or fail to match the programmed rate.

Inspect the Syringe and Accessory Setup

  • Confirm the syringe type and size were appropriate for the pump configuration and selected drug or program setup.
  • Check for a damaged syringe barrel, bent plunger, sticky plunger movement, or syringe installed at an angle.
  • If available, test with a known-good compatible syringe.
  • Expected: The syringe loads correctly and does not create abnormal resistance.
  • If this resolves the alarm, remove the bad syringe from use, verify pump operation, and return the pump to service if it passes functional testing.

Check the Plunger Driver Movement

  • With the pump removed from patient use, observe whether the plunger driver moves smoothly during controlled testing or diagnostic operation if available to Clinical Engineering.
  • Listen for abnormal sounds such as grinding, clicking, slipping, binding, or uneven motor movement.
  • Expected: Plunger driver movement is smooth and consistent with no abnormal noise.
  • Why it matters: Noise, slipping, or uneven motion may point toward worn drive-train parts rather than a clinical setup problem.

Inspect for Physical Damage or Drop Evidence

  • Check the pump housing, plunger driver, syringe clamp, and drive area for signs of impact, cracks, looseness, or misalignment.
  • Ask whether the pump was dropped, struck, or recently failed during transport.
  • Expected: No visible damage or misalignment is present.
  • Why it matters: A hard impact can damage motor mounting, drive-train alignment, or sensing components.

Confirm Power and Battery Condition

  • Connect the pump to a known-good AC power source.
  • Confirm the pump powers normally and does not show low battery, charging, or power-related warnings.
  • Expected: The pump has stable power during testing.
  • Why it matters: While Motor Rate Error is drive-related, unstable power conditions should be ruled out before assuming internal failure.

Run Basic Functional Verification

  • After external checks, run the pump under controlled bench conditions using an appropriate test syringe and standard test setup.
  • Observe whether the Motor Rate Error returns.
  • Expected: The pump completes the test without the alarm recurring.
  • If the alarm does not return, document the setup issue or obstruction found, complete functional verification, and return the pump to service if all checks pass.

Evaluate Whether the Issue Is Accessory, Setup, or Device Related

  • If the alarm only occurs with one syringe or setup, suspect syringe compatibility, syringe damage, or loading technique.
  • If the alarm occurs with multiple known-good syringes and normal setup, suspect a pump drive or sensing issue.
  • Expected: The issue is isolated to either the syringe/setup or the pump itself.

Do Not Continue Clinical Use if the Alarm Recurs

  • If Motor Rate Error returns after removing external causes, do not keep attempting to use the pump clinically.
  • A recurring Motor Rate Error may indicate incorrect motor sensing, mechanical or electrical drive failure, or worn drive-train parts.
  • Expected: The pump is removed from service for repair evaluation.

If the Problem Persists

If the Motor Rate Error continues after checking power, syringe setup, external obstruction, plunger movement, and physical condition, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent Motor Rate Error may indicate an internal motor sensing or drive-train problem. Possible failed or worn components may include the worm gear, worm coupling, motor bracket, motor assembly, or related drive-train parts.

Remove the pump from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first.

Do not troubleshoot a Motor Rate Error during an active infusion. Transfer the infusion to another verified pump before testing. A pump with an unresolved motor rate alarm should not be trusted for accurate delivery.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000 reported Motor Rate Error during infusion setup/use. Pump alarmed and could not be trusted to run at the programmed rate."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Checked external setup, syringe loading, power source, and plunger drive area. No syringe obstruction or accessory issue found. Alarm recurred with known-good syringe, indicating likely internal motor sensing or drive-train failure."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed pump from patient use, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair evaluation of motor assembly, worm gear, worm coupling, motor bracket, and related drive-train components."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Motor Rate Error should be treated as a delivery accuracy concern. Start with patient safety and simple external checks, then narrow the problem logically. If the alarm repeats after setup and obstruction checks, escalation is appropriate because the pump may have a motor, sensor, or drive-train failure. Clear CCR documentation supports safe repair decisions and prevents unreliable pumps from returning to patient care.

That is successful troubleshooting.
