Nihon Kohden NKV-330

Alarm: MAIN BATTERY EMPTY

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

NKV-330

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the MAIN BATTERY EMPTY alarm on a Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator. This alarm indicates that AC power has been lost and the remaining main battery power is critically low, typically below 10%.

This is a patient safety issue because the ventilator may shut down if battery power becomes fully depleted before AC power is restored or backup ventilation is provided.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the patient is being safely ventilated.
  • Notify clinical staff immediately that the ventilator has a critical power condition.
  • Prepare an alternate ventilator or manual ventilation source if AC power cannot be restored quickly.
  • Do not continue relying on the ventilator if the battery is nearly depleted and AC power is unavailable.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the displayed alarm is MAIN BATTERY EMPTY.
  • Verify whether the ventilator is currently disconnected from AC power.
  • Check whether the ventilator indicates it is operating on main battery power.
  • Check the remaining battery level and confirm it is below or near 10%.
  • Ask staff when the alarm started and whether the ventilator was recently moved, unplugged, or transported.

Restore AC Power Immediately

  • Plug the ventilator into a known working hospital-grade AC outlet.
  • Confirm the AC power cord is fully seated at the wall outlet and at the ventilator.
  • Avoid loose, damaged, or non-approved extension cords or power strips.
  • Verify that the ventilator recognizes AC power.
  • If AC power is restored and the alarm clears, allow the main battery to recharge and continue monitoring device status.

Check the Wall Outlet

  • Test the outlet with another approved device or outlet tester if available.
  • Try a different known working emergency-powered outlet.
  • Check whether the outlet is controlled by a switch, GFCI, breaker, or emergency power circuit issue.
  • If the alarm clears on a different outlet, suspect the original power source.

Inspect the Power Cord and Connection

  • Check the AC power cord for cuts, bent blades, loose fit, or physical damage.
  • Confirm the cord is fully inserted into the ventilator power inlet.
  • Check for strain on the cord from bed movement, transport, or cable routing.
  • Swap with a known good compatible power cord if allowed by facility policy.

Replace the Main Battery if AC Power Cannot Be Restored

  • If a fully charged spare main battery is available, prepare to replace the main battery as soon as possible.
  • Coordinate with clinical staff before replacing the battery.
  • Follow facility-approved battery replacement procedures.
  • Confirm the replacement battery is charged and recognized by the ventilator.
  • Continue working to restore AC power even after a spare battery is installed.

Determine Whether the Issue Is Power Source, Battery, or Device Related

  • If the alarm clears when connected to a known good AC outlet, suspect the original outlet or power source.
  • If the alarm continues despite verified AC power, inspect the power cord and AC input connection.
  • If the ventilator does not charge the battery on verified AC power, suspect a battery charging, power supply, or internal power-management issue.
  • If the battery drains unusually fast after charging, suspect a weak or failed main battery.

Confirm Normal Operation Before Returning to Use

  • Confirm the ventilator remains powered normally.
  • Confirm battery charging status is present when connected to AC power.
  • Verify no additional power-related alarms are active.
  • Notify clinical staff that the immediate power issue has been corrected.
  • Return the ventilator to service only if it operates normally and passes required checks.

If the Problem Persists

If the MAIN BATTERY EMPTY alarm remains active after verifying AC power, checking the outlet, inspecting the power cord, and replacing or charging the battery, external and common causes have been ruled out.

A persistent power or charging-related alarm may indicate an internal battery, charging circuit, power supply, or power-management failure.

  • Remove the ventilator from patient use.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Provide alternate ventilation support.
  • Send the ventilator for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a critical battery alarm for an extended time while the ventilator remains connected to an active patient. If AC power cannot be restored quickly or battery capacity is critically low, move the patient to a backup ventilator or approved ventilation method first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator displayed MAIN BATTERY EMPTY alarm while in use. Staff reported AC power was lost and the main battery showed critically low remaining power."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Ventilator was operating on main battery due to loss of AC power. Battery capacity was below 10%. Outlet, power cord, battery condition, and AC power recognition were checked to determine whether the issue was external power related or device related."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Connected ventilator to verified working AC power and confirmed alarm cleared with battery charging status present. If alarm did not clear, ventilator was removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The MAIN BATTERY EMPTY alarm should be treated as an urgent power and patient safety condition. Restore AC power or install a charged battery quickly, verify the external power path first, and remove the ventilator from service if it cannot reliably operate or charge.

That is successful troubleshooting.
