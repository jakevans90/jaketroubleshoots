GE Healthcare CARESCAPE VC150

Temperature Probe Errors

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE VC150

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting temperature probe errors on the CARESCAPE VC150. Symptoms may include “probe error,” “sensor disconnected,” inaccurate readings, or inability to display patient temperature. The focus is on external factors such as probe condition, connections, and monitor settings before assuming internal hardware failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Patient Safety First

  • Ensure the patient is monitored with an alternate device if temperature is critical.
  • Do not troubleshoot the probe while connected to an active patient if it risks safety.

Check Probe Connection

  • Confirm the temperature probe is fully seated in the correct port.
  • Inspect the connector for bent pins, corrosion, or debris.

Inspect Probe for Physical Damage

  • Look for frayed wires, kinks, or broken tips.
  • Replace the probe if any damage is visible.

Swap with a Known-Good Probe

  • Connect a verified working probe to the same port.
  • If the monitor now reads correctly, the original probe is likely faulty.

Check Monitor Port and Settings

  • Ensure the port type matches the probe (e.g., skin, esophageal).
  • Confirm the monitor is configured for the correct probe type in the settings menu.

Check for Software or Alarm Messages

  • Look for software updates or alerts that may affect probe readings.
  • Note any error codes for work order documentation.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out (probe, connection, settings).

Device should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Internal temperature sensor or circuit failure is possible; do not attempt internal repair on-site.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Always have a backup temperature measurement method available for patient safety.
  • Do not rely on a potentially faulty probe for critical clinical decisions.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Temperature probe error displayed; unable to measure patient temperature.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Probe connector pins were slightly bent; swapping with a known-good probe restored function.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced probe with verified working unit; monitor reading confirmed accurate. Device returned to service.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Probe type (skin, esophageal, rectal)
  • Any error codes displayed
  • Monitor port used
  • Observed damage or wear on probe
  • Final device status (operational, out of service)

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting prioritizes patient safety, systematically rules out external causes, and escalates appropriately. Documentation ensures trends are tracked, future errors are easier to resolve, and devices remain safe for patient care.

That is successful troubleshooting.
