Medtronic Capnostream 20p

Alarm: SpO2 HIGH ALARM

Asset Type

Capnography Monitor

Manufacturer

Medtronic

Model

Capnostream 20p

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot a SpO2 HIGH ALARM on a Medtronic Capnostream 20p capnography monitor. This alarm indicates that the displayed oxygen saturation is above the configured upper alarm limit, or that the SpO2 reading may be falsely elevated due to sensor, site, cable, configuration, or device-related issues.

Because this is a patient alarm, the patient must be assessed before assuming the issue is caused by the monitor or accessories.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Check the patient immediately and notify clinical staff.
  • Do not assume the alarm is equipment-related until the patient has been assessed.
  • If continuous monitoring is required, move the patient to another working monitor before removing sensors, swapping cables, or removing the Capnostream 20p from service.

Expected outcome: Patient condition is assessed before equipment troubleshooting begins.

Why it matters: A high SpO2 alarm may reflect a real clinical condition, excessive oxygen delivery, or alarm limits that need clinical review.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm that SpO2 HIGH ALARM is displayed on the monitor.
  • Observe whether the alarm occurs continuously, intermittently, only with patient movement, or only on one sensor site.
  • Check whether the SpO2 value is stable, unstable, or inconsistent with the patient condition.

Expected outcome: The alarm is confirmed and the behavior is understood.

Why it matters: This confirms a true system-reported alarm instead of a staff misunderstanding or transient artifact.

Confirm the Displayed SpO2 Value and Alarm Limit

  • Compare the displayed SpO2 value to the configured upper alarm limit.
  • Ask clinical staff whether the upper alarm limit is appropriate for the patient and care area.
  • Do not change alarm limits without clinical approval.

Expected outcome: The SpO2 value is actually above the upper alarm limit, or the alarm limit is found to be set too low.

Why it matters: This alarm may occur because the limit is clinically inappropriate, not because the device is failing.

Check for Signs That This Is a Real Patient Condition

  • Have clinical staff assess the patient, oxygen delivery, respiratory status, and therapy settings.
  • Confirm whether the displayed SpO2 value matches the patient condition.
  • Escalate any patient concern through clinical staff immediately.

Expected outcome: Clinical staff determine whether the high SpO2 reading requires intervention.

Why it matters: Biomedical troubleshooting should not delay clinical response to a patient alarm.

Inspect the SpO2 Sensor Placement

  • Check that the sensor is correctly positioned on the approved monitoring site.
  • Confirm the sensor is fully seated and not loose, twisted, misaligned, or partially detached.
  • Reposition the sensor only when it is clinically appropriate and staff are aware.

Expected outcome: Sensor placement is corrected and the reading becomes stable.

Why it matters: Poor placement can cause inaccurate or unstable SpO2 readings.

Check the Sensor Site

  • Inspect for motion, poor perfusion, cold extremities, nail polish, artificial nails, moisture, dirt, or excess ambient light.
  • Use a different approved site if clinical staff determine the current site is not reliable.
  • Confirm the sensor site is clean, stable, and appropriate for SpO2 monitoring.

Expected outcome: The sensor site is suitable for accurate SpO2 measurement.

Why it matters: SpO2 readings depend on light transmission through tissue, so site conditions can affect accuracy.

Check the SpO2 Cable and Sensor Connection

  • Confirm that the sensor is securely connected to the SpO2 cable.
  • Confirm that the cable is firmly connected to the monitor.
  • Look for bent pins, loose connectors, cuts, crushed cable sections, contamination, or fluid exposure.

Expected outcome: Connections are secure and no visible damage is found.

Why it matters: Intermittent sensor or cable connections can create false or unstable readings.

Swap the SpO2 Sensor

  • Replace the sensor with a known-good compatible SpO2 sensor if available.
  • Retest on the same patient only if clinically appropriate.
  • Use an approved simulator if available and patient testing is not appropriate.

Expected outcome: The alarm clears or the reading stabilizes with a known-good sensor.

If this resolves the issue: Remove the faulty sensor from use and return the monitor to service after confirming normal operation.

Swap the SpO2 Cable if Applicable

  • If replacing the sensor does not resolve the issue, try a known-good compatible SpO2 patient cable or interface cable.
  • Inspect the original cable for physical damage, connector wear, or intermittent behavior.

Expected outcome: The reading remains stable and the false alarm does not return.

If this resolves the issue: Replace the defective cable and document the finding.

Compare Against Another Monitoring Source

  • If available and clinically appropriate, compare the Capnostream 20p SpO2 reading with another monitor or pulse oximeter.
  • If available, use an approved SpO2 simulator to compare expected values with displayed values.

Expected outcome: Readings are reasonably consistent between devices, or the Capnostream 20p reading is identified as abnormal.

Why it matters: Comparison helps isolate whether the issue is patient-related, accessory-related, or monitor-related.

Check Monitor Configuration and Alarm Settings

  • Review alarm limits, patient type or mode, alarm profiles, and unit-specific settings.
  • Confirm settings with clinical staff before making any changes.
  • Verify that alarm limits match the intended clinical use.

Expected outcome: Alarm limits and configuration match the intended patient monitoring setup.

Why it matters: Incorrect configuration can create nuisance alarms even when the monitor is functioning normally.

Inspect the Monitor for Physical or Environmental Issues

  • Check for fluid intrusion, cracked housing, damaged connectors, contamination, signs of impact, overheating, unusual sounds, or abnormal odor.
  • Look for additional SpO2-related error messages or system faults.
  • Confirm the monitor is being used in an appropriate environment and is not exposed to obvious interference or damage.

Expected outcome: No physical damage or additional system fault is present.

Why it matters: External damage or contamination can point toward a device-level failure.

Perform a Functional Check

  • Use an approved SpO2 simulator if available.
  • Verify that the monitor reads appropriately.
  • Verify that the monitor alarms when simulated values exceed the configured upper limit.
  • Confirm that readings and alarms return to expected behavior after testing.

Expected outcome: The monitor responds correctly to simulated SpO2 values and alarm limits.

If this resolves the issue: Return the device to service after documenting the successful functional check.

If the Problem Persists

If the SpO2 HIGH ALARM continues after patient assessment, alarm-limit review, sensor replacement, cable checks, site checks, and functional testing, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent false SpO2 alarms may indicate an internal SpO2 measurement problem, connector issue, or monitor fault.

Remove the monitor from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation. Do not return the device to clinical use if SpO2 readings or alarms cannot be trusted.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot SpO2 alarms on an active patient in a way that delays clinical care. If monitoring is still required, move the patient to a backup monitor before removing sensors, swapping cables, changing accessories, or taking the device out of service.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Capnostream 20p reported SpO2 HIGH ALARM during patient monitoring. Staff reported SpO2 reading above the configured upper alarm limit."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Alarm was verified. Patient assessment was required first. Sensor placement, sensor site, SpO2 cable connection, alarm limits, and accessory condition were checked. Alarm was traced to incorrect upper alarm limit setting or accessory-related reading instability."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Verified alarm behavior with clinical staff, inspected SpO2 sensor and cable, confirmed alarm settings, tested with known-good accessories or simulator as available, and returned device to service after normal function was confirmed. Device was removed from service for repair if false alarm persisted."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A high SpO2 alarm must be treated as a patient alarm first. Clinical Engineering should confirm the alarm, rule out settings and accessory issues, verify performance, and escalate when readings or alarms cannot be trusted. Good documentation protects the patient, the technician, and the facility.

That is successful troubleshooting.
