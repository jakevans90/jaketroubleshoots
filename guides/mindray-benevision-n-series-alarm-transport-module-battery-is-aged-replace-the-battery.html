Mindray BeneVision N Series

Alarm: TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS AGED, REPLACE THE BATTERY

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

BeneVision N Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the Mindray BeneVision N Series alarm: TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS AGED, REPLACE THE BATTERY. This alarm indicates the transport module battery has reached an aged battery condition and may no longer provide reliable runtime for transport or backup operation.

An aged transport module battery may charge poorly, discharge quickly, or fail to support the monitor during patient transport. The monitor may continue to operate on AC power, but battery reliability should not be assumed until the issue is corrected.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Confirm the patient is being monitored safely.
  • If the patient is connected to the monitor, keep the monitor connected to AC power when possible.
  • Do not rely on the transport module battery for patient transport until the alarm is corrected.
  • If transport monitoring is needed, use another verified monitor or transport module with a known-good battery.
  • Do not troubleshoot or restart the monitor while it is actively needed for patient monitoring unless the patient has been moved to a backup device first.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact alarm displayed on the monitor is TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS AGED, REPLACE THE BATTERY.
  • Check whether the alarm appears at startup, while docked, during charging, during battery operation, or after transport.
  • Look for abnormal battery icons, charge status, or runtime estimates.
  • Ask clinical staff whether the alarm occurred during patient transport or while the monitor was connected to AC power.

Confirm AC Power and Charging Status

  • Verify the monitor is connected to a known-good AC outlet.
  • Confirm the AC power indicator is present.
  • Check that the monitor recognizes external power.
  • Allow the transport module battery time to charge if it was recently depleted.
  • Do not assume a battery is usable for transport just because the monitor is operating on AC power.

Inspect and Reseat the Transport Module

  • Remove the device from patient use before reseating the transport module if monitoring could be interrupted.
  • Undock and redock the transport module using normal hospital workflow.
  • Inspect the docking area for obvious obstruction, contamination, bent contacts, or physical damage.
  • Confirm the module seats fully and securely.
  • Recheck whether the alarm returns after the module is properly seated.

Restart the Monitor if Clinically Safe

  • Only restart the monitor when it is not being relied on for active patient monitoring.
  • Power cycle the monitor and allow it to fully restart.
  • Confirm whether the transport module battery alarm clears or returns.
  • If the alarm returns consistently after restart, treat it as a real battery reliability issue rather than a temporary display condition.

Replace the Transport Module Battery

  • Remove the affected monitor or transport module from clinical use.
  • Replace the transport module battery with an approved compatible replacement battery.
  • Confirm the replacement battery seats correctly.
  • Reconnect AC power and allow the battery to charge.
  • Recheck the monitor for normal battery status after replacement.

Verify Operation After Replacement

  • Confirm the alarm is no longer present.
  • Verify the battery icon or battery status displays normally.
  • Confirm the battery charges while connected to AC power.
  • Perform a basic functional check of the patient monitor and transport module.
  • If department procedure requires it, document the battery replacement date, battery part number, serial number, lot number, and final test results.

If the Problem Persists

If the alarm remains after installing a known-good replacement battery, external and common causes have been ruled out. AC power, charging status, module seating, visible docking condition, restart behavior, and the transport module battery itself should already have been checked.

At that point, suspect a device-side issue such as battery contact damage, battery detection failure, charging circuit failure, transport module fault, or monitor/module communication issue.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation. Do not return the monitor to clinical use with an unresolved transport module battery aging alarm.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a transport battery problem on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup monitor first if the troubleshooting could interrupt monitoring, require a restart, or affect transport readiness.

A monitor with an unresolved aged battery alarm should not be trusted for patient transport until the battery condition has been corrected and verified.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor displaying alarm: TRANSPORT MODULE BATTERY IS AGED, REPLACE THE BATTERY. Staff reported concern that the transport module battery may not be reliable for transport use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Confirmed transport module battery aged alarm. AC power and module seating were checked. Alarm returned after restart, indicating the transport module battery required replacement."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed monitor from patient use, replaced transport module battery, verified proper seating and charging status, confirmed alarm cleared, completed functional check, and returned device to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Transport module battery aging alarms should be treated as reliability warnings, especially when the monitor may be used away from AC power. Confirm the alarm, protect the patient, check simple external causes, replace the aged battery, and escalate the device if the alarm remains after a known-good battery is installed.

That is successful troubleshooting.
