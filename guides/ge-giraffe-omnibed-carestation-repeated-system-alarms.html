GE Healthcare Giraffe OmniBed Carestation

Repeated system alarms (fan, heater, communication faults)

Asset Type

Infant Incubator

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Giraffe OmniBed Carestation

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting repeated system alarms on the Giraffe OmniBed Carestation, including fan, heater, and communication faults. It focuses on basic external checks and safe troubleshooting practices before considering internal repair or manufacturer service.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power and Connections

  • Ensure the incubator is plugged into a verified live outlet.
  • Confirm that the main power switch is on.
  • Check that power cords and extension cables are secure and undamaged.

Why: Many alarms are triggered by intermittent power or loose connections.

Inspect External Components

  • Check fan intake and exhaust for obstructions or dust accumulation.
  • Ensure heater door is properly seated and latched.
  • Verify that canopy and rails are fully seated and sensors not blocked.

Why: Physical blockages or loose components can trigger multiple system alarms.

Check System Indicators

  • Observe status lights on the display panel. Compare with the user manual for fault codes.
  • Note any patterns: e.g., fan fault appears immediately at power-up, heater fault after warm-up.

Why: Recognizing consistent alarm patterns can isolate whether the fault is environmental, electrical, or communication-related.

Verify Communication and Network

  • Ensure the incubator’s data interface cable is securely connected.
  • Confirm network or central monitoring system availability if applicable.
  • Inspect for visible cable damage or connector corrosion.

Why: Communication faults can appear as repeated alarms if the device cannot report status.

Reset the Device Safely

  • If patient safety allows, power-cycle the incubator by turning it off, waiting 30 seconds, and powering back on.
  • Monitor for reoccurrence of alarms.

Why: Temporary software glitches can often clear after a controlled reboot.

Observe Environmental Factors

  • Check for strong electromagnetic interference nearby.
  • Ensure ambient temperature is within recommended limits.

Why: Extreme environments can cause system sensors to misreport or trigger alarms unnecessarily.

If the Problem Persists

All external and easily verifiable causes have been ruled out.

Likely causes: internal fan failure, heater module issue, or mainboard communication fault.

  • Remove incubator from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for manufacturer repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to escalate is a key part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot alarms while an infant is in the incubator.

Move the patient to another incubator before further testing.

Only perform checks that do not compromise patient safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Staff reported repeated alarms including fan, heater, and communication faults during routine use.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
External inspection showed power stable, all cables connected, no blockages, but alarms continued, indicating probable internal component fault.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Incubator removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent to manufacturer for repair.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified live.
  • Power cord and interface cables checked and reseated.
  • Fan and heater components visually inspected.
  • Alarm patterns noted and recorded.
  • Device status after power-cycle observed.

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting combines systematic checks, patient safety, and documentation. External factors often account for repeated alarms, but knowing when to escalate to repair ensures safe and reliable operation of critical infant care equipment.

That is successful troubleshooting.
