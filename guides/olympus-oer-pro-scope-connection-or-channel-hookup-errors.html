Olympus OER-Pro

Scope Connection or Channel Hookup Errors

Asset Type

Endoscope Reprocessor (AER)

Manufacturer

Olympus

Model

OER-Pro

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting scope connection errors, improper channel hookups, or cycle failures caused by incorrect tubing, adapters, or connection setup.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot while a contaminated scope is needed for patient care. Remove the scope and use a backup if necessary.
  • Expected: No impact to patient care or procedure delays
  • Why it matters: Prevents delays in reprocessing and ensures infection control

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Attempt to start a cycle and observe the exact error message or alert.
  • Note any “connection error,” “channel error,” or “setup error” messages
  • Identify which channel or port is referenced (if displayed)
  • Expected: Clear identification of which connection is failing
  • Why it matters: Narrows troubleshooting to a specific channel or connection point

Confirm Correct Scope Model and Compatibility (Most Common Cause)

  • Verify that the scope being processed matches the programmed cycle and connection setup.
  • Confirm scope type matches selected cycle
  • Verify correct connector set is being used for that scope
  • Expected: Scope and cycle are compatible
  • Why it matters: Incorrect scope setup is a frequent cause of connection errors

Inspect All Channel Tubing Connections

  • Check every channel connection from the AER to the scope.
  • Ensure all tubing is connected to the correct ports
  • Verify each channel (biopsy, suction, air/water, etc.) is connected as required
  • Confirm no ports are left open or misconnected
  • Expected: All channels securely and correctly connected
  • Why it matters: Misrouted or missing connections will trigger errors or prevent proper flow

Reseat and Secure All Connectors

  • Disconnect and firmly reconnect each channel line
  • Listen/feel for proper seating
  • Ensure luer locks or quick-connect fittings are tight
  • Expected: Firm, secure connections with no looseness
  • Why it matters: Even slight looseness can prevent pressure or flow detection

Inspect Tubing and Adapters for Damage or Wear

  • Look for cracks, kinks, or worn fittings
  • Replace any visibly damaged tubing or adapters
  • Ensure O-rings (if present) are intact
  • Expected: Tubing and adapters are intact and functional
  • Why it matters: Leaks or restrictions can trigger channel errors

Check for Channel Blockage or Residual Moisture

  • Ensure scope channels are pre-cleaned and flushed
  • Inspect for debris or residual fluid obstruction
  • Re-flush channels manually if needed
  • Expected: Free, unobstructed flow through all channels
  • Why it matters: Blockages prevent the AER from verifying proper connection and flow

Verify Proper Use of Channel Caps or Port Plugs

  • Confirm unused ports are sealed with correct caps
  • Ensure no open ports are left exposed
  • Expected: All required ports are either connected or properly capped
  • Why it matters: Open ports can cause pressure loss and trigger errors

Run a Test Cycle with Known Good Setup

  • Use verified tubing and connectors
  • Run a cycle to confirm system behavior
  • Expected: Cycle starts and runs without connection errors
  • Why it matters: Helps determine if issue is scope-specific or system-related

If the Problem Persists

All common external causes have been ruled out. The issue is likely related to internal flow sensors, pressure detection, or connection validation systems.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary downtime and ensures safe, reliable reprocessing.

Clinical Use Tip

Never attempt to troubleshoot scope connections while a contaminated scope is urgently needed for patient care. Always use a backup scope and complete troubleshooting afterward.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"AER reporting scope connection error and not allowing cycle start."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Biopsy channel tubing was misconnected to incorrect port."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Corrected tubing connections and verified proper channel setup; unit passed test cycle."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Specific error message displayed
  • Scope model being processed
  • Tubing/adapter condition
  • Whether issue occurs with multiple scopes
  • Any visible leaks or loose connections
  • Test cycle results
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Scope connection errors are most often caused by setup issues, not device failure. A structured approach—starting with compatibility, connections, and flow paths—resolves most problems quickly. Escalate appropriately when external causes are ruled out, and always prioritize patient safety and proper documentation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
