Skytron Stellar Surgical Light

Suspension Arm Drift or Sensor Misalignment

Asset Type

Surgical Light

Manufacturer

Skytron

Model

Stellar

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Skytron Stellar surgical light exhibits suspension arm drift, sagging, or misalignment of sensors that control position or illumination. These issues can impact proper positioning and stability of the light during procedures. The guide focuses on external, verifiable checks before assuming internal mechanical or sensor failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety

  • Confirm no procedure is active under the affected light.
  • Move the patient or surgical field to an alternative light if needed.

Visual Inspection of Arm Mechanics

  • Check for obvious obstructions, wear, or damage to the suspension arm joints.
  • Look for loose screws, bolts, or brackets that may affect balance.

Check Arm Tension Settings

  • Locate any tension adjustment knobs or springs on the arm.
  • Adjust according to manufacturer specifications to see if drift is corrected.
  • This step matters because improper tension is a common cause of drift.

Sensor Alignment Check

  • Inspect position or tilt sensors for obvious misalignment or obstruction.
  • Clean sensors gently with a dry cloth to remove dust or debris.
  • Misaligned sensors can cause automatic drift correction to malfunction.

Test Arm Movement

  • Slowly move the arm through its full range of motion.
  • Observe for smooth operation and if the arm holds position without drifting.
  • Listen for unusual friction sounds or catches that indicate mechanical wear.

Power Cycle the Light

  • Turn the light off and back on after mechanical adjustments.
  • Some sensor-based positioning systems require a reboot to recalibrate.

If the Problem Persists

External checks and adjustments have ruled out common causes. The issue is likely internal, such as worn bearings, damaged springs, or defective sensors.

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send for bench evaluation or manufacturer repair.

Knowing when to stop prevents further damage and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt to reposition or troubleshoot the suspension arm while a patient is underneath.
  • Ensure the surgical field has alternate lighting before testing or adjusting.
  • Document all observations and adjustments to support safe workflow and future maintenance.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Light arm drifts downward or does not hold position; sensor may appear misaligned.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Loose tension adjustment or obstruction/misalignment of suspension arm sensor.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Inspected and adjusted tension; cleaned and realigned sensor; tested arm motion; device continues to drift → sent for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet and power status verified
  • Arm tension settings checked
  • Sensor alignment checked and cleaned
  • Observed drift direction and degree
  • Arm movement smoothness and any unusual sounds
  • Final device status: sent for service if unresolved

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting emphasizes patient safety, methodical inspection, and knowing when to escalate. Maintaining clear documentation supports operational efficiency and reduces repeat issues.

That is successful troubleshooting.
