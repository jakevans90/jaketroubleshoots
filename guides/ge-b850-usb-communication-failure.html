GE Healthcare CARESCAPE B850 Patient Monitor

USB Communication Failure

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

CARESCAPE B850

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the CARESCAPE B850 fails to communicate with a USB device. Symptoms may include failure to read USB drives, inability to export data, unrecognized peripherals, or error messages indicating USB communication failure. Many USB issues are caused by external factors such as faulty cables, improper insertion, or USB port issues, rather than internal device failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Inspect USB Device and Port

  • Ensure the USB device is functioning by testing it on a known working computer or monitor.
  • Check the USB port for debris, damage, or bent pins.

Reason: Faulty devices or physical port issues are the most common causes of communication failure.

Reinsert the USB Device

  • Safely remove and reinsert the USB device into the same port.
  • Try an alternate USB port if available.

Reason: Loose connections can prevent proper communication.

Check for USB Compatibility

  • Verify that the device is formatted in a supported file system (e.g., FAT32).
  • Confirm the device does not exceed manufacturer size or type limits.

Reason: Unsupported devices may not communicate correctly with the monitor.

Restart the Monitor

  • Perform a controlled power cycle of the CARESCAPE B850.

Reason: Some USB errors are temporary and may resolve after a restart.

Test with a Known Good USB Device

  • Insert a USB device that is confirmed working on another CARESCAPE B850 or computer.

Reason: Helps isolate the issue between the device and the monitor.

Check System Logs

  • Review system or error logs on the B850 for USB-related messages.

Reason: Error logs can indicate whether the monitor detects the device or encounters a driver error.

If the Problem Persists

After external checks and alternate devices have failed, the issue is likely internal (USB controller, internal cabling, or motherboard).

  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send for vendor repair or bench evaluation.

Reason: Continuing to use a monitor with internal USB failure risks data loss and inconsistent operation.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not attempt USB troubleshooting with an active patient connected. Move the patient to another monitor before further testing. Only perform USB checks when patient safety is not compromised.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor unable to read USB drives or communicate with peripherals.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Tested USB device and ports; issue persisted with multiple known good devices. Likely internal USB controller fault.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • USB device type and format
  • USB port used
  • System logs/error messages
  • Any previous USB issues
  • Final device status before removal

Final Thought

Logical, stepwise troubleshooting prioritizes patient safety and avoids unnecessary internal repairs. Documenting each step ensures proper CCR records and supports effective escalation when external checks have been exhausted.

That is successful troubleshooting.
