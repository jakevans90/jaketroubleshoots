Nihon Kohden NKV-330

Alarm: CO2 BAROMETRIC SENSOR FAULT

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

NKV-330

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the CO2 BAROMETRIC SENSOR FAULT alarm on a Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator. This alarm indicates that the system cannot communicate with the CO2 pressure sensor or has detected an abnormal pressure value, such as 499 hPa or less, or 1061 hPa or more.

This condition may affect CO2 monitoring reliability. Troubleshooting should focus first on patient safety, then ambient pressure, the CO2 sensor kit, cable, adapter, contamination, and external connection issues before removing the ventilator from service for repair evaluation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot the ventilator while relying on questionable CO2 monitoring for patient assessment.
  • Confirm the patient is being monitored by another approved method if EtCO2 or CO2 monitoring is clinically required.
  • Notify clinical staff that the CO2 Barometric Sensor Fault may affect CO2 measurement reliability.
  • If accurate CO2 monitoring is required and cannot be verified, move the patient to a backup ventilator or alternate monitoring device before continuing troubleshooting.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the exact displayed alarm is CO2 BAROMETRIC SENSOR FAULT.
  • Check whether the alarm appears during startup, after connecting the CO2 sensor kit, during ventilation, or intermittently during use.
  • Observe whether CO2 values are missing, unstable, inaccurate, or unavailable.
  • Check for any related CO2 sensor, adapter, or measurement alarms.

3. Check Ambient Pressure Conditions

  • Confirm the ventilator is being used in a normal clinical environment.
  • Check whether the unit was recently moved from storage, transport, high elevation, or an unusual pressure environment.
  • If available, compare ambient pressure against the expected normal range of 500 hPa to 1060 hPa.
  • If ambient pressure is outside the expected range, correct the environmental issue before replacing parts.

4. Check the CO2 Sensor Kit Connection

  • Inspect the CO2 sensor kit connection at the ventilator.
  • Disconnect and reconnect the CO2 sensor kit.
  • Confirm the connector is fully seated and not loose.
  • Inspect the connector for bent pins, contamination, damage, or moisture.
  • If reseating the connector clears the alarm, continue monitoring and document the corrected connection issue.

5. Inspect the CO2 Sensor Kit and Cable

  • Check the CO2 sensor kit for cracked housing, damaged cable, kinks, strain near the connector, fluid contamination, or evidence of being dropped or pulled.
  • Inspect the sensor cable along its full visible length.
  • Replace the CO2 sensor kit if visible damage is found.
  • Do not attempt internal repair of the CO2 sensor kit during basic troubleshooting.

6. Check for Moisture or Contamination

  • Inspect the CO2 sensor area, airway adapter, cuvette, and related connection points for moisture, residue, or contamination.
  • Replace any contaminated disposable adapter or cuvette if applicable.
  • Allow approved reusable components to dry according to facility procedure.
  • Reconnect the CO2 sensor kit only after the setup is clean, dry, and properly assembled.

7. Power Cycle and Recheck the Alarm

  • Only perform this step with the ventilator removed from patient use.
  • Power the ventilator off and restart it.
  • Reconnect the CO2 sensor kit and confirm whether the alarm returns.
  • If the alarm clears and CO2 monitoring functions normally, complete the required functional verification before returning the device to service.

8. Replace the CO2 Sensor Kit

  • If ambient pressure is normal and the alarm persists, replace the CO2 sensor kit with a known-good or new CO2 sensor kit.
  • Reconnect the replacement kit and restart or retest the ventilator as needed.
  • Confirm whether the CO2 BAROMETRIC SENSOR FAULT alarm clears.
  • If replacement resolves the issue, verify CO2 monitoring operation and document the replaced accessory.

9. Determine Whether the Fault Is Device-Related

  • If the alarm remains after using a known-good CO2 sensor kit, inspect the ventilator-side CO2 sensor connection again.
  • Check for obvious port damage, looseness, contamination, or signs of liquid ingress.
  • Do not continue into internal board-level troubleshooting during basic Clinical Engineering response.
  • Persistent failure after replacing the sensor kit suggests the issue may be related to the ventilator-side communication or interface path.

If the Problem Persists

If ambient pressure is normal, the CO2 sensor kit connection has been checked, contamination has been ruled out, and a known-good CO2 sensor kit does not resolve the alarm, remove the ventilator from service.

Label the device:

Out of Service – CO2 Barometric Sensor Fault

Send the ventilator for repair or bench evaluation. Persistent CO2 Barometric Sensor Fault conditions may indicate an internal communication, sensor interface, or ventilator-side hardware failure.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first if accurate CO2 monitoring is required or if the ventilator cannot be verified safe for continued use.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Nihon Kohden NKV-330 ventilator displaying Alarm: CO2 BAROMETRIC SENSOR FAULT during use. Staff reported CO2 monitoring was unreliable or unavailable."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Ambient pressure was within expected range. CO2 sensor kit connection was inspected and reseated. Alarm persisted until CO2 sensor kit was replaced, indicating likely faulty sensor kit."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed ventilator from patient use during troubleshooting. Inspected CO2 sensor kit, connector, cable, adapter area, and device port. Replaced CO2 sensor kit, confirmed alarm cleared, verified CO2 monitoring operation, and returned device to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The CO2 BAROMETRIC SENSOR FAULT alarm should be treated as both a patient safety concern and a sensor communication issue. Start by protecting the patient, then confirm the alarm, verify ambient pressure, inspect the CO2 sensor kit and connections, rule out contamination, and replace the sensor kit when appropriate. If the fault remains after external checks and sensor replacement, escalation is the correct next step.

That is successful troubleshooting.
