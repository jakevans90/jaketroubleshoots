SMITHS MEDICAL CADD LEGACY

KEYPAD / BUTTONS UNRESPONSIVE

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

CADD Legacy

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Smiths Medical CADD Legacy infusion pump when the keypad or buttons do not respond. Symptoms may include:

  • Pump does not accept input from the keypad.
  • Display remains unchanged despite button presses.
  • Infusion cannot be started, paused, or stopped using the keypad.

The goal is to check external and easily verifiable causes before assuming an internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Device is Powered

  • Verify the pump is connected to a charged battery or AC power.
  • Observe any indicator lights or display startup sequence.
  • Why: A completely unpowered device can appear unresponsive.

Inspect for Obstructions or Debris

  • Check the keypad for dirt, liquids, or sticky residue.
  • Gently clean with a soft, lint-free cloth and approved cleaning solution.
  • Why: Physical obstruction can prevent button actuation.

Check for Keypad Lock or Security Mode

  • Some CADD Legacy pumps have keypad lock or programming lock features.
  • Confirm the lock is not engaged. Refer to pump label or display prompts.
  • Why: A locked keypad will not respond to input even if the device is functional.

Restart / Power Cycle the Pump

  • Turn off the pump, wait 30 seconds, then power back on.
  • Observe any boot-up errors or abnormal behavior.
  • Why: Temporary software glitches can cause the keypad to become unresponsive.

Verify Battery Condition

  • If the pump runs on battery, remove and reinstall the battery.
  • Test with a known good battery if available.
  • Why: Low voltage may prevent keypad scanning circuits from functioning correctly.

Check for Visible Damage

  • Inspect the keypad for cracked or dislodged buttons.
  • Inspect connectors at the keypad interface if externally accessible.
  • Why: Physical damage or loose connections can interrupt button signals.

Test External Controls if Applicable

  • If the pump supports external start/stop buttons or remote control, verify if those inputs work.
  • Why: Confirms whether the issue is isolated to the keypad or systemic.

If the Problem Persists

  • Remove the pump from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for internal bench evaluation or manufacturer repair.

Rationale: Continuing to troubleshoot internally may risk damage or void warranty. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt to troubleshoot the keypad on an active patient.
  • Move the patient to another functioning pump before performing any work.
  • Ensure any battery removal does not interrupt a current infusion.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Pump keypad unresponsive; cannot start or adjust infusion.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“During troubleshooting, pump powered on, battery checked, keypad cleaned; no response from buttons.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Pump removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Battery tested and/or replaced
  • AC power tested
  • Keypad physically inspected and cleaned
  • Device restarted / power cycled
  • Any external controls tested

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount. Troubleshooting should follow a logical, stepwise approach, ruling out simple external issues first. Proper escalation and documentation preserve device integrity and hospital resources.

That is successful troubleshooting.
