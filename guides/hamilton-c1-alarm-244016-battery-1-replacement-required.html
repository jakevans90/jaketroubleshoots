Hamilton C1

Alarm: 244016 Battery 1: Replacement required

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm: 244016 Battery 1: Replacement required on a Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates Battery 1 has reached a replacement condition and may no longer provide reliable backup runtime. Because ventilator battery backup is a patient safety concern, the device should not be relied on for patient use until Battery 1 is replaced, recognized, charging normally, and the alarm is cleared.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not rely on the ventilator battery system while this alarm is unresolved.
  • If the ventilator is supporting a patient, transfer the patient to another approved ventilator before troubleshooting or replacing Battery 1.
  • Keep the ventilator connected to external AC power while evaluating the alarm.
  • Remove the ventilator from patient use if battery operation may be required.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the ventilator displays Alarm: 244016 Battery 1: Replacement required.
  • Observe whether the alarm appears during startup, after self-check, while connected to AC power, during battery operation, or after moving the ventilator.
  • Check for related symptoms such as Battery 1 not charging, reduced runtime, battery icon warning, or Battery 1 not being recognized.
  • Confirm whether the ventilator is relying only on Battery 2 or external power.

Confirm External Power Is Stable

  • Verify the ventilator is connected to a working AC outlet.
  • Check that the AC power cord is fully seated at the ventilator and wall outlet.
  • Inspect the power cord for damage, looseness, bent pins, or strain.
  • Try a known-good outlet if power status is questionable.

Check Battery 1 Seating

  • Power down only if allowed by facility procedure and only when the ventilator is not in patient use.
  • Remove and reseat Battery 1.
  • Confirm the battery is fully inserted, latched, and physically secure.
  • Inspect the battery compartment for obvious obstruction, dirt, liquid, corrosion, or damage.

Inspect Battery 1 Contacts

  • Remove Battery 1 from its service position if safe and allowed by facility procedure.
  • Inspect the battery contacts and ventilator-side contacts for bent contacts, corrosion, debris, liquid contamination, burn marks, or physical damage.
  • Do not scrape, force, or modify battery contacts.
  • If contact damage is found, remove the ventilator from service for repair evaluation.

Verify Correct Battery Type

  • Confirm Battery 1 is an approved Hamilton-compatible battery for the C1 ventilator.
  • Check that the battery is not an incorrect model, damaged battery, expired battery, or non-approved substitute.
  • Compare the installed battery with facility-approved replacement stock if available.
  • Do not return the device to service with an unsupported or questionable battery installed.

Replace Battery 1

  • Replace Battery 1 with an approved compatible battery.
  • Ensure the replacement battery is fully seated and latched.
  • Power the ventilator on and allow it to recognize the replacement battery.
  • Confirm that Alarm: 244016 Battery 1: Replacement required clears.

Confirm Battery Charging Status

  • Leave the ventilator connected to external AC power.
  • Verify Battery 1 shows charging or normal charge status.
  • Allow charging time according to facility procedure.
  • Monitor for the same alarm returning after replacement.

Check Runtime If Required

  • If required by facility policy, perform a battery runtime or operational check using the approved method.
  • Do not perform extended battery testing while the ventilator is supporting an active patient.
  • Document the charge status and runtime result if tested.
  • Return the ventilator to service only after required checks are complete and the alarm does not return.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm: 244016 Battery 1: Replacement required remains after installing a known-good approved Battery 1, external and common causes have been ruled out. This may indicate battery bay contact damage, battery recognition failure, charging circuit fault, battery manager communication issue, or internal power management failure.

Remove the ventilator from service. Label it Out of Service. Send it for repair or bench evaluation. Do not work around a persistent battery fault on a ventilator.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup device first if battery reliability is in question. A ventilator with an unresolved battery alarm should not be used where backup power may be needed.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator displayed Alarm: 244016 Battery 1: Replacement required during startup while connected to AC power."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery 1 had reached replacement condition. External power, battery seating, battery type, and visible battery contacts were checked. No external power issue was found."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced Battery 1 with an approved compatible battery. Verified proper seating, normal charging status, alarm cleared, and required operational checks passed. Device returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery replacement alarms on ventilators should be treated as patient safety issues, not routine nuisance messages. Confirm the alarm, check external power and battery seating, verify the correct approved battery type, replace Battery 1, and confirm charging before returning the ventilator to service. If the alarm persists after a known-good battery is installed, escalate the ventilator for repair rather than relying on uncertain backup power.

That is successful troubleshooting.
