Cardinal Health Kangaroo ePump

Battery / Power Issues

Asset Type

Feeding Pump

Manufacturer

Cardinal Health

Model

Kangaroo ePump

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Kangaroo ePump feeding pump:

  • Fails to power on
  • Powers off unexpectedly
  • Shows low battery warnings despite charging
  • Does not hold a charge

The focus is on external, easily verifiable causes first—such as battery condition, charger, and connections—before assuming internal battery or electronics failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify External Power Source

  • Ensure the pump is connected to a known working AC outlet.
  • Check for tripped breakers or faulty outlets.

Why: If the outlet is dead, the pump cannot charge or operate.

Inspect Power Cord and AC Adapter

  • Examine the power cord and adapter for cuts, frays, or bent prongs.
  • Swap with a known good adapter if available.

Why: Damaged cords can prevent charging or intermittent power.

Check Battery Seating

  • Remove the battery and reseat it firmly.
  • Ensure contacts are clean, free of debris, and not corroded.

Why: Poor contact can prevent the pump from drawing power.

Verify Battery Charge Level

  • Allow the pump to charge for the recommended minimum time (typically several hours).
  • Observe battery indicator lights for charging status.

Why: A deeply discharged battery may need extended charging.

Swap with Known Good Battery (if available)

  • Install a fully charged spare battery to see if the pump powers on.

Why: Confirms whether the battery or pump electronics are at fault.

Inspect for Visible Damage

  • Look for signs of liquid exposure, cracks, or impact damage.

Why: External damage can cause internal power issues or short circuits.

Perform Soft Reset (if recommended by manufacturer)

  • Follow the ePump manual procedure for reset or power cycle.

Why: Clears temporary errors that can prevent boot-up.

If the Problem Persists

External causes (power source, cord, battery seating) have been ruled out.

Likely internal battery failure, charging circuit, or mainboard issue.

Action:

  • Remove the pump from service.
  • Label Out of Service.
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation.

Note: Knowing when to escalate is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not troubleshoot on an active feeding patient.
  • Transfer the patient to another working pump before testing.
  • Always ensure backup feeding options are available.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Pump will not turn on and shows battery empty.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Battery seated correctly but failed to hold charge; AC adapter verified working.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Pump removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for repair/bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and functional
  • AC adapter swapped and verified
  • Battery inspected for corrosion or swelling
  • Charging indicator behavior observed
  • Pump response to soft reset
  • Any unusual sounds or smells

Final Thought

Patient safety and logic-based troubleshooting are paramount. Start with external verifications before assuming internal failure. Accurate documentation ensures proper follow-up and helps track recurring issues. Escalation when necessary is part of responsible device management.

That is successful troubleshooting.
