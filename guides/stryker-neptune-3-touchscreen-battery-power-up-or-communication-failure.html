Stryker Neptune 3

Touchscreen, Battery, Power-Up, Or Communication Failure

Asset Type

Surgical Fluid Management System

Manufacturer

Stryker

Model

Neptune 3

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting Neptune 3 touchscreen response, battery charging, power-up, docking, or communication-related failures before repair escalation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Action: Confirm the Neptune 3 is not actively supporting a surgical procedure before troubleshooting. If the issue occurs during a case, have clinical staff transition to an approved backup suction or fluid management method.

Expected outcome: Patient care and surgical workflow are maintained using safe alternate equipment.

Why it matters: Loss of suction, power, screen control, or communication during a procedure can affect fluid removal and case safety.

Verify the Reported Problem

Action: Ask staff exactly what failed and when it happened. Confirm whether the issue is:

  • Touchscreen not responding
  • Unit will not power on
  • Battery will not charge or has short runtime
  • Unit powers up but freezes
  • Docking or rover communication issue
  • Error appears during startup or connection

Expected outcome: The failure is clearly identified before parts or internal faults are assumed.

Why it matters: Touchscreen, battery, power, and communication complaints can look similar but have different causes.

Check AC Power Source

Action: Confirm the power cord is plugged into a working hospital-grade outlet. Test the outlet or move the unit to a known-good outlet.

Expected outcome: The unit powers on or begins charging normally.

If resolved: Document the bad outlet or power source issue and stop troubleshooting.

Why it matters: A dead outlet or switched circuit can mimic battery, power supply, or charging failure.

Inspect the Power Cord and Plug

Action: Check the power cord for cuts, crushed insulation, loose plug blades, bent contacts, strain relief damage, or signs of overheating.

Expected outcome: Cord is intact, secure, and safe to use.

If damaged: Remove the device from service until the cord issue is corrected.

Why it matters: Damaged power cords create both power reliability and electrical safety concerns.

Confirm Proper Power-Up Sequence

Action: Turn the unit off, disconnect from AC power if safe to do so, wait at least 30 seconds, reconnect power, and attempt normal startup.

Expected outcome: The Neptune 3 completes startup without freezing or faulting.

If resolved: Perform a functional check and document that a power reset restored operation.

Why it matters: Temporary software lockups or incomplete shutdowns may cause startup or touchscreen issues.

Check Battery Charge Status

Action: Connect the unit to AC power and allow it to charge. Observe battery indicators, charging messages, or power status lights.

Expected outcome: The battery indicates charging and the unit remains powered on.

If the unit only works on AC power: Suspect weak battery capacity, failed battery charging circuit, or battery connection issue requiring bench evaluation.

Why it matters: Low or failed batteries can cause power-up failures, unexpected shutdowns, or communication interruptions.

Inspect for Battery-Related Symptoms

Action: Ask whether the unit shuts down quickly after being unplugged, fails during transport, or reports low battery soon after charging.

Expected outcome: Battery runtime is reasonable and consistent.

If battery runtime is poor: Remove from service for battery evaluation or replacement per facility procedure.

Why it matters: A weak battery may pass initial power-up but fail during movement or use.

Check Touchscreen Surface and Responsiveness

Action: Inspect the touchscreen for fluid residue, disinfectant buildup, cracks, delamination, pressure damage, or foreign material. Clean the screen using facility-approved methods and retest.

Expected outcome: Touchscreen responds accurately to touch inputs.

If resolved: Return to service after confirming normal screen function.

Why it matters: Residue, fluid exposure, or damaged screen surfaces can cause poor touch response or false touches.

Confirm the Unit Is Not Frozen

Action: Observe whether the display is active but buttons do not respond, the screen is stuck during startup, or the same message remains displayed.

Expected outcome: The user interface responds after a normal restart.

If still frozen: Remove the device from service for software, display, or controller evaluation.

Why it matters: A frozen display may indicate software lockup, touchscreen failure, or internal control communication failure.

Inspect Docking and Communication Connections

Action: If the issue involves docking, rover connection, canister recognition, or communication between Neptune components, inspect visible docking contacts and connection points for debris, fluid, bent pins, or poor seating.

Expected outcome: The device docks fully and recognizes connected components.

If resolved: Clean or reseat as appropriate, then perform functional checks.

Why it matters: Poor physical connection can appear as a communication fault even when electronics are functional.

Verify Accessories and Connected Components

Action: Remove and reconnect compatible accessories, rover components, manifolds, or collection components involved in the reported issue. Use known-good accessories when available.

Expected outcome: The system recognizes accessories and communicates normally.

If a swapped accessory resolves the issue: Replace or remove the faulty accessory from use.

Why it matters: Accessory faults can cause system errors that look like a main unit communication failure.

Check for Visible Fluid Intrusion or Contamination

Action: Inspect external surfaces, docking areas, vents, screen edges, and connection points for fluid, residue, corrosion, unusual odor, or signs of exposure.

Expected outcome: No evidence of fluid intrusion or contamination is present.

If fluid intrusion is suspected: Remove from service and send for evaluation.

Why it matters: Surgical fluid systems operate around liquids, and fluid exposure can affect power, communication, touchscreen function, and safety.

Listen and Observe During Startup

Action: During power-up, observe indicator lights, display behavior, fan or motor sounds, relay clicks, alarms, or unusual noises.

Expected outcome: Startup behavior is consistent and completes normally.

If abnormal sounds, heat, smell, or repeated faults occur: Stop troubleshooting and remove from service.

Why it matters: Startup behavior helps separate external power issues from internal electronics or power supply problems.

Perform a Basic Functional Check

Action: If the issue appears corrected, verify the unit powers on, touchscreen responds, battery charges, communication/docking functions normally, and no active errors remain.

Expected outcome: The Neptune 3 operates normally and is safe to return to service.

If all checks pass: Document findings and return the unit to service.

If the Problem Persists

If the Neptune 3 still has touchscreen failure, battery charging failure, power-up failure, freezing, or communication errors after external checks are completed, common user-accessible causes have been ruled out.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Do not continue troubleshooting into internal electronics, control boards, battery circuitry, or communication modules unless you are following approved service procedures and have the proper training, tools, and parts support.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A recurring power, screen, battery, or communication fault should not be returned to clinical use without a successful functional check.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a Neptune 3 on an active surgical case unless the patient and procedure are already supported by an alternate approved method. If suction or fluid collection is needed, clinical staff should transition to backup equipment first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported the Stryker Neptune 3 would power on but the touchscreen was frozen and would not respond during setup."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified AC power was good, power cord was intact, and the touchscreen remained unresponsive after restart and screen cleaning."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the Neptune 3 from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for bench evaluation of the touchscreen/control interface."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested or unit moved to known-good outlet
  • Power cord condition
  • Battery charge indicator behavior
  • Whether unit works on AC only
  • Touchscreen condition and response
  • Startup behavior or error messages
  • Docking or communication behavior
  • Accessories swapped or reseated
  • Any visible fluid residue or contamination
  • Unusual heat, smell, or noise
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Neptune 3 troubleshooting should stay focused on safety, power, battery status, touchscreen condition, docking connections, and accessory-related communication problems first. If those checks do not resolve the issue, escalation is appropriate. Clear CCR documentation helps show what was tested, what was ruled out, and why the device was removed from service.

That is successful troubleshooting.
