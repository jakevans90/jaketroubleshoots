Physio-Control LIFEPAK 15

ECG Misreading / Flatline Despite Leads Attached

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

Physio-Control

Model

LIFEPAK 15

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the LIFEPAK 15:

  • Shows no ECG waveform or a flatline despite leads being attached
  • Displays erratic or inaccurate ECG readings
  • Causes confusion about patient status due to poor signal quality

Focus is on external, verifiable causes before considering internal faults. Patient safety is the priority; do not rely on the device if readings are questionable.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Lead Placement

  • Ensure all ECG leads are connected to the correct patient locations.
  • Check for lead reversal or loose connections; even one loose lead can cause a flatline or misreading.
  • Why: Proper placement ensures accurate detection of cardiac signals.

Inspect ECG Cables and Electrodes

  • Examine cables for visible damage, kinks, or fraying.
  • Replace electrodes if they are dried out or past expiration.
  • Confirm good skin contact and electrode gel integrity.
  • Why: Damaged cables or poor electrode adhesion can prevent signal transmission.

Check Device Settings

  • Verify the LIFEPAK 15 is in the correct monitoring mode (e.g., 3-lead vs. 12-lead).
  • Confirm gain/amplitude settings and filter options are appropriate for patient condition.
  • Why: Incorrect settings may suppress or distort ECG waveform.

Power Cycle the Device

  • Turn the defibrillator off and back on.
  • Reconnect the leads after power-up.
  • Why: Temporary software glitches can interfere with signal display.

Test with Known Good Leads or Simulator

  • Connect a known working set of leads or an ECG simulator if available.
  • If waveform appears correctly, original leads or electrodes may be faulty.
  • Why: Confirms whether the issue is device or accessory-related.

Check for External Interference

  • Remove the patient and device from potential EMI sources (large motors, cell phones, other medical equipment).
  • Why: Electromagnetic interference can create false flatlines or noisy signals.

If the Problem Persists

All external and accessory-related causes have been ruled out.

  • Device should be removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for bench evaluation or repair

Reminder: Knowing when to escalate is proper troubleshooting. Do not attempt internal repairs.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Always confirm patient rhythm on a secondary monitor if available before making clinical decisions.
  • Never troubleshoot ECG leads on an active patient if it could delay or compromise care.
  • Move the patient to a different monitor if necessary for safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
User reports LIFEPAK 15 shows flatline ECG despite leads attached.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Leads, electrodes, or cable integrity were suspect; improper placement or dried-out electrodes possible.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Checked lead connections and placement; replaced electrodes and cables.
Verified correct device settings and power cycled.
Device functioned normally after external adjustments.
If unresolved, device labeled Out of Service and sent for repair.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Leads used (brand, type)
  • Electrode expiration date
  • Cable condition (visual check)
  • Device mode (3-lead/12-lead)
  • Any alarms or indicator lights
  • Patient skin prep status

Final Thought

Accurate ECG readings are critical to patient safety. Following a logical troubleshooting process—starting with the simplest external checks and escalating appropriately—prevents misdiagnosis and ensures patient care is never compromised. Always document findings clearly using the CCR method.

That is successful troubleshooting.
