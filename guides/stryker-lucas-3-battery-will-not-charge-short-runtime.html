Stryker LUCAS 3

Battery Will Not Charge / Short Runtime

Asset Type

Mechanical CPR Device

Manufacturer

Stryker

Model

LUCAS 3

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting LUCAS 3 battery charging failure, short runtime, charger issues, battery seating problems, or battery condition concerns.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

If the LUCAS 3 is in use or staged for an active resuscitation response, do not troubleshoot the device on a patient or during a code.

Remove the device from clinical use and replace it with another ready LUCAS 3 or approved manual CPR backup process.

Expected outcome: Patient care is not dependent on a device with uncertain battery performance.

Confirm the Reported Battery Problem

Verify the complaint with clinical staff. Determine whether the issue is:

  • Battery will not charge
  • Battery runtime is short
  • Device shuts down on battery
  • Battery indicator drops quickly
  • Battery does not show charged after being on charger
  • Device only works on external power

Expected outcome: The failure mode is clearly identified before testing.

Inspect the Battery Externally

Remove the battery and inspect it for:

  • Cracked housing
  • Swelling
  • Damaged contacts
  • Contamination or fluid exposure
  • Loose fit in the device or charger
  • Heat, odor, or discoloration

Do not continue using a battery with physical damage, swelling, overheating, or suspected fluid intrusion.

Expected outcome: The battery is physically safe to handle and test.

Verify Battery Seating in the LUCAS 3

Reinsert the battery fully into the LUCAS 3 and confirm it latches securely.

Power the device on and check whether the battery status displays normally.

Expected outcome: The battery is recognized and the device powers normally from battery power.

If reseating resolves the issue, document the battery seating issue and stop.

Check the Battery Contacts

Inspect the battery contacts and device battery compartment contacts for visible debris, corrosion, bent contacts, or residue.

Clean only according to facility-approved cleaning practices and manufacturer-compatible methods. Do not scrape, bend, or modify the contacts.

Expected outcome: Contacts are clean, dry, and undamaged.

Test With a Known-Good Battery

Install a known-good charged LUCAS 3 battery into the device.

Power the device on and observe whether it starts normally and maintains operation.

Expected outcome: The device operates normally with a known-good battery.

If the device works normally with another battery, the original battery is likely the issue and should be removed from circulation.

Test the Suspect Battery in a Known-Good Charger

Place the suspect battery into a known-good compatible LUCAS battery charger.

Confirm that the battery seats properly and the charger indicates normal charging behavior.

Expected outcome: The charger recognizes the battery and charging begins.

If the charger does not recognize the battery, or charging fails repeatedly, remove the battery from service.

Check the Charger and Power Source

Verify the charger is connected to a working outlet.

Check for:

  • Secure power cord connection
  • Charger indicator lights
  • Damaged charger housing
  • Damaged cord or plug
  • Unusual heat, smell, or noise

Test the outlet if needed, or move the charger to a verified outlet.

Expected outcome: The charger has reliable AC power and normal indicator behavior.

Try a Known-Good Battery on the Same Charger

Place a known-good LUCAS 3 battery into the same charger.

Expected outcome: The known-good battery charges normally.

If no batteries charge in that charger, suspect the charger, power cord, outlet, or charger power supply instead of the battery.

Verify Charging Time and Battery Rotation Practices

Ask whether the battery was allowed to charge fully and whether batteries are being rotated regularly.

Short runtime can occur if batteries are repeatedly placed into service before completing a full charge cycle.

Expected outcome: The battery has had adequate time on a working charger before being judged as failed.

Check Battery Age and Service History

Review the battery asset history, purchase date, service notes, and prior runtime complaints.

Batteries used in emergency response devices should not remain in service if they show repeated short runtime, intermittent charging, or unreliable charge indication.

Expected outcome: Battery condition is evaluated based on both current test results and history.

Perform a Controlled Runtime Check if Safe and Allowed

In a bench setting only, install a fully charged suspect battery and operate the LUCAS 3 according to facility test practice.

Monitor for:

  • Rapid battery indicator drop
  • Unexpected shutdown
  • Battery alarm or warning
  • Excessive heat
  • Intermittent power loss

Expected outcome: The battery supports expected operation without abnormal shutdown or rapid depletion.

If short runtime is confirmed, remove the battery from service.

Check External Power Operation

If available and appropriate for your facility workflow, verify whether the LUCAS 3 operates normally using the approved external power supply.

Expected outcome: The device operates normally on external power.

If the device fails on both battery and external power, the issue is more likely device-related and not isolated to the battery.

Do Not Attempt Internal Battery Repair

Do not open, rebuild, bypass, or modify the LUCAS 3 battery pack.

Do not attempt to use damaged lithium-ion batteries.

Expected outcome: Battery safety is maintained and unsafe battery handling is avoided.

If the Problem Persists

If the LUCAS 3 still will not charge batteries, rapidly depletes known-good batteries, shuts down unexpectedly, or shows inconsistent battery recognition after external checks, common external causes have been ruled out.

The device, battery, charger, or power supply should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair, battery replacement evaluation, or bench testing.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. A mechanical CPR device with uncertain battery reliability should not be returned to emergency response use.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot battery or charging issues during an active resuscitation. Move care to another ready device or manual CPR process first. Battery reliability is critical because the LUCAS 3 may be moved between locations where AC power is not immediately available.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the Stryker LUCAS 3 battery would not hold a charge and the device showed short runtime during readiness check."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery was recognized by the device but showed rapid depletion after full charging; known-good battery operated normally in the same LUCAS 3."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the suspect battery from service, verified the LUCAS 3 operated normally with a known-good charged battery, checked charger operation, and returned the device to service with a good battery."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Battery serial number or asset ID if tracked
  • Whether the battery charged in a known-good charger
  • Whether a known-good battery worked in the device
  • Charger indicator light behavior
  • Outlet tested or charger moved to known-good outlet
  • Battery age or prior runtime complaints
  • Any swelling, heat, odor, damage, or contamination
  • Whether device operated on external power
  • Final battery and device status

Final Thought

Battery issues on a LUCAS 3 should be handled conservatively because the device may be needed during high-acuity emergency response. Start with power source, charger, seating, contacts, and battery swap checks before suspecting internal device failure. If reliability cannot be verified, remove the battery or device from service and document the findings clearly using CCR.

That is successful troubleshooting.
