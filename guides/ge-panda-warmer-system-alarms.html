GE Healthcare Panda Warmer

System alarms (fan, heater, or communication errors)

Asset Type

Infant Warmer

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Panda Warmer

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting repeated system alarms on the GE Panda Warmer, including fan, heater, or communication errors. Symptoms may include alarms sounding without patient risk, error messages displayed on the user interface, or intermittent operation. The focus is on external and easily verifiable checks before assuming internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Device Power

  • Ensure the warmer is properly plugged into a verified working outlet.
  • Check for tripped circuit breakers or unplugged extension cords.

Why: Power fluctuations can cause false alarms and intermittent operation.

Inspect Alarm Indicators

  • Observe the display for specific alarm codes/messages.
  • Note any LEDs, audible alarms, or error messages.

Why: Knowing the specific alarm helps narrow the cause.

Check Environmental Factors

  • Confirm the warmer is not exposed to drafts or excessive heat that could affect fan operation.
  • Ensure the canopy or side rails are correctly positioned, as improper positioning can trigger safety alarms.

Why: Some alarms are interlocked with environmental or mechanical conditions.

Examine Connections and Cables

  • Inspect all power and communication cables for secure connections and visible damage.
  • Reseat connectors for the main control board and any fan or heater modules if accessible externally.

Why: Loose or damaged cables often cause intermittent communication faults.

Fan and Heater Functional Check

  • Observe if the fan operates when the warmer is powered on.
  • Check if the heater lamp or radiant panel warms up when activated.

Why: Verifying operation externally ensures alarms are not caused by minor obstructions or loose connections.

Restart the Warmer

  • Power off, wait 30–60 seconds, and power back on.
  • Observe if the alarm recurs.

Why: Simple resets can clear transient faults without requiring internal repair.

Check for Software or Firmware Alerts

  • Review on-screen diagnostics or error logs for repeated software warnings.

Why: Some alarms are software-based and may require vendor support if persistent.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out.

The issue is likely internal (control board, fan, heater module, or software).

  • Remove the warmer from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for vendor repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents further patient risk and unnecessary troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt troubleshooting while a patient is on the warmer.
  • Transfer the infant to another functional warmer before performing any tests.
  • Only operate the device in a safe, unoccupied state for checks.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Alarm sounding for fan failure, heater fault, or communication error.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
No external issues found; cables and power verified; fan and heater respond inconsistently. Likely internal control module or sensor fault.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent to vendor for repair.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet verified and functional
  • All cables checked and reseated
  • Fan audible and heater warms on initial test
  • Alarm codes recorded
  • Final status: Out of Service, awaiting repair

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount—do not compromise monitoring while troubleshooting alarms. Use systematic, logic-based checks, and escalate appropriately. Proper documentation ensures accurate tracking and supports timely repair.

That is successful troubleshooting.
