Philips Trilogy Ventilator

Power or Battery Failure

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

Trilogy

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting power or battery failures on a Philips Trilogy ventilator. Symptoms may include the ventilator failing to turn on, not charging, rapid battery depletion, or unexpected shutdowns. The guide focuses on external and easily verifiable checks before considering internal repair or replacement.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify AC Power Source

  • Ensure the wall outlet is live by testing it with a known working device or outlet tester.
  • Confirm the power cord is fully inserted into both the ventilator and the outlet.
  • Why: A faulty outlet or loose connection is a common cause of power failure.

Check the Power Cord and Adapter

  • Inspect for cuts, frays, or bent pins.
  • Swap with a verified working cord if available.
  • Why: Damaged cords prevent proper power delivery even if the outlet works.

Examine the Ventilator’s Battery

  • Remove the battery and inspect for physical damage, swelling, or leakage.
  • Reinsert the battery ensuring it clicks into place correctly.
  • Why: Improper seating or a damaged battery may prevent charging or power-on.

Test Battery Charge Status

  • Connect the ventilator to AC power with the battery installed and check if the charging indicator lights up.
  • If the battery does not charge, try a known working battery.
  • Why: Confirms whether the issue is the battery itself or the ventilator’s charging system.

Attempt Power-On Without Battery

  • Plug the ventilator into AC power and power on without the battery installed.
  • Why: Determines if the ventilator can operate directly from AC, isolating the battery as the issue.

Check for Error Messages

  • Observe any display notifications or alarms during power-on attempts.
  • Note specific codes for reference in work order documentation.
  • Why: Device error codes help identify charging circuit issues or battery faults.

If the Problem Persists

After completing external checks, if the ventilator still fails to power on or hold a charge:

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send it for bench evaluation or repair by qualified personnel.
  • Why: Continuing attempts beyond external checks risks further damage and patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt battery troubleshooting on a patient in use.
  • Move the patient to an alternative ventilator before testing the device.
  • Ensure the patient’s ventilation is uninterrupted during all troubleshooting steps.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Ventilator would not power on; battery appeared drained and device displayed “Low Battery” alarm.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Battery was swollen and not seating correctly; AC power supply tested functional.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
Battery removed and replaced with verified functioning battery; device successfully powered on and charged. Device returned to service.

Helpful Details to Include

  • AC outlet tested with known working device.
  • Power cord inspected and swapped if damaged.
  • Battery condition observed (swelling, leaks, seating).
  • Charging indicator status.
  • Any error codes noted from device display.
  • Final device status documented after testing.

Final Thought

Patient safety and logical troubleshooting are paramount. Always start with external power and battery checks before escalating to internal repairs. Proper documentation ensures clarity, accountability, and supports future maintenance decisions.

That is successful troubleshooting.
