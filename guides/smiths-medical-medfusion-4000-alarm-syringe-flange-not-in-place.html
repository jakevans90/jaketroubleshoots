Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000

Alarm: Syringe Flange Not In Place

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

Smiths Medical

Model

Medfusion 4000

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot a Syringe Flange Not In Place alarm on a Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000 infusion pump. This alarm may occur when the syringe flange is not fully seated in the flange clip, the syringe type does not fit correctly, the flange holder area is obstructed, or the flange sensor cannot reliably confirm syringe position.

A syringe flange detection problem can prevent the pump from accepting syringe setup, starting infusion, or maintaining reliable syringe recognition during use.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the pump is connected to a patient and the alarm affects infusion delivery, remove the pump from patient use.
  • Transfer the infusion to another verified pump if therapy must continue.
  • Do not repeatedly open, reload, or manipulate the syringe while the pump is being relied on for active delivery.
  • Expected outcome: The patient is not dependent on a pump with an unresolved syringe loading or sensing alarm.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the display shows Syringe Flange Not In Place.
  • Determine whether the alarm occurs during syringe loading, at infusion start, during delivery, or intermittently when the pump is touched or moved.
  • Check for related symptoms such as failure to start, paused delivery, repeated syringe setup prompts, or inability to recognize the loaded syringe.
  • Expected outcome: The alarm is confirmed and the failure pattern is clearly understood.

Check Syringe Loading

  • Remove and reload the syringe with the pump out of active patient use.
  • Confirm the syringe barrel is seated properly.
  • Confirm the syringe flange is fully inserted into the flange clip.
  • Make sure the syringe ears are not sitting above, behind, or partially outside the flange holder.
  • Expected outcome: The syringe flange sits fully in the flange clip without rocking, tilting, or slipping out.

Verify the Correct Syringe Type and Size Are Being Used

  • Confirm the syringe brand, model, and size match what is selected or expected by the pump configuration.
  • Check for syringe damage, unusual flange shape, incorrect syringe style, or a syringe not approved for the configured pump setup.
  • Try a known-good syringe of the same approved type and size.
  • Expected outcome: The syringe physically fits the holder and matches the pump’s programmed syringe selection.

Inspect the Flange Clip and Syringe Holder Area

  • Inspect the flange clip and holder area for visible damage, loose parts, cracked plastic, dried fluid, contamination, or obstruction.
  • Confirm the syringe can sit fully into place without being blocked by debris or a damaged holder component.
  • Do not force the syringe or flange clip.
  • Expected outcome: The flange clip is clean, intact, and able to hold the syringe flange securely.

Check for User Handling or Loading Technique Issues

  • Ask clinical staff whether the alarm happens only with certain users, certain syringes, or after syringe changes.
  • Review proper syringe loading technique if the syringe flange is not being fully seated before starting the infusion.
  • If the alarm clears after correct loading, verify normal setup and alarm-free operation before returning the pump to service.
  • Expected outcome: The alarm does not return after proper syringe loading is confirmed.

Check for Intermittent Flange Detection

  • With no patient connected, observe whether the alarm appears when the syringe is gently seated, removed, or repositioned.
  • Do not force or stress the syringe holder while checking detection behavior.
  • Watch for alarms that occur only when the pump is moved, touched, or lightly handled.
  • Expected outcome: Flange detection should respond consistently based on syringe position.

Use Biomed Diagnostics to Check the Flange Sensor

  • Enter the appropriate Biomed diagnostic menu.
  • Use Biomed > Diagnostics > Monitor Digital Sensors.
  • Observe the syringe flange sensor state while inserting and removing the syringe flange.
  • Expected outcome: The flange sensor status changes reliably when the syringe flange is placed into and removed from the flange clip.

Isolate the Problem

  • If the alarm clears with proper loading, the issue was likely loading technique.
  • If the alarm occurs only with one syringe type, size, or lot, suspect syringe compatibility, fit, or syringe damage.
  • If the flange clip is damaged, loose, cracked, or obstructed, suspect a mechanical holder issue.
  • If the flange sensor state does not change in diagnostics, suspect flange sensor or related internal failure.
  • If the sensor changes intermittently, suspect alignment, contamination, worn flange components, or sensor instability.

Stop When Resolved

  • If the alarm clears after correcting syringe position, replacing a bad syringe, cleaning the flange area, or correcting loading technique, verify normal syringe recognition.
  • Confirm the alarm does not return during functional verification.
  • Return the pump to service only after the syringe flange is detected correctly and the pump operates alarm-free.

If the Problem Persists

If the Syringe Flange Not In Place alarm continues after verifying syringe loading, syringe compatibility, flange clip condition, and digital sensor response, external and common causes have been ruled out.

Persistent flange detection alarms may indicate a flange sensor, flange clip, wiring, alignment, or internal electronics issue.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a syringe flange detection alarm while the pump is actively delivering medication to a patient. Move the patient to a backup device first, then continue troubleshooting in a controlled setting. A pump with unresolved syringe detection alarms should not be trusted for patient delivery.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Smiths Medical Medfusion 4000 reported Syringe Flange Not In Place alarm during syringe loading. User stated pump would not accept syringe setup and alarm returned after attempted reload."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Syringe flange was not fully seated in the flange clip. Flange sensor response verified in Biomed > Diagnostics > Monitor Digital Sensors after correct syringe placement."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reloaded syringe correctly, confirmed flange fully inserted into flange clip, verified alarm cleared, and confirmed flange sensor changed state properly in diagnostics. Pump passed functional check and was returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Syringe flange alarms should be handled as delivery safety concerns, not just setup annoyances. Start with patient safety, confirm the alarm, check syringe loading and flange seating, then use diagnostics to verify sensor behavior. If the flange sensor cannot be trusted, remove the pump from service and document the findings clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
