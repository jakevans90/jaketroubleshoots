Belimed WD 200 Series

Cycle will not start, aborts, or fails to complete

Asset Type

Washer-Disinfector

Manufacturer

Belimed

Model

WD 200 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting cycle start failures, aborted cycles, incomplete cycles, utility issues, door faults, loading problems, and external causes before repair escalation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Confirm the washer-disinfector is not needed for an urgent sterile processing workflow before troubleshooting. Do not bypass alarms, door locks, interlocks, or cycle requirements.

  • Expected outcome: Troubleshooting is performed safely without interrupting critical reprocessing workflow.
  • If this resolves the issue: Return the unit to normal workflow only after a successful cycle is completed.

Verify the Reported Problem

Ask SPD or clinical staff what happened: cycle would not start, stopped mid-cycle, aborted with an alarm, failed during wash/rinse/dry/disinfection, or would not complete. Check the display for any active message, alarm, or fault code.

  • Expected outcome: The failure point and symptom are clearly identified.
  • Why it matters: A start failure, fill failure, drain failure, temperature failure, and door lock fault can all appear as “cycle failed” to the user.

Confirm the Chamber Is Safe to Open Before Inspection

If the cycle aborted mid-process, verify that the chamber is not hot, pressurized, actively draining, or holding standing water before opening the door. Follow facility safety procedures for hot water, steam, chemicals, and contaminated instruments.

  • Expected outcome: The washer can be inspected without exposing staff to heat, chemical, or contamination hazards.

Check the Door Position and Door Latch Condition

Make sure the door is fully closed, the gasket is seated, and nothing is preventing the door from closing completely. Inspect for instrument trays, rack parts, debris, or gasket issues at the door opening.

  • Expected outcome: The door closes fully and the unit recognizes a safe door condition.
  • If this resolves the issue: Run a test cycle and stop troubleshooting.

Verify the Load and Rack Are Positioned Correctly

Check that the correct rack or cart is used, the rack is fully seated, and instruments are not blocking spray arms, chamber sensors, doors, or rack connections.

  • Expected outcome: The washer can recognize the load path and begin the programmed cycle.
  • Why it matters: Misloaded racks or blocked components can cause start failure, poor cycle progression, or mid-cycle aborts.

Confirm the Correct Cycle Was Selected

Verify that staff selected a valid cycle for the load type. Check for incomplete user inputs, invalid cycle selections, hold conditions, or prompts requiring acknowledgment.

  • Expected outcome: The cycle is ready to start without pending user action.
  • If this resolves the issue: Document user/setup issue and return the unit to service after successful operation.

Check Main Power and Control Panel Status

Confirm the unit is powered on, the display is active, and there are no facility power issues. Check the local disconnect, breaker status if accessible under facility policy, and any emergency stop or service switch condition.

  • Expected outcome: The washer has normal power and is not disabled by an external switch or facility electrical issue.

Check for Emergency Stop or Reset Condition

Inspect any emergency stop button or stop control. Confirm it is not pressed, stuck, or damaged. Reset only according to facility procedure.

  • Expected outcome: The washer is not being held in a stopped or safety-disabled state.
  • If this resolves the issue: Start a test cycle and confirm normal operation.

Check Water Supply Valves and Facility Utilities

Confirm hot water, cold water, treated/deionized water if applicable, and steam if used are available. Make sure supply valves are open and there are no known facility utility outages.

  • Expected outcome: Required utilities are available for the cycle to begin and continue.
  • Why it matters: Washer-disinfectors may abort or fail to advance if fill, temperature, or disinfection conditions cannot be reached.

Inspect the Drain Path Externally

Check for standing water in the chamber, drain blockage at the visible strainer area, kinked external drain hose if accessible, or signs that the facility drain is backing up. Remove only accessible debris according to facility procedure.

  • Expected outcome: The unit can drain normally and continue the cycle.
  • If this resolves the issue: Run a test cycle and verify complete drain operation.

Check Chemical Containers and Pickup Lines

Confirm detergent, rinse aid, neutralizer, or other required chemicals are present, not empty, not expired, and connected to the correct pickup lines. Look for pinched tubing, air gaps, or containers placed incorrectly.

  • Expected outcome: Required chemicals are available for the selected cycle.
  • Why it matters: Some cycles may alarm, abort, or fail validation if required dosing is not detected.

Inspect Spray Arms and Chamber Strainers

Verify spray arms rotate freely and are not blocked by instruments, racks, labels, packaging debris, or soil. Check accessible strainers/screens for heavy debris.

  • Expected outcome: Water circulation is not restricted by an obvious external blockage.
  • If this resolves the issue: Clean accessible components, reload properly, and rerun the cycle.

Check for Excessive Foam, Unusual Odor, Leaks, or Water in the Wrong Area

Look for evidence of incorrect chemical, over-concentration, leaking door gasket, leaking hoses, or water around the machine.

  • Expected outcome: No external leak, chemical misuse, or abnormal condition is present.
  • If leak, smoke, burning smell, or unusual heat is found: Stop troubleshooting, remove the unit from service, and escalate.

Review Alarm History or Displayed Fault Information If Available

Record the exact alarm, fault code, phase of cycle, and time of failure. Do not clear useful diagnostic information before documenting it.

  • Expected outcome: Repair staff or vendor support receives useful failure details.
  • Why it matters: “Cycle aborted during thermal disinfection” is much more useful than “washer does not work.”

Attempt One Controlled Test Cycle If Safe

After correcting any external issue, run an appropriate empty or approved test cycle per facility procedure. Observe whether the cycle starts, fills, washes, drains, heats, dries, and completes.

  • Expected outcome: The cycle completes without alarm or abnormal behavior.
  • If this resolves the issue: Return the device to service and document the corrective action.

Stop If the Failure Repeats

If the cycle will not start, aborts again, fails to complete, cannot heat, cannot drain, cannot fill, cannot lock the door, or displays repeated faults, do not continue repeated resets.

  • Expected outcome: The issue is escalated appropriately instead of temporarily masking a fault.

If the Problem Persists

If the Belimed WD 200 Series washer-disinfector still will not start, aborts, or fails to complete after checking the door, load, power, utilities, drain path, chemicals, spray arms, and visible chamber conditions, common external causes have been ruled out.

The problem may involve an internal sensor, control system, heating circuit, pump, valve, door lock assembly, dosing system, drain system, or utility interface issue.

The device must be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair, vendor service, or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Repeatedly restarting failed decontamination equipment can create reprocessing risk and poor documentation.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a washer-disinfector in a way that compromises instrument reprocessing. If sterile processing needs to continue, move the workload to another validated washer or follow the facility’s downtime procedure before troubleshooting. Do not troubleshoot the unit during active processing of instruments needed for patient care unless the load has been safely handled and therapy or procedure continuity is protected through backup workflow.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"SPD reported the Belimed WD 200 Series washer-disinfector would start a cycle but abort before completion."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found the chamber drain strainer heavily loaded with debris and standing water remaining after the drain portion of the cycle."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Cleared accessible debris, verified the door and rack were seated correctly, ran a test cycle, and confirmed the cycle completed normally."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Exact alarm or fault message displayed
  • Cycle selected
  • Cycle phase where failure occurred
  • Alarm behavior before, during, or after the cycle failure
  • Whether the door locked properly
  • Whether water filled the chamber
  • Whether water drained completely
  • Chemical levels checked
  • Chemical pickup lines inspected
  • Accessories, racks, carts, or trays swapped or repositioned
  • Spray arms checked for free rotation
  • Strainers/screens checked for debris
  • Utility valves verified open
  • Power behavior, display status, emergency stop status, or breaker condition
  • Environmental factors such as utility outage, drain backup, leak, foam, heat, odor, or water around the unit
  • Indicator lights, displayed prompts, or fault history observed
  • Any unusual sounds from pumps or valves
  • Whether a test cycle completed
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Cycle failures on washer-disinfectors should be approached carefully because the issue may affect instrument reprocessing quality. Start with the safest and most visible causes first: door, load, power, utilities, chemicals, drainage, and chamber blockages. If those checks do not correct the problem, remove the unit from service and escalate with clear CCR documentation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
