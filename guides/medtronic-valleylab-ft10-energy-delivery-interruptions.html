Medtronic Valleylab FT10

Energy Delivery Interruptions or Output Faults

Asset Type

Electrosurgical Unit (ESU)

Manufacturer

Medtronic

Model

Valleylab FT10

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Valleylab FT10 ESU when the unit fails to deliver energy consistently, experiences interruptions during procedures, or reports output faults. The focus is on verifying external connections, proper setup, and safe operation before considering internal failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Supply

  • Ensure the unit is plugged into a properly grounded outlet.
  • Confirm the circuit is live and not overloaded.
  • Check for tripped breakers or GFCI interruptions.

Why: Power fluctuations or poor grounding can cause energy interruptions or fault codes.

Inspect Electrical Cords and Connections

  • Examine the ESU power cord for frays, kinks, or damage.
  • Check instrument cables, patient return electrode (grounding pad) cables, and accessory connections.

Why: Damaged cables can interrupt energy delivery.

Check Patient Return Electrode (Grounding Pad) Placement and Contact

  • Ensure proper skin contact and pad adhesion.
  • Replace the pad if adhesive is insufficient or if pad is nearing expiration.

Why: Poor contact increases resistance, causing ESU faults.

Verify Instrument Compatibility and Connection

  • Confirm that the active electrode and instruments are compatible with FT10 outputs.
  • Check that connectors are fully seated.

Why: Misconnections or incompatible instruments can trigger output faults.

Review Settings and Output Modes

  • Confirm the selected energy mode and power settings are appropriate for the procedure.
  • Ensure no locked modes or software prompts are preventing output.

Why: Incorrect settings can appear as delivery interruptions.

Perform Basic Self-Test / Calibration

  • Run the ESU’s built-in self-test if available.
  • Observe any fault codes or error messages.

Why: Confirms internal diagnostics and identifies calibration issues.

Observe Unit During Operation in a Safe Environment

  • Test with a suitable load simulator (if allowed) instead of a patient.
  • Note if interruptions recur or if fault codes are displayed.

Why: Ensures troubleshooting does not compromise patient safety.

If the Problem Persists

All external and setup factors have been verified. The issue may be internal: power supply, control board, or output circuitry.

  • Remove the unit from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send for manufacturer repair or qualified bench evaluation.

Note: Stopping here is proper troubleshooting; do not attempt internal board-level repairs.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not deliver energy on a patient if output is inconsistent.
  • Move the patient to a backup ESU while troubleshooting.
  • Always ensure grounding pads are properly applied before reattempting procedures.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“User reports intermittent energy delivery during cauterization, causing procedure delays.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Patient return electrode had poor contact; all cords verified, settings correct.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Reapplied grounding pad with proper adhesion; tested ESU with load simulator—unit functioning normally.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Cables inspected and swapped if needed
  • Return electrode adhesion checked
  • Self-test or calibration run
  • Fault codes observed and logged
  • Final device status: cleared or removed for repair

Final Thought

Patient safety is the priority. Systematic troubleshooting—starting with power, connections, and patient interface—prevents misdiagnosis of internal failure. Proper documentation ensures traceability and supports future maintenance decisions. Escalate to repair when external issues are ruled out.

That is successful troubleshooting.
