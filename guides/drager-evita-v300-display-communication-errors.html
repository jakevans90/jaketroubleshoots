Drager Evita V300 Ventilator

Display Communication Errors

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V300

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting display communication errors on the Evita V300 ventilator. These errors typically indicate that the ventilator main unit is having difficulty communicating with the display module. The issue may be caused by loose cables, power interruptions, or external connection problems before assuming an internal hardware failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Confirm the Reported Error Message

  • Power on the ventilator and observe the display.
  • Verify the exact message related to display communication or display connection.
  • Document the exact wording of the alarm or error for the work order.

Ensure the Ventilator is Not in Active Patient Use

  • If the ventilator is connected to a patient: Move the patient to another ventilator first.
  • Only troubleshoot once the device is safe to remove from clinical use.

Check Power to the Ventilator

  • Verify the ventilator is receiving stable power.
  • Confirm the AC power cord is firmly connected.
  • Check that the outlet is functional.
  • Confirm the AC power indicator on the ventilator is illuminated.
  • Unstable power can sometimes cause temporary communication faults.

Power Cycle the Ventilator

  • Turn the ventilator off using the proper shutdown procedure if possible.
  • Disconnect AC power.
  • Wait approximately 30 seconds.
  • Reconnect power and restart the ventilator.
  • Temporary software or communication faults may clear after a full reboot.

Inspect the Display Cable Connection

  • Check for loose connectors, bent pins, partially seated cables, or visible cable damage.
  • Reseat the cable firmly on both ends.

Check for Physical Damage to the Display Assembly

  • Inspect the display for cracks or impact damage, signs of liquid exposure, and loose mounting connections.
  • Physical damage can interrupt internal display communication.

Verify the Display Powers On Normally

  • During startup, observe whether the display fully boots, remains blank, or intermittently disconnects.
  • Intermittent or blank display behavior may indicate internal display hardware failure.

Run the Ventilator Startup Self-Test

  • Allow the ventilator to complete its normal startup checks.
  • Observe whether the display communication error returns during initialization.

Check for Repeated Communication Alarms

  • Monitor the device for several minutes.
  • Repeated or persistent display communication alarms after cable reseating typically indicate an internal issue such as display module failure, internal communication board issue, or internal wiring fault.

If the Problem Persists

If the display communication error continues after verifying power, cables, and external connections, common external causes have been ruled out.

At this point the issue is likely internal to the display module or ventilator communication hardware.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for manufacturer service or bench evaluation

Avoid attempting internal repairs unless authorized and trained to do so.

Recognizing when the issue is beyond external troubleshooting is an important part of proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a ventilator that is actively supporting a patient.

Always transfer the patient to another ventilator before removing a unit from service. Display communication failures may result in loss of critical patient monitoring information, which can create a patient safety risk.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
Ventilator reported display communication error and screen intermittently froze during startup.

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Display communication fault persisted after power cycle and cable reseating. No visible external cable damage found.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Ventilator removed from service and labeled Out of Service. Unit sent for vendor repair to evaluate display communication hardware.

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and confirmed operational
  • AC power indicator verified
  • Display cable inspected and reseated
  • Display startup behavior observed
  • Alarm message documented
  • Device removed from service if fault persisted
  • Final device status should be clearly recorded in the work order

Final Thought

Ventilators are critical life-support devices. When communication errors occur between the display and the main system, it is essential to rule out simple external causes such as power interruptions or loose cables before escalating. Once those checks are complete, removing the device from service and documenting the findings ensures both patient safety and proper equipment management.

That is successful troubleshooting.
