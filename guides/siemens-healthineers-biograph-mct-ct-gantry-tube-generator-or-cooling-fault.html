Siemens Healthineers Biograph mCT

CT Gantry, Tube, Generator, or Cooling Fault

Asset Type

PET / CT System

Manufacturer

Siemens Healthineers

Model

Biograph mCT

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting CT gantry, tube, generator, exposure abort, tube heat, or cooling-related faults using safe external checks before escalation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Confirm whether a patient is on the table or positioned in the scanner bore.

If the CT fault occurs during an active PET/CT exam, stop troubleshooting until the patient is safely removed or clinical staff confirm it is safe to continue.

Expected outcome: The patient is not left dependent on a scanner with unreliable gantry, tube, generator, or cooling performance.

Confirm the Exact Reported Fault

Ask the technologist what message appeared and when it occurred.

Identify whether the issue involves:

  • CT exposure abort
  • Tube heat or tube cooling warning
  • Generator not ready
  • Gantry rotation fault
  • Tube warmup failure
  • CT acquisition unavailable
  • System cooling or temperature warning
  • Fault during scout/topogram, CT portion, or PET/CT workflow

Expected outcome: The failure type and timing are clearly understood before troubleshooting begins.

Check Whether the Fault Is Workflow-Related

Confirm the correct exam protocol was selected and the CT portion of the PET/CT study was properly initialized.

Verify the system was not interrupted during patient registration, table positioning, CT setup, or acquisition preparation.

Expected outcome: A workflow interruption or setup error is ruled out before assuming equipment failure.

Inspect the Gantry Area for Obstructions

Visually inspect around the gantry, table, bore opening, covers, floor area, and accessory zone.

Look for IV poles, patient lines, positioning aids, blankets, straps, phantoms, or other objects that may interfere with gantry or table movement.

Expected outcome: The gantry and scan path are physically clear.

If an obstruction is found, remove it, reset the workflow as appropriate, and retest only when safe. If the fault clears, return the system to service after verifying normal operation.

Verify Room Environmental Conditions

Check whether the CT room feels unusually warm or poorly ventilated.

Confirm that HVAC is operating and that airflow around equipment cabinets, gantry vents, and cooling-related areas is not blocked.

Cooling and temperature faults can occur when room conditions are outside normal operating expectations.

Expected outcome: No obvious room temperature, airflow, or blocked-vent condition is contributing to the fault.

Check for Visible Cooling System Concerns

Inspect externally for signs of leaks, water on the floor, unusual condensation, blocked vents, abnormal fan noise, or overheating smell near the gantry or equipment cabinets.

Do not open covers or bypass cooling safeguards.

Expected outcome: No visible external cooling hazard is present.

If fluid, burning smell, excessive heat, or abnormal noise is present, remove the system from service and escalate immediately.

Verify Main Power and Facility Conditions

Confirm the PET/CT system, operator console, gantry, and associated equipment cabinets have normal power indications.

Ask whether the department recently experienced a power outage, generator transfer, emergency power event, breaker trip, HVAC failure, or room shutdown.

Expected outcome: The fault is not tied to an obvious facility power or environmental event.

Check Emergency Stop and Safety Interlock Status

Verify that no emergency stop button is engaged in the scan room, control room, or equipment area.

Confirm doors, covers, access panels, and safety-related interlocks appear normally closed from the outside.

Expected outcome: The system is not being inhibited by an engaged emergency stop or obvious safety interlock condition.

Review Tube Warmup or Tube Conditioning Status

Ask the technologist whether tube warmup was completed according to the site’s normal startup process.

If the system indicates tube warmup is required, have the operator follow the approved operator workflow.

Expected outcome: CT exposure is not being inhibited by an incomplete warmup or normal readiness requirement.

If tube warmup fails repeatedly or aborts, stop and escalate.

Allow Proper Cooling Time if Tube Heat Is Reported

If the system reports tube heat, cooling, or exposure delay after heavy CT use, allow the system to cool according to the displayed system guidance.

Do not repeatedly force acquisitions against cooling warnings.

Expected outcome: The scanner becomes available after normal cooling time.

If the warning does not clear after reasonable cooling time or returns immediately, remove the system from service and escalate.

Check Console and System Readiness

Review the operator console for system-ready status, CT-ready status, gantry-ready status, generator-ready status, and any active fault messages.

Document the exact message, code, and when it appears.

Expected outcome: The active fault is captured accurately for service review.

Attempt a Controlled Restart Only If Safe and Site-Approved

If no patient is on the system and the fault appears software or communication-related, perform only the site-approved restart process for the operator console/system.

Do not cycle main power, gantry power, or equipment cabinet power unless that is part of local policy or directed by qualified service support.

Expected outcome: Temporary communication or readiness faults may clear after a normal restart.

If the CT subsystem returns to ready and test acquisition passes, verify operation with staff before returning the system to clinical use.

Run Only Approved Operational Checks

If the system appears ready after checks, have qualified staff perform an approved non-patient test, such as a scout/topogram readiness check or site-approved QC workflow.

Do not expose a patient to confirm repair.

Expected outcome: CT operation is verified without patient risk.

Stop if Faults Repeat

If gantry rotation faults, generator faults, tube faults, cooling warnings, exposure aborts, or CT-not-ready messages repeat after basic checks, stop troubleshooting.

Repeated CT subsystem faults may involve internal gantry components, X-ray tube, generator, cooling loop, detector communication, power distribution, or service-level calibration.

Expected outcome: The device is not repeatedly reset or forced back into use without repair evaluation.

If the Problem Persists

If the problem persists after external checks, common workflow, power, obstruction, room condition, warmup, and cooling causes have been ruled out.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for Siemens service, vendor repair, or qualified bench/system evaluation

A CT gantry, tube, generator, or cooling fault on a PET/CT system should not be treated as a simple operator issue once basic causes are ruled out. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot CT exposure, gantry motion, tube heat, or generator faults while a patient is actively positioned in the bore. Move the patient to a safe position first, then continue troubleshooting only when clinical staff confirm it is safe.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Technologist reported Biograph mCT CT exposure aborted during the CT portion of a PET/CT exam with a tube cooling warning displayed."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified no patient was on the table, gantry area was clear, emergency stops were released, room HVAC was operating, and the warning returned after normal cooling time."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed Biograph mCT from service, labeled system Out of Service, documented fault message and timing, and escalated to Siemens service for CT tube/generator/cooling evaluation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Exact CT fault message or code
  • Whether issue occurred during scout, CT acquisition, PET/CT workflow, warmup, or startup
  • Patient on table: yes or no
  • Gantry area checked for obstruction
  • Emergency stop status
  • Room temperature or HVAC concerns
  • Cooling warning behavior
  • Visible leaks, unusual heat, smell, or noise
  • Whether tube warmup completed
  • Whether restart was attempted
  • Whether approved QC or test acquisition passed
  • Final device status

Final Thought

CT gantry, tube, generator, and cooling faults require a patient-safety-first approach. Clinical Engineering should rule out external causes such as obstructions, workflow setup, power conditions, emergency stops, HVAC, and cooling delays before escalating. If faults repeat or involve heat, generator readiness, gantry motion, or exposure aborts, the safest action is removal from service and clear CCR documentation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
