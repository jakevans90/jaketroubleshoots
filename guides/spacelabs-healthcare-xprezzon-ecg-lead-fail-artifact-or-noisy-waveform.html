Spacelabs Healthcare Xprezzon

ECG Lead Fail, Artifact, or Noisy Waveform

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Spacelabs Healthcare

Model

Xprezzon

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting ECG lead fail, artifact, unstable waveform, or noisy ECG display caused by setup, cable, electrode, or connection issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

If the monitor is connected to an active patient and ECG monitoring is unreliable, notify clinical staff immediately.

Do not rely on a noisy or intermittent ECG waveform for rhythm assessment until the signal is corrected.

If continuous monitoring is required, move the patient to another verified monitor or alternate monitoring source before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: Patient monitoring is maintained safely before equipment troubleshooting begins.

Verify the Reported ECG Issue

Confirm the displayed problem on the Xprezzon monitor.

Look for:

  • ECG lead fail message
  • Flat ECG trace
  • Excessive artifact
  • Wandering baseline
  • Intermittent waveform dropout
  • Noisy waveform only on one lead
  • Noisy waveform on all displayed ECG leads

Expected outcome: The reported ECG issue is confirmed and the behavior is clearly identified.

If the issue is not present, document that the monitor passed functional observation and ask staff when the issue occurs.

Check the Patient and Electrode Setup

Inspect electrode placement and skin contact.

Verify that electrodes are:

  • Fresh
  • Properly adhered
  • Not dried out
  • Not loose at the snap connection
  • Applied to clean, dry skin
  • Not placed over excessive hair, lotion, sweat, or broken skin

Expected outcome: Poor electrode contact is corrected and ECG waveform stabilizes.

If replacing or reseating electrodes resolves the issue, return the monitor to service and stop.

Check for Motion or Muscle Artifact

Observe whether the artifact appears during patient movement, shivering, tremors, coughing, bed movement, or patient handling.

Ask staff whether the waveform becomes clean when the patient is still.

Expected outcome: Motion-related artifact is identified as a clinical or setup condition rather than a monitor failure.

If the ECG waveform is stable when the patient is still, document motion artifact and return the monitor to service if all hardware checks pass.

Inspect the ECG Lead Wires

Visually inspect each ECG lead wire from the patient end to the trunk cable connection.

Check for:

  • Cracked insulation
  • Bent pins
  • Loose snaps
  • Stretched wires
  • Fluid contamination
  • Broken strain relief
  • Intermittent signal when the wire is gently moved

Expected outcome: Damaged or intermittent lead wires are identified.

Replace the ECG lead set if damage or intermittency is found. If the waveform clears with a known-good lead set, return the monitor to service and stop.

Check the ECG Trunk Cable

Inspect the ECG trunk cable connection at the monitor or input module.

Confirm the cable is fully seated and not partially connected.

Gently flex the cable near the connector and strain relief while observing the ECG waveform.

Expected outcome: The ECG signal remains stable while the cable is handled.

If the waveform cuts in and out, alarms lead fail, or becomes noisy when the trunk cable is moved, replace the trunk cable or remove the device from service for further evaluation.

Swap With a Known-Good ECG Cable Set

Replace the ECG lead wires and trunk cable with a known-good compatible ECG cable set.

Use fresh electrodes during the test to avoid carrying over a setup problem.

Expected outcome: A known-good cable set produces a clean ECG waveform without lead fail.

If the issue resolves, the original ECG cable or lead set was the likely cause. Replace the defective accessory and return the monitor to service.

Check Whether the Issue Follows the Cable or Stays With the Monitor

If possible, test the suspected ECG cable set on another compatible monitor or test the Xprezzon monitor with a known-good cable set.

Expected outcome:

  • If the problem follows the cable, replace the cable or lead wires.
  • If the problem stays with the monitor, suspect the monitor ECG input path, parameter module, or internal hardware.

This helps avoid sending a good monitor for repair when the accessory is the true cause.

Check Monitor ECG Settings

Confirm the ECG source, lead selection, size, filter, and monitoring mode are appropriate for patient monitoring.

Make sure the staff is not viewing a lead with poor electrode contact while other leads are acceptable.

Expected outcome: ECG display settings are not causing the waveform to appear poor or misleading.

If adjusting the displayed lead or ECG size resolves the concern, document the settings correction and return the monitor to service.

Check for Nearby Electrical Interference

Look for possible interference sources near the monitor or patient cables.

Examples include:

  • Damaged power cords
  • Electric beds
  • Warming devices
  • Sequential compression devices
  • Poorly routed ECG cables near power supplies
  • Loose grounding or questionable outlet conditions

Route ECG cables away from power cords and high-current accessories when possible.

Expected outcome: External interference is reduced and the ECG waveform becomes cleaner.

Inspect the Monitor Input Area

Check the ECG input connector area for obvious external damage, bent pins, contamination, looseness, or evidence of fluid intrusion.

Do not open the monitor or perform internal board-level troubleshooting during floor support.

Expected outcome: No visible external damage or contamination is present.

If the ECG input appears damaged, loose, contaminated, or fluid-exposed, remove the monitor from service.

Perform a Bench Check With an ECG Simulator

Remove the monitor from patient use and test the ECG function with a known-good ECG simulator and known-good ECG cable set.

Confirm that the Xprezzon displays a stable simulated ECG waveform without lead fail or excessive artifact.

Expected outcome: The monitor displays a clean simulated ECG signal.

If the monitor passes simulator testing, the original issue was likely related to electrodes, patient movement, cable condition, or environmental interference.

If the monitor fails simulator testing with known-good accessories, remove it from service for repair evaluation.

If the Problem Persists

If ECG lead fail, artifact, or noisy waveform continues after electrodes, lead wires, trunk cable, settings, and external interference have been checked, common external causes have been ruled out.

The issue may involve the ECG input connector, parameter acquisition hardware, internal signal processing, or another device-level fault.

The monitor should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not continue using a patient monitor when the ECG signal cannot be trusted.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot ECG signal problems on an active patient if the waveform is needed for patient care. First ensure the patient is safely monitored by another device or clinical method. A clean ECG signal is not just a display preference; it affects alarm reliability, rhythm interpretation, and patient safety.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported ECG lead fail and noisy ECG waveform on a Spacelabs Xprezzon patient monitor during patient monitoring."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Found loose ECG electrode contact and intermittent artifact when the ECG lead wire set was flexed near the patient lead connection."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced ECG electrodes and swapped the ECG lead wire set with a known-good set; monitor displayed stable ECG waveform and passed ECG simulator check."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Patient was moved to another monitor before troubleshooting
  • ECG lead fail message observed
  • Specific lead affected, if known
  • Fresh electrodes applied
  • Skin prep or electrode contact issue found
  • ECG lead wires inspected
  • ECG trunk cable swapped
  • Known-good cable set tested
  • Artifact occurred with movement or cable flexing
  • ECG simulator test result
  • Monitor input connector condition
  • Final device status

Final Thought

ECG waveform problems should be approached logically and safely. Start with the patient, electrodes, lead wires, trunk cable, and setup before suspecting monitor failure. If the signal remains unreliable with known-good accessories and simulator testing, remove the Xprezzon from service and escalate appropriately. Clear CCR documentation helps show what was tested, what failed, and why the final decision was made.

That is successful troubleshooting.
