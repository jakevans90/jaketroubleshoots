Stryker 6090 / 6500 Series

Battery or Power Backup Failure

Asset Type

Stretcher

Manufacturer

Stryker

Model

6090 / 6500 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Stretcher battery not charging, no power to electric functions, or backup power failure during transport.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the stretcher from patient use if powered functions are needed. Transfer the patient to a safe, fully functional stretcher.

Expected: Patient care continues safely
Why it matters: Loss of powered functions (hi-lo, controls) can impact safe transport and positioning

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to use powered functions (hi-lo, backrest if powered).
  • Check if the stretcher:
    • Has no response at all
    • Works only when plugged in (if applicable)
    • Shows intermittent power loss

Expected: Clear confirmation of battery-related failure
Why it matters: Distinguishes between total power failure and battery backup issues

Check AC Power Connection (If Equipped)

  • Plug the stretcher into a known working outlet.
  • Inspect the power cord for:
    • Secure connection
    • Damage or fraying
    • Loose inlet connection

Expected: Indicator lights or system powers on
Why it matters: Rules out external power supply issues before suspecting battery failure

Inspect Battery Charging Indicators

  • Check for any battery or charging indicator lights on the stretcher.
  • No lights = possible no power or failed charger
  • Charging light present but no function = possible battery failure

Expected: Charging indicator responds when plugged in
Why it matters: Helps determine if the charger circuit is working

Check for Blown Fuses or Resettable Breakers (External Only)

  • Inspect accessible fuse holders or reset buttons near the power entry point.
  • Reset breaker if tripped
  • Replace fuse only if visibly blown and approved to do so

Expected: Power restored if protection device was tripped
Why it matters: Overcurrent protection can disable charging and mimic battery failure

Perform a Power Cycle

  • Disconnect from AC power.
  • Wait 30–60 seconds, then reconnect and attempt operation again.

Expected: System may reset and restore function
Why it matters: Clears minor control or charging faults

Evaluate Battery Condition

  • If the stretcher:
    • Only works when plugged in
    • Loses power quickly after unplugging
    • Has no battery response at all
  • This strongly indicates a failed or depleted battery.

Expected: Battery should hold charge and power basic functions
Why it matters: Batteries degrade over time and are a common failure point

Check for Physical Signs of Battery Issues

  • Without disassembly, inspect for:
    • Unusual heat near battery compartment
    • Swelling or deformation (if visible)
    • Burning smell

Expected: No abnormal signs
Why it matters: Indicates unsafe battery condition requiring immediate removal from service

If the Problem Persists

If all external power sources, connections, and reset attempts have been verified, the issue is likely internal (battery pack, charger board, or wiring).

Remove the stretcher from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and ensures proper repair handling.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not rely on a stretcher with known battery issues during patient transport. Always verify functionality before moving a patient, especially for powered positioning. Move the patient to a backup device first to ensure therapy continuity.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Stretcher will not power on or loses power when unplugged."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Failed internal battery not holding charge."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Verified AC power, inspected connections, removed stretcher from service for battery replacement."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Power cord condition checked
  • Charging indicator status
  • Function when plugged vs unplugged
  • Any fuse/breaker reset attempts
  • Presence of heat or odor
  • Final device status (in service / out of service)

Final Thought

Battery and power issues are often straightforward when approached logically. Always rule out external power and connection problems first, then identify battery failure as a common cause. Prioritize patient safety and escalate appropriately when internal repair is required. Clear documentation ensures continuity and accountability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
