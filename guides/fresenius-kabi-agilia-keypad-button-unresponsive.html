Fresenius Kabi Agilia Infusion Pump

Keypad / Button Unresponsive

Asset Type

Infusion Pump

Manufacturer

Fresenius Kabi

Model

Agilia

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Fresenius Kabi Agilia infusion pump when the physical keypad or buttons fail to respond. Symptoms may include:

  • Buttons do not register input
  • Delayed or intermittent response
  • Pump not accepting programming changes

Most causes are external and simple to verify before considering internal repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power Source

  • Check that the pump is properly connected to a known working AC outlet or that the battery is sufficiently charged.
  • Why: A low or intermittent power supply can cause unresponsive buttons.

Inspect the Keypad Surface

  • Ensure buttons are not stuck, wet, or obstructed by debris.
  • Why: Physical obstructions or moisture can prevent proper button contact.

Restart the Pump

  • Power cycle the pump by turning it off, waiting 10 seconds, and powering it back on.
  • Why: Temporary software glitches can sometimes cause unresponsive inputs.

Check for Locked or Inactive Mode

  • Confirm the pump is not in a locked state or a mode that disables certain buttons (e.g., programming lock).
  • Why: Some Agilia pumps lock the keypad to prevent accidental changes.

Test with Another User/Setup

  • If possible, move the pump to a different outlet and try different programming sequences.
  • Why: Eliminates environmental or operator error as a factor.

Observe for Error Codes or Alarms

  • Look for any keypad-related error messages on the display that may indicate software or hardware issues.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out; the issue is likely internal.

  • Remove the pump from service
  • Label it Out of Service
  • Send for bench evaluation or manufacturer repair

Reinforce: Stopping here avoids patient risk and unnecessary damage.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt to troubleshoot on a patient actively receiving therapy.
  • Move the patient to another functioning pump first.
  • Troubleshoot only in a safe, powered-off environment if possible.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Keypad unresponsive; cannot enter infusion parameters.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Observed no button response; power verified; keypad free of debris; pump restarted with no effect.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Removed pump from service; labeled Out of Service; sent for bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested with a known device
  • Battery checked or swapped
  • Pump restarted and error messages noted
  • Physical inspection of keypad for damage, debris, or moisture

Final Thought

Clinical Engineering troubleshooting prioritizes patient safety, logical stepwise checks, and proper escalation. Documenting CCR ensures clarity and supports cost avoidance by preventing unnecessary interventions. Knowing when to stop is as important as knowing what to check.

That is successful troubleshooting.
