CONMED Hall Power Tools

Motor Failure or Reduced Torque

Asset Type

Surgical Power Tool System

Manufacturer

CONMED

Model

Hall Power Tools

What This Guide Helps With

Loss of power, slow rotation, or weak cutting performance due to battery, attachment, or connection-related issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the tool is in use, stop immediately and remove it from the sterile field.
  • Provide an alternate handpiece or system.
  • Expected: Patient care continues safely with backup equipment
  • Why it matters: Inadequate torque can compromise surgical precision and safety

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Activate the handpiece and observe performance.
  • Weak rotation
  • Intermittent operation
  • Stalling under load
  • Expected: Reduced torque or failure is reproducible
  • Why it matters: Confirms a true mechanical/power issue vs user perception

Check Battery Condition (Most Common Cause)

  • Remove and inspect the battery.
  • Ensure it is fully charged
  • Try a known-good battery
  • Check for battery age or damage
  • Expected: Tool regains normal torque with a good battery
  • Why it matters: Low voltage is the most common cause of reduced motor output

If resolved, remove faulty battery from service.

Inspect Battery Contacts and Seating

  • Examine battery terminals on both battery and handpiece.
  • Look for corrosion, debris, or bent contacts
  • Ensure battery clicks firmly into place
  • Expected: Clean, secure connection
  • Why it matters: Poor electrical contact limits current to the motor

Check Attachment/Accessory Load

  • Remove the blade, drill, or attachment and test the motor unloaded.
  • Then reattach and test again
  • Expected:
  • Strong performance without load
  • Possible slowdown only with attachment
  • Why it matters: Worn or binding attachments increase resistance and reduce torque

If resolved, replace or service the attachment.

Inspect for Mechanical Resistance

  • Rotate the output shaft manually (if safe and possible).
  • Listen for grinding or resistance
  • Check for debris or contamination
  • Expected: Smooth, free movement
  • Why it matters: Internal wear or contamination can reduce motor efficiency

Check Handpiece Temperature and Odor

  • After operation, assess for:
  • Excessive heat
  • Burning smell
  • Expected: No abnormal heat or odor
  • Why it matters: Overheating suggests internal motor or bearing failure

Test with Known-Good Components

  • Swap with:
  • Known-good battery
  • Known-good attachment
  • Expected: Normal operation with verified components
  • Why it matters: Helps isolate whether issue is accessory-related or internal to the handpiece

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal (motor wear, internal gearing failure, or electrical fault).

  • Remove the device from service
  • Label Out of Service
  • Send for vendor repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and is part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a surgical power tool on an active patient. Always switch to a backup device before evaluating performance issues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Handpiece reported to have weak power and stalled during orthopedic procedure."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Degraded battery and worn attachment increasing load on motor."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced battery and attachment; verified normal torque and operation."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Battery swapped and tested
  • Attachment condition checked
  • Motor performance under load vs no load
  • Presence of heat or unusual odor
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent out)

Final Thought

Motor performance issues are most often caused by simple external factors like batteries or attachments. Always start with these before suspecting internal failure. Prioritizing patient safety, using logical troubleshooting, and escalating appropriately ensures reliable equipment performance and strong clinical support.

That is successful troubleshooting.
