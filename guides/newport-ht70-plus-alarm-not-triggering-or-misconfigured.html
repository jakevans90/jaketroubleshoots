Newport HT70 Plus

Alarm Not Triggering or Misconfigured

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Newport

Model

HT70 Plus

What This Guide Helps With

Addresses missing, delayed, or incorrect alarm activation due to configuration, settings, or external setup issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

If alarms are not functioning properly, immediately move the patient to a backup ventilator.

  • Expected: Patient remains safely ventilated with active alarm protection
  • Why it matters: Alarm failure removes a critical safety layer for detecting patient distress

Verify the Reported Issue

Power on the ventilator and observe alarm behavior.

Check:

  • Are alarms not sounding at all?
  • Are alarms delayed or inconsistent?
  • Are visual alarms present but no audible alarm?
  • Expected: Issue is clearly reproducible
  • Why it matters: Confirms true alarm failure vs misunderstanding of alarm behavior

Check Alarm Volume and Mute Status (Most Common Cause)

Inspect alarm audio settings:

  • Ensure alarm volume is not set to minimum
  • Confirm alarm silence/mute is not active
  • Check for temporary silence timers
  • Expected: Audible alarms function at appropriate volume
  • Why it matters: Muted or minimized alarms are a frequent cause of “no alarm” complaints

Review Alarm Limits and Settings

Check configured alarm thresholds:

  • High/low pressure limits
  • High/low tidal volume limits
  • Apnea settings

Ensure limits are set appropriately for patient condition.

  • Expected: Alarm limits are within a realistic range to trigger alerts
  • Why it matters: Limits set too wide may prevent alarms from activating

Trigger a Test Alarm Condition

Manually create a safe test condition (off patient):

  • Disconnect circuit briefly to trigger disconnection alarm
  • Simulate apnea if applicable
  • Expected: Alarm activates both visually and audibly
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether alarm system is functioning at all

Check Alarm Indicator Functionality

Observe:

  • Visual alarm indicators (lights, screen alerts)
  • Audible alarm tones

If visual alarms work but no sound → likely speaker or audio issue

If neither works → possible system-level issue

  • Expected: Both visual and audible alarms function together
  • Why it matters: Helps isolate audio vs system failure

Inspect External Accessories and Connections

Verify:

  • Patient circuit properly connected
  • Flow sensors and pressure lines correctly installed
  • No bypassed or disconnected components
  • Expected: Proper connections allow accurate alarm triggering
  • Why it matters: Improper setup can prevent the ventilator from detecting fault conditions

Check Power and System Status

Ensure stable power:

  • Connected to AC or adequately charged battery
  • No intermittent shutdowns or resets
  • Expected: Stable operation without power interruptions
  • Why it matters: Power instability can affect alarm system reliability

Perform a System Restart

Power cycle the ventilator fully.

  • Expected: Alarm function returns to normal if issue was software-related
  • Why it matters: Clears temporary faults or configuration glitches

If the Problem Persists

If alarm settings, volume, and external conditions have been verified and alarms still fail to trigger properly, the issue is likely internal (audio circuit, alarm processing, or system board fault).

Remove the ventilator from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot alarm issues on an active patient. Always transition the patient to a functioning ventilator before testing alarm behavior.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator alarms not sounding during use."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Alarm volume found set to minimum and alarm limits set outside normal range."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Adjusted alarm volume and reset alarm limits; verified proper alarm function with test condition."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm volume level verified
  • Mute/silence status checked
  • Alarm limits reviewed and adjusted
  • Test alarm condition performed
  • Visual vs audible alarm behavior noted
  • Circuit connections verified
  • Power source checked
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Alarm functionality is a critical safety feature on ventilators and must never be assumed to be working without verification. Logical troubleshooting—starting with settings and simple external causes—prevents unnecessary downtime while ensuring patient safety. When alarms cannot be reliably restored, immediate escalation is the correct action.

That is successful troubleshooting.
