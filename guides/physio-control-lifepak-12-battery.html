Physio-Control LIFEPAK 12

Battery Discharge / Poor Runtime

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

Physio-Control

Model

LIFEPAK 12

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the LIFEPAK 12:

  • Exhibits rapid battery discharge
  • Shows “Battery Low” warnings sooner than expected
  • Experiences shorter-than-normal operational runtime despite a full charge

Focus is on external, logical troubleshooting to identify common causes before assuming internal battery or board failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Check the Battery Charge Status

  • Ensure the battery is seated correctly and locked into place.
  • Verify the charge level via the device display.
  • Reason: Improper seating can prevent full contact, causing false low-battery warnings.

Inspect the Battery Physically

  • Look for cracks, swelling, corrosion on contacts, or leaks.
  • Reason: Physical damage can reduce capacity or create unsafe conditions.

Test the Charger and Charging Cable

  • Confirm the charger is plugged into a live outlet and indicator lights are normal.
  • Swap the charger with a known-good unit if available.
  • Reason: Faulty chargers or outlets are common causes of incomplete charging.

Check for Accessory Draw

  • Ensure no ECG/defibrillation leads, monitors, or other attachments are connected while charging.
  • Reason: Connected accessories may draw power during charge, extending charging time and reducing effective runtime.

Verify Environmental Conditions

  • Charge and operate the device within the recommended temperature range (typically 15–30°C / 59–86°F).
  • Reason: Extreme cold or heat can temporarily reduce battery performance.

Perform a Device Self-Test

  • Run a full self-test with the battery installed.
  • Observe battery runtime estimate and any fault codes.
  • Reason: Confirms whether the device recognizes the battery correctly and reports expected runtime.

Swap with a Known-Good Battery

  • Install a verified, fully charged battery and repeat self-test.
  • Reason: Isolates whether the battery or the device electronics are the issue.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out (charger, contacts, environmental conditions, battery seating).

The issue is likely due to internal battery degradation or device power circuitry fault.

  • Remove the LIFEPAK 12 from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send battery/device to manufacturer-approved repair or Clinical Engineering bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt to use a device with poor runtime on an active patient.
  • Always swap to a fully functional LIFEPAK 12 for patient care before troubleshooting.
  • Keep a spare, fully charged battery ready for critical areas.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Device battery drains too quickly; runtime less than 30 minutes on a full charge.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Battery seated correctly; charger, outlet, and environmental conditions verified; self-test confirms short runtime.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced with a verified battery; device sent for bench evaluation due to suspected internal battery degradation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified
  • Charger tested or swapped
  • Battery contacts clean and undamaged
  • Device self-test runtime recorded
  • Final device status (removed from service if problem persists)

Final Thought

Patient safety requires ensuring defibrillators are reliable. A logical approach, starting with simple checks and escalating appropriately, minimizes downtime and avoids unnecessary device damage. Proper documentation ensures accountability and supports future preventive maintenance.

That is successful troubleshooting.
