Welch Allyn Connex 6000 Series

SpO₂ Probe Not Detected

Asset Type

Vital Signs Monitor

Manufacturer

Welch Allyn

Model

Connex 6000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Connex 6000 Series monitor reports that the SpO₂ probe is not detected. Symptoms may include no SpO₂ waveform, missing SpO₂ values, or a message indicating the probe is not connected. Most causes are related to loose connections, damaged probes, or incompatible accessories rather than internal monitor failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Confirm the Patient Is Not Actively Being Monitored

  • Ensure the device is not currently connected to a patient for monitoring.
  • If the device is in use, move the patient to another monitor before troubleshooting.

Check the SpO₂ Probe Connection

  • Inspect the SpO₂ probe connection at the monitor.
  • Make sure the connector is fully seated in the SpO₂ port and not partially disconnected.
  • Loose connectors are a common cause of probe detection errors.

Verify the Correct Port Is Being Used

  • Confirm that the probe is connected to the correct SpO₂ port on the monitor or SpO₂ module.
  • Some monitors have multiple accessory ports, and incorrect placement may prevent detection.

Inspect the Probe Cable and Connector

  • Examine the probe cable for visible damage such as cuts, fraying, bent pins, or contamination.
  • Damaged cables can interrupt communication between the probe and the monitor.

Swap the SpO₂ Probe with a Known Good Probe

  • Disconnect the current probe and connect a known working probe.
  • If the new probe is detected, the original probe is likely faulty.
  • If the probe is still not detected, the issue may involve the cable, module, or monitor port.

Check the SpO₂ Extension Cable (If Present)

  • Some setups include an extension cable between the monitor and probe.
  • Remove the extension cable and connect the probe directly to the monitor.
  • Extension cables are common failure points.

Inspect for Contamination in the Port

  • Look inside the SpO₂ port for debris, fluid intrusion, or bent contacts.
  • Contamination can prevent proper electrical contact between the probe and the monitor.

Reboot the Monitor

  • Power cycle the monitor by turning it off, waiting several seconds, and turning it back on.
  • Temporary software communication issues may clear after a reboot.

Verify SpO₂ Module Recognition (If Modular Configuration)

  • If the device uses a removable SpO₂ module, confirm that the module is properly seated and recognized by the monitor.
  • A loose module connection may prevent probe detection.

Test the Monitor with Multiple Probes

  • Testing with two or more known-good probes can help confirm whether the issue is accessory-related or related to the monitor hardware.

If the Problem Persists

If multiple known-good probes are not detected and external checks have been completed, the issue may involve the internal SpO₂ module, input circuitry, or monitor interface.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or further bench evaluation

Recognizing when external troubleshooting has ruled out common causes is an important part of proper Clinical Engineering troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a monitoring accessory while it is connected to an active patient. Always move the patient to another monitor before disconnecting probes or cables to avoid interruption of vital sign monitoring.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nursing reported that the Connex 6000 monitor displays ‘SpO₂ probe not detected’ and no oxygen saturation values appear on the screen.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Damaged SpO₂ probe cable found during inspection. Monitor successfully detected a known-good probe.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced faulty SpO₂ probe with a working unit and verified SpO₂ waveform and numeric values returned to normal operation.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested (if power cycling occurred)
  • Probe swapped with known-good probe
  • Extension cable tested or removed
  • Connector pins inspected
  • Any contamination found in the port
  • Alarm behavior observed
  • Final operational status of SpO₂ measurement

Final Thought

Many SpO₂ detection issues are caused by accessories rather than the monitor itself. Following a structured troubleshooting process—checking probes, cables, connections, and modules first—can quickly identify the problem while keeping the device safely in service when possible. Proper escalation and clear documentation help maintain reliable patient monitoring systems.

That is successful troubleshooting.
