Nihon Kohden Life Scope G Series

Touchscreen Unresponsive

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

Life Scope G Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting situations where the Life Scope G Series monitor touchscreen does not respond to input. Symptoms may include:

  • No response to finger or stylus touch
  • Buttons on screen do not activate
  • Intermittent or erratic touchscreen behavior

Most touchscreen issues are caused by external factors such as contamination, improper connections, or software glitches, rather than hardware failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Device Safety

  • Confirm the monitor is not actively monitoring a patient.
  • If the monitor is in use, move the patient to a backup device first.
  • Why: Patient safety is the priority; avoid troubleshooting during active monitoring.

Check Power Status

  • Verify AC power connection and battery level.
  • Ensure the monitor is powered on and shows normal startup sequence.
  • Why: Touchscreen may appear unresponsive if device is not fully powered.

Inspect Touchscreen Surface

  • Clean the screen using a soft, lint-free cloth and approved monitor cleaning solution.
  • Remove any protective films, adhesive labels, or debris.
  • Why: Dirt, moisture, or residue can interfere with capacitive touch sensing.

Restart the Monitor

  • Power off the monitor completely, wait 30 seconds, and power on.
  • Why: Software or touchscreen drivers may reset, restoring functionality.

Check for Physical Damage

  • Inspect for cracks, loose bezels, or signs of impact.
  • Why: Physical damage can prevent proper touchscreen operation and may require repair.

Verify Peripheral Connections

  • Disconnect external cables (NIBP, SpO₂, ECG leads, USB devices) and check if touchscreen responds.
  • Why: Conflicting signals from peripherals can sometimes interfere with touchscreen response.

Check for System Alerts or Software Locks

  • Look for on-screen messages like “Touchscreen locked” or software update prompts.
  • Follow manufacturer instructions if the screen is locked.
  • Why: Some modes disable touch input temporarily.

If the Problem Persists

After completing the above steps, if the touchscreen remains unresponsive:

  • Remove the monitor from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send the device for bench evaluation or repair with Nihon Kohden service.

Knowing when to stop and escalate prevents further damage and ensures patient safety.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never attempt to force the touchscreen to work during patient monitoring.
  • Use an alternative monitor until the issue is resolved.
  • Document all steps and observations for traceability.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Touchscreen does not respond to input; monitor buttons and menus unresponsive.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
Screen contaminated with residue and software may have been in a locked or unresponsive state.

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
Cleaned touchscreen, verified power and peripheral connections, attempted restart; monitor remains unresponsive → removed from service and sent for repair.

Helpful Details to Include

  • AC power confirmed and battery charged
  • Screen cleaned and debris removed
  • Peripheral cables disconnected
  • Device restart attempted
  • No unusual sounds or lights observed
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting balances careful logic, patient safety, and knowing when to escalate. Proper documentation ensures traceability and supports preventive measures for future touchscreen issues.

That is successful troubleshooting.
