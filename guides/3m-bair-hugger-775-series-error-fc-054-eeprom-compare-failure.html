3M Bair Hugger 775 Series

Error: FC-054 EEPROM Compare Failure

Asset Type

Patient Warming System

Manufacturer

3M

Model

Bair Hugger 775 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Error FC-054 on the 3M Bair Hugger 775 Series Patient Warming System. FC-054 indicates an EEPROM compare failure between copy #1 and copy #2, which may affect stored memory, calibration, configuration, or control data. The unit may require a microprocessor reset, calibration verification, functional testing, or repair before it can be safely returned to patient use.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot the warming unit while it is actively warming a patient.
  • Remove the unit from patient use if Error FC-054 is active, recurring, or if the unit cannot be trusted to operate normally.
  • Have clinical staff transfer patient warming to another available warming device if warming is still needed.
  • Label the unit for Clinical Engineering evaluation before further troubleshooting.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Confirm that the displayed error is FC-054.
  • Power the unit on in a safe non-patient-care setting and observe the display or indicator behavior.
  • Determine whether the error appears at startup, after a power interruption, during operation, or intermittently.
  • Ask clinical staff whether the unit failed to heat, alarmed, shut down, or would not enter normal operation.
  • Document whether the error clears temporarily or returns immediately.

Check External Power First

  • Plug the unit into a known-good hospital-grade outlet.
  • Inspect the power cord and plug for damage, looseness, bent blades, overheating, or signs of strain.
  • Avoid using extension cords, loose power strips, or questionable outlets during testing.
  • Confirm the unit powers consistently without flickering, cycling, or losing power.

Inspect the Unit for Obvious Physical Issues

  • Check the housing, control panel, hose connection, and power entry area.
  • Look for signs of fluid intrusion, impact damage, contamination, heat damage, or unusual odor.
  • Verify the control panel buttons are not stuck, damaged, or held down.
  • Confirm the Bair Hugger logo button and Standby button can be pressed normally before attempting the reset.

Perform the Microprocessor Reset

  • Disconnect the unit from AC power.
  • Press and hold the Bair Hugger logo button and the Standby button at the same time.
  • While holding both buttons, connect the unit to the power source.
  • Continue holding both buttons until the Standby light remains on.
  • Release the buttons once the Standby light remains on.

Recheck for Error FC-054

  • Power cycle the unit normally after the reset.
  • Allow the unit to complete startup.
  • Confirm whether FC-054 clears or reappears.
  • Observe for abnormal sounds, failure to heat, unexpected shutdown, control issues, or repeated error behavior.

Calibrate the Unit Before Return to Service

  • If the reset clears the error, complete operating temperature calibration before returning the unit to clinical use.
  • Follow facility procedure and manufacturer service guidance for calibration and post-service checks.
  • Verify the unit responds properly at the expected temperature settings.
  • Confirm airflow, heater operation, controls, and indicators are functioning normally.
  • Document calibration and functional test results.

Return to Service Only if Fully Resolved

  • Return the device to service only if FC-054 clears, calibration passes, and functional testing is acceptable.
  • Remove the Out of Service tag only after the device passes all required checks.
  • Clean the unit as needed before returning it to the clinical area.
  • Document the reset, calibration, testing, and final device status.

If the Problem Persists

If Error FC-054 returns after the microprocessor reset, external power issues, obvious physical concerns, and common recoverable causes have been ruled out. Persistent FC-054 strongly suggests an internal memory, control, or processor-related failure.

  • Remove the unit from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Do not return the unit to patient use based only on temporary clearing.
  • Send the unit for repair, bench evaluation, or manufacturer/vendor service.
  • Complete calibration and functional testing after repair before returning the unit to service.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this error on an active patient. If patient warming is needed, move the patient to a backup warming device first, then troubleshoot the failed unit away from patient care. Do not rely on a patient warming unit with an unresolved EEPROM or calibration-related error.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"3M Bair Hugger 775 Series reported Error FC-054. Error indicates EEPROM compare failure copy #1 to copy #2. Unit removed from patient use for Clinical Engineering evaluation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Confirmed FC-054 memory compare error during startup. External power and physical condition checked. Error related to stored EEPROM data mismatch. Microprocessor reset required."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Performed microprocessor reset by holding Bair Hugger logo and Standby button while connecting AC power, then released buttons once Standby light remained on. Error cleared. Completed calibration and functional testing. Unit passed checks and was returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Error FC-054 should be treated as more than a nuisance code because it points to a stored memory comparison failure. The correct approach is to protect the patient first, verify the error, rule out simple external issues, perform the approved reset, and complete calibration before return to service. If the error returns, escalation is appropriate because the likely cause has moved beyond basic troubleshooting.

That is successful troubleshooting.
