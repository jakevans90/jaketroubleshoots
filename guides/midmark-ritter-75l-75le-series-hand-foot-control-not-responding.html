Midmark Ritter 75L / 75LE Series

Hand / Foot Control Not Responding

Asset Type

Exam Table

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

Ritter 75L / 75LE Series

What This Guide Helps With

No response from hand or foot controls causing loss of table movement due to power, connection, or accessory-related issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety

  • Remove the table from active patient use before troubleshooting.
  • Expected: Patient safely relocated to another table.
  • Why it matters: Loss of control can lead to unsafe positioning or unexpected movement.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Attempt to operate all functions (hi-lo, backrest) using both hand and foot controls (if equipped).
  • Expected: No response or intermittent movement.
  • Why it matters: Determines if the issue is isolated to one control or system-wide.

Check Power to the Table

  • Confirm the table is plugged into a known working outlet. Test the outlet with another device.
  • Expected: Table has power (listen for motor readiness or indicator lights if present).
  • Why it matters: No power will result in complete loss of control function.
  • If no power → restore power and retest

Inspect Power Cord and Connection

  • Check the power cord for damage, loose connections, or strain at the entry point to the base.
  • Expected: Cord intact and securely connected.
  • Why it matters: Intermittent or lost power can mimic control failure.
  • If damaged → remove from service

Check Hand Control Connection

  • Inspect where the hand control plugs into the table base. Ensure it is fully seated and not loose.
  • Expected: Secure connection with no movement at the port.
  • Why it matters: Loose or partially connected controls will not send signals to the system.
  • Reseat connection and retest

Inspect Hand Control Cable and Buttons

  • Check for cuts, pinching, or wear along the cable. Press each button and feel for proper tactile response.
  • Expected: Buttons press normally and cable is intact.
  • Why it matters: Internal wire breaks or stuck buttons prevent signal transmission.
  • If damage or unresponsive buttons → replace hand control

Check Foot Control (If Equipped)

  • Inspect the foot control for stuck pedals, debris, or fluid intrusion. Verify cable connection to the table.
  • Expected: Pedals move freely and cable is intact.
  • Why it matters: Foot controls are prone to damage from fluid exposure and floor use.
  • If contaminated or damaged → clean if appropriate or replace

Test Alternate Control (If Available)

  • If both hand and foot controls exist, test each independently.
  • Expected: One control may function while the other does not.
  • Why it matters: Helps isolate whether the issue is the control itself or the table system.
  • If one works → faulty control identified
  • If neither works → proceed to next step

Cycle Power to the Table

  • Unplug the table, wait 30 seconds, then reconnect power. Retest controls.
  • Expected: System resets and controls respond.
  • Why it matters: Clears minor electrical or control lockup conditions.
  • If resolved → return to service

Inspect for Accessory or Lockout Conditions

  • Ensure no accessory, drawer heater, or system interlock is causing a control lockout (if applicable).
  • Expected: No external conditions preventing operation.
  • Why it matters: Some configurations may inhibit movement for safety.

If the Problem Persists

If all external causes have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal (control board, actuator control circuit, or wiring harness).

Remove the table from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and aligns with proper Clinical Engineering troubleshooting practices.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot control issues with a patient on the table. Always transfer the patient first to prevent injury from unexpected movement or loss of positioning.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Staff reported exam table would not respond to hand or foot control inputs."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Loose hand control connection at base preventing signal transmission."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reseated hand control connection, tested all functions, and returned unit to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Outlet tested and confirmed working
  • Power cord condition verified
  • Hand/foot control cables inspected
  • Buttons/pedals tested for responsiveness
  • Indicator lights or motor sound observed
  • Alternate control tested
  • Final device status (working or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Control failures are often caused by simple connection or accessory issues. Always start with power and external components before assuming internal failure. Prioritize patient safety, use a logical approach, and document clearly to support future troubleshooting and repairs.

That is successful troubleshooting.
