GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series

Error: COLLIMATOR CAL REQUIRED - PRESS ANY KEY

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

OEC 9900 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series C-Arm error COLLIMATOR CAL REQUIRED - PRESS ANY KEY. This condition appears when the collimator iris and leaf position values do not match the stored reference values. While the error is active, generator functions, including X-rays, may be disabled until the condition is cleared and proper operation is confirmed.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the C-Arm from patient use if the error appears before or during a procedure.
  • Stop use of the C-Arm if generator functions or X-rays are disabled.
  • Notify clinical staff that the system is not available for imaging until the error is cleared and proper function is confirmed.
  • Use another C-Arm if imaging is needed for an active case.
  • Do not continue troubleshooting while the device is actively needed for patient imaging.

Verify the Reported Error and Behavior

  • Power on the OEC 9900 and observe the startup sequence.
  • Confirm the message displays exactly: COLLIMATOR CAL REQUIRED - PRESS ANY KEY.
  • Check whether the error appears during startup, after collimator movement, after restart, or intermittently during use.
  • Confirm whether X-ray or generator functions are disabled while the error is active.
  • Document the error behavior before clearing or restarting the system.

Clear the Message from the C-Arm Control Panel

  • Follow the displayed instruction first.
  • Press any C-Arm control panel key to acknowledge and clear the message.
  • Observe whether the message clears and whether normal system operation returns.
  • If the message clears, verify basic C-Arm function and collimator operation before returning the device to service.

Restart the System and Recheck

  • If the message clears but returns, power the system down using the normal shutdown procedure.
  • Wait briefly, then power the system back on.
  • Observe whether the error returns during startup.
  • A restart helps confirm whether the issue was temporary or a recurring calibration fault.

Check for Obvious Collimator Movement Problems

  • Inspect the collimator area externally for signs of damage, debris, binding, impact, or contamination.
  • Check whether iris and leaf movement appears smooth when commanded, if the system allows movement.
  • Do not force the collimator blades.
  • Do not perform internal disassembly during clinical troubleshooting.
  • If movement appears restricted or abnormal, remove the device from service for further evaluation.

Check Recent Handling or Service History

  • Ask staff whether the C-Arm was recently moved, bumped, serviced, stored, or powered off unexpectedly.
  • Check for visible damage around the C-Arm head, collimator area, cable strain points, and control panel.
  • Note any recent transport, impact, calibration, service work, or abnormal shutdown in the work order.

Perform Collimator Calibration Only If Qualified

  • If the error returns after acknowledgment and restart, collimator calibration may be required.
  • Perform calibration only if you are qualified, authorized, and have the correct GE service procedure and required tools.
  • After calibration, restart the system and confirm that the error does not return.
  • If calibration cannot be performed safely or correctly, remove the C-Arm from service and escalate for repair.

Verify Operation Before Return to Service

  • Confirm the system boots without the error.
  • Confirm collimator iris and leaf movement operates normally.
  • Confirm imaging functions are available according to facility procedure.
  • Check that no related collimator, generator, or X-ray disabled messages return.
  • Return the device to service only after proper function is verified.

If the Problem Persists

If the COLLIMATOR CAL REQUIRED - PRESS ANY KEY error returns after acknowledgment, restart, external inspection, and proper calibration attempts, common external causes have been ruled out.

Persistent recurrence may indicate a collimator position feedback problem, iris or leaf movement problem, collimator motor or sensor issue, wiring or connector issue, stored calibration/reference problem, or collimator control circuitry fault.

The C-Arm should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this error on an active patient. If imaging is needed, move the patient or imaging need to a backup C-Arm first, then troubleshoot the affected device after it has been removed from patient use.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"GE OEC 9900 displayed COLLIMATOR CAL REQUIRED - PRESS ANY KEY. Staff reported X-rays/generator functions were disabled and the unit could not be used for imaging."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Collimator iris and leaf position values did not match stored reference values. Error cleared initially but returned after restart, indicating a possible collimator calibration or position feedback issue."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed C-Arm from clinical use. Pressed C-Arm control panel key to acknowledge message, restarted system, and inspected for obvious external collimator obstruction or damage. Device remains Out of Service pending calibration or repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The COLLIMATOR CAL REQUIRED - PRESS ANY KEY error should be treated as a real imaging availability and patient safety issue because generator functions, including X-rays, may be disabled while the condition is active. Start with simple confirmation and acknowledgment, then move logically through restart, external inspection, qualified calibration, and repair escalation if the fault returns. Good troubleshooting protects the patient, avoids unsafe workarounds, and creates clear documentation.

That is successful troubleshooting.
