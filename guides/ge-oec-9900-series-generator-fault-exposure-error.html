GE Healthcare OEC 9900 Series

Generator Fault / Exposure Error

Asset Type

C-Arm

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

OEC 9900 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a GE Healthcare OEC 9900 C-Arm that displays generator faults or exposure errors during X-ray operation. Symptoms may include:

  • Inability to generate X-rays
  • Error messages on the display indicating generator or exposure issues
  • Aborted exposures during procedures
  • Footswitch activation not producing X-ray output

Focus is on external checks and safe troubleshooting before escalation to repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power and Connections

  • Ensure the C-Arm is plugged into a dedicated outlet with proper voltage.
  • Check for tripped circuit breakers or blown fuses.
  • Confirm all external power switches on the generator module are on.

Why: Power irregularities are a common cause of generator faults.

Check Footswitch Functionality

  • Ensure the footswitch is fully seated and undamaged.
  • Test the footswitch continuity using a multimeter if available.

Why: Faulty footswitches can prevent X-ray generation, mimicking generator errors.

Inspect Cables and Connectors

  • Examine high-voltage and control cables for fraying, loose connections, or visible damage.
  • Reseat or replace any suspicious cables.

Why: Loose or damaged cables can trigger exposure errors.

Review System Status and Error Messages

  • Access the control panel or service menu to note specific fault codes.
  • Document any recurring or unusual error messages.

Why: Error codes can guide further troubleshooting or repair escalation.

Reset System Safely

  • Power down the unit completely.
  • Wait 2–3 minutes, then power on and attempt exposure again.

Why: Temporary software or electronic glitches can sometimes resolve with a reset.

Check for Interlocks or Safety Sensors

  • Ensure the patient table, collimator, and detector interlocks are fully engaged.
  • Confirm that the detector arm is properly positioned.

Why: Open interlocks can inhibit X-ray generation for safety.

If the Problem Persists

All common external causes have been ruled out.

The generator or high-voltage module may be faulty.

  • Remove the unit from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Escalate to manufacturer repair or service bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop is part of proper troubleshooting and prevents patient risk.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Do not attempt exposures on an active patient while troubleshooting.
  • Move the patient to another verified working C-Arm.
  • Only troubleshoot when the patient is safely positioned elsewhere.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“C-Arm displays generator fault and exposure error during use.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Footswitch tested functional; power, cables, and interlocks verified. Error persists, indicating likely internal generator failure.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet voltage tested and stable
  • Footswitch continuity verified
  • HV cables inspected
  • Interlocks checked
  • Error code documentation
  • Unit power-cycled with same fault
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount. Systematic, logic-based troubleshooting reduces unnecessary interventions and highlights the importance of proper escalation. Accurate documentation ensures follow-up repair is informed and efficient.

That is successful troubleshooting.
