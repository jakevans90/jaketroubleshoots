Hamilton C1

Alarm: 244012 Battery 1: Defective

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Hamilton

Model

C1

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot Alarm 244012 Battery 1: Defective on the Hamilton C1 ventilator. This alarm indicates Battery 1 has failed, is not charging correctly, or is not being recognized safely by the ventilator. Because battery backup is part of safe ventilator operation, this condition should be treated as a patient safety concern before any equipment troubleshooting begins.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot a ventilator battery failure while relying on that battery for active patient ventilation.
  • If the ventilator is in use, transfer the patient to another ventilator or confirm continuous AC power and clinical backup before troubleshooting.
  • Remove the ventilator from patient use if battery backup is required, external power is unstable, or the device may need to be powered down.
  • Do not rely on Battery 1 for transport, emergency backup, or areas where external power may be interrupted until the alarm is resolved.

Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm the screen displays Alarm 244012 Battery 1: Defective.
  • Observe whether the alarm appears at startup, while connected to AC power, during battery operation, after battery installation, or intermittently during use.
  • Check whether Battery 1 shows not recognized, not charging, permanent failure, abnormal charge status, or reduced/no runtime.
  • Confirm the issue is specific to Battery 1 and not a general external power or battery communication problem.

Confirm External Power Is Connected

  • Connect the ventilator to a known-good AC outlet.
  • Verify the power cord is fully seated at the wall outlet and at the ventilator power input.
  • Check that the external power indicator confirms the ventilator is receiving AC power.
  • If the alarm clears after restoring external power and Battery 1 begins charging normally, monitor the device and complete functional checks before return to service.

Inspect Battery 1 Seating

  • Power the ventilator down only if it is safe and appropriate to do so.
  • Remove Battery 1 and inspect whether it was partially latched, loose, or misaligned.
  • Reinstall Battery 1 firmly into the correct battery position.
  • Restart the ventilator or check the battery status screen to see whether Battery 1 is detected normally.
  • If reseating resolves the alarm and Battery 1 charges normally, complete functional checks before returning the ventilator to service.

Inspect Battery Contacts

  • Remove Battery 1 and inspect the battery contacts and ventilator battery bay contacts.
  • Look for bent contacts, corrosion, debris, fluid contamination, burn marks, physical damage, or loose/recessed contact points.
  • Do not scrape, modify, bend, or force contacts.
  • If contamination or damage is found, remove the ventilator from service for cleaning, inspection, or repair according to facility procedure.

Replace Battery 1 With an Approved Compatible Battery

  • Install a known-good, approved Hamilton-compatible battery in the Battery 1 position.
  • Confirm the battery locks into place correctly.
  • Power the ventilator on and check the battery status.
  • Confirm the replacement battery is recognized and begins charging normally.
  • If the replacement battery resolves the alarm, remove the original Battery 1 from circulation.

Confirm Charging and Battery Recognition

  • With the replacement battery installed and AC power connected, verify Battery 1 is detected.
  • Confirm Battery 1 charging status appears normal.
  • Confirm no Battery 1 defective alarm returns.
  • Confirm no battery communication alarm appears.
  • Allow the ventilator enough time to confirm normal charging behavior before returning it to service.

Check for Battery Bay or Device-Side Issue

  • If allowed by facility procedure, test the suspected defective battery in another compatible ventilator or test a known-good battery in the same Battery 1 bay.
  • If the original battery fails in multiple units, the battery is likely defective.
  • If a known-good battery fails in the Battery 1 bay, suspect a ventilator-side issue.
  • If the alarm follows the battery, suspect the battery.
  • If the alarm stays with the device, suspect the battery bay, contacts, charging circuit, battery communication, or battery management hardware.

If the Problem Persists

If Alarm 244012 Battery 1: Defective remains after external power is verified, Battery 1 is reseated, contacts are inspected, and a known-good approved battery is installed, common external causes have been ruled out.

Persistent Battery 1 defective alarms may indicate battery bay contact failure, charging circuit failure, battery communication fault, internal battery management hardware failure, or another device-side power system problem.

The ventilator should be removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for repair or bench evaluation.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot on an active patient. Move the patient to a backup ventilator or approved backup ventilation method first. Do not rely on a ventilator with an unresolved Battery 1 defective alarm for patient transport, emergency backup, or any situation where external power may be interrupted.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Hamilton C1 ventilator reported Alarm 244012 Battery 1: Defective. Battery 1 showed defective status and was not considered reliable for backup power."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Battery 1 indicated permanent battery failure. External power was verified, battery seating and contacts were inspected, and the fault was isolated to the installed Battery 1."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed defective Battery 1 from service and installed an approved compatible replacement battery. Confirmed Battery 1 was recognized, charging normally, and no battery defective alarm returned. Ventilator returned to service after successful checks."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery alarms on ventilators should always be treated as patient safety concerns. Start with stable external power, battery seating, contacts, and approved battery replacement before suspecting internal failure. If the alarm persists with a known-good battery, remove the ventilator from service and escalate for repair. Clear CCR documentation shows what was reported, what was tested, what failed, and why the device was returned to service or removed from use.

That is successful troubleshooting.
