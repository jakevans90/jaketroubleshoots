GE Healthcare Giraffe Warmer

SKIN TEMPERATURE PROBE ERROR

Asset Type

Infant Warmer

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Giraffe Warmer

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting probe-related alarms, missing or unstable skin temperature readings, and incorrect Baby Mode operation caused by external probe or connection problems.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

If the warmer is in use and the skin temperature probe error occurs, have clinical staff move the infant to a safe alternate warming method or another verified warmer before troubleshooting.

Do not rely on Baby Mode while the device has an unresolved skin temperature probe issue, because Baby Mode uses the skin temperature probe reading to regulate heater output.

Expected outcome: The infant is safely supported without dependence on the affected warmer.

2. Confirm the Reported Problem

Power on the warmer while it is not being used on a patient and verify the reported behavior. Document whether the unit displays:

  • A skin temperature probe-related message or alarm.
  • Confirm Probe Jack Connection when the probe is disconnected or not recognized.
  • No baby temperature reading.
  • An intermittent, implausible, or fluctuating temperature value.
  • Inability to operate correctly in Baby Mode.

GE training material identifies Confirm Probe Jack Connection as an alarm associated with an unplugged skin temperature probe jack.

Expected outcome: The exact alarm message and operating condition are confirmed before parts are exchanged.

3. Confirm the Correct Probe Is Being Used

Verify that the connected skin temperature probe is intended for the Giraffe Warmer and is approved by facility policy.

GE HealthCare lists its Giraffe Panda disposable infant skin temperature probe as compatible with the Giraffe Warmer and Panda Warmers.

Expected outcome: An incompatible or incorrect accessory is ruled out.

If the error resolves after installing the correct probe, stop troubleshooting and document the correction.

4. Inspect the Skin Temperature Probe Externally

Disconnect the probe and inspect the accessible portions of the probe, cable, and connector for:

  • Bent, recessed, loose, or contaminated connector contacts.
  • Cracked connector housing.
  • Cuts, pinches, kinks, stretching, or exposed wiring.
  • Moisture, residue, or signs of improper cleaning.
  • Damage at the probe tip or cable strain relief.

Do not use a damaged reusable probe or a suspect disposable probe for clinical operation.

Expected outcome: The probe is visibly intact, clean, and free of external damage.

If damage is found, replace the probe with a known-good compatible probe and retest. If the error clears, stop.

5. Reseat the Probe Connection

Insert the skin temperature probe fully into the appropriate probe jack. Confirm the connector is seated securely and is not loose or easily disturbed.

Gently observe whether the displayed reading or alarm changes when the connector is stationary. Do not manipulate the connection aggressively.

Expected outcome: A properly seated probe produces a stable recognized connection without a probe-jack alarm.

If reseating clears the error and the reading remains stable, stop troubleshooting after completing functional verification.

6. Check for Probe Attachment or Setup Issues

If the original complaint occurred during use, ask whether the probe was:

  • Dislodged from the infant.
  • Pulled by linens, tubing, or repositioning.
  • Wet, soiled, or improperly secured.
  • Connected during a cleaning or patient turnover process.
  • Used without the appropriate heat-reflective probe cover when required by clinical protocol.

Probe attachment issues may cause inaccurate or unstable readings even when the warmer recognizes the probe.

Expected outcome: A clinical setup issue is identified or ruled out without troubleshooting on an active patient.

7. Test With a Known-Good Compatible Probe

With the warmer removed from patient use, connect a known-good compatible skin temperature probe.

Observe whether:

  • The probe-related alarm clears.
  • A reasonable and stable temperature value appears.
  • Baby Mode accepts the probe input without immediately generating the same error.

Expected outcome: The test separates a failed accessory from a device-side probe input problem.

If the known-good probe works properly: Remove the original probe from use and document it as defective or suspect.

If the same error remains with a known-good probe: Continue to the next step.

8. Inspect the Warmer Probe Jack Externally

Inspect the accessible probe jack on the warmer for:

  • Debris, fluid residue, or cleaning solution contamination.
  • Loose mounting or movement of the jack.
  • Bent or damaged internal contact appearance visible without disassembly.
  • Evidence that the connector cannot seat fully.

Do not insert tools into the jack or attempt internal repair during this troubleshooting process.

Expected outcome: The external jack condition is verified.

If contamination or visible damage prevents a reliable connection, remove the warmer from service for bench evaluation.

9. Verify Stable Temperature Display Off Patient

Using the known-good probe and an approved facility method for functional verification, confirm that the displayed skin temperature is stable and plausible.

Do not return the warmer to service solely because an alarm clears; the probe input must also display reliably.

Expected outcome: The warmer consistently recognizes the probe and displays a stable temperature input.

If the value is missing, erratic, or unreasonable with a known-good probe, remove the warmer from service.

10. Complete Functional Checkout Before Return to Service

After correcting an external issue, perform the applicable pre-use or post-repair functional checkout according to hospital procedure and available manufacturer documentation.

Confirm:

  • No active skin temperature probe alarm remains.
  • The probe connection is stable.
  • The displayed baby temperature is plausible.
  • The warmer is safe for clinical return.

Expected outcome: The warmer passes functional verification before being released for use.

If the Problem Persists

If the Giraffe Warmer continues to display a skin temperature probe error, does not recognize a known-good compatible probe, or produces unstable readings after external checks, common external causes have been ruled out. The issue may involve the probe jack, internal cabling, control electronics, or temperature measurement circuitry.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service.
  • Labeled Out of Service.
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation by qualified personnel.

Knowing when to stop troubleshooting and escalate is proper Clinical Engineering practice, especially when temperature regulation may affect neonatal safety.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a skin temperature probe error while an infant remains dependent on the affected warmer in Baby Mode. Move the patient to a verified alternate warming device first, then evaluate the warmer safely off patient.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"NICU reported the GE Healthcare Giraffe Warmer displayed a skin temperature probe error and would not provide a stable baby temperature reading in Baby Mode."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Inspection found the connected skin temperature probe cable damaged near the connector, and the alarm cleared when a known-good compatible probe was installed."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the damaged probe from use, verified stable skin temperature display with a replacement probe, completed functional checkout, and returned the warmer to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm or message displayed, including Confirm Probe Jack Connection
  • Operating mode when the issue occurred
  • Whether the unit was in use on a patient
  • Probe type and compatibility verified
  • Probe connector fully seated
  • Visible probe or cable damage
  • Known-good probe substitution result
  • Probe jack condition
  • Displayed temperature behavior
  • Functional checkout result
  • Final device status

Final Thought

A skin temperature probe error on an infant warmer must be approached cautiously because dependable temperature monitoring is essential to safe neonatal warming. Begin with the probe, connection, and setup before suspecting internal failure. When a known-good probe does not resolve the issue, remove the warmer from service and document the findings clearly.

That is successful troubleshooting.
