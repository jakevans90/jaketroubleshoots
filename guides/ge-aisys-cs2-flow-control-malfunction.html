GE Aisys CS2 Anesthesia Machine

Electronic Flow Control Malfunction

Asset Type

Anesthesia Machine

Manufacturer

GE Healthcare

Model

Aisys CS2

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Aisys CS2 anesthesia machine exhibits issues with electronic flow control, such as:

  • Inability to adjust fresh gas flows accurately
  • Flow settings not responding or jumping erratically
  • Alarms related to flow control or delivery of anesthetic gases

The focus is on external checks, power, and setup verification before assuming internal electronic or flow module failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power and Boot Sequence

  • Ensure the machine is plugged into a verified, live outlet.
  • Confirm the machine completes its startup self-check without unrelated errors.
  • Reason: Flow control electronics rely on stable power and a clean boot.

Check Circuit Breakers and Fuses

  • Inspect the main power switch and machine fuses (if externally accessible).
  • Reason: Blown fuses can disrupt electronic flow control circuits.

Inspect Flow Control Knobs and Interface

  • Rotate each flow control knob and observe display response.
  • Confirm there is no physical obstruction or stiffness.
  • Reason: Mechanical interference can mimic electronic faults.

Verify Gas Supply Connections

  • Check that oxygen, nitrous oxide, and air hoses are properly connected and valves are open.
  • Ensure gas pressures are within manufacturer specifications.
  • Reason: Low or missing supply can trigger flow control alarms or erratic behavior.

Check Software Settings

  • Confirm that electronic flow limits or safety interlocks are not preventing adjustments.
  • Verify that any recent software updates were completed successfully.
  • Reason: Incorrect settings can prevent flow changes even if hardware is functional.

Cycle Power and Observe Behavior

  • Perform a controlled shutdown and restart of the machine.
  • Attempt adjusting flows after restart.
  • Reason: Transient software glitches can cause temporary electronic flow control malfunctions.

Swap or Test External Accessories

  • If using flow sensors, modules, or patient circuits with integrated electronics, swap with a known good set.
  • Reason: Faulty accessories may feed incorrect readings to the flow control system.

If the Problem Persists

If all external checks pass and flow control remains erratic:

  • Remove the machine from clinical service.
  • Label Out of Service.
  • Escalate for manufacturer repair or bench evaluation.
  • Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting—do not attempt internal electronic repair.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never adjust flows on a patient when malfunctioning.
  • Move the patient to a backup anesthesia machine if available.
  • Ensure patient safety is always prioritized over troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Electronic flow control will not adjust oxygen or nitrous flows; display shows erratic readings.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“External inspection shows power stable, gas supplies connected, knobs intact; software settings verified. Issue persists after restart.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Machine removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent to GE Healthcare service for electronic module evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested with meter
  • Power cycle performed
  • Gas pressures verified
  • Flow knobs checked for mechanical issues
  • Any alarms displayed
  • Circuit or accessory swaps performed
  • Final machine status (Out of Service)

Final Thought

Proper troubleshooting balances patient safety, systematic logic, and timely escalation. Recording CCR details ensures a clear audit trail and communicates findings efficiently to repair teams. Avoid pushing a malfunctioning machine into service.

That is successful troubleshooting.
