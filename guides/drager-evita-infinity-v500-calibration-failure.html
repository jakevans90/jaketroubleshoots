Drager Evita V500

O₂ Sensor Drift or Calibration Failure

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V500

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses situations where the Drager Evita V500 ventilator shows:

  • Erratic O₂ readings or sudden changes in measured FiO₂
  • O₂ sensor calibration failures during pre-use checks
  • Alarm messages indicating O₂ sensor drift or failure
  • Unexpected patient O₂ delivery deviations

Focus is on external, logical troubleshooting steps first before assuming internal sensor failure.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify Power and Warm-Up

  • Ensure the ventilator has been powered on for the manufacturer-recommended warm-up period.
  • Sensors may report drift immediately after power-on; allowing proper warm-up can stabilize readings.

Check Gas Supply Integrity

  • Confirm wall O₂ and air pressures are within nominal range using the built-in pressure indicators or an independent manometer.
  • Inspect for kinks, leaks, or faulty flow meters that could affect sensor readings.

Inspect O₂ Sensor Module

  • Confirm the sensor is correctly seated in its connector. Loose connections can cause intermittent drift errors.
  • Look for visible contamination, corrosion, or moisture on sensor contacts.

Run Calibration Procedure

  • Perform a standard calibration using the ventilator menu or calibration kit.
  • Note whether the device completes calibration successfully or fails at a specific step.
  • A failed calibration suggests the sensor may be outside its usable lifespan or defective.

Swap With Known Good Sensor (If Available)

  • Replace the suspect O₂ sensor with a verified, functional sensor.
  • Re-run calibration and verify that readings stabilize.
  • If replacement resolves the issue, the original sensor is likely at fault.

Check Software and Firmware

  • Ensure the ventilator is running the latest approved firmware version.
  • Some sensor drift errors can be triggered by outdated software; updating may resolve false drift alarms.

Document Observations

  • Note all abnormal readings, calibration failure steps, alarms, and any corrective actions taken.

If the Problem Persists

External checks have been exhausted. The sensor or internal calibration circuit may have failed.

  • Remove the ventilator from service
  • Label as “Out of Service – O₂ Sensor Fault”
  • Send to Biomedical Engineering or Drager service for bench evaluation or sensor replacement

Knowing when to stop troubleshooting prevents further patient risk.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never adjust or troubleshoot the O₂ sensor with a patient connected.
  • Move the patient to a backup ventilator first.
  • Only perform O₂ sensor checks or calibration on a safe, powered-on, unoccupied unit.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator displayed O₂ sensor drift and failed calibration during pre-use check.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Sensor module failed calibration; readings remained unstable despite proper warm-up and correct gas supply.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Replaced sensor with verified module and successfully completed calibration. Device returned to service. Original sensor labeled for service/bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • O₂ sensor module serial number
  • Pre- and post-calibration FiO₂ readings
  • Any abnormal alarm messages
  • Gas supply pressures during troubleshooting
  • Sensor warm-up time and ventilator uptime

Final Thought

Patient safety and logical troubleshooting are paramount. Follow a stepwise approach, verify external causes first, and escalate when internal sensor failure is suspected. Proper CCR documentation ensures traceability and prevents repeated issues.

That is successful troubleshooting.
