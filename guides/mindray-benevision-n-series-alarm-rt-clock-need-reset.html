Mindray BeneVision N Series

Alarm: RT CLOCK NEED RESET

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Mindray

Model

BeneVision N Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide helps troubleshoot the RT CLOCK NEED RESET alarm on a Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor. This alarm means the monitor real-time clock needs to be reset. It may affect time stamps, trends, alarm history, event review, and documentation accuracy.

The condition may be caused by an incorrect date or time setting, a clock reset after power loss, depleted backup power, a weak button battery, or a main control board issue.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Do not troubleshoot the monitor while relying on it for active patient monitoring if the date or time is incorrect.
  • Confirm the patient is being monitored safely.
  • If accurate alarm history, trends, or event time stamps are clinically important, move the patient to another verified monitor before troubleshooting.
  • Do not rely on a monitor with an unresolved clock alarm for documentation-sensitive patient monitoring.

2. Verify the Reported Alarm

  • Confirm the alarm displayed on the monitor is RT CLOCK NEED RESET.
  • Check whether the alarm appears during startup, after restart, after being unplugged, or after battery depletion.
  • Check the current date and time shown on the monitor.
  • Confirm whether the clock is incorrect, reset to a default value, or not retaining the correct setting.

3. Reset the Date and Time

  • Access the monitor date and time settings using the approved facility workflow.
  • Set the correct date and time.
  • Confirm the correct time zone or facility time configuration if applicable.
  • Save the setting and restart the monitor.
  • Verify whether the RT CLOCK NEED RESET alarm clears after restart.

4. Confirm the Monitor Retains Time After Restart

  • After setting the correct time, power cycle or restart the monitor.
  • Allow the monitor to fully boot.
  • Recheck the displayed date and time.
  • Confirm whether the alarm returns.
  • If the time remains correct and the alarm clears, the issue may have been a simple clock reset condition.

5. Check External Power Conditions

  • Confirm the monitor is receiving normal AC power.
  • Check whether the monitor had been stored unplugged for an extended period.
  • Check whether the monitor battery was fully depleted.
  • Inspect the power cord, power supply connection, docking setup, and power source.
  • Correct any obvious external power issue before suspecting internal failure.

6. Inspect for Physical or Environmental Issues

  • Inspect the monitor for signs of physical damage.
  • Check for liquid intrusion, contamination, cracked housing, or impact damage.
  • Confirm the monitor is being used in an appropriate environment.
  • Check for unusual heat, odor, abnormal sounds, or other signs of internal electronic trouble.

7. Identify the Likely Cause

  • If the alarm clears after resetting the time and the monitor retains the correct time, return the monitor to service after normal function checks.
  • If the time resets every time the monitor restarts, suspect a clock backup battery issue.
  • If the alarm continues after supported battery service, suspect a main control board or internal storage issue.
  • Do not perform internal board-level repair while the monitor is in clinical use.

If the Problem Persists

If the RT CLOCK NEED RESET alarm returns after the time is reset, the monitor is restarted, external power is verified, and obvious external causes are ruled out, the issue is no longer a simple clock setting problem.

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send it for repair or bench evaluation. The monitor may require clock backup battery service, main control board evaluation, or vendor repair depending on facility policy and supported service procedures.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot this alarm on an active patient when accurate timing is important for care, documentation, alarm review, or trend review. Move the patient to a backup monitor first, then troubleshoot the affected device away from patient use.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Mindray BeneVision N Series patient monitor displayed RT CLOCK NEED RESET alarm during startup and the monitor date/time required reset."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Real-time clock setting was incorrect. Date and time were reset, monitor was restarted, and clock retention was checked. If alarm returns, suspected clock backup battery or main control board issue."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Reset monitor date and time, restarted monitor, and verified the correct time was retained. Alarm cleared and monitor passed normal startup checks. Returned to service."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

The RT CLOCK NEED RESET alarm should be handled logically. Protect patient safety first, verify the exact alarm, reset the clock, confirm the monitor can retain the corrected time, and rule out external power or storage conditions. If the alarm returns after reset and restart, remove the monitor from service and escalate it for proper repair evaluation. Clear CCR documentation shows what was reported, what was checked, and why the device was returned to service or removed from use.

That is successful troubleshooting.
