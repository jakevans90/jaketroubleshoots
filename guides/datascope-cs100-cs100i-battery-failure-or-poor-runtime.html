Datascope CS100 / CS100i

Battery Failure or Poor Runtime

Asset Type

Intra-Aortic Balloon Pump

Manufacturer

Datascope

Model

CS100 / CS100i

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting CS100 / CS100i battery alarms, poor battery runtime, charging concerns, or unexpected loss of battery operation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Confirm the IABP is not being evaluated on an active patient unless clinical staff have already secured alternate support.

If the pump is in use and battery reliability is questionable, keep the unit connected to verified AC power and have clinical staff prepare another functional IABP if transport or battery operation may be needed.

Expected outcome: Patient support is maintained without relying on an unstable battery.

If this resolves the immediate risk, stop troubleshooting during patient use and arrange bench evaluation when safe.

Confirm the Reported Battery Issue

Verify what was reported:

  • Battery failure alarm
  • Battery not charging
  • Short runtime
  • Unit shuts down on battery
  • Battery indicator drops quickly
  • Pump only operates on AC power
  • Battery fault occurs during transport or unplugging

Expected outcome: The complaint is clearly identified before testing.

Why it matters: “Battery failure” may be a true battery issue, an AC charging issue, or a power connection problem.

Check AC Power Connection First

Confirm the CS100 / CS100i power cord is fully seated at the pump and plugged into a known-good hospital-grade outlet.

Look for evidence that the unit is receiving AC power, such as AC indicator status, charging indication, or normal operation while plugged in.

Expected outcome: The pump recognizes AC power and charging should be possible.

If the AC connection was loose or the outlet was bad and the unit begins charging normally, document the finding and stop.

Test the Wall Outlet

Plug the unit into a different verified outlet, preferably on emergency power if clinically appropriate.

Avoid assuming battery failure until the power source has been ruled out.

Expected outcome: The pump should recognize AC power consistently on a known-good outlet.

If the issue only occurs at one outlet, report the outlet concern to Facilities and document the device as functional on another outlet.

Inspect the Power Cord

Check the power cord for:

  • Bent blades
  • Loose plug fit
  • Cuts or crushed insulation
  • Damaged strain relief
  • Signs of overheating
  • Intermittent AC detection when the cord is moved

If available, test with a known-good compatible power cord.

Expected outcome: AC power should remain stable with no flickering, resets, or intermittent charging indication.

If replacing the power cord resolves the issue, remove the damaged cord from service and stop.

Allow Adequate Charge Time

If the unit was recently unplugged, stored, transported, or left off charge, connect it to AC power and allow sufficient charging time per site practice.

Do not immediately fail the battery solely because the indicator is low after storage.

Expected outcome: Battery charge level should increase and battery fault behavior should clear after charging.

If the battery accepts charge and runtime returns to normal, document low charge due to storage or unplugged condition and stop.

Check for Charging Indication

While connected to AC power, confirm whether the pump shows charging activity or battery status improvement.

Watch for:

  • No charge indication
  • Battery fault alarm while on AC
  • Charge indicator stuck low
  • Unit operating normally on AC but not charging
  • Battery icon or status behaving abnormally

Expected outcome: The device should show that the battery is being charged or maintained while connected to AC.

If there is no charging indication with verified AC input, escalation is appropriate.

Perform a Safe Battery Runtime Check

Only perform a battery runtime check when the unit is not supporting a patient.

With the unit fully charged or charged as much as it will accept, disconnect AC power and observe whether the unit remains powered on battery.

Check for:

  • Immediate shutdown
  • Battery alarm
  • Rapid battery drop
  • Runtime significantly below expected clinical use
  • Pump reset or unstable operation

Expected outcome: The pump should continue operating on battery long enough to support safe transport and temporary AC interruption.

If the unit shuts down quickly or runtime is poor, remove it from service.

Check for Excessive Heat, Odor, or Physical Damage

Inspect the battery area and power inlet externally for unusual heat, odor, swelling, residue, or signs of electrical damage.

Do not open the device for internal inspection during initial troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: No abnormal heat, smell, or visible damage should be present.

If heat, smell, smoke, or physical battery damage is suspected, disconnect from use when safe, label Out of Service, and escalate immediately.

Review Battery Age and Maintenance History

Check the asset history for:

  • Battery replacement date
  • Prior runtime failures
  • Recent power-related work orders
  • Storage off charge
  • Repeated transport failures
  • Missed battery checks or PM findings

Expected outcome: History may confirm whether the battery is aged, repeatedly failing, or recently replaced.

If repeated battery complaints are documented, do not return the unit to service without repair evaluation.

Verify Final Device Status

If the battery charges normally and passes a safe runtime check, return the device to service only if it meets your department’s acceptance criteria.

If there is any doubt about battery reliability, especially for transport use, remove the device from service.

Expected outcome: The pump is either verified operational or appropriately escalated.

If the Problem Persists

If verified AC power, outlet condition, power cord condition, charge time, and basic external checks do not resolve the issue, common external causes have been ruled out.

The problem is likely related to the internal battery, charging circuit, power supply, battery connection, or power management system.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

For an intra-aortic balloon pump, poor battery runtime is not a minor issue. The pump may be needed for transport or emergency AC power interruption. Knowing when to stop troubleshooting is proper Clinical Engineering practice.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot battery runtime on an active patient. If battery reliability is in question, keep the IABP on verified AC power and have clinical staff move support to another verified pump before any unplugged testing or transport. Therapy continuity should be protected before troubleshooting continues.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported the Datascope CS100 / CS100i IABP displayed a battery failure alarm and had poor runtime during transport preparation."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified AC outlet and power cord were functional, but the pump battery did not hold charge and the unit showed rapid battery depletion during bench testing."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the IABP from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for battery/charging system evaluation and repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Whether the outlet was tested
  • Whether emergency power or another outlet was used
  • Whether the power cord was inspected or swapped
  • AC power indicator status
  • Charging indicator behavior
  • Battery alarm text or indicator behavior
  • Approximate battery runtime observed
  • Whether the unit shut down immediately on battery
  • Any unusual heat, odor, sound, or visible damage
  • Battery replacement or service history
  • Whether the device was returned to service or removed from service
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Battery troubleshooting on a CS100 / CS100i should start with patient safety, then move through power source, cord, charging, and runtime checks. Once external causes are ruled out, the safest action is to remove the pump from service and escalate for repair. Clear CCR documentation protects patient safety and helps identify repeat battery or charging failures.

That is successful troubleshooting.
