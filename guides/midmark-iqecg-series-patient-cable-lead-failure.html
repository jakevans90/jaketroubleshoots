Midmark IQecg Series

Patient Cable / Lead Failure

Asset Type

Electrocardiograph (EKG) Machine

Manufacturer

Midmark

Model

IQecg Series

What This Guide Helps With

No ECG signal, intermittent waveform, or excessive noise caused by faulty patient cables, lead wires, or poor connections.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Remove the device from active patient use before troubleshooting.
  • Expected: Patient safely moved to another ECG device.
  • Why it matters: Signal issues can lead to misdiagnosis or delayed care.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Connect the patient cable and attempt to acquire a waveform. Observe for flatline, noise, or intermittent signal.
  • Expected: Confirm presence of poor or no signal.
  • Why it matters: Confirms a true acquisition issue vs software or user error.

Inspect Patient Cable and Leads (External Check)

  • Visually inspect the main cable and individual lead wires for cracks, fraying, exposed wiring, or bent pins.
  • Expected: Cable and leads are intact with no visible damage.
  • Why it matters: Physical damage is the most common cause of signal failure.
  • If damage is found → replace cable/lead set and retest

Check Lead Wire Connections

  • Ensure all lead wires are fully seated into the trunk cable and properly snapped or plugged in.
  • Expected: Secure, tight connections with no looseness.
  • Why it matters: Loose leads can cause intermittent or missing signals.
  • If loose → reconnect and retest

Inspect Electrode Contact Points

  • Check the ends of the lead wires (clips or snaps) for corrosion, residue, or wear.
  • Expected: Clean, intact metal contact surfaces.
  • Why it matters: Poor contact prevents proper signal transmission.
  • If dirty → clean with approved method and retest
  • If worn → replace leads

Test with Known-Good Cable/Leads

  • Swap the patient cable and/or lead set with a known working set.
  • Expected: Normal ECG waveform returns.
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether the issue is isolated to the cable assembly.
  • If resolved → faulty cable/lead set should be removed from service

Check Connection to Acquisition Module

  • Ensure the patient cable is fully seated into the ECG acquisition module (USB module).
  • Expected: Firm connection with no play or intermittent disconnect.
  • Why it matters: Loose module connection can mimic lead failure.
  • If loose → reseat and retest

Observe for Artifact or Noise Sources

  • Check for environmental or setup issues (patient movement, dried electrodes, poor skin prep).
  • Expected: Clean waveform with minimal artifact.
  • Why it matters: Not all “lead failures” are hardware-related.
  • If artifact improves with repositioning or new electrodes → issue was external

If the Problem Persists

If cable, leads, and external connections have been ruled out, the issue may involve the acquisition module or internal circuitry.

Remove device from service

Label as Out of Service

Send for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and avoids wasted troubleshooting time.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot ECG signal issues on an active patient. Always transfer the patient first to ensure continuous and reliable monitoring.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"ECG not acquiring signal; leads reported as not working."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Patient cable found with intermittent internal break causing signal loss."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced patient cable with known-good unit; verified normal ECG waveform."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Cable condition (cracked, frayed, intact)
  • Lead connection security
  • Electrode condition and placement
  • Signal quality before/after cable swap
  • USB module connection status
  • Final device status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Effective troubleshooting starts with the simplest and most failure-prone components. Patient cables and leads are high-use items and frequent failure points. Prioritizing external checks protects patient safety, saves time, and supports accurate documentation and decision-making.

That is successful troubleshooting.
