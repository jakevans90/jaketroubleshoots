Puritan Bennett 840

Alarm: DEVICE ALERT POST or background diagnostic failure

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Puritan Bennett

Model

840

What This Guide Helps With

DEVICE ALERT alarm indicating a POST (Power-On Self-Test) or background diagnostic failure that may cause startup issues, persistent alarms, or unreliable ventilator operation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Ensure Patient Safety First

  • Immediately remove the ventilator from patient use
  • Transition the patient to a backup ventilator or alternate ventilation method
  • Confirm adequate ventilation is maintained

2. Verify the Reported Alarm and Behavior

  • Confirm “DEVICE ALERT” is displayed on screen
  • Determine if it occurs during startup (POST) or during operation
  • Observe for audible alarms or system limitations
  • Check for restricted functionality or lockout

3. Perform a Full Power Cycle

  • Turn the ventilator off completely
  • Disconnect AC power for 30–60 seconds
  • Reconnect and power the unit back on

4. Check AC Power Source and Stability

  • Test the wall outlet with a known-good device
  • Ensure a hospital-grade outlet is used
  • Inspect the power cord for damage or loose connections

5. Inspect External Connections and Modules

  • Verify all cables are fully seated
  • Remove and reseat any external modules or accessories
  • Inspect for damage, contamination, or loose components

6. Review System Diagnostic Log

  • Access system or service diagnostic logs if available
  • Look for repeated faults or subsystem errors
  • Identify patterns pointing to specific hardware failures

7. Rerun POST / Extended Self-Test (EST)

  • Restart the ventilator to trigger POST
  • Run EST if available in service mode
  • Observe pass/fail results

8. Stop if Resolved

  • If the alarm clears and diagnostics pass, return the device to service
  • Monitor for recurrence during use

If the Problem Persists

If the DEVICE ALERT alarm continues after completing all external checks and diagnostics:

  • External causes have been ruled out
  • Indicates internal failure (processor, sensor, or system board)
  • Remove the device from service
  • Label as Out of Service
  • Send for repair or bench evaluation

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot a ventilator with active diagnostic alarms on a patient. Always move the patient to a verified, fully functional device before proceeding.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Ventilator displaying DEVICE ALERT alarm during startup."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"POST/diagnostic failure detected; alarm persisted after power cycle and external checks."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Performed power cycle, verified power source, checked connections, reviewed logs, reran diagnostics. Alarm persisted. Unit removed from service and sent for repair."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Diagnostic alarms indicate the ventilator has identified an internal issue. After ruling out external causes, escalation is the correct and safe path to ensure reliable patient care.

That is successful troubleshooting.
