Nihon Kohden ECG-1500 Series

Lead Fail or Poor Signal Acquisition

Asset Type

Electrocardiograph (EKG) Machine

Manufacturer

Nihon Kohden

Model

ECG-1500 Series

What This Guide Helps With

Lead fail messages, noisy ECG waveforms, or inability to acquire a clean signal due to electrode, cable, or connection issues.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the ECG is being used for diagnosis, confirm the patient is stable and move to another functioning ECG machine if needed.
  • Expected: Patient monitoring continues without interruption.
  • Why it matters: Poor signal quality can lead to inaccurate interpretation or missed cardiac events.

Verify the Reported Issue

  • Confirm the lead fail message or poor waveform quality.
  • Observe:
    • Which leads are affected
    • If signal is absent, intermittent, or noisy
    • Any baseline wander or artifact
  • Expected: Issue is reproducible and tied to specific leads or overall signal quality.
  • Why it matters: Helps isolate whether the issue is localized or system-wide.

Check Electrode Placement and Condition

  • Inspect all electrodes on the patient.
  • Actions:
    • Ensure proper anatomical placement
    • Replace dried, loose, or expired electrodes
    • Clean skin (remove oils, sweat, hair if needed)
  • Expected: Electrodes adhere well and provide stable contact.
  • Why it matters: Poor electrode contact is the most common cause of signal issues.

Inspect Lead Wires and Patient Cable

  • Examine the ECG lead wires and trunk cable.
  • Actions:
    • Look for broken clips, bent pins, or frayed wires
    • Ensure all leads are firmly connected to electrodes and cable
    • Gently move cables to check for intermittent signal loss
  • Expected: No visible damage and stable signal when cables are stationary and gently manipulated.
  • Why it matters: Internal wire breaks often cause intermittent or failed leads.

Swap with Known Good Accessories

  • Replace suspected components.
  • Actions:
    • Swap lead wires
    • Swap patient trunk cable
  • Expected: Signal improves or normalizes with known good accessories.
  • Why it matters: Confirms whether the issue is accessory-related rather than device-related.

Check Input Ports on the Device

  • Inspect the ECG input connector on the machine.
  • Actions:
    • Ensure connector is clean and free of debris
    • Confirm cable is fully seated
    • Check for bent or damaged pins in the port
  • Expected: Secure connection with no visible damage.
  • Why it matters: Poor connection at the device can mimic lead failure.

Evaluate Environmental Interference

  • Assess for electrical or motion artifacts.
  • Actions:
    • Ensure patient is still during acquisition
    • Check for nearby equipment causing interference (pumps, beds, cords)
    • Verify proper grounding environment
  • Expected: Reduced noise when interference sources are minimized.
  • Why it matters: External interference can degrade ECG signal quality.

Perform System Self-Test (If Available)

  • Run the ECG machine’s built-in test function.
  • Expected: Device passes self-test without errors.
  • Why it matters: Helps determine if the issue is internal to the ECG unit.

If the Problem Persists

If electrode, cable, and environmental causes have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal (input board or signal processing failure).

Remove the device from service, label it Out of Service, and send for repair or bench evaluation.

Knowing when to stop prevents unnecessary risk and wasted troubleshooting time.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot signal issues on an active patient when diagnostic accuracy is required. Move the patient to a functioning ECG machine first.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"ECG-1500 showing lead fail and poor waveform quality during patient acquisition."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Defective patient lead wire set causing intermittent signal loss."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Replaced lead wires with known good set; verified normal waveform acquisition across all leads."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Electrodes replaced
  • Lead wires swapped
  • Patient cable condition
  • Signal quality before/after intervention
  • Any artifact or interference observed
  • Device passed/failed self-test
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Most ECG signal issues are external and easily correctable. Start with electrodes and cables, work logically, and avoid assuming device failure too early. Always prioritize patient safety and accurate diagnostics, and document clearly for future reference.

That is successful troubleshooting.
