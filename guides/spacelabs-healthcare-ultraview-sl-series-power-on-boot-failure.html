Spacelabs Healthcare UltraView SL Series

Power-On / Boot Failure

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Spacelabs Healthcare

Model

UltraView SL Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting a Spacelabs UltraView SL Series patient monitor that fails to power on or complete its boot sequence. Symptoms may include:

  • Blank display when power is applied
  • No response from the power button
  • Partial or stalled boot sequence
  • Unexpected error messages during startup

Focus is on external checks and safe troubleshooting before considering internal repair.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Never troubleshoot the monitor while it is connected to an active patient. Move the patient to a functional monitor if necessary.

Verify Power Source

  • Check that the monitor is plugged into a live outlet.
  • Confirm the outlet is working by testing with another device.

Why it matters: A non-functional outlet is a common and easily fixable cause of boot failure.

Inspect Power Cord and Connections

  • Examine the power cord for visible damage, kinks, or fraying.
  • Ensure the cord is fully seated into both the monitor and wall outlet.

Why it matters: Loose or damaged cords prevent proper power delivery.

Check Battery Status (if applicable)

  • If the unit has an internal battery, verify it is charged or try powering the monitor with AC only.
  • Swap with a known good battery if available.

Why it matters: A dead or failed battery can prevent startup even when plugged in.

Perform a Hard Reset / Power Cycle

  • Disconnect the monitor from AC power and remove the battery (if removable).
  • Wait 30–60 seconds, then reconnect AC and battery.
  • Attempt to power on the monitor.

Why it matters: Residual charge or minor firmware lockups can be cleared with a reset.

Observe Indicator Lights or Audible Alarms

  • Note any LED patterns, beeps, or error messages.
  • Record these exactly, as they can indicate specific faults.

Why it matters: External indicators help pinpoint the failure without opening the device.

Verify Peripherals and Network Cables

  • Disconnect all non-essential peripherals, USB drives, and network cables.
  • Attempt to boot with minimal connections.

Why it matters: Faulty peripherals can stall the boot sequence.

Check for Recent Firmware or Configuration Changes

  • Confirm with staff if any updates were applied prior to failure.

Why it matters: Corrupted firmware or interrupted updates may prevent startup.

If the Problem Persists

  • Remove the device from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send the monitor for repair or bench evaluation by Spacelabs or qualified Clinical Engineering personnel.

Note: Continuing attempts to power on internally may risk further damage. Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Never troubleshoot the monitor while it is connected to an active patient. If monitoring is required, move the patient to a functional monitor before performing troubleshooting steps. Ensure patient safety is prioritized over equipment diagnostics.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Monitor will not power on; display remains blank when plugged in.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Power cord and outlet verified; battery checked; hard reset performed; monitor still fails to boot.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Monitor removed from service, labeled Out of Service, sent for repair.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Outlet tested and verified live
  • Power cord inspected and swapped if necessary
  • Battery checked or replaced
  • LED/error indicator patterns observed
  • Peripherals disconnected
  • Firmware or configuration notes
  • Final device status: Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount; troubleshooting must follow a logical, stepwise approach. External issues are the most common causes of boot failure and should be ruled out first. Accurate documentation ensures proper follow-up and supports departmental cost avoidance when equipment is repaired efficiently.

That is successful troubleshooting.
