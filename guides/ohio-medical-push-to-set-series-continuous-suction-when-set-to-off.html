Ohio Medical Push-To-Set Series

Continuous Suction When Set To Off

Asset Type

Suction Regulator

Manufacturer

Ohio Medical

Model

Push-To-Set Series

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting suction that continues when the regulator is turned off, including setup, mode selection, canister, tubing, and regulator fault checks.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Continuous suction when set to OFF can create unintended suction exposure.

Action:

  • Remove the suction setup from active patient use if suction cannot be controlled.
  • Provide an alternate suction source if suction is clinically needed.
  • Do not continue using a regulator that cannot shut suction off reliably.

Expected outcome: The patient is not exposed to unintended or uncontrolled suction.

Why it matters: Suction that remains active when OFF may indicate a control, mode, connection, or internal regulator failure.

Verify the Reported Condition

Action:

  • Confirm the suction regulator is physically set to OFF.
  • Observe whether suction continues at the patient tubing, canister, or regulator outlet.
  • Listen for continuous vacuum flow or bubbling in the collection canister.

Expected outcome: The reported issue is confirmed as continuous suction while the regulator is set to OFF.

Why it matters: Staff may report “off” based on expected behavior, but the regulator may actually be in the wrong mode or not fully switched off.

If the regulator was not fully set to OFF and correcting the setting stops suction, return the device to service after confirming normal operation.

Check Mode Selection and Push-To-Set Operation

Action:

  • Confirm the regulator is not still in a suction mode such as REG, LINE, or INTERMITTENT, depending on the model configuration.
  • Set the control fully to OFF.
  • If applicable, verify the Push-To-Set knob is not stuck, partially engaged, or returning abnormally.

Expected outcome: Suction stops when the regulator is fully placed in OFF.

Why it matters: A partially engaged selector or stuck control can make the regulator behave as if suction is still enabled.

If the mode selector was mispositioned and suction stops after proper setting, document the correction and stop.

Verify Wall Vacuum Source and Connection

Action:

  • Disconnect the regulator from the wall vacuum outlet.
  • Observe whether suction immediately stops at the patient tubing/canister side.
  • Inspect the wall outlet connection for damaged fittings, improper adapters, or loose engagement.

Expected outcome: Suction stops when the regulator is removed from the wall vacuum source.

Why it matters: This confirms the suction source is coming through the regulator and not from an alternate connection path.

If suction continues after disconnecting from wall vacuum, check for another vacuum source or incorrect tubing setup.

Inspect Tubing and Canister Setup

Action:

  • Trace tubing from the wall outlet to the regulator, collection canister, overflow protection device, and patient connection.
  • Confirm tubing is not bypassing the regulator.
  • Check that the canister is connected to the regulator outlet correctly and not directly to wall vacuum.

Expected outcome: All suction flow passes through the suction regulator before reaching the collection system.

Why it matters: Incorrect tubing can create continuous suction even when the regulator appears to be off.

If incorrect tubing or bypassing is found, correct the setup, verify OFF stops suction, and stop.

Check for Stuck Vacuum Control or Damaged Knob

Action:

  • Turn the suction control through its normal range and back to OFF.
  • Feel for binding, looseness, broken detents, stripped knob movement, or failure to seat in the OFF position.
  • Do not force the control.

Expected outcome: The control moves normally and clearly reaches the OFF position.

Why it matters: Mechanical damage can prevent the internal valve from closing even though the external knob appears to be off.

If the knob or control feels damaged, remove the regulator from service.

Check for Contamination or Fluid Intrusion Evidence

Action:

  • Inspect the regulator exterior, fittings, tubing connection points, and nearby canister setup for signs of fluid exposure, dried secretions, odor, residue, or missing overflow protection.
  • Check whether the regulator was used with a full canister or failed overflow protection.

Expected outcome: No signs of aspirate contamination, fluid intrusion, or improper collection setup are present.

Why it matters: Fluid intrusion can damage internal regulator components and may cause unsafe or unreliable suction control.

If contamination or fluid intrusion is suspected, remove the regulator from service and send it for proper evaluation.

Test With a Known-Good Canister and Tubing Setup

Action:

  • Connect the regulator to a known-good wall vacuum outlet using a correct canister and tubing setup.
  • Set the regulator to OFF.
  • Confirm whether suction stops completely.
  • Then briefly set suction to an active mode, verify suction is generated, and return it to OFF.

Expected outcome: Suction starts only when selected and stops fully when set to OFF.

Why it matters: This separates regulator failure from tubing, canister, outlet, or setup issues.

If suction continues with a known-good setup while set to OFF, the regulator should be considered defective.

Remove From Service if OFF Does Not Stop Suction

Action:

  • If suction continues after setup, mode, tubing, canister, outlet, and external control checks are complete, remove the regulator from service.
  • Label it Out of Service.
  • Send it for repair, replacement, or bench evaluation.

Expected outcome: A suction regulator with unreliable OFF control is not returned to clinical use.

Why it matters: A regulator that cannot shut off suction is a patient safety risk and should not remain available for use.

If the Problem Persists

If continuous suction remains after common external causes have been ruled out, the issue is likely internal to the suction regulator.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair, replacement, or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not attempt deep internal repair or return the regulator to service if OFF does not reliably stop suction.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot an uncontrolled suction regulator on an active patient. Move suction support to another regulator or safe suction source first. A regulator that continues suction while set to OFF should be treated as a patient safety issue until proven otherwise.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the Ohio Medical Push-To-Set suction regulator continued pulling suction even when set to OFF."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified suction continued with the regulator in OFF after checking mode selection, tubing setup, canister connection, and wall vacuum source."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the suction regulator from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for bench evaluation or replacement."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Alarm behavior
  • Accessories swapped
  • Power behavior
  • Environmental factors
  • Indicator lights
  • Final device status
  • Wall vacuum outlet tested
  • Regulator setting verified as OFF
  • Mode selector position checked
  • Tubing setup traced
  • Canister setup verified
  • Overflow protection checked
  • Known-good tubing/canister tested
  • Suction behavior in OFF documented
  • Any bubbling, hissing, or continuous vacuum flow noted
  • Evidence of fluid intrusion, residue, odor, or contamination

Final Thought

Continuous suction while set to OFF is not a minor complaint. Start with patient safety, verify the setup, rule out tubing or canister errors, and stop once the regulator itself is suspected. Clear documentation protects the patient, the department, and the next technician.

That is successful troubleshooting.
