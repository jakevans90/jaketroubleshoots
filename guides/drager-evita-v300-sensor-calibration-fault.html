Drager Evita V300 Ventilator

Oxygen Sensor Calibration Fault

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Drager

Model

Evita V300

What This Guide Helps With

This guide addresses an Oxygen Sensor Calibration Fault on the Evita V300 ventilator. This alarm typically occurs when the ventilator cannot properly calibrate the internal oxygen (FiO₂) sensor. The cause is often related to gas supply, sensor warm-up time, calibration environment, or sensor age rather than an internal electronics failure.

This guide focuses on verifying gas supply, setup conditions, and sensor readiness before assuming the oxygen sensor itself has failed.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

  • If the ventilator is connected to a patient, move the patient to another ventilator before troubleshooting.
  • Oxygen calibration faults may affect delivered FiO₂ accuracy.

Verify Oxygen and Air Gas Supply

  • Check that both medical air and oxygen supplies are connected and turned on.
  • Confirm:
  • Wall gas hoses are connected securely
  • Gas pressures are within normal hospital ranges (typically ~50 psi)
  • There are no kinked or disconnected hoses
  • Calibration requires stable gas supply. Incorrect or unstable pressure can cause calibration to fail.

Check for Proper Startup Conditions

  • Oxygen sensor calibration usually occurs during startup or pre-use checks.
  • Verify:
  • The ventilator has completed its full startup sequence
  • No gas supply alarms are present
  • The device has been powered on long enough for the sensor to stabilize

Allow Sensor Warm-Up Time

  • Electrochemical oxygen sensors require warm-up and stabilization time after power-up.
  • If the ventilator was just powered on:
  • Allow 5–10 minutes
  • Attempt the calibration again

Perform the Oxygen Sensor Calibration Again

  • From the system menu, initiate the oxygen sensor calibration procedure.
  • Ensure during calibration:
  • The ventilator is connected to normal room air or stable gas supply as required by the calibration prompt
  • No gas flow disturbances occur during calibration

Inspect the Oxygen Sensor Installation (External Check Only)

  • Without disassembling internal components, verify:
  • The oxygen sensor cable is securely connected
  • The sensor compartment door is properly closed
  • No visible contamination or moisture is present

Check Sensor Age (If Known)

  • Oxygen sensors are consumable components with a limited lifespan (commonly around 12–18 months depending on usage).
  • If the sensor is near or beyond expected service life, calibration failure may indicate normal sensor depletion.

Power Cycle the Ventilator

  • If the fault persists:
  • Power the ventilator off
  • Wait approximately 30 seconds
  • Restart the ventilator
  • Run the pre-use test and calibration again

If the Problem Persists

If oxygen supply, warm-up time, and calibration procedures have been verified and the fault continues, the issue is likely due to:

  • Failed oxygen sensor
  • Internal sensor interface issue
  • Calibration circuit fault

At this point the device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when external causes have been ruled out is part of proper troubleshooting.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot oxygen calibration faults while the ventilator is connected to a patient. Oxygen sensor errors may result in inaccurate FiO₂ delivery. Always transfer the patient to a verified working ventilator before investigating the issue.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Nursing reported ventilator displaying ‘Oxygen Sensor Calibration Fault’ during startup.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“Oxygen sensor calibration repeatedly failed during pre-use testing despite stable gas supply.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Ventilator removed from service and sent for repair. Oxygen sensor scheduled for replacement and calibration verification.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • Oxygen and air supply pressures verified
  • Gas hoses inspected and reseated
  • Calibration attempted multiple times
  • Warm-up time allowed before calibration
  • Sensor age if known
  • Any additional alarms present
  • Final device status (in service or removed from service)

Final Thought

Ventilator oxygen sensors are critical for accurate FiO₂ delivery. Many calibration faults are caused by simple issues such as gas supply instability or sensor warm-up conditions. Systematically verifying these external factors protects patient safety while preventing unnecessary internal repair. Clear documentation ensures proper follow-up and equipment reliability.

That is successful troubleshooting.
