Vyaire VELA Ventilator

Power or Battery Failure

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Vyaire

Model

VELA

What This Guide Helps With

This guide assists Clinical Engineering in troubleshooting power or battery failures on a Vyaire VELA ventilator. Symptoms may include the ventilator not powering on, failing to charge, rapid battery depletion, or unexpected shutdowns. The focus is on external and easily verifiable checks before considering internal repair, ensuring patient safety and proper escalation.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Verify AC Power Source

  • Ensure the ventilator is plugged into a known working outlet.
  • Check for tripped breakers or GFCI interruptions.

Why: A common cause of “power failure” is simply no AC supply.

Inspect Power Cord and Connector

  • Look for fraying, damage, or bent pins.
  • Confirm the connector seats securely into the ventilator.

Why: Damaged cords or loose connections can prevent charging or powering on.

Check Battery Status Indicator

  • Observe the battery LED or display icon.
  • If the battery is critically low, allow the device to charge for at least 30 minutes.

Why: Some failures occur because the battery is fully discharged and needs time to accept a charge.

Swap or Test with Known Good Battery

  • If the device has a removable battery, insert a fully charged spare.

Why: Batteries degrade over time; a failing battery often mimics a device power failure.

Attempt Power-On Without Accessories

  • Remove non-essential accessories (humidifier, external modules) and try to power on.

Why: Faulty accessories can sometimes prevent proper startup.

Inspect Ventilator for External Damage

  • Check for signs of liquid ingress, physical damage, or unusual odors.

Why: External damage can affect the power circuitry and may require repair.

If the Problem Persists

Common external causes have been ruled out. The device is likely experiencing an internal power supply or battery controller failure.

  • Remove the ventilator from service.
  • Label as Out of Service.
  • Send for bench evaluation or manufacturer repair.

Knowing when to stop and escalate is proper troubleshooting and prevents patient risk.

Clinical Use Tip

  • Never troubleshoot power or battery issues on a ventilator attached to a patient.
  • Move the patient to another ventilator before performing tests.
  • Always ensure the environment is safe, and the ventilator is disconnected from patients during troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
“Ventilator will not power on; battery indicator shows empty.”

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
“AC outlet confirmed functional; power cord intact; battery fully charged but ventilator still does not start.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
“Device removed from service, labeled Out of Service, and sent for bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include

  • AC outlet tested and functional
  • Power cord inspected
  • Battery charged and/or swapped
  • Indicator lights observed (LED or display)
  • Device removed from patient and labeled Out of Service

Final Thought

Patient safety is paramount. A systematic, logic-based approach ensures that external and easily verifiable issues are ruled out first. Escalation to repair is not a failure but a key part of effective Clinical Engineering practice. Documentation provides continuity and ensures compliance with hospital protocols.

That is successful troubleshooting.
