Karl Storz C-MAC Pocket Monitor

Video laryngoscope not recognized / no image after connection

Asset Type

Video Laryngoscope

Manufacturer

Karl Storz

Model

C-MAC Pocket Monitor

What This Guide Helps With

Troubleshooting no image, black screen, or unrecognized C-MAC video laryngoscope after connection to the Pocket Monitor.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

Ensure Patient Safety First

Confirm the device is not being troubleshot during an active airway attempt.

If the monitor or laryngoscope is needed for patient care, provide clinical staff with another ready video laryngoscope system before troubleshooting.

Expected outcome: Patient care is not delayed by a device with unresolved video or recognition failure.

Confirm the Reported Problem

Power on the C-MAC Pocket Monitor and connect the reported video laryngoscope.

Observe whether the monitor shows:

  • No image
  • Black screen
  • Frozen image
  • No recognition after connection
  • Intermittent image when the cable or connector is moved

Expected outcome: The failure mode is confirmed and documented before parts are swapped.

Check Monitor Power and Battery Status

Verify the Pocket Monitor powers on normally and has adequate battery charge.

Connect the monitor to its approved charger or use a known-good charged monitor if available.

Expected outcome: The monitor remains powered on and does not shut down during connection.

If charging or power issues are present, troubleshoot the monitor power problem first.

Inspect the Video Laryngoscope Connector

Disconnect the scope and inspect the connector area for:

  • Bent pins
  • Damaged contacts
  • Fluid contamination
  • Debris
  • Cracked connector housing
  • Loose or worn connector fit

Do not force the connector into the monitor.

Expected outcome: Connector contacts are clean, dry, undamaged, and able to seat properly.

Verify Proper Connection and Seating

Reconnect the video laryngoscope carefully, making sure it is fully seated in the Pocket Monitor connection port.

Avoid pulling, twisting, or side-loading the cable during the test.

Expected outcome: The monitor recognizes the connected laryngoscope and displays a live image.

If the image appears and remains stable, the issue was likely poor seating. Stop troubleshooting and return the device to service after functional verification.

Inspect the Laryngoscope Cable or Handle

Check the reusable video laryngoscope for obvious external damage, including:

  • Cut or crushed cable
  • Kinked strain relief
  • Loose connector
  • Cracked handle or blade interface
  • Evidence of impact or liquid intrusion

Expected outcome: No external damage is present that could interrupt video signal or device recognition.

Test with a Known-Good Compatible Monitor

Connect the reported laryngoscope to a known-good compatible C-MAC monitor, if available.

Expected outcome: The laryngoscope is recognized and displays an image on the known-good monitor.

If the scope works on another monitor, suspect the original Pocket Monitor or its input connection.

Test the Pocket Monitor with a Known-Good Compatible Laryngoscope

Connect a known-good compatible video laryngoscope to the reported Pocket Monitor.

Expected outcome: The monitor recognizes the known-good laryngoscope and displays a live image.

If the known-good scope also fails, suspect the Pocket Monitor input port, internal video processing, or monitor-side connection fault.

Check for Intermittent Connection Behavior

With the device connected and safely on the bench, gently move the cable near the connector and strain relief.

Do not aggressively flex or stress the cable.

Expected outcome: The image remains stable and the device stays recognized.

If the image cuts in and out, the likely cause is connector wear, cable damage, or internal connection failure.

Inspect for Cleaning or Fluid Damage

Check the monitor connection area and laryngoscope connector for signs of excessive moisture, residue, corrosion, or cleaning solution intrusion.

Allow externally damp areas to dry only if no corrosion or visible damage is present.

Expected outcome: Connection surfaces are dry and free of residue.

If corrosion, fluid intrusion, or residue inside the connector is found, remove the device from service.

Perform a Final Functional Check

Once image is restored, confirm:

  • Monitor powers on normally
  • Scope is recognized consistently
  • Image is live and correctly oriented
  • Image remains stable during gentle cable handling
  • No physical damage is present
  • Battery status is acceptable

Expected outcome: The Pocket Monitor and connected video laryngoscope operate reliably before return to service.

If the Problem Persists

If the C-MAC Pocket Monitor still does not recognize a compatible video laryngoscope after power, connection, cable, connector, and known-good device checks, common external causes have been ruled out.

The device should be:

  • Removed from service
  • Labeled Out of Service
  • Sent for repair or bench evaluation

Possible causes include monitor input port failure, laryngoscope cable failure, damaged connector contacts, or internal video/recognition circuitry failure.

Knowing when to stop is proper troubleshooting. Do not continue repeated connection attempts if connector damage, intermittent signal loss, or suspected internal failure is present.

Clinical Use Tip

Do not troubleshoot a video laryngoscope recognition or no-image issue during an active airway event. Provide another ready airway device first, then evaluate the failed monitor and laryngoscope away from the patient care area.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the clinical staff.

Example:
"Clinical staff reported that the Karl Storz C-MAC Pocket Monitor did not recognize the connected video laryngoscope and displayed no image."

Cause

What was observed during troubleshooting.

Example:
"Verified no image after connection; monitor powered on normally, but the issue followed the laryngoscope during known-good monitor testing, indicating a likely scope-side connector or cable fault."

Resolution

What action was taken.

Example:
"Removed the affected video laryngoscope from service, labeled it Out of Service, and sent it for repair evaluation; Pocket Monitor passed functional check with a known-good compatible scope."

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Pocket Monitor battery status
  • Charger tested, if used
  • Exact screen behavior
  • Whether image was black, frozen, or absent
  • Whether the scope was recognized at all
  • Connector condition
  • Cable or strain relief damage
  • Known-good monitor test result
  • Known-good laryngoscope test result
  • Intermittent behavior when cable was moved
  • Evidence of moisture, residue, corrosion, heat, or impact
  • Final device status

Final Thought

Video laryngoscope recognition problems should be approached logically. Confirm patient safety first, then separate monitor-side issues from scope-side issues using careful inspection and known-good testing. Clear documentation helps prevent unreliable airway equipment from returning to service prematurely.

That is successful troubleshooting.
