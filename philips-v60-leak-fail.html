Philips V60 Ventilator

Fails Self-Test / Leak Detected

Asset Type

Ventilator

Manufacturer

Philips

Model

V60

What This Guide Helps With

This guide walks you through simple checks when a Philips V60 ventilator fails its self-test due to a leak detected. These steps focus on the most common and easiest things to verify before assuming the ventilator has failed.

You do not need advanced technical skills to perform these checks — just follow the steps and observe carefully.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Check the Patient Circuit and Tubing

  • Make sure the breathing circuit is fully connected at all connection points.
  • Confirm there are no loose fittings, cracks, or obvious holes in the tubing.
  • Ensure the circuit is properly seated on the ventilator outlet.
  • Even a small gap or loose connection can cause a leak failure during self-test.

2. Inspect the Exhalation Port and Filter

  • Verify the exhalation port is installed correctly.
  • Check that the filter is present and seated properly.
  • Look for moisture buildup or blockage that could affect airflow.
  • Improper installation or obstruction can cause the ventilator to detect a leak.

3. Check the Humidifier (If Installed)

  • Confirm the humidifier chamber is seated correctly.
  • Make sure the chamber is not cracked or leaking.
  • Verify all humidifier tubing connections are tight.
  • Leaks in the humidifier setup are a common cause of failed leak tests.

4. Inspect the Mask or Test Plug

  • If a mask is attached, make sure it is sealed properly.
  • If running a self-test without a patient, ensure the test plug or circuit is capped correctly.
  • An open or poorly sealed patient interface will cause the self-test to fail.

5. Try a Simple Reset

  • Power the ventilator off.
  • Re-seat the circuit, exhalation port, and humidifier connections.
  • Power the ventilator back on and rerun the self-test.
  • If the self-test passes, the ventilator may be returned to service.

If the Self-Test Still Fails

If the ventilator continues to fail the self-test with a leak detected after completing the steps above, that’s okay — you have already checked the most common and simplest causes.

You have already:

  • confirmed the circuit is connected and free of visible damage
  • verified the exhalation port and filter are installed correctly
  • checked the humidifier chamber and tubing
  • ensured the patient interface or test plug is sealed
  • attempted a simple reset

The best next step is to:

  • remove the ventilator from clinical service
  • clearly label it as Out of Service
  • send it for repair or bench evaluation

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What the user reported.

Example: “Ventilator fails self-test; leak detected.”

Cause

What you observed or determined during troubleshooting.

Example: “Patient circuit, exhalation port, and humidifier inspected and reseated. No visible damage noted. Self-test continues to fail with leak detected. Suspected internal leak or valve failure.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Removed ventilator from service, labeled out of service, sent for repair.”
or
“Tagged for bench evaluation by CE.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Circuit inspected / replaced
  • Exhalation port reseated
  • Humidifier chamber checked
  • Self-test passed / failed
  • Any visible damage or moisture
  • Final disposition (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Not every self-test failure is caused by an external setup issue — sometimes the ventilator’s internal components develop leaks that cannot be corrected at the bedside.

Completing these steps means you have:

  • ✔ ruled out common causes
  • ✔ protected patient safety
  • ✔ documented properly
  • ✔ avoided unnecessary guesswork

That is successful troubleshooting.
