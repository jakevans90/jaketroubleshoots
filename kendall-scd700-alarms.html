Kendall SCD 700 Series

Alarms Going Off Unnecessarily

Asset Type

Sequential Compression Device (SCD)

Manufacturer

Cardinal Health

Model

Kendall SCD 700 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide walks you through simple checks when a Kendall SCD 700 Series unit is alarming frequently or unnecessarily.

This may present as:

  • Repeated “Leak” alarms
  • Disconnection alarms when tubing appears connected
  • Pressure fault alarms during normal use
  • Intermittent alarms that clear and then return
  • Alarm sounds but sleeves appear to inflate normally

These steps focus on the most common and easily verified causes before assuming an internal sensor or control board issue.

You do not need advanced technical skills — just follow the steps and observe carefully.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Identify the Exact Alarm Message

  • Read the alarm message carefully.
  • Document the exact wording.
  • Note whether it is constant or intermittent.

Precise alarm wording helps determine whether the issue is leak-related, pressure-related, or sensor-related.

2. Check Tubing Connections

  • Disconnect and fully reseat all tubing connectors.
  • Ensure connectors are inserted completely until firmly seated.
  • Gently tug to confirm they are secure.

Loose connectors are the most common cause of nuisance alarms.

3. Inspect Tubing for Micro-Leaks or Kinks

Check for:

  • Hairline cracks
  • Pinholes
  • Soft spots
  • Sharp bends

Make sure tubing is not pinched under bed rails or wheels. Even very small leaks can trigger repeated leak or pressure alarms.

4. Inspect Sleeves

Check sleeves for:

  • Torn seams
  • Worn Velcro
  • Poor wrap

Ensure sleeves are wrapped snugly and evenly and confirm correct sleeve size. Improperly applied sleeves can cause inconsistent pressure readings.

5. Swap Sleeves and Tubing

  • Replace sleeves with a known-good set if available.
  • Swap left and right sides to see if the alarm follows the accessory.

Observe:

  • If the alarm follows the sleeve/tubing → accessory issue.
  • If alarm stays on the same port → possible internal valve or sensor issue.

6. Observe Pump and Inflation Behavior

  • Does the pump sound normal?
  • Do sleeves inflate and deflate fully?
  • Does the alarm trigger during inflation, hold, or deflation phase?

Consistent pump sound with normal inflation often points to minor leaks or sensor sensitivity rather than major failure.

7. Power Cycle the Unit

  • Turn the unit off.
  • Wait at least 10 seconds.
  • Restart and begin therapy again.

Temporary software or logic faults can sometimes cause repeated nuisance alarms.

8. Evaluate the Environment

  • Ensure tubing is not being moved excessively.
  • Check that the unit is not positioned where tubing is constantly flexing.
  • Verify no condensation or fluid contamination is present at connectors.

Environmental movement can intermittently break the air seal.

If Alarms Continue After These Checks

If the unit continues to alarm after completing the steps above, the issue is likely internal and cannot be corrected through bedside troubleshooting.

Possible internal causes include:

  • Internal pressure sensor drift
  • Valve manifold issue
  • Pump wear
  • Control board fault

Next Steps

  • Remove the unit from clinical service
  • Clearly label it as Out of Service
  • Send for bench evaluation by Clinical Engineering

Knowing when to escalate is part of good troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

Complaint

Example: “Repeated leak alarm despite tubing appearing connected.”

Cause

Examples:

“Tubes reseated, sleeves swapped, alarm persisted on same port. Suspected internal pressure sensor or valve issue.”

or

“Alarm followed sleeve when swapped. Sleeve found leaking at seam.”

Resolution

Examples:

“Replaced defective sleeve; unit returned to service.”
“Removed from service and sent for bench evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Exact alarm message
  • Intermittent or constant
  • Which side affected (if applicable)
  • Sleeve swapped (yes / no)
  • Tubing replaced (yes / no)
  • Pump sounds normal (yes / no)
  • Power cycled (yes / no)
  • Final disposition

Final Thought

Most nuisance alarms are caused by loose tubing, small accessory leaks, improper sleeve application, or connector seating issues.

Completing these steps ensures you have:

  • ✔ ruled out accessory causes
  • ✔ reduced unnecessary device swaps
  • ✔ protected patient safety
  • ✔ documented properly

That is successful troubleshooting.
