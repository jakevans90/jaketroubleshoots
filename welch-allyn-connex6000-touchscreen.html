Welch Allyn Connex 6000 Series

Touchscreen Calibration Off / Dead Spots

Asset Type

Patient Monitor

Manufacturer

Welch Allyn

Model

Connex 6000 Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide walks you through simple checks when a Connex 6000 Series monitor has a touchscreen that does not respond correctly, appears misaligned, or has areas that do not register touches (dead spots).

These steps focus on the most common and easily verified causes before assuming the touchscreen has failed internally.

You do not need advanced technical skills — just follow the steps and observe carefully.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Check for Obstructions or Contamination

  • Inspect the screen for dirt, moisture, or residue.
  • Clean the screen using an approved disinfectant wipe and allow it to dry fully.
  • Make sure there is no plastic film, tape, or label covering the screen.
  • Debris or moisture can interfere with touch detection and make the screen appear miscalibrated.

2. Confirm the Monitor Is Not Frozen

  • Try pressing several different areas of the screen.
  • Observe whether any buttons respond at all.
  • Check if the display is updating or appears stuck.
  • A frozen screen can look like a calibration or dead-spot issue.

3. Power Cycle the Monitor

  • Turn the monitor off.
  • Wait at least 10 seconds.
  • Power the monitor back on and allow it to fully boot.
  • Temporary software issues can sometimes affect touchscreen response.

4. Check for Visible Screen Damage

  • Look closely for cracks, pressure marks, or discoloration.
  • Gently touch different areas of the screen and note where input is not detected.
  • Physical damage can cause permanent dead zones on the touchscreen.

5. Observe the Behavior

  • Note whether touches register in the wrong location (misalignment).
  • Note whether certain areas do not respond at all.
  • Record any error messages or unusual behavior.
  • This helps determine whether the issue is calibration-related or a failed touch panel.

If the Touchscreen Issue Persists

If the touchscreen continues to behave incorrectly after completing the steps above, that’s okay — you have already checked the most common and simplest causes.

You have already:

  • cleaned and inspected the screen
  • ruled out a frozen display
  • power-cycled the monitor
  • checked for visible damage
  • observed the pattern of non-response

At this point, the issue is likely internal (touch panel failure, digitizer issue, or internal electronics) and cannot be corrected through basic bedside checks.

The best next step is to:

  • remove the monitor from clinical service
  • clearly label it as Out of Service
  • send it for repair or bench evaluation

Knowing when to stop and escalate is part of good troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

Example: “Touchscreen not responding correctly; dead spots present.”

Cause

Example: “Screen cleaned and inspected, power cycled, no visible damage. Touch input still misaligned / unresponsive in certain areas. Suspected touchscreen digitizer failure.”

Resolution

Examples:
“Removed monitor from service, labeled out of service, sent for repair.”
or
“Tagged for bench evaluation by CE.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Screen cleaned
  • Power cycled
  • Areas of screen not responding
  • Misalignment observed (yes / no)
  • Visible cracks or damage
  • Final disposition (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Not every touchscreen issue can be corrected through software or cleaning — sometimes the touch panel itself fails or drifts out of calibration.

Completing these steps ensures you have:

  • ✔ ruled out simple external causes
  • ✔ protected patient safety
  • ✔ documented properly
  • ✔ avoided unnecessary guesswork

That is successful troubleshooting.
