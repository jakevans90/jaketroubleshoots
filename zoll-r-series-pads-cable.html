ZOLL R Series Defibrillator

Pads Not Detected / Therapy Cable Failure

Asset Type

Defibrillator

Manufacturer

ZOLL

Model

R Series

What This Guide Helps With

This guide walks you through simple checks when a ZOLL R Series defibrillator reports that the therapy pads are not detected, often due to a therapy cable failure. These steps focus on the most common and easiest things to verify first before assuming the cable or device has failed.

You do not need advanced technical skills to perform these checks.

Step-by-Step Troubleshooting

1. Check That the Pads Are Connected Properly

  • Ensure both therapy pads are securely attached to the therapy cable.
  • Confirm the pads are fully inserted into the connector port on the defibrillator.
  • Loose connections can prevent the device from recognizing the pads.

2. Inspect the Pads and Cable for Damage

Look for:

  • Cracked or torn pads
  • Exposed wires
  • Frayed or twisted cable

Replace pads or cable if any visible damage is present. Even small damage can cause the device to detect a failure.

3. Try a Known-Good Pad Set or Cable (If Available)

  • Remove the current pads or therapy cable and connect a known-good set.
  • Observe if the defibrillator recognizes them correctly.
  • This helps determine whether the issue is with the cable, pads, or device itself.

4. Observe the Device Display

  • Note exactly what the display or status indicator shows: “Pads Not Detected” or “Therapy Cable Failure”.
  • Pay attention to whether the message appears immediately or intermittently.
  • Even small details help identify intermittent cable or connector issues.

5. Check Pad Expiration

  • Verify that the pads are within their expiration date.
  • Expired pads may not properly connect or be recognized by the device.

If the Pads Still Are Not Detected

If the defibrillator still reports pads are not detected after completing the steps above, that’s okay — you’ve already addressed the most common and simplest causes.

You have already:

  • ensured the pads are connected properly
  • inspected for visible damage
  • tested with a known-good pad set or cable
  • checked pad expiration

The best next step is to:

  • remove the defective pads/cable from clinical use
  • label them as Out of Service
  • replace with a known-good set or send for evaluation

Knowing when to escalate is part of safe and effective troubleshooting.

Work Order Documentation (CCR Method)

CCR = Complaint, Cause, Resolution

Complaint

What was reported by the user.

Example: “Defibrillator reports pads not detected / therapy cable failure.”

Cause

What you observed or determined during troubleshooting.

Example: “Pads and cable inspected, no visible damage. Tested with known-good pads/cable: issue resolved. Suspected faulty therapy cable.”

Resolution

What action was taken.

Examples:
“Removed faulty cable/pads from service and replaced with known-good set.”
or
“Tagged pads/cable out of service and sent for evaluation.”

Helpful Details to Include (If Known)

  • Pads/cable reseated: yes / no
  • Pads/cable inspected for damage: yes / no
  • Alternate pads/cable tested: yes / no
  • Device display message
  • Expiration date of pads
  • Final status (returned to service or sent for repair)

Final Thought

Pads and therapy cables are consumable and subject to wear. A “pads not detected” alarm prevents the device from delivering therapy with a potentially unreliable connection.

Completing these steps means you have:

  • ✔ ruled out simple connection or cable issues
  • ✔ protected patient safety
  • ✔ documented properly
  • ✔ avoided unnecessary guesswork

That is successful troubleshooting.
