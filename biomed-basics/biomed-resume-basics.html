Biomed Resume Basics

How to Show What You Actually Do

A practical guide for BMETs, clinical engineers, students, and HTM staff who need to turn real biomedical equipment work into a stronger resume.

Back to Biomed Basics

What This Page Explains

Biomed resumes can be weirdly hard to write.

A BMET may work on patient monitors, infusion pumps, beds, defibrillators, telemetry systems, integration issues, PMs, repairs, recalls, parts, vendors, documentation, and clinical support every week.

Then the resume says:

Performed preventive maintenance and repaired medical equipment.

That is true, but it barely explains anything.

This page is about building a stronger biomed resume by showing equipment scope, troubleshooting ability, documentation, safety awareness, clinical communication, vendor coordination, project work, and the actual value you bring to a healthcare technology program.

The goal is not to exaggerate.

The goal is to describe the work clearly enough that someone outside your shop understands what you actually do.

Why Biomed Resumes Are Hard to Write

Biomed work is broad.

One day you may be replacing a broken NIBP connector on a patient monitor.

The next day you may be chasing a telemetry communication problem, checking an electrical safety failure, coordinating vendor service on imaging equipment, documenting a PM failure, or explaining to a department why an accessory issue is not a device failure.

The hard part is that all of that can get flattened into vague resume language.

Examples of vague biomed resume language include:

  • Performed PMs
  • Repaired equipment
  • Worked with vendors
  • Maintained documentation
  • Supported hospital departments

Those statements are not wrong.

They are just too generic.

A stronger resume helps the reader understand the type of equipment, the type of problems, the level of responsibility, and the clinical environment you supported.

The Simple Version

A good biomed resume should answer five basic questions:

  • What equipment do you work on?
  • What kind of problems do you solve?
  • What level of risk or clinical environment do you support?
  • What systems, software, documentation, or compliance work are you responsible for?
  • What makes your experience stronger than a generic repair statement?

In plain English:

Do not just say you fix medical equipment. Show the equipment, the troubleshooting, the documentation, the clinical impact, and the systems you support.

What Hiring Managers Need to See

A hiring manager, supervisor, recruiter, or hospital HR screen may be trying to figure out whether you can support the equipment and workflow they actually have.

They may look for:

  • BMET, clinical engineering, HTM, or biomedical equipment experience
  • Preventive maintenance experience
  • Corrective maintenance and troubleshooting experience
  • Electrical safety testing experience
  • CMMS or work order documentation experience
  • Experience with common hospital equipment types
  • Experience working with clinical staff
  • Vendor coordination experience
  • Networking, integration, or software exposure
  • Certifications, training, or degree information
  • Evidence that you understand patient safety and clinical risk

Your resume should make those things easy to find.

Show Equipment Scope

Equipment scope matters.

A resume that says “repaired medical equipment” does not tell the reader whether you worked on low-risk accessories, general biomedical equipment, life support equipment, OR equipment, imaging support systems, integration systems, or a mix of everything.

Add real equipment categories when they are true.

Examples:

  • Patient monitors
  • Telemetry equipment
  • Infusion pumps
  • Syringe pumps
  • Defibrillators
  • Bladder scanners
  • Vital signs monitors
  • Beds and stretchers
  • Suction regulators
  • Compression devices
  • Ventilators
  • Anesthesia support equipment
  • ESUs
  • OR tables and booms
  • ECG machines
  • Stress testing equipment
  • Pulmonary function systems
  • Device integration hardware or software

You do not need to list every model you have ever touched.

But you should give enough scope that the reader understands your real clinical engineering exposure.

Show Troubleshooting, Not Just PMs

Preventive maintenance is important, but troubleshooting shows how you think.

A stronger resume explains that you can verify complaints, isolate problems, test equipment, identify accessory issues, replace failed parts, escalate appropriately, and return equipment safely to service.

Weak:

Fixed equipment.

Better:

Troubleshot reported equipment failures by verifying complaints, isolating accessory/device/user setup issues, performing functional checks, replacing failed parts, and documenting corrective actions.

That tells a much better story.

Show Risk and Patient Care Awareness

Biomed is not just repair work.

It happens in a patient care environment.

Your resume should show that you understand clinical risk, downtime, patient safety, and when equipment should not be returned to use.

Examples:

  • Removed unsafe or unreliable equipment from service pending repair or evaluation
  • Performed functional checks before returning equipment to clinical use
  • Communicated equipment status and downtime concerns to clinical departments
  • Escalated safety-related issues according to department policy
  • Supported high-use clinical areas where equipment uptime mattered

That kind of language shows judgment, not just tool use.

Show Systems, Software, and Integration Work

Modern biomed work increasingly overlaps with IT, networking, integration, and software-supported equipment.

If you support any of that, say it clearly.

Examples may include:

  • Device integration workflows
  • Patient monitoring networks
  • Telemetry systems
  • Central stations
  • Gateway devices
  • Middleware or interface troubleshooting
  • EMR-connected medical devices
  • Networked medical equipment
  • Software configuration support
  • Communication between devices and clinical systems

Weak:

Worked with IT.

Better:

Supported networked medical equipment and device integration workflows by assisting with connectivity checks, device configuration, central station communication, and escalation between clinical engineering, IT, vendors, and clinical departments.

Show Documentation and Compliance

Documentation is a major part of biomed work.

If your work orders are clear, complete, and useful, that is a skill.

Resume language can include:

  • Maintained service history in a CMMS
  • Documented complaints, findings, corrective actions, parts, test results, and return-to-service checks
  • Completed scheduled PM documentation according to department policy
  • Recorded failed PMs, repair findings, and escalation details
  • Supported audit readiness through accurate equipment service records

Documentation is not just paperwork.

Good documentation shows what happened, what was found, what was corrected, and why the equipment was safe to return to use.

For more on writing clearer work order notes, see Biomed Work Order Notes Using the CCR Method.

Show Vendor Coordination

Vendor coordination is not just “called vendor.”

It may include troubleshooting, quote review, parts coordination, scheduling, service follow-up, downtime communication, warranty questions, and making sure the issue actually gets resolved.

Weak:

Worked with vendors.

Better:

Coordinated vendor service for escalated repairs, quotes, parts availability, and equipment downtime while communicating repair status to clinical departments.

That shows ownership.

Show Project Work

Project work can separate your resume from someone who only lists daily repair tasks.

Examples:

  • Equipment replacements
  • New device deployments
  • Incoming inspections
  • Department moves
  • Device integration go-lives
  • Recall or field correction support
  • PM cleanup projects
  • Asset inventory corrections
  • Standardization efforts
  • Accessory or cable management improvements
  • Training support for clinical departments

A project bullet should show what changed because of your work.

Example:

Assisted with deployment and validation of networked patient care equipment, including incoming inspection, device configuration, functional testing, clinical coordination, and CMMS documentation.

Show Certifications and Training

Certifications, degrees, manufacturer training, and relevant coursework can help, especially when they match the role you are applying for.

Include items such as:

  • BMET, biomedical equipment, electronics, or clinical engineering education
  • CBET, CRES, CLES, CET, BMD, or related certifications when applicable
  • Manufacturer service training
  • Electrical safety training
  • Networking or IT-related training
  • Cybersecurity or medical device security training
  • Software or clinical systems training

Do not bury certifications at the bottom if they are important for the job.

Make them easy to find.

Entry-Level BMET Resume Tips

If you are new to biomed, you may not have a long list of hospital equipment experience yet.

That is okay.

Focus on transferable skills:

  • Electronics troubleshooting
  • Mechanical repair
  • Test equipment use
  • Electrical safety awareness
  • Customer service
  • Documentation habits
  • Computer and networking basics
  • Internships, labs, military experience, field service, or repair experience
  • Willingness to learn clinical equipment and hospital workflow

Entry-level resumes should show that you are trainable, careful, technically curious, and able to work around clinical staff professionally.

Experienced BMET Resume Tips

If you already have biomed experience, your resume should not read like an entry-level task list.

Show scope and independence.

Include:

  • Equipment categories you support
  • Departments or clinical areas you support
  • Troubleshooting responsibility
  • PM completion and repair support
  • Vendor coordination
  • Parts and downtime follow-up
  • CMMS documentation
  • Safety or escalation judgment
  • Any specialty areas you own

Do not make the reader guess what kind of biomed you are.

Lead or Senior BMET Resume Tips

Senior or lead-level resumes should show more than technical repair ability.

They should show ownership, judgment, coordination, and influence.

Examples:

  • Supported higher-risk or specialty equipment areas
  • Assisted junior technicians
  • Reviewed difficult troubleshooting cases
  • Coordinated projects or go-lives
  • Improved PM or documentation processes
  • Served as point of contact for specific departments or systems
  • Worked across Biomed, IT, vendors, supply chain, and clinical leadership
  • Helped reduce repeat failures, downtime, or unclear documentation

Senior experience should read like responsibility, not just years on the job.

Bad Resume Bullets vs Better Resume Bullets

These examples are not meant to be copied blindly.

Use them as a way to think about your own work more clearly.

Preventive Maintenance

Weak:

Performed PMs on medical equipment.

Better:

Performed scheduled preventive maintenance, functional checks, electrical safety testing, and service documentation for patient care equipment in a hospital clinical engineering program.

Corrective Maintenance

Weak:

Fixed broken equipment.

Better:

Troubleshot reported medical equipment failures by verifying complaints, isolating device, accessory, setup, or user-related causes, completing functional checks, and documenting corrective actions.

Clinical Support

Weak:

Helped nurses with equipment.

Better:

Responded to clinical equipment support requests, verified reported issues with staff, explained equipment status, and coordinated safe return-to-service or escalation when needed.

Vendor Work

Weak:

Worked with vendors.

Better:

Coordinated vendor service for escalated repairs, parts quotes, warranty questions, and equipment downtime while maintaining communication with clinical departments and documenting service activity.

Device Integration

Weak:

Worked on device integration.

Better:

Supported medical device integration workflows by assisting with connectivity checks, device identification, central station communication, middleware/vendor escalation, and troubleshooting between clinical equipment and hospital information systems.

Examples by Equipment Type

Equipment-specific bullets can make your resume more believable and searchable.

Patient Monitoring

Supported bedside monitoring equipment, telemetry workflows, central station communication, parameter modules, ECG/SpO2/NIBP accessories, and clinical troubleshooting requests.

Infusion Pumps

Performed PMs, functional checks, corrective repairs, battery checks, channel/module troubleshooting, and documentation for infusion pump systems used in patient care areas.

Defibrillators

Completed scheduled checks and corrective maintenance support for defibrillators, including functional testing, accessory inspection, battery-related concerns, and return-to-service documentation.

Beds and Stretchers

Troubleshot bed and stretcher complaints involving power, controls, movement, scale functions, nurse call connections, siderails, brakes, and clinical usability concerns.

Networked or Integrated Devices

Assisted with troubleshooting networked medical equipment by verifying device identity, location, connectivity, configuration, communication path, and escalation details for IT or vendor support.

How to Describe PM Work

PM work should not sound like button-clicking.

Strong PM language can include:

  • Scheduled preventive maintenance
  • Functional verification
  • Electrical safety testing
  • Battery/runtime checks
  • Alarm and indicator checks
  • Accessory inspection
  • Manufacturer procedure review
  • Failed PM troubleshooting
  • CMMS documentation
  • Return-to-service decisions

Better PM bullet:

Performed scheduled preventive maintenance and safety checks by following manufacturer procedures, verifying device function, documenting test results, and escalating failed PM findings for corrective repair.

How to Describe Troubleshooting Work

Troubleshooting bullets should show how you move from complaint to cause to resolution.

You can describe:

  • Complaint verification
  • Visual inspection
  • Accessory checks
  • Power and battery checks
  • Functional testing
  • Error or alarm review
  • Parts replacement
  • Software or configuration checks
  • Vendor escalation
  • Return-to-service testing

Better troubleshooting bullet:

Investigated reported device failures by confirming symptoms, checking accessories and setup, isolating likely causes, performing functional testing, replacing failed components when applicable, and documenting the final resolution.

How to Describe Device Integration Work

Device integration can be difficult to explain because it sits between equipment, IT, vendors, and clinical systems.

Resume language can include:

  • Connected medical devices
  • Gateway devices
  • Central stations
  • Patient association workflows
  • Device identifiers
  • HL7 or EMR-adjacent workflows
  • Network communication checks
  • Middleware or interface escalation
  • Clinical data flow troubleshooting

Better integration bullet:

Supported clinical device integration by troubleshooting communication between patient care equipment, network infrastructure, middleware, central stations, and hospital information systems while coordinating with IT, vendors, and clinical departments.

For a plain-English introduction to this topic, see Hospital EMRs and Medical Device Integration.

How to Describe Electrical Safety Testing

Electrical safety testing is worth mentioning if it is part of your PM or incoming inspection workflow.

Strong language may include:

  • Ground resistance testing
  • Leakage current testing
  • Applied part testing
  • Class I or Class II equipment awareness
  • Safety analyzer use
  • Failed electrical safety escalation
  • Documentation of safety test results

Better bullet:

Performed electrical safety testing and documented results as part of PM, incoming inspection, and return-to-service workflows for clinical equipment.

For a deeper explanation, see Electrical Safety Testing for Medical Equipment.

How to Describe CMMS / Work Order Experience

CMMS experience is more than closing tickets.

You may be documenting service history, PM completion, failed checks, parts usage, downtime, vendor service, department communication, and asset status.

Better bullet:

Maintained accurate CMMS records by documenting equipment complaints, troubleshooting findings, corrective actions, parts usage, test results, vendor involvement, and return-to-service status.

If you help maintain asset records, add that too.

Example:

Assisted with equipment database accuracy by updating asset status, locations, PM details, model information, and service history as part of clinical engineering operations.

Common Resume Mistakes

Common biomed resume mistakes include:

  • Using only generic repair language
  • Not listing equipment types
  • Not showing troubleshooting ability
  • Forgetting documentation and CMMS experience
  • Leaving out electrical safety testing
  • Ignoring software, networking, or integration experience
  • Listing every small task but not showing responsibility
  • Using acronyms without context
  • Exaggerating skills beyond actual experience
  • Making the resume too long without adding useful detail
  • Not tailoring the resume to the job posting

The biggest mistake is making real experience sound smaller than it is.

Connect This to a Biomed Job Search

A resume should connect to the jobs you are actually applying for.

If a job posting emphasizes PM completion, equipment repair, electrical safety, documentation, and customer service, make sure those items are easy to find on your resume.

If a role emphasizes device integration, networking, telemetry, monitoring, or clinical systems, make sure your related experience is not buried under a generic “repaired equipment” statement.

A job search is not only about finding openings.

It is also about understanding what those openings are asking for and making sure your resume clearly answers the question:

Can this person support the equipment, departments, documentation, and clinical environment we need help with?

You can also use the Biomed Jobs Search page to look for current BMET, clinical engineering, and HTM-related openings.

Final Thoughts

A strong biomed resume does not need to sound fancy.

It needs to be clear.

Show the equipment you support.

Show the problems you troubleshoot.

Show that you understand documentation, patient safety, clinical workflow, and escalation.

Show the systems, vendors, departments, and projects you have been trusted with.

Do not exaggerate.

But do not undersell yourself either.

A lot of biomed work is invisible when it is done well.

Your resume is where you make that work visible.
